It is the\n       <a href="/wiki/Energy" title="Energy">\n        energy\n       </a>\n       associated with the motion and position of an object. The principle of conservation of mechanical energy states that in an isolated system that is only subject to\n       <a href="/wiki/Conservative_force" title="Conservative force">\n        conservative forces\n       </a>\n       the mechanical energy is constant. If an object is moved in the opposite direction of a conservative net force, the potential energy will increase and if the\n       <a href="/wiki/Speed" title="Speed">\n        speed\n       </a>\n       (not the\n       <a href="/wiki/Velocity" title="Velocity">\n        velocity\n       </a>\n       ) of the object is changed, the kinetic energy of the object is changed as well. In all real systems, however,\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nonconservative_force" title="Nonconservative force">\n        nonconservative forces\n       </a>\n       , like\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Frictional_force" title="Frictional force">\n        frictional forces\n       </a>\n       , will be present, but often they are of negligible values and the mechanical energy\'s being constant can therefore be a useful approximation. In\n       <a href="/wiki/Elastic_collision" title="Elastic collision">\n        elastic collisions\n       </a>\n       , the mechanical energy is conserved but in\n       <a href="/wiki/Inelastic_collision" title="Inelastic collision">\n        inelastic collisions\n       </a>\n       , some mechanical energy is converted into heat. The equivalence between lost mechanical energy (\n       <a href="/wiki/Dissipation" title="Dissipation">\n        dissipation\n       </a>\n       ) and an increase in\n       <a Prescott Joule\n       </a>\n       .\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       Many devices are used to convert mechanical energy to or from other forms of energy, e.g. an\n       <a href="/wiki/Electric_motor" point to another, the work done by the conservative force is independent of the path. On the contrary, when a non-conservative force acts upon an object, the work done by the non-conservative force is dependent of the path.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MIT_Physics_13-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-MIT_Physics-13">\n         [11]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">\n        <a href="#cite_note-14">\n         [12]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <h2>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Conservation_of_mechanical_energy">\n        Conservation of mechanical energy\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Mechanical_energy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Conservation of mechanical energy">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h2>\n      <div class="thumb tright">\n       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;">\n        <div class="PopUpMediaTransform" id="mwe_player_0" style="width:200px;" videopayload=\'&lt;div class="mediaContainer" style="width:320px"&gt;&lt;video id="mwe_player_1" poster="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c4/Physicsworks.ogv/320px--Physicsworks.ogv.jpg" controls="" preload="none" autoplay="" style="width:320px;height:240px" class="kskin" data-durationhint="203.33333333333" data-startoffset="0" data-mwtitle="Physicsworks.ogv" data-mwprovider="wikimediacommons"&gt;&lt;source src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/c/c4/Physicsworks.ogv" type="video/ogg; In any real situation, frictional forces and other non-conservative forces are present, but in many cases their effects on the system are so small that the principle of conservation of mechanical energy can be used as a fair\n       <a href="/wiki/Approximation" title="Approximation">\n        approximation\n       </a>\n       . Though energy cannot be created or destroyed in an isolated system, it can be\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Energy_conversion" title="Energy conversion">\n        converted\n       </a>\n       to another form of energy.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Access_CL_1-3">\n        <a href="#cite_note-Access_CL-1">\n         [1]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Access_CoE_15-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-Access_CoE-15">\n         [13]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <h3>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Swinging_pendulum">\n        Swinging pendulum\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Mechanical_energy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Swinging pendulum">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h3>\n      <div class="thumb tright">\n       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;">\n        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Pendulum_animation.gif">\n         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="231" data-file-width="257" height="180" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/21/Pendulum_animation.gif/200px-Pendulum_animation.gif" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/2/21/Pendulum_animation.gif 1.5x" width="200"/>\n        </a>\n        <div class="thumbcaption">\n         <div class="magnify">\n          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Pendulum_animation.gif" title="Enlarge">\n          </a>\n         </div>\n         A swinging pendulum with the velocity vector (green) and acceleration vector (blue). The magnitude of the velocity vector, the speed, of the pendulum is greatest in the vertical position and the pendulum is farthest from Earth in its extreme positions.\n        </div>\n       </div>\n      </div>\n      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">\n       Main article:\n       <a href="/wiki/Pendulum" title="Pendulum">\n        Pendulum\n       </a>\n      </div>\n      <p>\n       In a\n       <a href="/wiki/Mechanical_system" title="Mechanical system">\n        mechanical system\n       </a>\n       like a swinging\n       <a href="/wiki/Pendulum" title="Pendulum">\n        pendulum\n       </a>\n       subjected to the conservative\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gravitational_force" title="Gravitational force">\n        gravitational force\n       </a>\n       where frictional forces like air drag and friction at the pivot are negligible, energy passes back and forth between kinetic and potential energy but never leaves the system. The pendulum reaches greatest kinetic energy and least potential energy when in the vertical position, because it will have the greatest speed and be nearest the Earth at this point. On the other hand, it will have its least kinetic energy and greatest potential energy at the extreme positions of its swing, because it has zero speed and is farthest from Earth at these points. However, when taking the frictional forces into account, the system loses mechanical energy with each swing because of the work done by the pendulum to oppose these non-conservative forces.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Britannica_mechE_2-1">\n        <a href="#cite_note-Britannica_mechE-2">\n         [2]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <h3>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Irreversibilities">\n        Irreversibilities\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Mechanical_energy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Irreversibilities">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h3>\n      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">\n       Main article:\n       <a href="/wiki/Irreversible_process" title="Irreversible process">\n        Irreversible process\n       </a>\n      </div>\n      <p>\n       That the loss of mechanical energy in a system always resulted in an increase of the system\'s temperature has been known for a long time, but it was the amateur physicist\n       <a href="/wiki/James_Prescott_Joule" title="James Prescott Joule">\n        James Prescott Joule\n       </a>\n       who first experimentally demonstrated how a certain amount of work done against friction resulted in a definite quantity of\n       <a href="/wiki/Heat" title="Heat">\n        heat\n       </a>\n       which should be conceived as the random motions of the particles that comprise matter.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">\n        <a href="#cite_note-16">\n         [14]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       This equivalence between mechanical energy and heat is especially important when considering colliding objects. In an\n       <a href="/wiki/Elastic_collision" title="Elastic collision">\n        elastic collision\n       </a>\n       , mechanical energy is conserved \xe2\x80\x93 the sum of the mechanical energies of the colliding objects is the same before and after the collision. After an\n       <a href="/wiki/Inelastic_collision" title="Inelastic collision">\n        inelastic collision\n       </a>\n       , however, the mechanical energy of the system will have changed. Usually, the mechanical energy before the collision is greater than the mechanical energy after the collision. In inelastic collisions, some of the mechanical energy of the colliding objects is transformed into kinetic energy of the constituent particles. This increase in kinetic energy of the constituent particles is perceived as an increase in temperature. The collision can be described by saying some of the mechanical energy of the colliding objects has been converted into an equal amount of heat. Thus, the total energy of the system remains unchanged though the mechanical energy of the system has reduced.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Access_CL_1-4">\n        <a
