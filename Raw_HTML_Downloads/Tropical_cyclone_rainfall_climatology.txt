to determine rain, storm size, and degree of vertical wind shear. One of the most significant threats from\n       <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone" title="Tropical cyclone">\n        tropical cyclones\n       </a>\n       is heavy rainfall. Large, slow moving, and non-sheared tropical cyclones produce the heaviest rains. The intensity of a tropical cyclone appears to have little bearing on its potential for rainfall over land, but satellite measurements over the last several years show that more intense tropical cyclones produce noticeably more rainfall over water. Flooding from tropical cyclones remains a significant cause of fatalities, particularly in low-lying areas.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n      </p>\n      <div class="toc" id="toc">\n       <div id="Anticipating_a_flood_event">\n        Anticipating a flood event\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Tropical_cyclone_rainfall_climatology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Anticipating a flood event">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h2>\n      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">\n       See also:\n       <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone_rainfall_forecasting" title="Tropical cyclone rainfall forecasting">\n        Tropical cyclone rainfall forecasting\n       </a>\n      </div>\n      <p>\n       While inland\n       <a href="/wiki/Flood" title="Flood">\n        flooding\n       </a>\n       is common to tropical cyclones, there are factors which lead to excessive rainfall from tropical cyclones. Slow motion, as was seen during\n       <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Danny_(1997)" title="Hurricane Danny (1997)">\n        Hurricane Danny (1997)\n       </a>\n       and\n       <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Wilma" title="Hurricane Wilma">\n        Hurricane Wilma\n       </a>\n       , can lead to high amounts of rainfall. The presence of mountains/hills near the coast, like across much of\n       <a href="/wiki/Mexico" title="Mexico">\n        Mexico\n       </a>\n       ,\n       <a href="/wiki/Haiti" title="Haiti">\n        Haiti\n       </a>\n       , the\n       <a href="/wiki/Dominican_Republic" title="Dominican Republic">\n        Dominican Republic\n       </a>\n       ,\n       <a href="/wiki/Central_America" title="Central America">\n        Central America\n       </a>\n       ,\n       <a href="/wiki/Madagascar" title="Madagascar">\n        Madagascar\n       </a>\n       ,\n       <a href="/wiki/R%C3%A9union" title="R\xc3\xa9union">\n        R\xc3\xa9union\n       </a>\n       ,\n       <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">\n        China\n       </a>\n       , and\n       <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">\n        Japan\n       </a>\n       acts to magnify rainfall potential due to forced upslope flow into the mountains. Strong upper level forcing from a trough moving through the Westerlies and its associated\n       <a href="/wiki/Cold_front" title="Cold front">\n        cold front\n       </a>\n       , as was the case during\n       <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Floyd" title="Hurricane Floyd">\n        Hurricane Floyd\n       </a>\n       , can lead to high amounts even from systems moving at an average forward motion. Larger tropical cyclones drop more rainfall as they precipitate upon one spot for a longer time frame than average or small tropical cyclones. A combination of two of these factors could be especially crippling, as was seen during\n       <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Mitch" title="Hurricane Mitch">\n        Hurricane Mitch\n       </a>\n       in\n       <a href="/wiki/Central_America" title="Central America">\n        Central America\n       </a>\n       .\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Are_You_Ready.3F_1-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-Are_You_Ready.3F-1">\n         [1]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       During the 2005 season, flooding related to slow-moving\n       <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Stan" title="Hurricane Stan">\n        Hurricane Stan\n       </a>\n       \'s broad circulation led to 1,662\xe2\x80\x932,000 deaths.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Standeaths_2-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-Standeaths-2">\n         [2]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <h2>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="General_distribution_within_a_tropical_cyclone">\n        General distribution within a tropical cyclone\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a    <td>\n         448\n        </td>\n        <td>\n         1.18\n        </td>\n        <td>\n         30\n        </td>\n       </tr>\n      </table>\n      <p>\n       <a href="/wiki/Isaac_Cline" title="Isaac Cline">\n        Isaac Cline\n       </a>\n       was the first to investigate\n       <a href="/wiki/Rain" title="Rain">\n        rainfall\n       </a>\n       distribution around tropical cyclones in the early 1900s. He found that a larger proportion of rainfall falls in advance of the center (or eye) than after the center\'s passage, with the highest percentage falling in the right front quadrant. Father Vi\xc3\xb1es of\n       <a href="/wiki/Cuba" title="Cuba">\n        Cuba\n       </a>\n       found that some tropical cyclones have their highest rainfall rates in the rear quadrant within a training (non-moving) inflow band.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">\n        <a href="#cite_note-3">\n         [3]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       Normally, as a tropical cyclone intensifies, its heavier rainfall rates become more concentrated around its center.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">\n        <a href="#cite_note-4">\n         [4]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       Rainfall is found to be heaviest in tropical cyclone\'s inner core, whether it be the\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eyewall" title="Eyewall">\n        eyewall\n       </a>\n       or\n       <a href="/wiki/Central_dense_overcast" title="Central dense overcast">\n        central dense overcast\n       </a>\n       , within a degree latitude of the center, with lesser amounts farther away from the center.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">\n        <a href="#cite_note-5">\n         [5]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       Most of the rainfall in tropical cyclones is concentrated within its radius of gale-force (34\xc2\xa0knots/39\xc2\xa0mph/63\xc2\xa0km/h) winds.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CORENE_6-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-CORENE-6">\n         [6]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       Rainfall is more common near the center of tropical cyclones overnight. Over land, outer bands are more active during the heating of the day, which can act to restrict inflow into the center of the cyclone. Recent studies have shown that half of the rainfall within a tropical cyclone is stratiform in nature.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DR072007_7-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-DR072007-7">\n         [7]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       The chart to the right was developed by Riehl in 1954 using meteorological equations that assume a gale radius of about 140 miles (230\xc2\xa0km), a fairly symmetric cyclone, and does not consider topographic effects or vertical wind shear. Local amounts can exceed this chart by a factor of two due to topography. Wind shear tends to lessen the amounts below what is shown on the table.\n      </p>\n      <h2>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Relation_to_storm_size">\n        Relation to storm size\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Tropical_cyclone_rainfall_climatology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Relation to storm size">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h2>\n      <div class="thumb tright">\n       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:290px;">\n        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Typhoonsizes.jpg">\n         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="180" data-file-width="288" height="180" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/4/4e/Typhoonsizes.jpg" width="288"/>\n        </a>\n        <div class="thumbcaption">\n         The relative sizes of\n         <a href="/wiki/Typhoon_Tip" title="Typhoon Tip">\n          Typhoon Tip\n         </a>\n         ,\n         <a href="/wiki/Cyclone_Tracy" title="Cyclone Tracy">\n          Cyclone Tracy\n         </a>\n         , and the United States.\n        </div>\n       </div>\n      </div>\n      <p>\n       Larger tropical cyclones have larger rain shields, which can lead to higher rainfall amounts farther from the cyclone\'s center.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CORENE_6-1">\n        <a href="#cite_note-CORENE-6">\n         [6]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       This is generally due to the longer time frame rainfall falls at any one spot in a larger system, when compared to a smaller system. Some of the difference seen concerning rainfall between larger and small storms could be the increased sampling of rainfall within a larger tropical cyclone when compared to that of a compact cyclone; in other words, the difference could be the result of a statistical problem.\n      </p>\n      <h2>\n       <span id="Slow/looping_motion_on_rainfall_magnitude">\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Slow.2Flooping_motion_on_rainfall_magnitude">\n        Slow/looping motion on rainfall magnitude\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Tropical_cyclone_rainfall_climatology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Slow/looping motion on rainfall magnitude">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h2>\n      <p>\n       Storms which have moved slowly, or loop, over a succession of days lead to the highest rainfall amounts for several countries. Riehl calculated that 33.97\xc2\xa0inches (863\xc2\xa0mm) of rainfall per day can be expected within one-half degree, or 35\xc2\xa0miles (56\xc2\xa0km), of the center of a mature tropical cyclone. Many tropical cyclones progress at a forward motion of 10\xc2\xa0knots, which would limit the duration of this excessive rainfall to around one-quarter of a day, which would yield about 8.50\xc2\xa0inches (216\xc2\xa0mm) of rainfall. This would be true over water, within 100\xc2\xa0miles (160\xc2\xa0km) of the coastline,\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">\n        <a href="#cite_note-8">\n         [8]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       and outside topographic features. As a cyclone moves farther inland and is cut off from its supply of warmth and moisture (the ocean), rainfall amounts from tropical cyclones and their remains decrease quickly.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-statemaxima_9-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-statemaxima-9">\n         [9]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <h2>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Vertical_wind_shear_impact_on_rainfall_shield">\n        Vertical wind shear impact on rainfall shield\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Tropical_cyclone_rainfall_climatology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Vertical wind shear impact on rainfall shield">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h2>\n      <p>\n       Vertical\n       <a href="/wiki/Wind_shear" title="Wind shear">\n        wind shear\n       </a>\n       forces the rainfall pattern around a tropical cyclone to become highly asymmetric, with most of the precipitation falling to the left and downwind of the shear vector, or downshear left. In other words, southwesterly shear forces the bulk of the rainfall north-northeast of the center.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">\n        <a href="#cite_note-10">\n         [10]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       If the wind shear is strong enough, the bulk of the rainfall will move away from the center leading to what is known as an exposed circulation center. When this occurs, the potential magnitude of rainfall with the tropical cyclone will be significantly reduced.\n      </p>\n      <h3>\n       <span id="Effect_of_interaction_with_frontal_boundaries/upper_level_troughs">\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Effect_of_interaction_with_frontal_boundaries.2Fupper_level_troughs">\n        Effect of interaction with frontal boundaries/upper level troughs\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Tropical_cyclone_rainfall_climatology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Effect of interaction with frontal boundaries/upper level troughs">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h3>\n      <p>\n       As a\n       <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone" title="Tropical cyclone">\n        tropical cyclone\n       </a>\n       interacts with an upper-level\n       <a href="/wiki/Trough_(meteorology)" title="Trough (meteorology)">\n        trough\n       </a>\n       and the related\n       <a href="/wiki/Surface_weather_analysis" title="Surface weather analysis">\n        surface front\n       </a>\n       , a distinct northern area of precipitation is seen along the front ahead of the axis of the upper level trough. This type of interaction can lead to the appearance of the heaviest rainfall falling along and to the left of the tropical cyclone track, with the precipitation streaking hundreds of miles or kilometers downwind from the tropical cyclone.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WES_11-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-WES-11">\n         [11]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       The stronger the upper trough picking up the tropical cyclone, the more significant the left of track shift in the rainfall distribution tends to be.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DR072007_7-1">\n        <a href="#cite_note-DR072007-7">\n         [7]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <h2>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Mountains">\n        Mountains\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Tropical_cyclone_rainfall_climatology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Mountains">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h2>\n      <p>\n       Moist air forced up the slopes of coastal hills and mountain chains can lead to much heavier rainfall than in the coastal plain. This heavy rainfall can lead to landslides, which still cause significant loss of life such as seen during\n       <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Mitch" title="Hurricane Mitch">\n        Hurricane Mitch\n       </a>\n       in\n       <a href="/wiki/Central_America" title="Central America">\n        Central America\n       </a>\n       .\n      </p>\n      <h2>\n       <span class="mw-headline" class="thumbcaption">\n         <div class="magnify">\n          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Globaltcrainfalldistribution2005.jpg" title="Enlarge">\n          </a>\n         </div>\n         Global tropical cyclone rainfall in 2005\n        </div>\n       </div>\n      </div>\n      <p>\n       Globally, tropical cyclone rainfall is more common across the northern hemisphere than across the southern hemisphere. This is mainly due to the normal annual tropical cyclone distribution, as between half and two-thirds of all tropical cyclones form north of the equator. Rainfall is concentrated near the 15th parallel in both hemispheres, with a less steep dropoff seen with latitude across the northern hemisphere, due to the stronger warm water currents seen in that hemisphere which allow tropical cyclones to remain tropical in nature at higher latitudes than south of the equator. In the southern hemisphere, rainfall impacts will be most common between January and March, while north of the equator, tropical cyclone rainfall impacts are more common between June and November.\n href="/wiki/Hurricane_Katrina" title="Hurricane Katrina">\n        Hurricane Katrina\n       </a>\n       \'s impact alone shifted the most deadly aspect of tropical cyclones back to\n       <a The highest point total was seen during\n       <a href="/wiki/Tropical_Storm_Amelia_(1978)" title="Tropical Storm Amelia (1978)">\n        Amelia 1978\n       </a>\n       , when 48\xc2\xa0inches (1,218\xc2\xa0mm) fell upon central\n       <a href="/wiki/Texas" title="Texas">\n        Texas\n       </a>\n       .\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WPCMAX_15-0">\n        <a
