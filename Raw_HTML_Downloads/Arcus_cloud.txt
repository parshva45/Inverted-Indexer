cloud\n       </b>\n       is a low, horizontal\n       <a href="/wiki/Cloud" title="Cloud">\n        cloud\n       </a>\n       formation, usually appearing as an accessory cloud to a\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cumulonimbus" title="Cumulonimbus">\n        cumulonimbus\n       </a>\n       .\n       <b>\n        Roll clouds\n       </b>\n       and\n       <b>\n        shelf clouds\n       </b>\n       are the two main types of arcus. Arcus clouds most frequently form along the leading edge or "gust fronts" of\n       <a href="/wiki/Thunderstorm" title="Thunderstorm">\n        thunderstorm\n       </a>\n       <a href="/wiki/Outflow_boundary" title="Outflow boundary">\n        outflow\n       </a>\n       ; some of the most dramatic arcus formations mark the gust fronts of\n       <a href="/wiki/Derecho" title="Derecho">\n        derecho\n       </a>\n       -producing convective systems. Roll clouds also may arise in the absence of thunderstorms, forming along the shallow cold air currents of some\n       <a href="/wiki/Sea_breeze" title="Sea breeze">\n        sea breeze\n       </a>\n       boundaries and\n       <a href="/wiki/Cold_front" title="Cold front">\n        cold fronts\n       </a>\n       .\n      </p>\n      <p>\n      </p>\n      <div class="toc" id="toc">\n <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Arcus_cloud&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit </div>\n       </div>\n      </div>\n      <p>\n       A\n       <b>\n        shelf cloud\n       </b>\n       is a low, horizontal, wedge-shaped arcus cloud. A shelf cloud is attached to the base of the parent\n       <a href="/wiki/Cloud" title="Cloud">\n        cloud\n       </a>\n       , which is usually a thunderstorm, but could form on any type of convective clouds. Rising cloud motion often can be seen in the leading (outer) part of the shelf cloud, while the underside often appears\n       <a href="/wiki/Turbulence" title="Turbulence">\n        turbulent\n       </a>\n       and wind-torn. Cool, sinking air from a\n       <a href="/wiki/Thunderstorm" title="Thunderstorm">\n        storm cloud\n       </a>\n       \'s\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Downdraft" title="Downdraft">\n        downdraft\n       </a>\n       spreads out across the land surface, with the leading edge called a\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gust_front" title="Gust front">\n        gust front\n       </a>\n       . This outflow cuts under warm air being drawn into the storm\'s\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Updraft" title="Updraft">\n        updraft\n       </a>\n       . As the lower cooler air lifts the warm moist air, its water condenses, creating a cloud which often rolls with the different winds above and below (\n       <a href="/wiki/Wind_shear" title="Wind shear">\n        wind shear\n       </a>\n       ).\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       People seeing a shelf cloud may believe they have seen a\n       <a href="/wiki/Wall_cloud" title="Wall cloud">\n        wall cloud\n       </a>\n       . This is likely a mistake, since an approaching shelf cloud appears to form a wall made of cloud. A shelf cloud usually appears on the leading edge of a storm, and a wall cloud will usually be at the rear of the storm.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       A sharp, strong gust front will cause the lowest part of the leading edge of a shelf cloud to be ragged and lined with rising\n       <a href="/wiki/Fractus_cloud" title="Fractus cloud">\n        fractus clouds\n       </a>\n       . In a severe case there will be\n       <a href="/wiki/Vortex" title="Vortex">\n        vortices\n       </a>\n       along the edge, with twisting masses of\n       <a href="/wiki/Scud_(cloud)" title="Scud (cloud)">\n        scud\n       </a>\n       that may reach to the ground or be accompanied by rising dust. A very low shelf cloud accompanied by these signs is the best indicator that a potentially violent wind\n       <a href="/wiki/Squall" title="Squall">\n        squall\n       </a>\n       is approaching. An extreme example of this phenomenon looks almost like a tornado and is known as a\n       <a href="/wiki/Gustnado" title="Gustnado">\n        gustnado\n       </a>\n       .\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EC_1-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-EC-1">\n         [1]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <h3>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Roll_cloud">\n        <span id="volutus">\n        </span>\n        <span id="Volutus">\n        </span>\n        Roll cloud\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Arcus_cloud&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Roll cloud">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h3>\n      <p>\n       A\n       <b>\n        roll cloud\n       </b>\n       (\n       <i>\n        Cloud Atlas\n       </i>\n       name\n       <i>\n        <b>\n         volutus\n        </b>\n       </i>\n       ) is a low, horizontal, tube-shaped, and relatively rare type of arcus cloud. They differ from shelf clouds by being completely detached from other cloud features. Roll clouds usually appear to be "rolling" about a horizontal axis. They are a solitary wave called a\n       <a href="/wiki/Soliton" title="Soliton">\n        soliton\n       </a>\n       , which is a wave that has a single crest and moves without changing speed or shape. One of the most famous frequent occurrences is the\n       <a href="/wiki/Morning_Glory_cloud" title="Morning Glory cloud">\n        Morning Glory cloud\n       </a>\n       in\n       <a href="/wiki/Queensland" title="Queensland">\n        Queensland\n       </a>\n       ,\n       <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">\n        Australia\n       </a>\n       , which can occur up to four out of ten days in October.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">\n        <a href="#cite_note-2">\n         [2]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       One of the main causes of the Morning Glory cloud is the\n       <a href="/wiki/Mesoscale_meteorology" title="Mesoscale meteorology">\n        mesoscale\n       </a>\n       circulation associated with sea breezes that develop over the\n       <a href="/wiki/Cape_York_Peninsula" title="Cape York Peninsula">\n        Cape York Peninsula\n       </a>\n       and the\n       <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_Carpentaria" title="Gulf of Carpentaria">\n        Gulf of Carpentaria\n       </a>\n       . However, similar features can be created by\n       <a href="/wiki/Microburst" title="Microburst">\n        downdrafts\n       </a>\n       from thunderstorms and are not exclusively associated with coastal regions.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       Coastal roll clouds have been seen in many places, including\n       <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">\n        California\n       </a>\n       , the\n       <a href="/wiki/English_Channel" title="English Channel">\n        English Channel\n       </a>\n       ,\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shetland_Islands" title="Shetland Islands">\n        Shetland Islands\n       </a>\n       , the North Sea coast, coastal regions of Australia, and Nome, Alaska.\n      </p>\n      <div class="thumb tmulti tnone center">\n       <div
