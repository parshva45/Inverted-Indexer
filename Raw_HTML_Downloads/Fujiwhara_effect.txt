    </div>\n      </div>\n      <p>\n       The\n       <b>\n        Fujiwhara effect\n       </b>\n       , sometimes referred to as\n       <b>\n        Fujiwara interaction\n       </b>\n       or\n       <b>\n        binary interaction\n       </b>\n       , is when two nearby\n       <a href="/wiki/Cyclone" title="Cyclone">\n        cyclonic\n       </a>\n       <a href="/wiki/Vortex" title="Vortex">\n        vortices\n       </a>\n       <a href="/wiki/Orbit" title="Orbit">\n        orbit\n       </a>\n       each other and close the distance between the circulations of their corresponding\n       <a href="/wiki/Low-pressure_area" title="Low-pressure area">\n        low-pressure areas\n       </a>\n       . The effect is named after\n       <a href="/wiki/Sakuhei_Fujiwhara" title="Sakuhei Fujiwhara">\n        Sakuhei Fujiwhara\n       </a>\n       , the Japanese meteorologist who initially described the effect. Binary interaction of smaller circulations can cause the development of a larger cyclone, or cause two cyclones to merge into one.\n       <a href="/wiki/Extratropical_cyclone" title="Extratropical cyclone">\n        Extratropical cyclones\n       </a>\n       typically engage in binary interaction when within 2,000 kilometres (1,200\xc2\xa0mi) of one another, while\n       <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone" title="Tropical cyclone">\n        tropical cyclones\n       </a>\n       typically interact within 1,400 kilometres (870\xc2\xa0mi) of each other.\n      </p>\n  </div>\n      </div>\n      <p>\n       When cyclones are in proximity of one another, their centers will begin orbiting cyclonically (counter-clockwise in the Northern Hemisphere and clockwise in the Southern Hemisphere)\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">\n        <a href="#cite_note-2">\n         [2]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       about a point between the two systems due to their cyclonic wind circulations. The two vortices will be attracted to each other, and eventually spiral into the center point and merge. It has not been agreed upon whether this is due to the divergent portion of the\n       <a href="/wiki/Wind" title="Wind">\n        wind\n       </a>\n       or\n       <a href="/wiki/Vorticity" title="Vorticity">\n        vorticity\n       </a>\n       advection.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">\n        <a href="#cite_note-3">\n         [3]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       When the two vortices are of unequal size, the larger vortex will tend to dominate the interaction, and the smaller vortex will orbit around it. The effect is named after Sakuhei Fujiwhara, the Japanese meteorologist who initially described it in a 1921 paper about the motion of vortices in water.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fujiwhara1291_4-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-Fujiwhara1291-4">\n         [4]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">\n        <a href="#cite_note-5">\n         [5]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <h2>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Tropical_cyclones">\n        Tropical cyclones\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Fujiwhara_effect&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Tropical cyclones">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h2>\n      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">\n       See also:\n       <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone" title="Tropical cyclone">\n        Tropical cyclone\n       </a>\n      </div>\n      <p>\n       Tropical cyclones can\n       <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclogenesis" title="Tropical cyclogenesis">\n        form\n       </a>\n       when smaller circulations within the\n       <a href="/wiki/Intertropical_Convergence_Zone" title="Intertropical Convergence Zone">\n        Intertropical Convergence Zone\n       </a>\n       merge.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">\n        <a href="#cite_note-6">\n         [6]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       The effect is often mentioned in relation to the motion of tropical cyclones, although the final merging of the two storms is uncommon. The effect becomes noticeable when they approach within 1,400 kilometres (870\xc2\xa0mi) of each other. Rotation rates within binary pairs accelerate when tropical cyclones close within 650 kilometres (400\xc2\xa0mi) of each other.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ziv1995_7-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-Ziv1995-7">\n         [7]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       Merger of the two systems (or shearing out of one of the pair) becomes realized when they are within 300 kilometres (190\xc2\xa0mi) of one another.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">\n        <a href="#cite_note-8">\n         [8]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <h3>\n       <span Extratropical cyclone\n       </a>\n      </div>\n      <p>\n       Binary interaction is seen between nearby extratropical cyclones when within 2,000 kilometres (1,200\xc2\xa0mi) of each other, with significant acceleration occurring when the low-pressure areas are within 1,100 kilometres (680\xc2\xa0mi) of one another. Interactions between their circulations at the 500 hPa\xc2\xa0level (18,000\xc2\xa0feet above\n       <a href="/wiki/Sea_level" title="Sea level">\n        sea level\n       </a>\n       ) behave more predictably than their surface circulations.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ziv1995_7-1">\n        <a href="#cite_note-Ziv1995-7">\n         [7]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       This most often results in a merging of the two low-pressure systems into a single extratropical cyclone, or can less commonly result in a mere change of direction of one or both of the cyclones.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ExtraFujiwhara_18-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-ExtraFujiwhara-18">\n         [18]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       The precise results of such interactions depend on factors such as the size of the two cyclones, their distance from each other, and the prevailing atmospheric conditions around them.\n      </p>\n      <div style="clear:both;">\n
