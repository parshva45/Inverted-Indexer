Cupola\n        </div>\n       </div>\n      </div>\n      <p>\n       The Cupola provides an observation and work area for the ISS crew giving visibility to support the control of the space station\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Canadarm2" title="Canadarm2">\n        remote manipulator system\n       </a>\n       and general external viewing of\n       <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">\n        Earth\n       </a>\n       , celestial objects and visiting vehicles. Its name derives from Italian word\n       <i>\n        <span lang="it" xml:lang="it">\n         <a href="/wiki/Cupola" title="Cupola">\n          cupola\n         </a>\n        </span>\n       </i>\n       , which means "dome". The Cupola project was started by NASA and\n       <a href="/wiki/Boeing" title="Boeing">\n        Boeing\n       </a>\n       , but canceled due to budget cuts. A barter agreement between NASA and the\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ESA" title="ESA">\n        ESA\n       </a>\n       resulted in the Cupola\'s development being resumed in 1998 by ESA. The existence of the Cupola is considered important to astronauts aboard the ISS,\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">\n        <a href="#cite_note-2">\n         [2]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       as previously they have been confined to looking out of small\n       <a href="/wiki/Porthole" title="Porthole">\n        portholes\n       </a>\n       or at best the 20-inch (50\xc2\xa0cm) window in the US\n       <a href="/wiki/Destiny_(ISS_module)" title="Destiny (ISS module)">\n        <i>\n         Destiny\n        </i>\n        laboratory\n       </a>\n       . The Cupola is berthed onto the Earth-facing port of\n       <a href="/wiki/Tranquility_(ISS_module)" title="Tranquility (ISS module)">\n        Node 3\n       </a>\n       \xe2\x80\x94the final of three modules, including\n       <a href="/wiki/Unity_(ISS_module)" title="Unity (ISS module)">\n        Node 1\n       </a>\n       and\n       <a href="/wiki/Harmony_(ISS_module)" title="Harmony (ISS module)">\n        Node 2\n       </a>\n       . Because of its shape and multi window configuration the Cupola has been compared to the cockpit window of the\n       <a href="/wiki/Millennium_Falcon" title="Millennium Falcon">\n        Millennium Falcon\n       </a>\n       .\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-YT-1300_3-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-YT-1300-3">\n         [3]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <h2>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Design_and_construction">\n        Design and construction\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Cupola_(ISS_module)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Design and construction">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h2>\n      <p>\n       The International Space Station Cupola was first conceived in 1987 by Space Station Man-Systems Architectural Control Manager Gary Kitmacher as a workstation for operating the station\xe2\x80\x99s\n       <a href="/wiki/Mobile_Servicing_System" title="Mobile Servicing System">\n        Canadarm2 robotic arm\n       </a>\n       , maneuvering vehicles outside the station, and observing and supporting\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spacewalk" title="Spacewalk">\n        spacewalks\n       </a>\n       . He likened the use as similar to that of the\n       <a href="/wiki/Space_Shuttle_orbiter" title="Space Shuttle orbiter">\n        Shuttle Orbiter\n       </a>\n       Aft Flight Deck. There were to have been two Cupolas, one on either end of the racetrack shape formed by the station modules and nodes. It was initially named the "windowed workstation", to distinguish it from other computer-based workstations inside of the station and from which the crew could operate the station\xe2\x80\x99s systems. Once the idea was initially accepted, a number of people went to work. Human factors specialist Frances Mount began to develop the rationale and operational scenarios for the Cupola, and got considerable support from Chief Astronaut\n       <a href="/wiki/John_Young_(astronaut)" title="John Young (astronaut)">\n        John Young\n       </a>\n       and Shuttle Commander\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gordon_Fullerton" title="Gordon Fullerton">\n        Gordon Fullerton\n       </a>\n       . Charles Wheelwright, who had defined the specifications for every window on every prior United States manned spacecraft, began to define the design specifications of the Cupola windows. Laurie Weaver, who had just started with NASA as a student intern ("Co-Op"), began to work on a series of different configurations for the Cupola. She started with Kitmacher\xe2\x80\x99s idea, based on the Shuttle Aft Flight Deck, in this case two Aft Flight Decks mounted back to back, placed atop a short cylinder. An inexpensive mock-up made of\n       <a href="/wiki/Polyvinyl_chloride" title="Polyvinyl chloride">\n        PVC\n       </a>\n       tubes was built and tested underwater, where critical dimensions could be measured to ensure that two crew members in zero-g would have adequate access. Then she built a series of small cardboard models, looking at a variety of different alternative shapes. The different configurations and their positive and negative attributes were presented at a series of Crew Station Reviews over the next year in which participants rated each. The Cupola that evolved was octagonal in shape, with eight similar windows around the periphery, four quadrant windows overhead, and mounted on a cylinder. The module was designed to fully contain at least two crewmembers "floating" side by side in\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Zero-g" title="Zero-g">\n        zero-g\n       </a>\n       <a href="/wiki/Neutral_body_posture" title="Neutral body posture">\n        neutral body posture\n       </a>\n       . About this time, Kitmacher and designer Jay Cory applied the term Cupola for the first time. Kitmacher wrote the requirements and the name into the Man-Systems Architectural Control Document and into the\n       <a href="/wiki/Request_for_proposal" title="Request for proposal">\n        requests for proposals\n       </a>\n       for Work Package 1 at MSFC and Work Package 2 at\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lyndon_B._Johnson_Space_Center" title="Lyndon B. Johnson Space Center">\n        JSC\n       </a>\n       . Later, Kitmacher went on to lead the Man-Systems group, leading the first lunar outpost and moonbase studies and the Cupola reappeared on several of his rover and module designs.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">\n        <a href="#cite_note-4">\n         [4]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       Because of confusion between the responsibilities of the two contracts, both\n       <a href="/wiki/Boeing" title="Boeing">\n        Boeing\n       </a>\n       , which won Work Package 1, and\n       <a href="/wiki/McDonnell_Douglas" title="McDonnell Douglas">\n        McDonnell Douglas\n       </a>\n       , which won Work Package 2, bid to build the Cupola. The McDonnell Douglas design was basically the same as NASA\xe2\x80\x99s, but Boeing\xe2\x80\x99s was smaller\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x94 a hexagon, with a single large circular overhead window, and a much shorter cylinder. The Boeing design had been considered but previously discarded, because it was unable to accommodate two crew members completely, and would instead require the crew to dangle their legs and bodies in the Node to which the Cupola would be attached. This design was the one ultimately built. Components of the Cupola were initially fabricated in California, and the windows in New York in the late 1980s, but as budgets were cut, the Cupola was a favorite target. Several times it was fought back into the program only to be cut again as not technically required. More than once the responsibilities for fabrication were bartered to one international partner and then another in exchange for Shuttle launch services and resources that the United States would provide on board the station in orbit. The Cupola made the rounds to Brazil and then finally wound up with ESA and the Italians in 1998, who completed it in 2003. Even then, the launch of the Cupola was repeatedly delayed until it finally made it into orbit in February, 2010. The delivery method also changed from the Cupola being mounted on a Spacelab pallet accompanying\n       <i>\n        Kibo\'\n       </i>\n       s external facility to being mounted on the same European pallet with Node 3.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       With final design and assembly by the Italian contractor Alenia Spazio (now\n       <a href="/wiki/Thales_Alenia_Space" title="Thales Alenia Space">\n        Thales Alenia Space\n       </a>\n       ), it is nearly 3 metres in diameter and 1.5 metres tall. It has six side windows and a top window, all of which are equipped with shutters to protect them from damage by\n       <a href="/wiki/Micrometeoroid" title="Micrometeoroid">\n        micrometeoroids\n       </a>\n       and\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Orbital_debris" title="Orbital debris">\n        orbital debris\n       </a>\n       . It features a thermal control system, audio, video and\n       <a href="/wiki/MIL-STD-1553" title="MIL-STD-1553">\n        MIL-STD-1553\n       </a>\n       bus interfaces, as well as the connections needed for installing one of the two identical robotic workstations that control the\n       <a href="/wiki/Mobile_Servicing_System" title="Mobile Servicing System">\n        Canadarm2\n       </a>\n       into it.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">\n        <a href="#cite_note-5">\n         [5]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       The window shutters are manually controlled. Each window has a knob with a direct mechanical linkage to the outside shutter assembly.\n       <a href="/wiki/O-ring" title="O-ring">\n        O-rings\n       </a>\n       are used to prevent air leakage.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">\n        <a href="#cite_note-6">\n         [6]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <h2>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Installation">\n        Installation\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Cupola_(ISS_module)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Installation">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h2>\n      <p>\n       Cupola was launched aboard the\n       <a href="/wiki/Space_Shuttle" title="Space Shuttle">\n        Space Shuttle\n       </a>\n       on mission\n       <a href="/wiki/STS-130" title="STS-130">\n        STS-130\n       </a>\n       , on February 8, 2010. It was berthed to the forward port of the\n       <a href="/wiki/Tranquility_(ISS_module)" title="Tranquility (ISS module)">\n        Tranquility\n       </a>\n       module for launch, and was later transferred to the\n       <a href="/wiki/Nadir" title="Nadir">\n        nadir\n       </a>\n       -facing port of Tranquility by the Canadarm2, once Tranquility had been berthed to the\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Unity_Module" title="Unity Module">\n        Unity Module\n       </a>\n       of the ISS. The installation of the Cupola, along with Tranquility, marks one of the last main components to be added to the International Space Station.\n      </p>\n      <h2>\n       <span class="mw-headline"
