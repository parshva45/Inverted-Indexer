This is the major axis of the\n       <a href="/wiki/Ellipse" title="Ellipse">\n        ellipse\n       </a>\n       , its greatest diameter. For a two-body system the\n       <a href="/wiki/Center_of_mass" title="Center of mass">\n        center of mass\n       </a>\n       of the system lies on this line at one of the two\n       <a href="/wiki/Conic_section" title="Conic section">\n        foci\n       </a>\n       of the ellipse. When one body is sufficiently larger than the other it may be taken to be at this focus. However whether or not this is the case, both bodies are in\n       <a href="/wiki/Similarity_(geometry)" title="Similarity (geometry)">\n        similar\n       </a>\n       elliptical orbits each having one focus at the system\'s center of mass, with their respective lines of apsides being of length inversely proportional to their masses. Historically, in\n       <a href="/wiki/Geocentric_model" title="Geocentric model">\n        geocentric systems\n       </a>\n       , apsides were measured from the center of the Earth. However, in the case of the Moon, the center of mass of the Earth\xe2\x80\x93Moon system, or\n       <a href="/wiki/Barycenter" title="Barycenter">\n        Earth\xe2\x80\x93Moon barycenter\n       </a>\n       , as the common focus of both the Moon\'s and Earth\'s orbits about each other, is about 75% of the way from Earth\'s center to its surface.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       In\n       <a href="/wiki/Orbital_mechanics" title="Orbital mechanics">\n        orbital mechanics\n       </a>\n       , the apsis technically refers to the distance measured between the\n       <a href="/wiki/Center_of_mass" title="Center of mass">\n        centers of mass\n       </a>\n       of the central and orbiting body. However, in the case of\n       <a href="/wiki/Spacecraft" title="Spacecraft">\n        spacecraft\n       </a>\n       , the family of terms are commonly used to refer to the orbital\n       <a href="/wiki/Altitude" title="Altitude">\n        altitude\n       </a>\n       of the spacecraft from the surface of the central body (assuming a constant, standard reference radius).\n      </p>\n Landis">\n        Geoffrey A. Landis\n       </a>\n       in 1998 before\n       <i>\n        peri/aponigricon\n       </i>\n       (from a Latin) appeared in the scientific literature in 2002.\n       <sup class="reference" The variation of the seasons is primarily controlled by the annual cycle of the elevation angle of the Sun, which is a result of the tilt of the axis of the Earth measured from the\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Plane_of_the_ecliptic" title="Plane of the ecliptic">\n        plane of the ecliptic\n       </a>\n       . The Earth\'s\n       <a href="/wiki/Orbital_eccentricity" title="Orbital eccentricity">\n        eccentricity\n       </a>\n       and other orbital elements are not constant, but vary slowly due to the perturbing effects of the planets and other objects in the solar system. See\n       <a href="/wiki/Milankovitch_cycles" title="Milankovitch cycles">\n        Milankovitch cycles\n       </a>\n       .\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       On a very long time scale, the dates of the perihelion and of the aphelion progress through the seasons, and they make one complete cycle in 22,000 to 26,000 years. There is a corresponding movement of the position of the stars as seen from Earth that is called the\n       <a href="/wiki/Apsidal_precession" title="Apsidal precession">\n        apsidal precession\n       </a>\n       . (This is closely related to the\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Axial_precession_(astronomy)" title="Axial precession (astronomy)">\n        precession of the axis\n       </a>\n       .)\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       Currently, the Earth reaches perihelion in early January, approximately 14 days after the\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/December_Solstice" title="December Solstice">\n        December Solstice\n       </a>\n       . At perihelion, the Earth\'s center is about\n       <span class="nowrap">\n        <span class="sortkey" style="display:none">\n         6999983290000000000\xe2\x99\xa0\n        </span>\n        0.983\n        <span style="margin-left:.25em;">\n         29\n        </span>\n       </span>\n       <a href="/wiki/Astronomical_unit" title="Astronomical unit">\n        astronomical units\n       </a>\n       (AU) or 147,098,070\xc2\xa0km (91,402,500\xc2\xa0mi) from the Sun\'s center.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       The Earth reaches aphelion currently in early July, approximately 14 days after the\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/June_Solstice" title="June Solstice">\n        June Solstice\n       </a>\n       . The aphelion distance between the Earth\'s and Sun\'s centers is currently about\n       <span class="nowrap">\n        <span class="sortkey" style="display:none">\n         7011152097651119397\xe2\x99\xa0\n        </span>\n        1.016\n        <span style="margin-left:.25em;">\n         71\n        </span>\n        AU\n       </span>\n       or 152,097,700\xc2\xa0km (94,509,100\xc2\xa0mi).\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       Astronomers commonly express the timing of perihelion relative to the\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vernal_equinox" title="Vernal equinox">\n        vernal equinox\n       </a>\n       not in terms of days and hours, but rather as an angle of orbital displacement, the so-called\n       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Longitude_of_the_pericenter&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Longitude of the pericenter (page does not exist)">\n        longitude of the pericenter\n       </a>\n       .
