</b>\n       </i>\n       is the second experimental\n       <a href="/wiki/Space_habitat" title="Space habitat">\n        space habitat\n       </a>\n       designed and built by the private American firm\n       <a href="/wiki/Bigelow_Aerospace" title="Bigelow Aerospace">\n        Bigelow Aerospace\n       </a>\n       , launched in 2007. As the second module sent into orbit by the company, this spacecraft builds on the data and experience gleaned from its previously orbited sister-ship\n       <i>\n        <a href="/wiki/Genesis_I" title="Genesis I">\n         Genesis I\n        </a>\n       </i>\n       . Like its sister-ship and other modules being designed by Bigelow Aerospace, this spacecraft is based on the\n       <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">\n        NASA\n       </a>\n       <a href="/wiki/TransHab" title="TransHab">\n        TransHab\n       </a>\n       design, which provides increased interior volume and reduced launch diameter along with potentially reduced mass compared to traditional rigid structures.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       <i>\n        Genesis II\n       </i>\n       was "retired" when its avionics systems stopped working after two and a half years. It remains in orbit which allows researchers to continue testing the long-term viability of expandable space structures.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n      </p>\n      <div class="toc" id="toc">\n links\n          </span>\n         </a>\n        </li>\n       </ul>\n      </div>\n      <p>\n      </p>\n      <h2>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Spacecraft_history">\n        Spacecraft history\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Genesis_II&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Spacecraft history">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h2>\n      <p>\n       Similar to the process endured by Bigelow for\n       <i>\n        Genesis I\n       </i>\n       , transporting\n       <i>\n        Genesis II\n       </i>\n       to Russia for launch was the end result of nearly a year of regulatory processes due to restrictions imposed by\n       <a href="/wiki/International_Traffic_in_Arms_Regulations" title="International Traffic in Arms Regulations">\n        International Traffic in Arms Regulations\n       </a>\n       (ITAR) and other procedures, both in the United States and abroad. After leaving\n       <a href="/wiki/North_Las_Vegas,_Nevada" title="North Las Vegas, Nevada">\n        North Las Vegas, Nevada\n       </a>\n       in the United States, the spacecraft made a stopover in\n       <a href="/wiki/Luxembourg" title="Luxembourg">\n        Luxembourg\n       </a>\n       before being flown on an\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Antonov_An-124" title="Antonov An-124">\n        Antonov An-124\n       </a>\n       to\n       <a href="/wiki/Orsk" title="Orsk">\n        Orsk\n       </a>\n       , Russia, and transported over ground to the Dombarovskiy base.\n       <i>\n        Genesis II\n       </i>\n       made its final move into the Assembly, Integration and Test Building on 29 March 2007.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dispyasny_6-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-dispyasny-6">\n         [6]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       Originally slated for an 6 August 2006, launch, ISC Kosmotras delayed the launch to 30 January 2007, due to the failure of a Dnepr rocket in July 2006. The launch was delayed an additional four times (1 April 19 April, 26 April, and 23 May) due to technical and scheduling reasons before its eventual launch on 28 June 2007 at 15:02\n       <a href="/wiki/Coordinated_Universal_Time" title="Coordinated Universal Time">\n        UTC\n       </a>\n       .\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RSWdnepr2_1-2">\n        <a href="#cite_note-RSWdnepr2-1">\n         [1]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       As with\n       <i>\n        Genesis I\n       </i>\n       , it was launched aboard an\n       <a href="/wiki/ISC_Kosmotras" title="ISC Kosmotras">\n        ISC Kosmotras\n       </a>\n       <a href="/wiki/Dnepr_(rocket)" title="Dnepr (rocket)">\n        Dnepr rocket\n       </a>\n       from\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dombarovsky_(air_base)" title="Dombarovsky (air base)">\n        Dombarovskiy missile base\n       </a>\n       near\n       <a href="/wiki/Yasny,_Orenburg_Oblast" title="Yasny, Orenburg Oblast">\n        Yasniy\n       </a>\n       , Russia. It successfully reached orbit after separation from the rocket at 15:16 UTC. Due to the mechanics of its orbit, first contact with the craft was established once it passed over\n       <a href="/wiki/SpaceQuest,_Ltd." title="SpaceQuest, Ltd.">\n        SpaceQuest, Ltd.\n       </a>\n       \'s\n       <a href="/wiki/Fairfax,_Virginia" title="Fairfax, Virginia">\n        Fairfax, Virginia\n       </a>\n       receiving station at 22:20 UTC, confirming that it was functioning nominally with power and air pressure at expected levels.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BAnews070628-1_7-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-BAnews070628-1-7">\n         [7]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BAnews070628-2_8-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-BAnews070628-2-8">\n         [8]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ps20070702_9-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-ps20070702-9">\n         [9]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       Externally,\n       <i>\n        Genesis II\n       </i>\n       is identical in size to\n       <i>\n        Genesis I\n       </i>\n       ; as such, it is a one-third scale of the full-size\n       <i>\n        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/BA_330" title="BA 330">\n         BA 330\n        </a>\n       </i>\n       model, with on-orbit measurements of 4.4 meters (14.4\xc2\xa0ft) in length and 2.54 meters (8.3\xc2\xa0ft) in diameter, with an interior habitable volume of 11.5 cubic meters (406.1\xc2\xa0cu\xc2\xa0ft). As part of its inflatable design,\n       <i>\n        Genesis II\n       </i>\n       launched with a diameter of 1.6 meters (5.2\xc2\xa0ft), expanding to its full size after entering orbit.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BAnews070628-1_7-1">\n        <a href="#cite_note-BAnews070628-1-7">\n         [7]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ps20070702_9-1">\n        <a href="#cite_note-ps20070702-9">\n         [9]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-space070628_10-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-space070628-10">\n         [10]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       Within two days of launch, attitude control systems had damped all rotation and oriented antennae toward Earth.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BAperfG2_4-1">\n        <a href="#cite_note-BAperfG2-4">\n         [4]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       On 12 December 2007, Bigelow Aerospace provided an update indicating that\n       <i>\n        Genesis II\n       </i>\n       was in good health. All cameras had been tested and more than 4,000 photographs had been taken. The craft was in a nearly circular orbit with an\n       <a href="/wiki/Orbital_eccentricity" title="Orbital eccentricity">\n        eccentricity\n       </a>\n       of 0.028, and had only lost 5 kilometers (3\xc2\xa0mi) from launch to that time. Attitude control systems and all eight solar arrays were operational, and no damage to the outer surface of the craft was observed. Internal pressure was noted as holding between 69.6 and 72.4\n       <a href="/wiki/Pascal_(unit)" title="Pascal (unit)">\n        kilopascals\n       </a>\n       (10.1 and 10.5\n       <a href="/wiki/Pounds_per_square_inch" title="Pounds per square inch">\n        psi\n       </a>\n       ), with the variation caused by\n       <i>\n        Genesis II\n       </i>\n       moving in and out of sunlight during its orbit.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BAperfG2_4-2">\n        <a href="#cite_note-BAperfG2-4">\n         [4]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       On 23 April 2009, Bigelow Aerospace announced that\n       <i>\n        Genesis II\n       </i>\n       had surpassed the 10,000 orbit mark, having been in space for 665 days and travelling over 270 million miles (430 million kilometers).\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ba-g2-10000_11-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-ba-g2-10000-11">\n         [11]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       In February 2011, Bigelow reported that the vehicle had "performed flawlessly in terms of pressure maintenance and thermal control-environmental containment."\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8nn20110204_12-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-8nn20110204-12">\n         [12]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       Although the design life of the spacecraft avionics was only six months, the avionics systems worked flawlessly for over two and a half years before failure. The data received after the first six months was a re-verification of the validation test suite that was accomplished during the design life period.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mab20111201_13-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-mab20111201-13">\n         [13]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       The orbital life is estimated to be 12 years, with a gradually decaying orbit resulting in\n       <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_entry" title="Atmospheric entry">\n        re-entry\n       </a>\n       into\n       <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">\n        Earth\n       </a>\n       \'s atmosphere and burn-up expected. Its operations lasted approximately 2.5 years, significantly longer than its expected 6-month mission duration. As of 2016\n       <sup class="plainlinks noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;">\n        <a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Genesis_II&amp;action=edit">\n         [update]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       , the vehicle remains in orbit.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2_5y_2-1">\n        <a href="#cite_note-2_5y-2">\n         [2]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-heavens-above_5-1">\n        <a href="#cite_note-heavens-above-5">\n         [5]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <h2>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Systems">\n        Systems\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Genesis_II&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Systems">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h2>\n      <p>\n       <i>\n        Genesis II\n       </i>\n       features a number of improvements over the first pathfinder that was launched. In addition to the standard guidance control systems used on\n       <i>\n        Genesis I\n       </i>\n       , it has\n       <a href="/wiki/Reaction_wheel" title="Reaction wheel">\n        reaction wheel\n       </a>\n       assemblies and a precision measurement system, which are used to affect the spacecraft\'s\n       <a href="/wiki/Rotation" title="Rotation">\n        rotation\n       </a>\n       rate and\n       <a href="/wiki/Angular_momentum" title="Angular momentum">\n        angular momentum\n       </a>\n       without expending fuel. It carries 22 cameras (nine more than the 13 on\n       <i>\n        Genesis I\n       </i>\n       ) for photographing and filming cargo and ship conditions both inside and out. Some of these are on\n       <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/articulation" title="wikt:articulation">\n        articulated\n       </a>\n       platforms, and one wireless camera that is capable of additional exterior imaging. Instead of the single-tank inflation system used on the first craft,\n       <i>\n        Genesis II\n       </i>\n       employs multiple tanks for added reliability and to allow for more finely tuned gas control.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       Additional layers have been added to the outer shield for increased protection and thermal management. Finally, the on-board sensor suite was enhanced with additional sensors for pressure, temperature, attitude control, and radiation detection, which will help determine the impact of the orbital environment on the integrity of shipboard systems.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BAdiffII_14-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-BAdiffII-14">\n         [14]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       <i>\n        Genesis II\n       </i>\n       does not have any propulsion.\n      </p>\n      <h2>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Payload">\n        Payload\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Genesis_II&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Payload">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h2>\n      <p>\n       On both the interior and exterior,\n       <i>\n        Genesis II\n       </i>\n       carries several non-critical systems for scientific, commercial and entertainment purposes.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       For the science aspect,\n       <i>\n        Genesis II\n       </i>\n       carries an upgraded version of the original life-sciences module and is colloquially termed "Life in a Box". This module includes habitats for three organisms: the\n       <a href="/wiki/Madagascar_hissing_cockroach" title="Madagascar hissing cockroach">\n        Madagascar hissing cockroach\n       </a>\n       , previously carried aboard\n       <i>\n        Genesis I\n       </i>\n       ; the\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Flat_rock_scorpion" title="Flat rock scorpion">\n        South African flat rock scorpion\n       </a>\n       ,\n       <i>\n        Hadogenes troglodytes\n       </i>\n       ; and a colony of\n       <a href="/wiki/Red_harvester_ant" title="Red harvester ant">\n        seed-harvester ants\n       </a>\n       ,\n       <i>\n        Pogonomyrmex californicus\n       </i>\n       , along with the queen ant for long-term colonization possibilities. This biobox system includes automated food and water delivery systems, and fans keep fresh air available by circulating internal air with that inside the rest of the spacecraft. Sensors and cameras will monitor the health and activities of the biobox inhabitants, and images of the interior are intended for display on Bigelow\'s website.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BAlifebox_15-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-BAlifebox-15">\n         [15]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       There are two commercial payloads included on\n       <i>\n        Genesis II\n       </i>\n       . The first is the "Fly Your Stuff" program, which allowed individuals and customers to send photographs and other small items into orbit for a fee. Several dozen of these objects were launched, and were later photographed and filmed by cameras in the spacecraft and posted on the Bigelow website for the customers to view.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BAflystuff_16-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-BAflystuff-16">\n         [16]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       By December 2007, all objects launched as part of this program had been photographed and distributed to customers.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BAperfG2_4-3">\n        <a href="#cite_note-BAperfG2-4">\n         [4]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       A secondary payload is the external image projection system that tested the capability for flashing images and messages on the spacecraft\'s hull. Two projectors and associated cameras are positioned on the tips of solar arrays, and the company has eventual plans for allowing the public to send images and video to be displayed. As of August 2007\n       <sup class="plainlinks noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;">\n        <a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Genesis_II&amp;action=edit">\n         [update]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       , there were no firm plans on how this would be handled due to current limits of uplink bandwidth, and was considered an experimental "fun" project.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-msnbc3924_17-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-msnbc3924-17">\n         [17]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-space070326_18-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-space070326-18">\n         [18]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       For entertainment,\n       <i>\n        Genesis II\n       </i>\n       carries a "Space\n       <a href="/wiki/Bingo_(U.S.)" title="Bingo (U.S.)">\n        Bingo\n       </a>\n       " game intended to foster public interest in the program.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BAbingo_19-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-BAbingo-19">\n         [19]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <h2>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">\n        See also\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n
