Chapman">\n        John H. Chapman\n       </a>\n       embarked on a project initially known simply as S-27 or the Topside Sounder Project. This work would soon lead to the development of Canada\'s first satellite known as\n       <a href="/wiki/Alouette_1" title="Alouette 1">\n        Alouette 1\n       </a>\n       .\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       With the launch of Alouette 1 in September 1962 Canada became the third country to put an artificial satellite into space. At the time, Canada only possessed upper atmospheric launch capabilities (\n       <a href="/wiki/Sounding_rocket" title="Sounding rocket">\n        sounding rockets\n       </a>\n       ), therefore, Alouette 1 was sent aloft by the American\n       <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">\n        National Aeronautics and Space Administration\n       </a>\n       (NASA) from\n       <a href="/wiki/Vandenberg_Air_Force_Base" title="Vandenberg Air Force Base">\n        Vandenberg Air Force Base\n       </a>\n       in\n       <a href="/wiki/Lompoc,_California" title="Lompoc, California">\n        Lompoc\n       </a>\n       ,\n       <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">\n        California\n       </a>\n       . The technical excellence of the satellite, which lasted for ten years instead of the expected one, prompted the further study of the\n       <a href="/wiki/Ionosphere" title="Ionosphere">\n        ionosphere\n       </a>\n       with the joint Canadian-designed, US-launched\n       <a href="/wiki/ISIS_(satellite)" title="ISIS (satellite)">\n        ISIS satellite program\n       </a>\n       . This undertaking was designated an International Milestone of Electrical Engineering by\n       <a href="/wiki/Institute_of_Electrical_and_Electronics_Engineers" title="Institute of Electrical and Electronics Engineers">\n        IEEE\n       </a>\n       in 1993. The launch of\n       <a href="/wiki/Anik_(satellite)" title="Anik (satellite)">\n        Anik A-1\n       </a>\n       in 1972 made Canada the first country in the world to establish its own domestic\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Geostationary" title="Geostationary">\n        geostationary\n       </a>\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Communication_satellite" title="Communication satellite">\n        communication satellite\n       </a>\n       network.\n       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">\n        [\n        <i>\n         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">\n          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2014)">\n           citation needed\n          </span>\n         </a>\n        </i>\n        ]\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       These and other space related activities in the 1980s compelled the Canadian government to promulgate the\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Canadian_Space_Agency_Act" title="Canadian Space Agency Act">\n        Canadian Space Agency Act\n       </a>\n       which established the Canadian Space Agency. The Act received\n       <a href="/wiki/Royal_assent" title="Royal assent">\n        royal assent\n       </a>\n       on May 10, 1990 and\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Came_into_force" title="Came into force">\n        came into force\n       </a>\n       on December 14, 1990.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">\n        <a href="#cite_note-5">\n         [5]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       In 1999 the CSA was moved from project based to \'A-base\' funding and given a fixed annual budget of $300 Million.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2015-16_CSA_Report_1-3">\n        <a href="#cite_note-2015-16_CSA_Report-1">\n         [1]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       The actual budget varies from year to year due to additional earmarks and special projects.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       The mandate of the Canadian Space Agency is to promote the peaceful use and development of space, to advance the knowledge of space through science and to ensure that space science and technology provide social and economic benefits for Canadians. class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h3>\n      <p>\n       The CSA has been a cooperating state of the\n       <a href="/wiki/European_Space_Agency" title="European Space Agency">\n        European Space Agency\n       </a>\n       (ESA) since the 1970s,\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-assoc_14-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-assoc-14">\n         [14]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2010_renewal_15-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-2010_renewal-15">\n         [15]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       and has several formal and informal partnerships and collaborative programs with space agencies in other countries, such as\n       <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">\n        NASA\n       </a>\n       ,\n       <a href="/wiki/Indian_Space_Research_Organisation" title="Indian Space Research Organisation">\n        ISRO\n       </a>\n       ,\n       <a href="/wiki/JAXA" title="JAXA">\n        JAXA\n       </a>\n       , and\n       <a href="/wiki/Swedish_National_Space_Board" title="Swedish National Space Board">\n        SNSB\n       </a>\n       .\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       Canada\'s collaboration with Europe in space activities predated both the European Space Agency and the Canadian Space Agency.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-assoc_14-1">\n        <a href="#cite_note-assoc-14">\n         [14]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       From 1968, Canada held observer status in the European Space Conference (ESC), a ministerial-level organization set up to determine future European space activities, and it continued in this limited role after ESA was created in 1975.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-assoc_14-2">\n        <a href="#cite_note-assoc-14">\n         [14]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       Since January 1, 1979, Canada has had the special status of a "Cooperating State" with the ESA,\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2010_renewal_15-1">\n        <a href="#cite_note-2010_renewal-15">\n         [15]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       paying for the privilege and also investing in working time and providing scientific instruments which are placed on ESA probes. Canada is allowed to participate in optional programs; it also has to contribute to the General Budget but not as much as associate membership would have entailed. This status was unique at the time and remains so today.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       On 15 December 2010 the accord was renewed for a further 10 years, until 2020.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2010_renewal_15-2">\n        <a href="#cite_note-2010_renewal-15">\n         [15]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       By virtue of this accord, Canada takes part in ESA deliberative bodies and decision-making and in ESA\'s programmes and activities. Canadian firms can bid for and receive contracts to work on programmes. The accord has a provision specifically ensuring a fair industrial return to Canada. The head of the Canadian delegation to ESA is the president of the Canadian Space Agency. As of February 2009, there are currently 30 Canadians that are employed as staff members at ESA. <h2>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Future_programs">\n        Future programs\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Canadian_Space_Agency&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Future programs">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h2>\n      <p>\n       With the successful launching of\n       <a href="/wiki/Radarsat-2" title="Radarsat-2">\n        Radarsat-2\n       </a>\n       in December 2007 and completion of Canada\'s C$1.4 billion contribution to the\n       <a href="/wiki/International_Space_Station" title="International Space Station">\n        International Space Station\n       </a>\n       in early 2008, the CSA found itself with no major follow-on projects. This fact was highlighted by\n       <a href="/wiki/Marc_Garneau" title="Marc Garneau">\n        Marc Garneau\n       </a>\n       , Canada\'s first astronaut and former head of the CSA who in the fall of 2007 called upon the\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Canadian_government" title="Canadian government">\n        Canadian government\n       </a>\n       to develop and institute a space policy for Canada.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       A modest step has been taken to resolve this problem. In November 2008, the Agency signed a $40 million 16-month contract with\n       <a href="/wiki/MacDonald,_Dettwiler_and_Associates" title="MacDonald, Dettwiler and Associates">\n        MacDonald, Dettwiler and Associates\n       </a>\n       of\n       <a href="/wiki/Vancouver" title="Vancouver">\n        Vancouver\n       </a>\n       to begin the design of the\n       <a href="/wiki/RADARSAT_Constellation" title="RADARSAT Constellation">\n        RADARSAT Constellation\n       </a>\n       (3 satellite) earth observation mission.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Boucher_2011_33-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-Boucher_2011-33">\n         [33]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       In August 2010 further funding was awarded for detailed design work scheduled for completion by 2012. Launch of the three satellites is scheduled for 2018. Also in the 2009 Federal budget, the agency was awarded funding for the preliminary design of robotic\n       <a href="/wiki/Lunar_rover" title="Lunar rover">\n        Lunar\n       </a>\n       /\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Martian_rovers" title="Martian rovers">\n        Martian rovers\n       </a>\n       .\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34">\n        <a href="#cite_note-34">\n         [34]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       However, a number of initiatives are without funding. The CSA is the lead agency for the Polar Communication and Weather mission (PCW) which involves the planned launch of two satellites in polar orbit to provide Canadian authorities with improved weather information and communications capabilities in the high arctic.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35">\n        <a href="#cite_note-35">\n         [35]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       Launch of the two satellites was proposed to take place in 2016. Funding for further development of the mission has yet to be approved.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36">\n        <a href="#cite_note-36">\n         [36]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      [37]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       Canada relies on other countries, such as the U.S., India and Russia, to launch its spacecraft into orbit, but both the Defence Department and the space
