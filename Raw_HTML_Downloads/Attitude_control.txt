class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Attitude_control&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit ambox ambox-move" role="presentation">\n       <tr>\n        <td class="mbox-image">\n         <div style="width:52px">\n          <img alt="" data-file-height="20" data-file-width="60" height="20" class="mbox-text-span">\n          <b>\n           This article or section may need to be cleaned up.\n          </b>\n          It has been merged from\n          <i>\n           <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/3-axis_stabilized_spacecraft" title="3-axis stabilized spacecraft">\n            3-axis stabilized spacecraft\n           </a>\n          </i>\n          .\n         </div>\n        </td>\n       </tr>\n      </table>\n      <p>\n       A spacecraft\'s attitude must typically be stabilized and controlled for a variety of reasons. It is oftentimes needed so that the spacecraft\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/High-gain_antenna" title="High-gain antenna">\n        high-gain antenna\n       </a>\n       may be accurately pointed to Earth for communications, so that onboard experiments may accomplish precise pointing for accurate collection and subsequent interpretation of data, so that the heating and cooling effects of sunlight and shadow may be used intelligently for thermal control, and also for guidance: short propulsive maneuvers must be executed in the right direction.\n      </p>\n      <h3>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Types_of_stabilization">\n        Types of stabilization\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Attitude_control&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Types of stabilization">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h3>\n      <p>\n       There are two principal approaches to stabilizing attitude control on spacecraft:\n       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">\n        [\n        <i>\n         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">\n          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2014)">\n           citation needed\n          </span>\n         </a>\n        </i>\n        ]\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <ul>\n       <li>\n        <b>\n         <span id="Spin_stabilization">\n          Spin stabilization\n         </span>\n        </b>\n        is accomplished by setting the spacecraft spinning, using the gyroscopic action of the rotating spacecraft mass as the stabilizing mechanism. Propulsion system thrusters are fired only occasionally to make desired changes in spin rate, or in the spin-stabilized attitude. If desired, the spinning may be stopped through the use of thrusters or by\n        <a href="/wiki/Yo-yo_de-spin" title="Yo-yo de-spin">\n         yo-yo de-spin\n        </a>\n        . The\n        <a href="/wiki/Pioneer_10" title="Pioneer 10">\n         Pioneer 10\n        </a>\n        and\n        <a href="/wiki/Pioneer_11" title="Pioneer 11">\n         Pioneer 11\n        </a>\n        probes in the outer solar system are examples of spin-stabilized spacecraft.\n       </li>\n       <li>\n        <b>\n         <span id="Three-axis_stabilization">\n          Three-axis stabilization\n         </span>\n        </b>\n        is an alternative method of spacecraft attitude control in which the spacecraft is held fixed in the desired orientation without any rotation.\n        <ul>\n         <li>\n          One method is to use small thrusters to continually nudge the spacecraft back and forth within a\n          <a href="/wiki/Deadband" title="Deadband">\n           deadband\n          </a>\n          of allowed attitude error. Thrusters may also be referred to as mass-expulsion control (MEC)\n          <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">\n           <a href="#cite_note-1">\n            [1]\n           </a>\n          </sup>\n          systems, or\n          <a href="/wiki/Reaction_control_system" title="Reaction control system">\n           reaction control systems\n          </a>\n          (RCS). The space probes\n          <a href="/wiki/Voyager_1" title="Voyager 1">\n           Voyager 1\n          </a>\n          and\n          <a href="/wiki/Voyager_2" title="Voyager 2">\n           Voyager 2\n          </a>\n          employ this method, and have used up about three quarters\n          <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">\n           <a href="#cite_note-2">\n            [2]\n           </a>\n          </sup>\n          of their 100\xc2\xa0kg of propellant as of July 2015.\n         </li>\n         <li>\n          Another method for achieving three-axis stabilization is to use electrically powered\n          <a href="/wiki/Reaction_wheel" title="Reaction wheel">\n           reaction wheels\n          </a>\n          , also called momentum wheels, which are mounted on three orthogonal axes aboard the spacecraft. They provide a means to trade\n          <a href="/wiki/Angular_momentum" title="Angular momentum">\n           angular momentum\n          </a>\n          back and forth between spacecraft and wheels. To rotate the vehicle on a given axis, the reaction wheel on that axis is accelerated in the opposite direction. To rotate the vehicle back, the wheel is slowed. Excess momentum that builds up in the system due to external torques from, for example, solar photon pressure or\n          <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gravity_gradient" title="Gravity gradient">\n           gravity gradients\n          </a>\n          , must be occasionally removed from the system by applying controlled torque to the spacecraft to allowing the wheels to return to a desired speed under computer control. This is done during maneuvers called momentum desaturation or momentum unload maneuvers. Most spacecraft use a system of thrusters to apply the torque for desaturation maneuvers. A different approach was used by the Hubble Space Telescope, which had sensitive optics that could be contaminated by thruster exhaust, and instead used\n          <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Magnetic_torquers" title="Magnetic torquers">\n           magnetic torquers\n          </a>\n          for desaturation maneuvers.\n         </li>\n        </ul>\n       </li>\n      </ul>\n      <p>\n       There are advantages and disadvantages to both spin stabilization and three-axis stabilization. Spin-stabilized craft provide a continuous sweeping motion that is desirable for fields and particles instruments, as well as some optical scanning instruments, but they may require complicated systems to de-spin antennas or optical instruments that must be pointed at targets for science observations or communications with Earth. Three-axis controlled craft can point optical instruments and antennas without having to de-spin them, but they may have to carry out special rotating maneuvers to best utilize their fields and particle instruments. If thrusters are used for routine stabilization, optical observations such as imaging must be designed knowing that the spacecraft is always slowly rocking back and forth, and not always exactly predictably. Reaction wheels provide a much steadier spacecraft from which to make observations, but they add mass to the spacecraft, they have a limited mechanical lifetime, and they require frequent momentum desaturation maneuvers, which can perturb navigation solutions because of accelerations imparted by the use of thrusters.\n       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">\n        [\n        <i>\n         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">\n          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. Thrusters must be organized as a system to provide stabilization about all three axes, and at least two thrusters are generally used in each axis to provide torque as a\n       <a href="/wiki/Couple_(mechanics)" title="Couple (mechanics)">\n        couple\n       </a>\n       in order to prevent imparting a\n       <a href="/wiki/Translation_(geometry)" title="Translation (geometry)">\n        translation\n       </a>\n       to the vehicle. The fuel efficiency of an attitude control system is determined by its\n       <a href="/wiki/Specific_impulse" title="Specific impulse">\n        specific impulse\n       </a>\n       (proportional to exhaust velocity) and the smallest torque impulse it can provide (which determines how often the thrusters must fire to provide precise control). Thrusters must be fired in one direction to start rotation, and again in the opposing direction if a new orientation is to be held. Thruster systems have been used on most manned space vehicles, including\n       <a href="/wiki/Vostok_(spacecraft)" title="Vostok (spacecraft)">\n        Vostok\n       </a>\n       ,\n       <a href="/wiki/Project_Mercury" title="Project Mercury">\n        Mercury\n       </a>\n       ,\n       <a href="/wiki/Project_Gemini" title="Project Gemini">\n        Gemini\n       </a>\n       ,\n       <a href="/wiki/Apollo_(spacecraft)" title="Apollo (spacecraft)">\n        Apollo\n       </a>\n       ,\n       <a href="/wiki/Soyuz_(spacecraft)" title="Soyuz (spacecraft)">\n        Soyuz\n       </a>\n       , and the\n       <a href="/wiki/Space_Shuttle" title="Space Shuttle">\n        Space Shuttle\n       </a>\n       .\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       To minimize the fuel limitation on mission duration, auxiliary attitude control systems may be used to reduce vehicle rotation to lower levels, such as small\n       <a href="/wiki/Ion_thruster" title="Ion thruster">\n        ion thrusters\n       </a>\n       that accelerate ionized gases electrically to extreme velocities, using power from solar cells.\n      </p>\n      <h3>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Spin_stabilization">\n        Spin stabilization\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Attitude_control&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Spin stabilization">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h3>\n      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">\n       Main article:\n       <a href="/wiki/Spin-stabilisation" title="Spin-stabilisation">\n        Spin-stabilisation\n       </a>\n      </div>\n      <p>\n       The entire space vehicle itself can be spun up to stabilize the orientation of a single vehicle axis. This method is widely used to stabilize the final stage of a launch vehicle. The entire spacecraft and an attached solid rocket motor are spun up about the rocket\'s thrust axis, on a "spin table" oriented by the attitude control system of the lower stage on which the spin table is mounted. When final orbit is achieved, the satellite may be de-spun by various means, or left spinning. Spin stabilization of satellites is only applicable to those missions with a primary axis of orientation that need not change dramatically over the lifetime of the satellite and no need for extremely high precision pointing. It is also useful for missions with instruments that must scan the star field or Earth\'s surface or atmosphere.\n       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">\n        [\n        <i>\n         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">\n          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2011)">\n           citation needed\n          </span>\n         </a>\n        </i>\n        ]\n       </sup>\n       See\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spin-stabilized_satellite" title="Spin-stabilized satellite">\n        spin-stabilized satellite\n       </a>\n       .\n      </p>\n      <h3>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Momentum_wheels">\n        Momentum wheels\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Attitude_control&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Momentum wheels">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h3>\n      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">\n       Main article:\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Momentum_wheel" title="Momentum wheel">\n        Momentum wheel\n       </a>\n      </div>\n      <p>\n       These are\n       <a href="/wiki/Electric_motor" title="Electric motor">\n        electric motor\n       </a>\n       driven rotors made to spin in the direction opposite to that required to re-orient the vehicle. Because momentum wheels make up a small fraction of the spacecraft\'s mass and are computer controlled, they give precise control. Momentum wheels are generally suspended on\n       <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_bearing" title="Magnetic bearing">\n        magnetic bearings\n       </a>\n       to avoid bearing friction and breakdown problems.\n       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">\n        [\n        <i>\n         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">\n          <span title="(September 2015)">\n           citation needed\n          </span>\n         </a>\n        </i>\n        ]\n       </sup>\n       To maintain orientation in three dimensional space a minimum of three must be used,\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">\n        <a href="#cite_note-6">\n         [6]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       with additional units providing single failure protection. See\n       <a href="/wiki/Euler_angles" title="Euler angles">\n        Euler angles\n       </a>\n       .\n      </p>\n      <h3>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Control_moment_gyros">\n        Control moment gyros\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Attitude_control&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Control moment gyros">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h3>\n      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">\n       Main article:\n       <a href="/wiki/Control_moment_gyroscope" title="Control moment gyroscope">\n        Control moment gyroscope\n       </a>\n      </div>\n      <p>\n       These are rotors spun at constant speed, mounted on\n       <a href="/wiki/Gimbal" title="Gimbal">\n        gimbals\n       </a>\n       to provide attitude control. Although a CMG provides control about the two axes orthogonal to the gyro spin axis, triaxial control still requires two units. A CMG is a bit more expensive in terms of cost and mass, because gimbals and their drive motors must be provided. The maximum torque (but not the maximum angular momentum change) exerted by a CMG is greater than for a momentum wheel, making it better suited to large spacecraft. A major drawback is the additional complexity, which increases the number of failure points. For this reason, the\n       <a href="/wiki/International_Space_Station" title="International Space Station">\n        International Space Station\n       </a>\n       uses a set of four CMGs to provide dual failure tolerance.\n      </p>\n      <h3>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Solar_sails">\n        Solar sails\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Attitude_control&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Solar sails">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h3>\n      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">\n       Main article:\n       <a href="/wiki/Solar_sail" title="Solar sail">\n        Solar sail\n       </a>\n      </div>\n      <p>\n       Small solar sails (devices that produce thrust as a reaction force induced by reflecting incident light) may be used to make small attitude control and velocity adjustments. This application can save large amounts of fuel on a long-duration mission by producing control moments without fuel expenditure. For example,\n       <a href="/wiki/Mariner_10" title="Mariner 10">\n        Mariner 10\n       </a>\n       adjusted its attitude using its solar cells and antennas as small solar sails.\n      </p>\n      <h3>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Gravity-gradient_stabilization">\n        Gravity-gradient stabilization\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Attitude_control&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Gravity-gradient stabilization">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h3>\n      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">\n       Main article:\n       <a href="/wiki/Gravity-gradient_stabilization" title="Gravity-gradient stabilization">\n        Gravity-gradient stabilization\n       </a>\n      </div>\n      <p>\n       In orbit, a spacecraft with one axis much longer than the other two will spontaneously orient so that its long axis points at the planet\'s center of mass. This system has the virtue of needing no active control system or expenditure of fuel. The effect is caused by a\n       <a href="/wiki/Tidal_force" title="Tidal force">\n        tidal force\n       </a>\n       . The upper end of the vehicle feels less gravitational pull than the lower end. This provides a restoring torque whenever the long axis is not co-linear with the direction of gravity. Unless some means of damping is provided, the spacecraft will oscillate about the local vertical. Sometimes\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tether_propulsion" title="Tether propulsion">\n        tethers\n       </a>\n       are used to connect two parts of a satellite, to increase the stabilizing torque. A problem with such tethers is that meteoroids as small as a grain of sand can part them.\n      </p>\n      <h3>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Magnetic_torquers">\n        Magnetic torquers\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Attitude_control&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Magnetic torquers">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h3>\n      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">\n       Main article:\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Magnetic_torquers" title="Magnetic torquers">\n        Magnetic torquers\n       </a>\n      </div>\n      <p>\n       <a href="/wiki/Electromagnetic_coil" title="Electromagnetic coil">\n        Coils\n       </a>\n       or (on very small satellites)\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Permanent_magnets" title="Permanent magnets">\n        permanent magnets\n       </a>\n       exert a moment against the local magnetic field. This method works only where there is a magnetic field against which to react. One classic field "coil" is actually in the form of a\n       <a href="/wiki/Electrodynamic_tether" title="Electrodynamic tether">\n        conductive tether\n       </a>\n       in a planetary magnetic field. Such a conductive tether can also generate electrical power, at the expense of\n       <a href="/wiki/Orbital_decay" title="Orbital decay">\n        orbital decay\n       </a>\n       . Conversely, by inducing a counter-current, using solar cell power, the orbit may be raised. Due to massive variability in Earth\'s magnetic field from an ideal radial field, control laws based on torques coupling to this field will be highly non-linear. Moreover, only two-axis control is available at any given time meaning that a vehicle reorient may be necessary to null all rates.\n      </p>\n      <h3>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Pure_passive_attitude_control">\n        Pure passive attitude control\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Attitude_control&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Pure passive attitude control">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h3>\n      <p>\n       There exist two main passive control types for satellites. The first one uses gravity gradient, and it leads to four stable states with the long axis (axis with smallest moment of inertia) pointing towards Earth. As this system has four stable states, if the satellite has a preferred orientation, e.g. a camera pointed at the planet, some way to flip the satellite and its tether end-for-end is needed. The other passive system orients the satellite along Earth\'s magnetic field thanks to a magnet.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">\n        <a href="#cite_note-7">\n         [7]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       These purely passive attitude control systems have limited pointing accuracy, because the spacecraft will oscillate around energy minima. This drawback is overcome by adding damper, which can be hysteretic materials or a viscous damper. The viscous damper is a small can or tank of fluid mounted in the spacecraft, possibly with internal baffles to increase internal friction. Friction within the damper will gradually convert oscillation energy into heat dissipated within the viscous damper.\n      </p>\n      <h2>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">\n
