 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Temperateness" title="Temperateness">\n        temperate\n       </a>\n       <a href="/wiki/Season" title="Season">\n        seasons\n       </a>\n       , following\n       <a href="/wiki/Winter" title="Winter">\n        winter\n       </a>\n       and preceding\n       <a href="/wiki/Summer" title="Summer">\n        summer\n       </a>\n       . There are various technical definitions of spring, but local usage of the term varies according to local climate, cultures and customs. When it is spring in the\n       <a href="/wiki/Northern_Hemisphere" title="Northern Hemisphere">\n        Northern Hemisphere\n       </a>\n       , it will be\n       <a href="/wiki/Autumn" title="Autumn">\n        autumn\n       </a>\n       in the\n       <a href="/wiki/Southern_Hemisphere" title="Southern Hemisphere">\n        Southern Hemisphere\n       </a>\n       and vice versa. At the spring\n       <a href="/wiki/Equinox" title="Equinox">\n        equinox\n       </a>\n       ,\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Daytime_(astronomy)" title="Daytime (astronomy)">\n        days\n       </a>\n       are approximately 12 hours long with day length increasing as the season progresses.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       Spring and "springtime" refer to the season, and also to ideas of rebirth, rejuvenation, renewal, resurrection and regrowth.\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Subtropical" title="Subtropical">\n        Subtropical\n       </a>\n       and\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tropical" title="Tropical">\n        tropical\n       </a>\n       areas have climates better described in terms of other seasons, e.g. dry or wet, monsoonal or cyclonic. Often, cultures have locally defined names for seasons which have little equivalence to the terms originating in Europe. Spring is the time when many plants begin to grow and flower.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n      </p>\n      <div class="toc" id="toc">\n       <div class="toctitle">\n        <h2>\n         Contents\n class="toctext">\n           External links\n          </span>\n         </a>\n        </li>\n       </ul>\n      </div>\n      <p>\n      </p>\n      <h2>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Meteorological_reckoning">\n        Meteorological reckoning\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Spring_(season)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Meteorological reckoning">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h2>\n      <p>\n       Meteorologists generally define four seasons in many climatic areas: spring, summer, autumn (fall) and winter. These are demarcated by the values of their average temperatures on a monthly basis, with each season lasting three months. The three warmest months are by definition summer, the three coldest months are winter and the intervening gaps are spring and autumn. Spring, when defined in this manner, can start on different dates in different regions.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       In most Northern Hemisphere\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Temperate" title="Temperate">\n        temperate\n       </a>\n       locations, spring months are March, April and May, although differences exist from country to country.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">\n        <a href="#cite_note-1">\n         [1]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       (Summer is June, July, August; autumn is September, October, November; winter is December, January, February.) Most Southern Hemisphere temperate locations have opposing seasons with spring in September, October and November.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       <a href="/wiki/Sweden" title="Sweden">\n        Swedish\n       </a>\n       meteorologists define the beginning of spring as the first occasion on which the average daytime temperature exceeds zero degrees Celsius for seven consecutive days, thus the date varies with latitude and elevation.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">\n        <a href="#cite_note-2">\n         [2]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       In\n       <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">\n        Australia\n       </a>\n       and\n       <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand" title="New Zealand">\n        New Zealand\n       </a>\n       , spring conventionally begins on 1 September and ends 30 November.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">\n        <a href="#cite_note-3">\n         [3]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <h2>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Astronomical_and_solar_reckoning">\n        Astronomical and solar reckoning\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Spring_(season)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Astronomical and solar reckoning">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h2>\n      <p>\n       In some cultures in the Northern Hemisphere (e.g. Germany), the astronomical\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vernal_equinox" title="Vernal equinox">\n        Vernal equinox\n       </a>\n       (varying between 19 and 21 March) is taken to mark the first day of spring, and the\n       <a href="/wiki/Summer_solstice" title="Summer solstice">\n        Summer solstice\n       </a>\n       (around 21 June) is taken as the first day of summer. In Persian culture the first day of spring is the first day of the first month (called Farvardin) which begins on 20 or 21 March. In other traditions, the equinox is taken as mid-spring.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       In the\n       <a href="/wiki/Chinese_calendar" title="Chinese calendar">\n        traditional Chinese calendar\n       </a>\n       , the\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chun_(season)" title="Chun (season)">\n        "spring" season\n       </a>\n       (\n       <span lang="zh" xml:lang="zh">\n        <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E6%98%A5" title="wikt:\xe6\x98\xa5">\n         \xe6\x98\xa5\n        </a>\n       </span>\n       ) consists of the end of winter (from 4 February) and beginning of spring (to 5 May), roughly taking the equinox as its midpoint. Similarly, according to the\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Celtic_tradition" title="Celtic tradition">\n        Celtic tradition\n       </a>\n       , which is based solely on daylight and the strength of the noon sun, spring begins in early February (near\n       <a href="/wiki/Imbolc" title="Imbolc">\n        Imbolc\n       </a>\n       or\n       <a href="/wiki/Candlemas" title="Candlemas">\n        Candlemas\n       </a>\n       ) and continues until early May (\n       <a href="/wiki/Beltane" title="Beltane">\n        Beltane\n       </a>\n       ).\n      </p>\n class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Spring_(season)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Other calendar-based reckoning">\n title="Ireland">\n        Ireland\n       </a>\n       , spring traditionally starts on February 1,\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/St_Brigid%27s_Day" title="St Brigid\'s Day">\n        St Brigid\'s Day\n       </a>\n       ,\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">\n        <a href="#cite_note-4">\n         [4]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       although Irish meteorologists consider the whole of February to be part of winter.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">\n        <a href="#cite_note-5">\n         [5]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">\n        <a href="#cite_note-6">\n         [6]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <h2>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Ecological_reckoning">\n        Ecological reckoning\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Spring_(season)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Ecological reckoning">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h2>\n      <p>\n       The beginning of spring is not always determined by fixed calendar dates. The phenological or ecological definition of spring relates to biological indicators, such as the blossoming of a range of plant species, the activities of animals, and the special smell of soil that has reached the temperature for micro\n       <a href="/wiki/Flora" title="Flora">\n        flora\n       </a>\n       to flourish. These indicators, along with the beginning of spring, vary according to the local\n       <a href="/wiki/Climate" title="Climate">\n        climate\n       </a>\n       and according to the specific\n       <a href="/wiki/Weather" title="Weather">\n        weather\n       </a>\n       of a particular year. Most ecologists divide the year into six seasons that have no fixed dates. In addition to spring, ecological reckoning identifies an earlier separate\n       <b>\n        prevernal\n       </b>\n       (early or pre-spring) season between the hibernal (winter) and vernal (spring) seasons. This is a time when only the hardiest flowers like the crocus are in bloom, sometimes while there is still some snowcover on the ground.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">\n        <a The\n       <a href="/wiki/Lunar_calendar" title="Lunar calendar">\n        lunar calendar\n       </a>\n       is used mainly to divide the year into seasons for\n       <a href="/wiki/Agriculture" title="Agriculture">\n        agriculture\n       </a>\n       purposes.\n       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">\n        [\n        <i>\n         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">\n          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2016)">\n           citation needed\n          </span>\n         </a>\n        </i>\n        ]\n       </sup>\n       In the old days, the celebration used to last the entire month of January of the lunar calendar. Traditionally,\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Firecrackers" title="Firecrackers">\n        firecrackers\n       </a>\n       are used on New Year\'s Eve, or\n       <i>\n        \xc4\x91\xc3\xaam giao th\xe1\xbb\xaba\n       </i>\n       in Vietnamese, to scare away bad spirits and souls.\n       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">\n        [\n        <i>\n         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">\n          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2016)">\n           citation needed\n          </span>\n         </a>\n        </i>\n        ]\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <h2>\n       <span id=\'"Spring"_as_a_metaphor\'>\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id=".22Spring.22_as_a_metaphor">\n        "Spring" as a metaphor\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Spring_(season)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=27" title=\'Edit section: "Spring" as a metaphor\'>\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h2>\n      <p>\n       Spring is seen as a time of growth, renewal, of new life (both plant and animal) being born.
