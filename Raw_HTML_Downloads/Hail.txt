</a>\n           </li>\n          </ul>\n         </div>\n        </td>\n       </tr>\n      </table>\n      <p>\n       <b>\n        Hail\n       </b>\n       is a form of solid\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Precipitation_(meteorology)" title="Precipitation (meteorology)">\n        precipitation\n       </a>\n       . It is distinct from\n       <a href="/wiki/Ice_pellets" title="Ice pellets">\n        ice pellets\n       </a>\n       (American sleet), though the two are often confused.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">\n        <a href="#cite_note-1">\n         [1]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       It consists of balls or irregular lumps of ice, each of which is called a\n       <b>\n        hailstone\n       </b>\n       . Ice pellets (American sleet) falls generally in cold weather while hail growth is greatly inhibited during cold surface temperatures.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-webster_2-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-webster-2">\n         [2]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       Unlike other forms of\n       <a href="/wiki/Ice" title="Ice">\n        water ice\n       </a>\n       such as\n       <a href="/wiki/Graupel" title="Graupel">\n        graupel\n       </a>\n       , which is made of\n       <a href="/wiki/Hard_rime" title="Hard rime">\n        rime\n       </a>\n       , and\n       <a href="/wiki/Ice_pellets" title="Ice pellets">\n        ice pellets\n       </a>\n       , which are smaller and\n       <a href="/wiki/Transparency_and_translucency" title="Transparency and translucency">\n        translucent\n       </a>\n       , hailstones usually measure between 5 millimetres (0.2\xc2\xa0in) and 15 centimetres (6\xc2\xa0in) in diameter. The\n       <a href="/wiki/METAR" title="METAR">\n        METAR\n       </a>\n       reporting code for hail 5\xc2\xa0mm (0.20\xc2\xa0in) or greater is\n       <b>\n        GR\n       </b>\n       , while smaller hailstones and graupel are coded\n       <b>\n        GS\n       </b>\n       .\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       Hail is possible within most\n       <a href="/wiki/Thunderstorm" title="Thunderstorm">\n        thunderstorms\n       </a>\n       as it is produced by\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cumulonimbus" title="Cumulonimbus">\n        cumulonimbus\n       </a>\n       ,\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gloss_3-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-gloss-3">\n         [3]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       and within 2 nautical miles (3.7\xc2\xa0km) of the parent storm. Hail formation requires environments of strong, upward motion of air with the parent thunderstorm (similar to\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tornadoes" title="Tornadoes">\n        tornadoes\n       </a>\n       ) and lowered heights of the freezing level. In the\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mid-latitude" title="Mid-latitude">\n        mid-latitudes\n       </a>\n       , hail forms near the\n       <a href="/wiki/Continental_climate" title="Continental climate">\n        interiors of continents\n       </a>\n       , while in the\n       <a href="/wiki/Tropics" title="Tropics">\n        tropics\n       </a>\n       , it tends to be confined to high\n       <a href="/wiki/Elevation" title="Elevation">\n        elevations\n       </a>\n       .\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       There are methods available to detect hail-producing thunderstorms using\n       <a href="/wiki/Weather_satellite" title="Weather satellite">\n        weather satellites\n       </a>\n       and\n       <a href="/wiki/Weather_radar" title="Weather radar">\n        weather radar\n       </a>\n       imagery. Hailstones generally fall at higher speeds as they grow in size, though complicating factors such as melting, friction with air, wind, and interaction with rain and other hailstones can slow their descent through\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Earth%27s_atmosphere" title="Earth\'s atmosphere">\n        Earth\'s atmosphere\n       </a>\n       . Severe weather warnings are issued for hail when the stones reach a damaging size, as it can cause serious damage to human-made structures and, most commonly, farmers\' crops.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n </li>\n       </ul>\n      </div>\n      <p>\n      </p>\n      <h2>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Definition">\n        Definition\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Hail&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Definition">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h2>\n      <p>\n       Any thunderstorm which produces hail that reaches the ground is known as a hailstorm.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">\n        <a href="#cite_note-4">\n         [4]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       Hail has a diameter of 5 millimetres (0.20\xc2\xa0in) or more.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gloss_3-1">\n        <a href="#cite_note-gloss-3">\n         [3]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       Hailstones can grow to 15 centimetres (6\xc2\xa0in) and weigh more than 0.5 kilograms (1.1\xc2\xa0lb).\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">\n        <a href="#cite_note-5">\n         [5]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       Unlike ice pellets, hailstones are layered and can be irregular and clumped together. Hail is composed of transparent ice or alternating layers of transparent and translucent ice at least 1 millimetre (0.039\xc2\xa0in) thick, which are deposited upon the hailstone as it travels through the cloud, suspended aloft by air with strong upward motion until its weight overcomes the\n       <a href="/wiki/Vertical_draft" title="Vertical draft">\n        updraft\n       </a>\n       and falls to the ground. Although the diameter of hail is varied, in the United States, the average observation of damaging hail is between 2.5\xc2\xa0cm (1\xc2\xa0in) and\n       <a href="/wiki/Golf_ball" title="Golf ball">\n        golf ball\n       </a>\n       -sized (1.75\xc2\xa0in).\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">\n        <a href="#cite_note-6">\n         [6]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       Stones larger than 2\xc2\xa0cm (0.80\xc2\xa0in) are usually considered large enough to cause damage. The\n       <a href="/wiki/Meteorological_Service_of_Canada" title="Meteorological Service of Canada">\n        Meteorological Service of Canada\n       </a>\n       issues severe thunderstorm warnings when hail that size or above is expected.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">\n        <a href="#cite_note-7">\n         [7]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       The US\n       <a href="/wiki/National_Weather_Service" title="National Weather Service">\n        National Weather Service\n       </a>\n       has a 2.5\xc2\xa0cm (1\xc2\xa0in) or greater in diameter threshold, effective January 2010, an increase over the previous threshold of \xc2\xbe-inch hail.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">\n        <a href="#cite_note-8">\n         [8]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       Other countries have different thresholds according local sensitivity to hail; for instance grape growing areas could be adversely impacted by smaller hailstones. Hailstones can be very large or very small, depending on how strong the updraft is: weaker hailstorms produce smaller hailstones than stronger hailstorms (such as\n       <a href="/wiki/Supercell" title="Supercell">\n        supercells\n       </a>\n       ).\n      </p>\n      <h2>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Formation">\n        Formation\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Hail&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Formation">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h2>\n      <p>\n       Hail forms in strong\n       <a href="/wiki/Thunderstorm" title="Thunderstorm">\n        thunderstorm\n       </a>\n       clouds, particularly those with intense\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Updraft" title="Updraft">\n        updrafts\n       </a>\n       , high liquid water content, great vertical extent, large water droplets, and where a good portion of the cloud layer is below freezing 0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xb0C (32\xc2\xa0\xc2\xb0F).\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gloss_3-2">\n        <a href="#cite_note-gloss-3">\n         [3]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       These types of strong updrafts can also indicate the presence of a tornado.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">\n        <a href="#cite_note-9">\n         [9]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       The growth rate is maximized where air is near a temperature of \xe2\x88\x9213\xc2\xa0\xc2\xb0C (9\xc2\xa0\xc2\xb0F).\n       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">\n        [\n        <i>\n         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">\n          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2012)">\n           citation needed\n          </span>\n         </a>\n        </i>\n        ]\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <h3>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Layer_nature_of_the_hailstones">\n        Layer nature of the hailstones\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Hail&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Layer nature of the hailstones">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h3>\n      <div class="thumb tright">\n       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">\n        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Hailshaft.jpg">\n         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="575" data-file-width="600" height="211" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f3/Hailshaft.jpg/220px-Hailshaft.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f3/Hailshaft.jpg/330px-Hailshaft.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f3/Hailshaft.jpg/440px-Hailshaft.jpg 2x" width="220"/>\n        </a>\n        <div class="thumbcaption">\n         <div class="magnify">\n          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Hailshaft.jpg" title="Enlarge">\n          </a>\n         </div>\n         Hail shaft\n        </div>\n       </div>\n      </div>\n      <div class="thumb tright">\n       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">\n        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Hail_clouds.jpg">\n         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1200" data-file-width="1600" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/05/Hail_clouds.jpg/220px-Hail_clouds.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/05/Hail_clouds.jpg/330px-Hail_clouds.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/05/Hail_clouds.jpg/440px-Hail_clouds.jpg 2x" width="220"/>\n        </a>\n        <div class="thumbcaption">\n         <div class="magnify">\n          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Hail_clouds.jpg" title="Enlarge">\n          </a>\n         </div>\n         Severe thunderstorms containing hail can exhibit a characteristic green coloration\n         <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">\n          <a href="#cite_note-10">\n           [10]\n          </a>\n         </sup>\n        </div>\n       </div>\n      </div>\n      <p>\n       Like other precipitation in cumulonimbus clouds, hail begins as water droplets. As the droplets rise and the temperature goes below freezing, they become\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Supercooled" title="Supercooled">\n        supercooled\n       </a>\n       <a href="/wiki/Water" title="Water">\n        water\n       </a>\n       and will freeze on contact with\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Condensation_nuclei" title="Condensation nuclei">\n        condensation nuclei\n       </a>\n       . A cross-section through a large hailstone shows an onion-like structure. This means the hailstone is made of thick and translucent layers, alternating with layers that are thin, white and opaque. Former theory suggested that hailstones were subjected to multiple descents and ascents, falling into a zone of humidity and refreezing as they were uplifted. This up and down motion was thought to be responsible for the successive layers of the hailstone. New research, based on theory as well as field study, has shown this is not necessarily true.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       The storm\'s\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Updraft" title="Updraft">\n        updraft\n       </a>\n       , with upwardly directed wind speeds as high as 110 miles per hour (180\xc2\xa0km/h),\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ncar_11-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-ncar-11">\n         [11]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       blows the forming hailstones up the cloud. As the hailstone ascends it passes into areas of the cloud where the concentration of humidity and supercooled water droplets varies. The hailstone\xe2\x80\x99s growth rate changes depending on the variation in humidity and supercooled water droplets that it encounters. The accretion rate of these water droplets is another factor in the hailstone\xe2\x80\x99s growth. When the hailstone moves into an area with a high concentration of water droplets, it captures the latter and acquires a translucent layer. Should the hailstone move into an area where mostly water vapour is available, it acquires a layer of opaque white ice.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nelson_12-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-Nelson-12">\n         [12]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       Furthermore, the hailstone\xe2\x80\x99s speed depends on its position in the cloud\xe2\x80\x99s updraft and its mass. This determines the varying thicknesses of the layers of the hailstone. The accretion rate of supercooled water droplets onto the hailstone depends on the relative velocities between these water droplets and the hailstone itself. This means that generally the larger hailstones will form some distance from the stronger updraft where they can pass more time growing.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nelson_12-1">\n        <a href="#cite_note-Nelson-12">\n         [12]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       As the hailstone grows it releases\n       <a href="/wiki/Latent_heat" title="Latent heat">\n        latent heat\n       </a>\n       , which keeps its exterior in a liquid phase. Because it undergoes \'wet growth\', the outer layer is\n       <i>\n        sticky\n       </i>\n       (i.e. more adhesive), so a single hailstone may grow by collision with other smaller hailstones, forming a larger entity with an irregular shape.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Brimlow_13-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-Brimlow-13">\n         [13]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       The hailstone will keep rising in the thunderstorm until its mass can no longer be supported by the updraft. This may take at least 30 minutes based on the force of the updrafts in the hail-producing thunderstorm, whose top is usually greater than 10\xc2\xa0km high. It then falls toward the ground while continuing to grow, based on the same processes, until it leaves the cloud. It will later begin to melt as it passes into air above freezing temperature.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">\n        <a href="#cite_note-14">\n         [14]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       Thus, a unique trajectory in the thunderstorm is sufficient to explain the layer-like structure of the hailstone. The only case in which multiple trajectories can be discussed is in a multicellular thunderstorm, where the hailstone may be ejected from the top of the "mother" cell and captured in the updraft of a more intense "daughter" cell. This, however, is an exceptional case.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nelson_12-2">\n        <a href="#cite_note-Nelson-12">\n         [12]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <h3>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Factors_favoring_hail">\n        Factors favoring hail\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Hail&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Factors favoring hail">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h3>\n      <p>\n       Hail is most common within continental interiors of the mid-latitudes, as hail formation is considerably more likely when the freezing level is below the altitude of 11,000 feet (3,400\xc2\xa0m).\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mesoanal_15-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-mesoanal-15">\n         [15]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       <a href="/wiki/Entrainment_(meteorology)" title="Entrainment (meteorology)">\n        Movement\n       </a>\n       of dry air into strong thunderstorms over continents can increase the frequency of hail by promoting evaporational cooling which lowers the freezing level of thunderstorm clouds giving hail a larger volume to grow in. Accordingly, hail is less common in the tropics despite a much higher frequency of thunderstorms than in the mid-latitudes because the atmosphere over the tropics tends to be warmer over a much greater altitude. Hail in the tropics occurs mainly at higher elevations.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">\n        <a href="#cite_note-16">\n         [16]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       Hail growth becomes vanishingly small when air temperatures fall below \xe2\x88\x9230\xc2\xa0\xc2\xb0C (\xe2\x88\x9222\xc2\xa0\xc2\xb0F) as supercooled water droplets become rare at these temperatures.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mesoanal_15-1">\n        <a href="#cite_note-mesoanal-15">\n         [15]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       Around thunderstorms, hail is most likely within the cloud at elevations above 20,000 feet (6,100\xc2\xa0m). Between 10,000 feet (3,000\xc2\xa0m) and 20,000 feet (6,100\xc2\xa0m), 60\xc2\xa0percent of hail is still within the thunderstorm, though 40\xc2\xa0percent now lies within the clear air under the anvil. Below 10,000 feet (3,000\xc2\xa0m), hail is equally distributed in and around a thunderstorm to a distance of 2 nautical miles (3.7\xc2\xa0km).\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17">\n        <a href="#cite_note-17">\n         [17]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <h2>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Climatology">\n        Climatology\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Hail&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Climatology">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h2>\n      <p>\n       Hail occurs most frequently within continental interiors at mid-latitudes and is less common in the tropics, despite a much higher frequency of thunderstorms than in the mid-latitudes.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18">\n        <a href="#cite_note-18">\n         [18]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       Hail is also much more common along mountain ranges because mountains force horizontal winds upwards (known as\n       <a href="/wiki/Orographic_lift" title="Orographic lift">\n        orographic lifting\n       </a>\n       ), thereby intensifying the updrafts within thunderstorms and making hail more likely.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19">\n        <a href="#cite_note-19">\n         [19]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       The higher elevations also result in there being less time available for hail to melt before reaching the ground. One of the more common regions for large hail is across mountainous northern\n       <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">\n        India\n       </a>\n       , which reported one of the highest hail-related death tolls on record in 1888.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Oliver_20-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-Oliver-20">\n         [20]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">\n        China\n       </a>\n       also experiences significant hailstorms.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21">\n        <a href="#cite_note-21">\n         [21]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       Central Europe and southern Australia also experience a lot of hailstorms. Popular regions for hailstorms are southern and western\n       <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">\n        Germany\n       </a>\n       , northern and eastern\n       <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">\n        France\n       </a>\n       and southern and eastern\n       <a href="/wiki/Benelux" title="Benelux">\n        Benelux\n       </a>\n       . In south-eastern Europe,\n       <a href="/wiki/Croatia" title="Croatia">\n        Croatia\n       </a>\n       and\n       <a href="/wiki/Serbia" title="Serbia">\n        Serbia\n       </a>\n       experience frequent occurrences of hail.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22">\n        <a href="#cite_note-22">\n         [22]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       In\n       <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">\n        North America\n       </a>\n       , hail is most common in the area where\n       <a href="/wiki/Colorado" title="Colorado">\n        Colorado\n       </a>\n       ,\n       <a href="/wiki/Nebraska" title="Nebraska">\n        Nebraska\n       </a>\n       , and\n       <a href="/wiki/Wyoming" title="Wyoming">\n        Wyoming\n       </a>\n       meet, known as "Hail Alley".\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ncarhail_23-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-ncarhail-23">\n         [23]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       Hail in this region occurs between the months of March and October during the afternoon and evening hours, with the bulk of the occurrences from May through September.\n       <a href="/wiki/Cheyenne,_Wyoming" title="Cheyenne, Wyoming">\n        Cheyenne, Wyoming\n       </a>\n       is North America\'s most hail-prone city with an average of nine to ten hailstorms per season.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nolanhail_24-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-Nolanhail-24">\n         [24]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       To the north of this area and also just downwind of the Rocky Mountains is the\n       <a href="/wiki/Hailstorm_Alley" title="Hailstorm Alley">\n        Hailstorm Alley\n       </a>\n       region of\n       <a href="/wiki/Alberta" title="Alberta">\n        Alberta\n       </a>\n       , which also experiences an increased incidence of significant hail events.\n      </p>\n      <h2>\n       <span class="mw-headline" href="/wiki/Weather_radar" title="Weather radar">\n        Weather radar\n       </a>\n       is a very useful tool to detect the presence of hail-producing thunderstorms. However, radar data has to be complemented by a knowledge of current atmospheric conditions which can allow one to determine if the current atmosphere is conducive to hail development.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       Modern radar scans many angles around the site. Reflectivity values at multiple angles above ground level in a storm are proportional to the precipitation rate at those levels. Summing reflectivities in the\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vertically_Integrated_Liquid" title="Vertically Integrated Liquid">\n        Vertically Integrated Liquid\n       </a>\n       or VIL, gives the\n       <a href="/wiki/Liquid_water_content" title="Liquid water content">\n        liquid water content\n       </a>\n       in the cloud. Research shows that hail development in the upper levels of the storm is related to the evolution of VIL. VIL divided by the vertical extent of the storm, called VIL density, has a relationship with hail size, although this varies with atmospheric conditions and therefore is not highly accurate.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25">\n        <a href="#cite_note-25">\n         [25]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       Traditionally, hail size and probability can be estimated from radar data by computer using algorithms based on this research. Some algorithms include the height of the freezing level to estimate the melting of the hailstone and what would be left on the ground.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       Certain patterns of reflectivity are important clues for the meteorologist as well. The\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Three_body_scatter_spike" title="Three body scatter spike">\n        three body scatter spike\n       </a>\n       is an example. This is the result of energy from the radar hitting hail and being deflected to the ground, where they deflect back to the hail and then to the radar. The energy took more time to go from the hail to the ground and back, as opposed to the energy that went direct from the hail to the radar, and the echo is further away from the radar than the actual location of the hail on the same radial path, forming a cone of weaker reflectivities.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       More recently, the\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dual_polarization" led to a variety of hail classification algorithms.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28">\n        <a href="#cite_note-28">\n         [28]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       Visible The UK organisation,\n       <a href="/wiki/TORRO" title="TORRO">\n        TORRO\n       </a>\n       , also scales for both hailstones and hailstorms.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31">\n        <a href="#cite_note-31">\n         [31]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       When observed at an\n       <a href="/wiki/Airport" title="Airport">\n        airport\n       </a>\n       ,\n       <a href="/wiki/METAR" title="METAR">\n        METAR\n       </a>\n       code is used within a\n       <a href="/wiki/Surface_weather_observation" title="Surface weather observation">\n        surface weather observation\n       </a>\n       which relates to the size of the hailstone. It is estimated that a hailstone of 1 centimetre (0.39\xc2\xa0in) in diameter falls at a rate of 9 metres per second (20\xc2\xa0mph), while stones the size of 8 centimetres (3.1\xc2\xa0in) in diameter fall at a rate of 48 metres per second (110\xc2\xa0mph). Hailstone velocity is dependent on the size of the stone, friction with air it is falling through, the motion of\n       <a href="/wiki/Wind" title="Wind">\n        wind\n       </a>\n       it is falling through, collisions with raindrops or other hailstones, and melting as the stones fall through a warmer\n       <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere" title="Atmosphere">\n        atmosphere\n       </a>\n       . As hail stones are not perfect spheres it is difficult to calculate their speed accurately.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39">\n        <a href="#cite_note-39">\n         [39]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <h2>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Hazards">\n        Hazards\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Hail&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Hazards">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h2>\n      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">\n       Main article:\n       <a href="/wiki/List_of_costly_or_deadly_hailstorms" title="List of costly or deadly hailstorms">\n        List of costly or deadly hailstorms\n       </a>\n      </div>\n      <p>\n       Hail can cause serious damage, notably to\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Automobile" title="Automobile">\n        automobiles\n       </a>\n       , aircraft, skylights, glass-roofed structures,\n       <a href="/wiki/Livestock" title="Livestock">\n        livestock\n       </a>\n       , and most commonly, farmers\'\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Crops" title="Crops">\n        crops\n       </a>\n       .\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nolanhail_24-1">\n        <a href="#cite_note-Nolanhail-24">\n         [24]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       Hail damage to roofs often goes unnoticed until further structural damage is seen, such as leaks or cracks. It is hardest to recognize hail damage on shingled roofs and flat roofs, but all roofs have their own hail damage detection problems.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Adjusting_Today_40-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-Adjusting_Today-40">\n         [40]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       Metal roofs are fairly resistant to hail damage, but may accumulate cosmetic damage in the form of dents and damaged coatings.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41">\n        <a href="#cite_note-41">\n         [41]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       Hail is one of the most significant thunderstorm hazards to aircraft.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42">\n        <a href="#cite_note-42">\n         [42]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       When hailstones exceed 0.5 inches (13\xc2\xa0mm) in diameter, planes can be seriously damaged within seconds.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43">\n        <a href="#cite_note-43">\n         [43]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       The hailstones accumulating on the ground can also be hazardous to landing aircraft. Hail is also a common nuisance to drivers of automobiles, severely denting the vehicle and cracking or even shattering\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Windshields" title="Windshields">\n        windshields\n       </a>\n       and\n       <a href="/wiki/Car_glass" title="Car glass">\n        windows\n       </a>\n       . Wheat, corn, soybeans, and tobacco are the most sensitive crops to hail damage.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Oliver_20-1">\n        <a href="#cite_note-Oliver-20">\n         [20]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       Hail is one of Canada\'s most expensive hazards.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44">\n        <a href="#cite_note-44">\n         [44]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       Rarely, massive hailstones have been known to cause\n       <a href="/wiki/Concussion" title="Concussion">\n        concussions\n       </a>\n       or fatal head\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Physical_trauma" title="Physical trauma">\n        trauma\n       </a>\n       . Hailstorms have been the cause of costly and deadly events throughout history. One of the earliest known incidents occurred around the 9th century in\n       <a href="/wiki/Roopkund" title="Roopkund">\n        Roopkund\n       </a>\n       ,\n       <a href="/wiki/Uttarakhand" title="Uttarakhand">\n        Uttarakhand\n       </a>\n       ,\n       <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">\n        India\n       </a>\n       , where 200 to 600 nomads seem to have died of injuries from hail the size of cricket balls.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45">\n        <a href="#cite_note-45">\n         [45]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       The largest hailstone in terms of diameter and weight ever recorded in the\n       <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">\n        United States\n       </a>\n       fell on July 23, 2010 in\n       <a href="/wiki/Vivian,_South_Dakota" title="Vivian, South Dakota">\n        Vivian, South Dakota\n       </a>\n       ; it measured 8 inches (20\xc2\xa0cm) in diameter and 18.62 inches (47.3\xc2\xa0cm) in circumference, weighing in at 1.93 pounds (0.88\xc2\xa0kg).\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Record_46-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-Record-46">\n         [46]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       This broke the previous record for diameter set by a hailstone 7\xc2\xa0inches diameter and 18.74\xc2\xa0inches circumference (still the greatest\n       <i>\n        circumference\n       </i>\n       hailstone) which fell in\n       <a href="/wiki/Aurora,_Nebraska" title="Aurora, Nebraska">\n        Aurora, Nebraska\n       </a>\n       in the United States on June 22, 2003, as well as the record for weight, set by a hailstone of 1.67 pounds (0.76\xc2\xa0kg) that fell in\n       <a href="/wiki/Coffeyville,_Kansas" title="Coffeyville, Kansas">\n        Coffeyville, Kansas\n       </a>\n       in 1970.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Record_46-1">\n        <a href="#cite_note-Record-46">\n         [46]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <h2>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Accumulations">\n        Accumulations\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Hail&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Accumulations">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h2>\n      <div class="thumb tright">\n       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">\n        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Hail_in_Charlton,_Massachusetts.jpg">\n         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2448" data-file-width="2448" height="220" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/79/Hail_in_Charlton%2C_Massachusetts.jpg/220px-Hail_in_Charlton%2C_Massachusetts.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/79/Hail_in_Charlton%2C_Massachusetts.jpg/330px-Hail_in_Charlton%2C_Massachusetts.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/79/Hail_in_Charlton%2C_Massachusetts.jpg/440px-Hail_in_Charlton%2C_Massachusetts.jpg 2x" width="220"/>\n        </a>\n        <div class="thumbcaption">\n         <div class="magnify">\n          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Hail_in_Charlton,_Massachusetts.jpg" title="Enlarge">\n          </a>\n         </div>\n         Accumulated hail after a storm in\n         <a href="/wiki/Charlton,_Massachusetts" title="Charlton, Massachusetts">\n          Charlton, Massachusetts\n         </a>\n        </div>\n       </div>\n      </div>\n      <p>\n       Narrow zones where hail accumulates on the ground in association with thunderstorm activity are known as hail streaks or hail swaths,\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47">\n        <a href="#cite_note-47">\n         [47]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       which can be detectable by satellite after the storms pass by.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48">\n        <a href="#cite_note-48">\n         [48]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       Hailstorms normally last from a few minutes up to 15 minutes in duration.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nolanhail_24-2">\n        <a href="#cite_note-Nolanhail-24">\n         [24]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       Accumulating hail storms can blanket the ground with over 2 inches (5.1\xc2\xa0cm) of hail, cause thousands to lose power, and bring down many trees. Flash flooding and mudslides within areas of steep terrain can be a concern with accumulating hail.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49">\n        <a href="#cite_note-49">\n         [49]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       Depths of up to 18\xc2\xa0in (0.46\xc2\xa0m) have been reported. A landscape covered in accumulated hail generally resembles one covered in accumulated snow and any significant accumulation of hail has the same restrictive effects as snow accumulation, albeit over a smaller area, on transport and infrastructure.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50">\n        <a href="#cite_note-50">\n         [50]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       Accumulated hail can also cause flooding by blocking drains, and hail can be carried in the floodwater, turning into a snow-like slush which is deposited at lower elevations.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       On somewhat rare occasions, a thunderstorm can become stationary or nearly so while prolifically producing hail and significant depths of accumulation do occur; this tends to happen in mountainous areas, such as the July 29, 2010 case\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51">\n        <a href="#cite_note-51">\n         [51]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       of a foot of hail accumulation in\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Boulder_County" title="Boulder County">\n        Boulder County\n       </a>\n       , Colorado. On June 5, 2015, hail up to four feet deep fell on one city block in\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Denver,_Colorado" title="Denver, Colorado">\n        Denver, Colorado\n       </a>\n       . The hailstones, described as between the size of bumble bees and ping pong balls, were accompanied by rain and high winds. The hail fell in only the one area, leaving the surrounding area untouched. It fell for one and a half hours between 10 p.m. and 11:30\xc2\xa0p.m. A meteorologist for the National Weather Service in Boulder said, "It\'s a very interesting phenomenon. We saw the storm stall. It produced copious amounts of hail in one small area. It\'s a meteorological thing." Tractors used to clear the area filled more than 30 dump-truck loads of hail.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-The_Denver_Post_52-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-The_Denver_Post-52">\n         [52]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       Research focused on four individual days that accumulated more than 5.9 inches (15\xc2\xa0cm) of hail in 30 minutes on the Colorado front range has shown that these events share similar patterns in observed synoptic weather, radar, and lightning characteristics,\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53">\n        <a href="#cite_note-53">\n         [53]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       suggesting the possibility of predicting these events prior to their occurrence. A fundamental problem in continuing research in this area is that, unlike hail diameter, hail depth is not commonly reported. The lack of data leaves researchers and forecasters in the dark when trying to verify operational methods. A cooperative effort between the University of Colorado and the National Weather Service is in progress. The joint project\'s goal is to enlist the help of the general public to develop a database of hail accumulation depths.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54">\n        <a href="#cite_note-54">\n         [54]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <h2>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Suppression_and_prevention">\n        Suppression and prevention\n    During the\n       <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">\n        Middle Ages\n       </a>\n       , people in Europe used to ring church bells and fire\n       <a href="/wiki/Cannon" title="Cannon">\n        cannons\n       </a>\n       to try to prevent hail, and the subsequent damage to crops. Updated versions of this approach are available as modern\n       <a href="/wiki/Hail_cannon" title="Hail cannon">\n        hail cannons\n       </a>\n       .\n       <a href="/wiki/Cloud_seeding" title="Cloud seeding">\n        Cloud seeding\n       </a>\n       after\n       <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">\n        World War II\n       </a>\n       was done to eliminate the hail threat,\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ncar_11-1">\n        <a href="#cite_note-ncar-11">\n         [11]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       particularly across\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Soviet_Union" title="The Soviet Union">\n        the Soviet Union\n       </a>\n       \xe2\x80\x93 where it was claimed a 70 to 98\xc2\xa0percent reduction in crop damage from hail storms was achieved by deploying\n       <a href="/wiki/Silver_iodide" title="Silver iodide">\n        silver iodide\n       </a>\n       in clouds using\n       <a href="/wiki/Rocket" title="Rocket">\n        rockets\n       </a>\n       and\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Artillery_shell" title="Artillery shell">\n        artillery shells\n       </a>\n       .\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55">\n        <a href="#cite_note-55">\n         [55]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56">\n        <a href="#cite_note-56">\n         [56]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       Hail suppression programs have been undertaken by 15\xc2\xa0countries between 1965 and 2005.\n       <sup class="reference"
