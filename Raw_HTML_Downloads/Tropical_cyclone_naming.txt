</a>\n       and\n       <a href="/wiki/Subtropical_cyclone" title="Subtropical cyclone">\n        subtropical cyclones\n       </a>\n       are named by various warning centers to provide ease of communication between forecasters and the general public regarding forecasts, watches, and warnings. The names are intended to reduce confusion in the event of concurrent storms in the same\n       <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone_basins" title="Tropical cyclone basins">\n        basin\n       </a>\n       . Generally once storms produce sustained wind speeds of more than 33 knots (61\xc2\xa0km/h; 38\xc2\xa0mph), names are assigned in order from predetermined lists depending on which basin they originate. However, standards vary from basin to basin: some tropical depressions are named in the Western Pacific, while tropical cyclones must have a significant amount of\n       <a href="/wiki/Gale" title="Gale">\n        gale\n       </a>\n       -force winds occurring around the centre before they are named in the\n       <a href="/wiki/Southern_Hemisphere" title="Southern Hemisphere">\n        Southern Hemisphere\n       </a>\n       .\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       Before the formal start of naming, tropical cyclones were named after places, objects, or saints\' feast days on which they occurred. The credit for the first usage of personal names for weather systems is generally given to the\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Queensland_Government" title="Queensland Government">\n        Queensland Government\n       </a>\n       Meteorologist\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Clement_Wragge" title="Clement Wragge">\n        Clement Wragge\n       </a>\n       , who named systems between 1887 and 1907. This system of naming weather systems subsequently fell into disuse for several years after Wragge retired, until it was revived in the latter part of\n       <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">\n        World War II\n       </a>\n       for the Western Pacific. Formal naming schemes and naming lists have subsequently been introduced and developed for the\n       <a href="/wiki/Pacific_hurricane" title="Pacific hurricane">\n        Eastern, Central\n       </a>\n       ,\n       <a href="/wiki/Typhoon" title="Typhoon">\n        Western\n       </a>\n       and\n       <a href="/wiki/South_Pacific_tropical_cyclone" title="South Pacific tropical cyclone">\n        Southern Pacific\n       </a>\n       basins, as well as the\n       <a href="/wiki/Australian_region_tropical_cyclone" title="Australian region tropical cyclone">\n        Australian region\n       </a>\n       ,\n       <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Ocean" title="Atlantic Ocean">\n        Atlantic Ocean\n       </a>\n       and\n       <a href="/wiki/Indian_Ocean" title="Indian Ocean">\n        Indian Ocean\n       </a>\n       .\n      </p>\n      <div class="toclimit-2">\n       <div class="toc" id="toc">\n        <div class="toctitle">\n         <h2>\n tropical cyclones were often named after places, objects, or saints\' feast days on which they occurred.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mahina_8-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-Mahina-8">\n         [8]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       The credit for the first usage of personal names for weather systems is generally given to the\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Queensland_Government" title="Queensland Government">\n        Queensland Government\n       </a>\n       Meteorologist\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Clement_Wragge" title="Clement Wragge">\n        Clement Wragge\n       </a>\n       , who named systems between 1887 and 1907.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mahina_8-1">\n        <a href="#cite_note-Mahina-8">\n         [8]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       This system of naming weather systems subsequently fell into disuse for several years after Wragge retired until it was revived in the latter part of\n       <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">\n        World War II\n       </a>\n       for the Western Pacific.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mahina_8-2">\n        <a href="#cite_note-Mahina-8">\n         [8]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       Formal naming schemes have subsequently been introduced for the North Atlantic, Eastern, Central, Western and Southern Pacific basins as well as the Australian region and Indian Ocean.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mahina_8-3">\n        <a href="#cite_note-Mahina-8">\n         [8]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       At present, tropical cyclones are officially named by one of eleven warning centers and retain their names throughout their lifetimes to facilitate the effective communication of forecasts and storm-related hazards to the general public.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TCFAQB1_9-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-TCFAQB1-9">\n         [9]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       This is especially important when multiple storms are occurring simultaneously in the same ocean basin.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TCFAQB1_9-1">\n        <a href="#cite_note-TCFAQB1-9">\n         [9]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       Names are generally assigned in order from predetermined lists, once they produce one, three, or ten-minute sustained wind speeds of more than 65\xc2\xa0km/h (40\xc2\xa0mph).\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ATL.2FEPAC_TCOP_1-2">\n        <a href="#cite_note-ATL.2FEPAC_TCOP-1">\n         [1]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IMD_2009_4-1">\n        <a href="#cite_note-IMD_2009-4">\n         [4]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SWIO_TCOP_5-1">\n        <a href="#cite_note-SWIO_TCOP-5">\n         [5]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       However, standards vary from basin to basin, with some systems named in the Western Pacific when they develop into tropical depressions or enter PAGASA\'s area of responsibility.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GMA_3-1">\n        <a href="#cite_note-GMA-3">\n         [3]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       Within the\n       <a href="/wiki/Southern_Hemisphere" title="Southern Hemisphere">\n        Southern Hemisphere\n       </a>\n       , systems must be characterized by a significant amount of\n       <a href="/wiki/Gale" title="Gale">\n        gale\n       </a>\n       -force winds occurring around the center before they are named.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SWIO_TCOP_5-2">\n        <a href="#cite_note-SWIO_TCOP-5">\n         [5]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SPAC_TCOP_6-2">\n        <a href="#cite_note-SPAC_TCOP-6">\n         [6]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       <span id="Retirement">\n       </span>\n       Any member of the\n       <a href="/wiki/World_Meteorological_Organization" title="World Meteorological Organization">\n        World Meteorological Organization\n       </a>\n       \'s hurricane, typhoon and tropical cyclone committees can request that the name of a tropical cyclone be retired or withdrawn from the various tropical cyclone naming lists.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ATL.2FEPAC_TCOP_1-3">\n        <a href="#cite_note-ATL.2FEPAC_TCOP-1">\n         [1]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WPAC_TCOP_2-1">\n        <a href="#cite_note-WPAC_TCOP-2">\n         [2]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SPAC_TCOP_6-3">\n        <a href="#cite_note-SPAC_TCOP-6">\n         [6]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       A name is retired or withdrawn if a consensus or majority of members agree that the system has acquired a special notoriety, such as causing a large number of deaths and amounts of damage, impact, or for other special reasons.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ATL.2FEPAC_TCOP_1-4">\n        <a href="#cite_note-ATL.2FEPAC_TCOP-1">\n         [1]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       A replacement name is then submitted to the committee concerned and voted upon, but these names can be rejected and replaced with another name for various reasons:\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ATL.2FEPAC_TCOP_1-5">\n        <a href="#cite_note-ATL.2FEPAC_TCOP-1">\n         [1]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WPAC_TCOP_2-2">\n        <a href="#cite_note-WPAC_TCOP-2">\n         [2]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       these reasons include the spelling and pronunciation of the name, the similarity to the name of a recent tropical cyclone or on another list of names, and the length of the name for modern communication channels such as social media.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ATL.2FEPAC_TCOP_1-6">\n        <a href="#cite_note-ATL.2FEPAC_TCOP-1">\n         [1]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Aus_names_10-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-Aus_names-10">\n         [10]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       PAGASA also retires the names of significant tropical cyclones when they have caused at least\n       <span class="sortkey" style="display:none">\n        7009100000000000000\xe2\x99\xa0\n       </span>\n       <a href="/wiki/Philippine_peso" title="Philippine peso">\n        \xe2\x82\xb1\n       </a>\n       1\xc2\xa0billion in damage or have caused at least 300\xc2\xa0deaths.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PAGASA_2014_11-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-PAGASA_2014-11">\n         [11]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <h2>\n       <span class="mw-headline" </div>\n      <p>\n       Within the North Atlantic Ocean, tropical or subtropical cyclones are named by the\n       <a href="/wiki/National_Hurricane_Center" title="National Hurricane Center">\n        National Hurricane Center\n       </a>\n       (NHC/RSMC Miami) when they are judged to have intensified into a tropical storms with winds of at least 34\xc2\xa0kn (39\xc2\xa0mph; 63\xc2\xa0km/h).\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ATL.2FEPAC_TCOP_1-7">\n        <a href="#cite_note-ATL.2FEPAC_TCOP-1">\n         [1]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       There are six lists of names which rotate every six years and begin with the first letters A\xe2\x80\x94W used, skipping Q and U, and alternating between male and female names.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ATL.2FEPAC_TCOP_1-8">\n        <a href="#cite_note-ATL.2FEPAC_TCOP-1">\n         [1]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       The names of significant tropical cyclones are\n       <a href="/wiki/List_of_retired_Atlantic_hurricane_names" title="List of retired Atlantic hurricane names">\n        retired\n       </a>\n       from the lists, with a replacement name selected at the next World Meteorological Organization\'s Hurricane Committee meeting.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ATL.2FEPAC_TCOP_1-9">\n        <a href="#cite_note-ATL.2FEPAC_TCOP-1">\n         [1]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       If all of the names on a list are used, storms are named after the letters of the\n       <a href="/wiki/Greek_alphabet" title="Greek alphabet">\n        Greek alphabet\n       </a>\n       .\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ATL.2FEPAC_TCOP_1-10">\n        <a href="#cite_note-ATL.2FEPAC_TCOP-1">\n         [1]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       The current naming scheme began with the\n       <a href="/wiki/1979_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="1979 Atlantic hurricane season">\n        1979 season\n       </a>\n       . It uses alternating women\'s and men\'s names, and also includes some Spanish and a few French names. </div>\n       </div>\n      </div>\n      <p>\n       Within the Eastern Pacific Ocean, there are two warning centers that assign names to tropical cyclones on behalf of the World Meteorological Organization when they are judged to have intensified into a tropical storm with winds of at least 34\xc2\xa0kn (39\xc2\xa0mph; 63\xc2\xa0km/h).\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ATL.2FEPAC_TCOP_1-12">\n        <a href="#cite_note-ATL.2FEPAC_TCOP-1">\n         [1]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       Tropical cyclones that intensify into tropical storms between the coast of Americas and 140\xc2\xb0W are named by the National Hurricane Center (NHC/RSMC Miami), while tropical cyclones intensifying into tropical storms between 140\xc2\xb0W and 180\xc2\xb0 are named by the Central Pacific Hurricane Center (CPHC/RSMC Honolulu).\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ATL.2FEPAC_TCOP_1-13">\n        <a href="#cite_note-ATL.2FEPAC_TCOP-1">\n         [1]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       Significant tropical cyclones have their names retired from the lists and a replacement name selected at the next World Meteorological Organization Hurricane Committee.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ATL.2FEPAC_TCOP_1-14">\n        <a href="#cite_note-ATL.2FEPAC_TCOP-1">\n         [1]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       The current naming scheme began with the\n       <a href="/wiki/1978_Pacific_hurricane_season" title="1978 Pacific hurricane season">\n        1978 season\n       </a>\n       , one year before the Atlantic basin (and which anomalously used the list that will be used next in 2018, rather than the one for 2020). As with the Atlantic basin, it uses alternating women\'s and men\'s names, and also includes some Spanish and a few French names. Before then, only women\'s names were used. Because Eastern Pacific hurricanes mainly threaten western Mexico and Central America, the lists contain more Spanish names than the Atlantic lists.\n      </p>\n      <h3>\n       <span id="North_Pacific_east_of_140\xc2\xb0W">\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="North_Pacific_east_of_140.C2.B0W">\n        North Pacific east of 140\xc2\xb0W\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Tropical_cyclone_naming&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: North Pacific east of 140\xc2\xb0W">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h3>\n      <p>\n       When a tropical depression intensifies into a tropical storm to the north of the Equator between the coastline of the Americas and 140\xc2\xb0W, it will be named by the NHC. There are six lists of names which rotate every six years and begin with the letters A\xe2\x80\x94Z used, skipping Q and U, with each name alternating between a male or a female name.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ATL.2FEPAC_TCOP_1-15">\n        <a href="#cite_note-ATL.2FEPAC_TCOP-1">\n         [1]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       The names of significant tropical cyclones are\n       <a href="/wiki/List_of_retired_Pacific_hurricane_names" title="List of retired Pacific hurricane names">\n        retired\n       </a>\n       from the lists, with a replacement name selected at the next World Meteorological Organization\'s Hurricane Committee.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ATL.2FEPAC_TCOP_1-16">\n        <a href="#cite_note-ATL.2FEPAC_TCOP-1">\n         [1]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       If all of the names on a list are used, storms are named using the letters of the Greek alphabet.\n       <sup class="reference" Tropical cyclones that occur within the Northern Hemisphere between the\n       <a href="/wiki/180th_meridian" title="180th meridian">\n        anti-meridian\n       </a>\n       and\n       <a class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Tropical_cyclone_naming&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: International names">\n </div>\n      <p>\n       Tropical cyclones within the Western Pacific are assigned international names by the JMA when they become a tropical storm with 10-minute sustained winds of at least 34\xc2\xa0kn (39\xc2\xa0mph; 63\xc2\xa0km/h).\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WPAC_TCOP_2-4">\n        <a href="#cite_note-WPAC_TCOP-2">\n         [2]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       The names are used sequentially without regard to year and are taken from five lists of names that were prepared by the ESCAP/WMO Typhoon Committee, after each of the 14 members submitted 10 names in 1998.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WPAC_TCOP_2-5">\n        <a href="#cite_note-WPAC_TCOP-2">\n         [2]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       The order of the names to be used was determined by placing the English name of the members in alphabetical order.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WPAC_TCOP_2-6">\n        <a href="#cite_note-WPAC_TCOP-2">\n         [2]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       Members of the committee are allowed to request the\n       <a href="/wiki/List_of_retired_Pacific_typhoon_names#Background" title="List of retired Pacific typhoon names">\n        retirement or replacement\n       </a>\n       of a system\'s name if it causes extensive destruction or for other reasons such as number of deaths.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WPAC_TCOP_2-7">\n        <a href="#cite_note-WPAC_TCOP-2">\n         [2]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       Unlike other basins, storms are also named after plants, animals, objects,     </div>\n       </div>\n      </div>\n      <p>\n       Within the South-West Indian Ocean in the Southern Hemisphere between Africa and 90\xc2\xb0E, a tropical or subtropical disturbance is named when it is judged to have intensified into a tropical storm with winds of at least 34\xc2\xa0kn (39\xc2\xa0mph; 63\xc2\xa0km/h).\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SWIO_TCOP_5-3">\n        <a href="#cite_note-SWIO_TCOP-5">\n         [5]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Reunion_Names_22-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-Reunion_Names-22">\n Meteorological Service names it.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SWIO_TCOP_5-6">\n        <a href="#cite_note-SWIO_TCOP-5">\n         [5]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       The names are
