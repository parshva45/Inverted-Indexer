Extratropical\n       <a href="/wiki/Cyclone" title="Cyclone">\n        cyclones\n       </a>\n       are capable of producing anything from cloudiness and mild\n       <a href="/wiki/Rain" title="Rain">\n        showers\n       </a>\n       to heavy\n       <a href="/wiki/Gale" title="Gale">\n        gales\n       </a>\n       ,\n       <a href="/wiki/Thunderstorm" title="Thunderstorm">\n        thunderstorms\n       </a>\n       ,\n       <a href="/wiki/Blizzard" title="Blizzard">\n        blizzards\n       </a>\n       , and\n       <a href="/wiki/Tornado" title="Tornado">\n        tornadoes\n       </a>\n       . These types of cyclones are defined as\n       <a href="/wiki/Synoptic_scale_meteorology" title="Synoptic scale meteorology">\n        large scale (synoptic)\n       </a>\n       low pressure\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Weather_system" title="Weather system">\n        weather systems\n       </a>\n       that occur in the\n       <a href="/wiki/Middle_latitudes" title="Middle latitudes">\n        middle latitudes\n       </a>\n       of the Earth. In contrast with\n       <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone" title="Tropical cyclone">\n        tropical cyclones\n       </a>\n       , extratropical cyclones produce rapid changes in temperature and\n       <a href="/wiki/Dew_point" title="Dew point">\n        dew point\n       </a>\n       along broad lines, called\n       <a href="/wiki/Weather_front" title="Weather front">\n        weather fronts\n       </a>\n       , about the center of the cyclone.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ExtraLessonMillUni_1-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-ExtraLessonMillUni-1">\n         [1]\n <a href="/wiki/Cyclone" title="Cyclone">\n        cyclone\n       </a>\n       " applies to numerous types of low pressure areas, one of which is the extratropical cyclone. The descriptor\n       <i>\n        extratropical\n       </i>\n       signifies that this type of cyclone generally occurs outside the tropics and in the middle\n       <a href="/wiki/Latitude" title="Latitude">\n        latitudes\n       </a>\n       of Earth between 30\xc2\xb0 and 60\xc2\xb0 latitude. They are termed\n       <i>\n        mid-latitude cyclones\n       </i>\n       if they form within those latitudes, or\n       <a href="/wiki/Post-tropical_cyclone" title="Post-tropical cyclone">\n        post-tropical cyclones\n       </a>\n       if a tropical cyclone has intruded into the mid latitudes.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ExtraLessonMillUni_1-1">\n        <a href="#cite_note-ExtraLessonMillUni-1">\n         [1]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ExtratropicalPhases_2-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-ExtratropicalPhases-2">\n         [2]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       Weather forecasters and the general public often describe them simply as "\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Depression_(weather)" title="Depression (weather)">\n        depressions\n       </a>\n       " or "lows". Terms like frontal cyclone, frontal depression, frontal low, extratropical low, non-tropical low and hybrid low are often used as well.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       Extratropical cyclones are classified mainly as\n       <a href="/wiki/Baroclinity" title="Baroclinity">\n        baroclinic\n       </a>\n       , because they form along zones of temperature and dewpoint\n       <a href="/wiki/Gradient" title="Gradient">\n        gradient\n       </a>\n       known as\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Weather_fronts" title="Weather fronts">\n        frontal zones\n       </a>\n       . They can become\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Barotropic" title="Barotropic">\n        barotropic\n       </a>\n       late in their life cycle, when the distribution of heat around the cyclone becomes fairly uniform with its radius.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">\n        <a href="#cite_note-3">\n         [3]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <h2>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Formation">\n        Formation\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n  ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h2>\n      <div class="thumb tleft">\n       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">\n        <a class="image" extratropical cyclone formation worldwide\n        </div>\n       </div>\n      </div>\n      <div class="thumb tleft">\n       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">\n        <a class="image"   <div class="thumbcaption">\n         <div class="magnify">\n          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Jetstreak.png" title="Enlarge">\n          </a>\n         </div>\n         An upper level jet streak. DIV areas are regions of divergence aloft, which will lead to surface convergence and aid cyclogenesis.\n        </div>\n       </div>\n      </div>\n      <p>\n       Extratropical cyclones form anywhere within the extratropical regions of the Earth (usually between 30\xc2\xb0 and 60\xc2\xb0 latitude from the\n       <a href="/wiki/Equator" title="Equator">\n        equator\n       </a>\n       ), either through cyclogenesis or extratropical transition. A study of extratropical cyclones in the\n       <a href="/wiki/Southern_Hemisphere" title="Southern Hemisphere">\n        Southern Hemisphere\n       </a>\n       shows that between the\n       <a href="/wiki/30th_parallel_south" title="30th parallel south">\n        30th\n       </a>\n       and\n       <a href="/wiki/70th_parallel_south" title="70th parallel south">\n        70th parallels\n       </a>\n       , there are an average of 37 cyclones in existence during any 6-hour period.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-VarSouthCyc_4-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-VarSouthCyc-4">\n         [4]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       A separate study in the\n       <a href="/wiki/Northern_Hemisphere" title="Northern Hemisphere">\n        Northern Hemisphere\n       </a>\n       suggests that approximately 234 significant extratropical cyclones form each winter.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WinterStormsNorth_5-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-WinterStormsNorth-5">\n         [5]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <h3>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Cyclogenesis">\n        Cyclogenesis\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Extratropical_cyclone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Cyclogenesis">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h3>\n      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">\n       Main article:\n       <a href="/wiki/Cyclogenesis" title="Cyclogenesis">\n        Cyclogenesis\n       </a>\n      </div>\n      <p>\n       Extratropical cyclones form along linear bands of temperature/dewpoint gradient with significant vertical\n       <a href="/wiki/Wind_shear" title="Wind shear">\n        wind shear\n       </a>\n       , and are thus classified as baroclinic cyclones. Initially,\n       <a href="/wiki/Cyclogenesis" title="Cyclogenesis">\n        cyclogenesis\n       </a>\n       , or low pressure formation, occurs along\n       <a href="/wiki/Surface_weather_analysis" title="Surface weather analysis">\n        frontal zones\n       </a>\n       near a favorable quadrant of a maximum in the\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jetstream" title="Jetstream">\n        upper level jetstream\n       </a>\n       known as a jet streak. The favorable quadrants are usually at the right rear and left front quadrants, where\n       <a href="/wiki/Divergence" title="Divergence">\n        divergence\n       </a>\n       ensues.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">\n        <a href="#cite_note-6">\n         [6]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       The divergence causes air to rush out from the top of the air column. As mass in the column is reduced,\n       <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_pressure" title="Atmospheric pressure">\n        atmospheric pressure\n       </a>\n       at surface level (the weight of the air column) is reduced. The lowered pressure strengthens the cyclone (a low pressure system). The lowered pressure acts to draw in air, creating\n       <a href="/wiki/Convergence_zone" title="Convergence zone">\n        convergence\n       </a>\n       in the low-level wind field. Low-level convergence and upper-level divergence imply upward motion within the column, making cyclones tend to be cloudy. As the cyclone strengthens, the cold front sweeps towards the\n       <a href="/wiki/Equator" title="Equator">\n        equator\n       </a>\n       and moves around the back of the cyclone. Meanwhile, its associated\n       <a href="/wiki/Warm_front" title="Warm front">\n        warm front\n       </a>\n       progresses more slowly, as the cooler air ahead of the system is\n       <a href="/wiki/Density" title="Density">\n        denser\n       </a>\n       , and therefore more difficult to dislodge. Later, the cyclones\n       <a href="/wiki/Occluded_front" title="Occluded front">\n        occlude\n       </a>\n       as the poleward portion of the cold front overtakes a section of the warm front, forcing a tongue, or\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Trowal" title="Trowal">\n        trowal\n       </a>\n       , of warm air aloft. Eventually, the cyclone will become barotropically cold and begin to weaken.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_pressure" title="Atmospheric pressure">\n        Atmospheric pressure\n       </a>\n       can fall very rapidly when there are strong upper level forces on the system. When pressures fall more than 1\n       <a href="/wiki/Bar_(unit)" title="Bar (unit)">\n        millibar\n       </a>\n       (0.030\n       <a href="/wiki/Inch_of_mercury" title="Inch of mercury">\n        inHg\n       </a>\n       ) per hour, the process is called explosive cyclogenesis, and the cyclone can be described as a\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bomb_(meteorology)" title="Bomb (meteorology)">\n        (weather) bomb\n       </a>\n       .\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BombNWAtlantic_7-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-BombNWAtlantic-7">\n         [7]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BombDef_8-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-BombDef-8">\n         [8]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bomb_Ref_9-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-Bomb_Ref-9">\n         [9]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       These bombs rapidly drop in pressure to below 980 millibars (28.94\xc2\xa0inHg) under favorable conditions such as near a natural\n       <a href="/wiki/Temperature_gradient" title="Temperature gradient">\n        temperature gradient\n       </a>\n       like the\n       <a href="/wiki/Gulf_Stream" title="Gulf Stream">\n        Gulf Stream\n       </a>\n       , or at a preferred quadrant of an upper level jet streak, where upper level divergence is best. The stronger the upper level divergence over the cyclone, the deeper the cyclone can become. Hurricane-force extratropical cyclones are most likely to form in the northern Atlantic and northern Pacific oceans in the months of December and January.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HurrForceExtraTropCyc_10-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-HurrForceExtraTropCyc-10">\n         [10]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       On 14 and 15 December 1986, an extratropical cyclone near Iceland deepened to below 920 hectopascals (27\xc2\xa0inHg),\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">\n        <a href="#cite_note-11">\n         [11]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       which is a pressure equivalent to a\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saffir-Simpson_Hurricane_Scale" title="Saffir-Simpson Hurricane Scale">\n        category 5 hurricane\n       </a>\n       . In the\n       <a href="/wiki/Arctic_Ocean" title="Arctic Ocean">\n        Arctic\n       </a>\n       , the average pressure for cyclones is 980 millibars (28.94\xc2\xa0inHg) during the winter, and 1,000 millibars (29.53\xc2\xa0inHg) during the summer.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ArcticCycloneStats_12-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-ArcticCycloneStats-12">\n         [12]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <h3>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Extratropical_transition">\n        Extratropical transition\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Extratropical_cyclone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Extratropical transition">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h3>\n      <div class="thumb tright">\n       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">\n        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:ETS_Florence_2006.jpg">\n         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="7200" data-file-width="7200" height="220" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0b/ETS_Florence_2006.jpg/220px-ETS_Florence_2006.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0b/ETS_Florence_2006.jpg/330px-ETS_Florence_2006.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0b/ETS_Florence_2006.jpg/440px-ETS_Florence_2006.jpg 2x" width="220"/>\n        </a>\n        <div class="thumbcaption">\n         <div class="magnify">\n          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:ETS_Florence_2006.jpg" title="Enlarge">\n          </a>\n         </div>\n         <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Florence_(2006)" title="Hurricane Florence (2006)">\n          Hurricane Florence\n         </a>\n         in the north Atlantic after completing its transition to an extratropical cyclone from a hurricane\n        </div>\n       </div>\n      </div>\n      <p>\n       <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone" title="Tropical cyclone">\n        Tropical cyclones\n       </a>\n       often transform into extratropical cyclones at the end of their tropical existence, usually between 30\xc2\xb0 and 40\xc2\xb0 latitude, where there is sufficient\n       <a href="/wiki/Troposphere#Forcing" title="Troposphere">\n        forcing\n       </a>\n       from upper-level troughs or\n       <a href="/wiki/Shortwave_(meteorology)" title="Shortwave (meteorology)">\n        shortwaves\n       </a>\n       riding the\n       <a href="/wiki/Westerlies" title="Westerlies">\n        Westerlies\n       </a>\n       for the process of extratropical transition to begin.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ETClimo_13-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-ETClimo-13">\n         [13]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       During this process, a cyclone in extratropical transition (known across the eastern North Pacific and North Atlantic oceans as the post-tropical stage),\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CanHurrCenterGlossary_14-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-CanHurrCenterGlossary-14">\n         [14]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">\n        <a href="#cite_note-15">\n         [15]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       will invariably form or connect with nearby fronts and/or troughs consistent with a baroclinic system. Due to this, the size of the system will usually appear to increase, while the core weakens. However, after transition is complete, the storm may re-strengthen due to baroclinic energy, depending on the environmental conditions surrounding the system.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ETClimo_13-1">\n        <a href="#cite_note-ETClimo-13">\n         [13]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       The cyclone will also distort in shape, becoming less symmetric with time.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CPS-ET_16-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-CPS-ET-16">\n         [16]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CPS1_17-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-CPS1-17">\n         [17]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ETCompositesHEE_18-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-ETCompositesHEE-18">\n         [18]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       During extratropical transition, the cyclone begins to tilt back into the colder airmass with height, and the cyclone\'s primary energy source converts from the release of latent\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heat_of_condensation" title="Heat of condensation">\n        heat from condensation\n       </a>\n       (from thunderstorms near the center) to\n       <a href="/wiki/Baroclinity" title="Baroclinity">\n        baroclinic\n       </a>\n       processes. The low pressure system eventually loses its warm core and becomes a\n       <a href="/wiki/Cold-core_low" title="Cold-core low">\n        cold-core system\n       </a>\n       .\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ETCompositesHEE_18-1">\n        <a href="#cite_note-ETCompositesHEE-18">\n         [18]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CPS-ET_16-1">\n        <a href="#cite_note-CPS-ET-16">\n         [16]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       The peak time of\n       <a href="/wiki/Subtropical_cyclone" title="Subtropical cyclone">\n        subtropical\n       </a>\n       cyclogenesis (the midpoint of this transition) in the North Atlantic is in the months of September and October, when the difference between the temperature of the air aloft and the\n       <a href="/wiki/Sea_surface_temperature" title="Sea surface temperature">\n        sea surface temperature\n       </a>\n       is the greatest, leading to the greatest potential for instability.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NAtlSTClimo_19-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-NAtlSTClimo-19">\n         [19]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       On rare occasions, an extratropical cyclone can transit into a tropical cyclone if it reaches an area of ocean with warmer waters and an environment with less vertical wind shear.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NAtlSTCases_20-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-NAtlSTCases-20">\n         [20]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       An example of this happening is in the\n       <a href="/wiki/1991_Perfect_Storm" title="1991 Perfect Storm">\n        1991 Perfect Storm\n       </a>\n       .\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HistSubTropCyclones_21-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-HistSubTropCyclones-21">\n         [21]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       The process known as "tropical transition" involves the usually slow development of an extratropically cold core vortex into a tropical cyclone.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TropicalTransition_22-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-TropicalTransition-22">\n         [22]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TropicalTransition2_23-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-TropicalTransition2-23">\n         [23]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       The\n       <a href="/wiki/Joint_Typhoon_Warning_Center" title="Joint Typhoon Warning Center">\n        Joint Typhoon Warning Center\n       </a>\n       uses the extratropical transition (XT) technique to subjectively estimate the intensity of tropical cyclones becoming extratropical based on visible and infrared\n       <a href="/wiki/Weather_satellite" title="Weather satellite">\n        satellite imagery\n       </a>\n       . Loss of central convection in transitioning tropical cyclones can cause the\n       <a href="/wiki/Dvorak_technique" title="Dvorak technique">\n        Dvorak technique\n       </a>\n       to fail;\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24">\n        <a href="#cite_note-24">\n         [24]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       the loss of convection results in unrealistically low estimates using the Dvorak technique.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25">\n        <a href="#cite_note-25">\n         [25]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       The system combines aspects of the Dvorak technique, used for estimating tropical cyclone intensity, and the\n       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Hebert-Poteat_technique&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Hebert-Poteat technique (page does not exist)">\n        Hebert-Poteat technique\n       </a>\n       , used for estimating\n       <a href="/wiki/Subtropical_cyclone" title="Subtropical cyclone">\n        subtropical cyclone\n       </a>\n       intensity.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26">\n        <a href="#cite_note-26">\n         [26]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       The technique is applied when a tropical cyclone interacts with a\n       <a href="/wiki/Surface_weather_analysis#Fronts" title="Surface weather analysis">\n        frontal boundary\n       </a>\n       or loses its central convection while maintaining its forward speed or accelerating.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated2_27-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-autogenerated2-27">\n         [27]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       The XT scale corresponds to the Dvorak scale and is applied in the same way, except that "XT" is used instead of "T" to indicate that the system is undergoing extratropical transition.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_28-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-28">\n         [28]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       Also, the XT technique is only used once extratropical transition begins; the Dvorak technique is still used if the system begins dissipating without transition.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated2_27-1">\n        <a href="#cite_note-autogenerated2-27">\n         [27]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       Once the cyclone has completed transition and become\n       <a href="/wiki/Cold-core_low" title="Cold-core low">\n        cold-core\n       </a>\n       , the technique is no longer used.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_28-1">\n        <a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-28">\n         [28]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <h2>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Structure">\n        Structure\n       </span>\n <h3>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Surface_pressure_and_wind_distribution">\n        Surface pressure and wind distribution\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Extratropical_cyclone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Surface pressure and wind distribution">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h3>\n      <div class="thumb tright">\n       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">\n        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Quikscatcyclone.jpg">\n         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="290" data-file-width="450" height="142" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/08/Quikscatcyclone.jpg/220px-Quikscatcyclone.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/08/Quikscatcyclone.jpg/330px-Quikscatcyclone.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/08/Quikscatcyclone.jpg/440px-Quikscatcyclone.jpg 2x" width="220"/>\n        </a>\n        <div class="thumbcaption">\n         <div class="magnify">\n          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Quikscatcyclone.jpg" title="Enlarge">\n          </a>\n         </div>\n         <a href="/wiki/QuikSCAT" title="QuikSCAT">\n          QuikSCAT\n         </a>\n         image of typical extratropical cyclones over the ocean. Note the maximum winds are on the outside of the occlusion.\n        </div>\n       </div>\n      </div>\n      <p>\n       The windfield of an extratropical cyclone constricts with distance in relation to surface level pressure, with the lowest pressure being found near the center, and the highest winds typically just on the cold/poleward side of warm fronts, occlusions, and\n       <a href="/wiki/Cold_front" title="Cold front">\n        cold fronts\n       </a>\n       , where the\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pressure_gradient_force" title="Pressure gradient force">\n        pressure gradient force\n       </a>\n       is highest.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ww2010PGF_29-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-ww2010PGF-29">\n         [29]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       The area poleward and west of the cold and warm fronts connected to extratropical cyclones is known as the cold sector, while the area equatorward and east of its associated cold and warm fronts is known as the warm sector.\n      </p>\n      <div class="thumb tleft">\n       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">\n        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Extratropical_cyclone_off_Australia_2016-12-28_0230Z.jpg">\n         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1080" data-file-width="1440" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/08/Extratropical_cyclone_off_Australia_2016-12-28_0230Z.jpg/220px-Extratropical_cyclone_off_Australia_2016-12-28_0230Z.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/08/Extratropical_cyclone_off_Australia_2016-12-28_0230Z.jpg/330px-Extratropical_cyclone_off_Australia_2016-12-28_0230Z.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/08/Extratropical_cyclone_off_Australia_2016-12-28_0230Z.jpg/440px-Extratropical_cyclone_off_Australia_2016-12-28_0230Z.jpg 2x" width="220"/>\n        </a>\n        <div class="thumbcaption">\n         <div class="magnify">\n          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Extratropical_cyclone_off_Australia_2016-12-28_0230Z.jpg" title="Enlarge">\n          </a>\n         </div>\n         Extratropical cyclones are clockwise spinning in the Southern Hemisphere, just like tropical cyclones.\n        </div>\n       </div>\n      </div>\n      <p>\n       The wind flow around an extratropical cyclone is\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Clockwise_and_counterclockwise" title="Clockwise and counterclockwise">\n        counterclockwise\n       </a>\n       in the northern hemisphere, and clockwise in the southern hemisphere, due to the\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coriolis_effect" title="Coriolis effect">\n        Coriolis effect\n       </a>\n       (this manner of rotation is generally referred to as\n       <i>\n        cyclonic\n       </i>\n       ). Near this center, the pressure gradient force (from the pressure at the center of the cyclone compared to the pressure outside the cyclone) and the Coriolis force must be in an approximate balance for the cyclone to avoid collapsing in on itself as a result of the difference in pressure.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30">\n        <a href="#cite_note-30">\n         [30]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       The central pressure of the cyclone will lower with increasing maturity, while outside of the cyclone, the\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mean_sea_level_pressure" title="Mean sea level pressure">\n        sea-level pressure\n       </a>\n       is about average. In most extratropical cyclones, the part of the cold front ahead of the cyclone will develop into a warm front, giving the frontal zone (as drawn on\n       <a href="/wiki/Surface_weather_analysis" title="Surface weather analysis">\n        surface weather maps\n       </a>\n       ) a wave-like shape. Due to their appearance on satellite images, extratropical cyclones can also be referred to as frontal waves early in their life cycle. In the\n       <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">\n        United States\n       </a>\n       , an old name for such a system is "warm wave".\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GlobalAverageSLP_31-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-GlobalAverageSLP-31">\n         [31]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       In the northern hemisphere, once a cyclone occludes, a\n       <b>\n        tr\n       </b>\n       ough\n       <b>\n        o\n       </b>\n       f\n       <b>\n        w\n       </b>\n       arm air\n       <b>\n        al\n       </b>\n       oft\xe2\x80\x94or "trowal" for short\xe2\x80\x94will be caused by strong southerly winds on its eastern periphery rotating aloft around its northeast, and ultimately into its northwestern periphery (also known as the warm conveyor belt), forcing a surface trough to continue into the cold sector on a similar curve to the occluded front. The trowal creates the portion of an occluded cyclone known as its\n       <b>\n        comma head\n       </b>\n       , due to the\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Comma_(punctuation)" title="Comma (punctuation)">\n        comma\n       </a>\n       -like shape of the mid-tropospheric cloudiness that accompanies the feature. It can also be the focus of locally heavy precipitation, with thunderstorms possible if the atmosphere along the trowal is unstable enough for convection.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TROW_32-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-TROW-32">\n         [32]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <h3>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Vertical_structure">\n        Vertical structure\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Extratropical_cyclone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Vertical structure">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h3>\n      <p>\n       Extratropical cyclones slant back into colder air masses and strengthen with height, sometimes exceeding 30,000\xc2\xa0feet (approximately 9\xc2\xa0km) in depth.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MidLatVertStructure_33-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-MidLatVertStructure-33">\n         [33]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       Above the surface of the earth, the air temperature near the center of the cyclone is increasingly colder than the surrounding environment. These characteristics are the direct opposite of those found in their counterparts,\n       <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone" title="Tropical cyclone">\n        tropical cyclones\n       </a>\n       ; thus, they are sometimes called "cold-core lows".\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AnlFcastHelp_34-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-AnlFcastHelp-34">\n         [34]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       Various charts can be examined to check the characteristics of a cold-core system with height, such as the 700 millibars (20.67\xc2\xa0inHg) chart, which is at about 10,000 feet (3,048 meters) altitude. Cyclone phase diagrams are used to tell whether a cyclone is tropical, subtropical, or extratropical.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CycPhaseEvolution_35-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-CycPhaseEvolution-35">\n         [35]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <h2>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Cyclone_evolution">\n        Cyclone evolution\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Extratropical_cyclone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Cyclone evolution">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h2>\n      <div class="thumb tright">\n       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">\n        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Alex_2016-01-10_1635Z.jpg">\n         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="5600" data-file-width="4200" height="293" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/eb/Alex_2016-01-10_1635Z.jpg/220px-Alex_2016-01-10_1635Z.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/eb/Alex_2016-01-10_1635Z.jpg/330px-Alex_2016-01-10_1635Z.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/eb/Alex_2016-01-10_1635Z.jpg/440px-Alex_2016-01-10_1635Z.jpg 2x" width="220"/>\n        </a>\n        <div class="thumbcaption">\n         <div class="magnify">\n          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Alex_2016-01-10_1635Z.jpg" title="Enlarge">\n          </a>\n         </div>\n         A\n         <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Alex_(2016)" title="Hurricane Alex (2016)">\n          hurricane-force extratropical cyclone\n         </a>\n         in January 2016 with a distinct eye-like feature, caused by a warm seclusion\n        </div>\n       </div>\n      </div>\n      <p>\n       There are two models of cyclone development and lifecycles in common use\xe2\x80\x94the Norwegian model and the Shapiro-Keyser Model.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-UnifiedSurfaceAnalysisManual_36-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-UnifiedSurfaceAnalysisManual-36">\n         [36]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <h3>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Norwegian_cyclone_model">\n        Norwegian cyclone model\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Extratropical_cyclone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Norwegian cyclone model">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h3>\n      <p>\n       Of the two theories on extratropical cyclone structure and life cycle, the older is the Norwegian Cyclone Model, developed during\n       <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">\n        World War I\n       </a>\n       . In this theory, cyclones develop as they move up and along a frontal boundary, eventually\n       <a href="/wiki/Occluded_front" title="Occluded front">\n        occluding\n       </a>\n       and reaching a barotropically cold environment.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NorCycMod_37-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-NorCycMod-37">\n         [37]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       It was developed completely from surface-based weather observations, including descriptions of clouds found near frontal boundaries. This theory still retains merit, as it is a good description for extratropical cyclones over continental landmasses.\n      </p>\n      <h3>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Shapiro-Keyser_model">\n        Shapiro-Keyser model\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Extratropical_cyclone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Shapiro-Keyser model">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h3>\n      <p>\n       A second competing theory for extratropical cyclone development over the oceans is the Shapiro-Keyser model, developed in 1990.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CIMMSStrucEvoTLF_38-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-CIMMSStrucEvoTLF-38">\n         [38]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       Its main differences with the Norwegian Cyclone Model are the fracture of the cold front, treating warm-type occlusions and warm fronts as the same, and allowing the cold front to progress through the warm sector\n       <a href="/wiki/Perpendicular" title="Perpendicular">\n        perpendicular\n       </a>\n       to the warm front. This model was based on oceanic cyclones and their frontal structure, as seen in surface observations and in previous projects which used aircraft to determine the vertical structure of fronts across the northwest Atlantic.\n      </p>\n      <h4>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Warm_seclusion">\n        Warm seclusion\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Extratropical_cyclone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Warm seclusion">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h4>\n      <p>\n       A warm seclusion is the mature phase of the extratropical cyclone lifecycle. This was conceptualized after the\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ERICA_(Meteorology)" title="ERICA (Meteorology)">\n        ERICA\n       </a>\n       field experiment of the late 1980s, which produced observations of intense marine cyclones that indicated an anomalously warm low-level thermal structure, secluded (or surrounded) by a bent-back warm front and a coincident chevron-shaped band of intense surface winds.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WarmSeclusionCycMet_39-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-WarmSeclusionCycMet-39">\n         [39]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       The\n       <a href="/wiki/Norwegian_cyclone_model" title="Norwegian cyclone model">\n        Norwegian Cyclone Model\n       </a>\n       , as developed by the\n       <a href="/wiki/Bergen_School_of_Meteorology" title="Bergen School of Meteorology">\n        Bergen School of Meteorology\n       </a>\n       , largely observed cyclones at the tail end of their lifecycle and used the term occlusion to identify the decaying stages.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       Warm seclusions may have cloud-free,\n       <a href="/wiki/Eye_(cyclone)" title="Eye (cyclone)">\n        eye\n       </a>\n       -like features at their center (reminiscent of\n       <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone" title="Tropical cyclone">\n        tropical cyclones\n       </a>\n       ), significant pressure falls, hurricane-force winds, and moderate to strong\n       <a href="/wiki/Convection" title="Convection">\n        convection\n       </a>\n       . The most intense warm seclusions often attain pressures less than 950\xc2\xa0millibars (28.05\xc2\xa0inHg) with a definitive lower to mid-level warm core structure.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WarmSeclusionCycMet_39-1">\n        <a href="#cite_note-WarmSeclusionCycMet-39">\n         [39]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       A warm seclusion, the result of a baroclinic lifecycle, occurs at latitudes well poleward of the tropics.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       As latent heat\n       <a href="/wiki/Flux" title="Flux">\n        flux\n       </a>\n       releases are important for their development and intensification, most warm seclusion events occur over the\n       <a href="/wiki/Ocean" title="Ocean">\n        oceans\n       </a>\n       ; they may impact coastal nations with hurricane force\n       <a href="/wiki/Wind" title="Wind">\n        winds\n       </a>\n       and torrential\n       <a href="/wiki/Rain" title="Rain">\n        rain\n       </a>\n       .\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CIMMSStrucEvoTLF_38-1">\n        <a href="#cite_note-CIMMSStrucEvoTLF-38">\n         [38]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Blizzicanes_40-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-Blizzicanes-40">\n         [40]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       Climatologically, the Northern Hemisphere sees warm seclusions during the cold season months, while the Southern Hemisphere may see a strong cyclone event such as this during all times of the year.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       In all tropical basins, except the Northern Indian Ocean, the extratropical transition of a tropical cyclone may result in reintensification into a warm seclusion. For example,\n       <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Maria_(2005)" title="Hurricane Maria (2005)">\n        Hurricane Maria\n       </a>\n       of 2005 reintensified into a strong baroclinic system and achieved warm seclusion status at maturity (or lowest pressure).\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Maria_41-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-Maria-41">\n         [41]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <h2>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Motion">\n        Motion\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Extratropical_cyclone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Motion">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h2>\n      <div class="thumb tright">\n       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;">\n        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Zonalflow.gif">\n         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="265" data-file-width="413" height="192" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0c/Zonalflow.gif/300px-Zonalflow.gif" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/0/0c/Zonalflow.gif 1.5x" width="300"/>\n        </a>\n        <div class="thumbcaption">\n         <div class="magnify">\n          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Zonalflow.gif" title="Enlarge">\n          </a>\n         </div>\n         A zonal flow regime. Note the dominant west-to-east flow as shown in the 500 hPa height pattern.\n        </div>\n       </div>\n      </div>\n      <div class="thumb tright">\n       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;">\n        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Feb242007_blizzard.gif">\n         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1600" data-file-width="3400" height="141" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2c/Feb242007_blizzard.gif/300px-Feb242007_blizzard.gif" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2c/Feb242007_blizzard.gif/450px-Feb242007_blizzard.gif 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2c/Feb242007_blizzard.gif/600px-Feb242007_blizzard.gif 2x" width="300"/>\n        </a>\n        <div class="thumbcaption">\n         <div class="magnify">\n          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Feb242007_blizzard.gif" title="Enlarge">\n          </a>\n         </div>\n         A February 24, 2007 radar image of a large extratropical cyclonic storm system at its peak over the central United States.\n        </div>\n       </div>\n      </div>\n      <p>\n       Extratropical cyclones are generally driven, or "steered", by deep westerly winds in a general west to east motion across both the Northern and Southern hemispheres of the Earth. This general motion of atmospheric flow is known as "zonal".\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ZonalFlowDef_42-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-ZonalFlowDef-42">\n         [42]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       Where this general trend is the main steering influence of an extratropical cyclone, it is known as a "\n       <a href="/wiki/Zonal_flow" title="Zonal flow">\n        zonal flow regime\n       </a>\n       ".\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       When the general flow pattern buckles from a zonal pattern to the meridional pattern,\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MeridonialFlow_43-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-MeridonialFlow-43">\n         [43]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       a slower movement in a north or southward direction is more likely.\n       <a href="/wiki/Meridional_flow" title="Meridional flow">\n        Meridional flow\n       </a>\n       patterns feature strong, amplified troughs and ridges, generally with more northerly and southerly flow.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       Changes in direction of this nature are most commonly observed as a result of a cyclone\'s interaction with other\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Low_pressure_system" title="Low pressure system">\n        low pressure systems\n       </a>\n       ,\n       <a href="/wiki/Trough_(meteorology)" title="Trough (meteorology)">\n        troughs\n       </a>\n       ,\n       <a href="/wiki/Ridge_(meteorology)" title="Ridge (meteorology)">\n        ridges\n       </a>\n       , or with\n       <a href="/wiki/Anticyclone" title="Anticyclone">\n        anticyclones\n       </a>\n       . A strong and stationary anticyclone can effectively block the path of an extratropical cyclone. Such\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Blocking_anticyclone" title="Blocking anticyclone">\n        blocking patterns\n       </a>\n       are quite normal, and will generally result in a weakening of the cyclone, the weakening of the anticyclone, a diversion of the cyclone towards the anticyclone\'s periphery, or a combination of all three to some extent depending on the precise conditions. It is also common for an extratropical cyclone to strengthen as the blocking anticyclone or ridge weakens in these circumstances.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AnticycBlocking_44-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-AnticycBlocking-44">\n         [44]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       Where an extratropical cyclone encounters another extratropical cyclone (or almost any other kind of cyclonic\n       <a href="/wiki/Vortex" title="Vortex">\n        vortex\n       </a>\n       in the atmosphere), the two may combine to become a binary cyclone, where the vortices of the two cyclones rotate around each other (known as the "\n       <a href="/wiki/Fujiwhara_effect" title="Fujiwhara effect">\n        Fujiwhara effect\n       </a>\n       "). This most often results in a merging of the two low pressure systems into a single extratropical cyclone, or can less commonly result in a mere change of direction of either one or both of the cyclones.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ExtraFujiwhara_45-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-ExtraFujiwhara-45">\n         [45]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       The precise results of such interactions depend on factors such as the size of the two cyclones, their strength, their distance from each other, and the prevailing atmospheric conditions around them.\n      </p>\n      <h2>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Effects">\n        Effects\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Extratropical_cyclone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Effects">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h2>\n      <div class="thumb tleft">\n       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;">\n        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Snowcsi.gif">\n         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="671" data-file-width="993" height="203" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/49/Snowcsi.gif/300px-Snowcsi.gif" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/49/Snowcsi.gif/450px-Snowcsi.gif 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/49/Snowcsi.gif/600px-Snowcsi.gif 2x" width="300"/>\n        </a>\n        <div class="thumbcaption">\n         <div class="magnify">\n          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Snowcsi.gif" title="Enlarge">\n          </a>\n         </div>\n         Preferred region of snowfall in an extratropical cyclone\n        </div>\n       </div>\n      </div>\n      <h3>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="General">\n        General\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Extratropical_cyclone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: General">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h3>\n      <p>\n       Extratropical cyclones can bring mild weather with a little rain and surface\n       <a href="/wiki/Wind" title="Wind">\n        winds\n       </a>\n       of 15\xe2\x80\x9330\xc2\xa0km/h (9.3\xe2\x80\x9318.6\xc2\xa0mph), or they can be cold and dangerous with torrential rain and winds exceeding 119\xc2\xa0km/h (74\xc2\xa0mph),\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MarinersWeatherLog_46-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-MarinersWeatherLog-46">\n         [46]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       (sometimes referred to as\n       <a href="/wiki/European_windstorm" title="European windstorm">\n        windstorms\n       </a>\n       in Europe). The band of\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Precipitation_(meteorology)" title="Precipitation (meteorology)">\n        precipitation\n       </a>\n       that is associated with the\n       <a href="/wiki/Warm_front" title="Warm front">\n        warm front\n       </a>\n       is often extensive. In mature extratropical cyclones, an area known as the\n       <b>\n        comma head\n       </b>\n       on the northwest periphery of the surface low can be a region of heavy precipitation, frequent\n       <a href="/wiki/Thunderstorm" title="Thunderstorm">\n        thunderstorms\n       </a>\n       , and\n       <a href="/wiki/Thundersnow" title="Thundersnow">\n        thundersnows\n       </a>\n       . Cyclones tend to move along a predictable path at a moderate rate of progress. During\n       <a href="/wiki/Autumn" title="Autumn">\n        fall\n       </a>\n       , winter, and spring, the atmosphere over continents can be cold enough through the depth of the\n       <a href="/wiki/Troposphere" title="Troposphere">\n        troposphere\n       </a>\n       to cause snowfall.\n      </p>\n      <h3>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Severe_weather">\n        Severe weather\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Extratropical_cyclone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Severe weather">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h3>\n      <p>\n       Squall lines, or solid bands of strong thunderstorms, can form ahead of cold fronts and\n       <a href="/wiki/Trough_(meteorology)#Lee_trough" title="Trough (meteorology)">\n        lee troughs\n       </a>\n       due to the presence of significant atmospheric moisture and strong upper level divergence, leading to\n       <a href="/wiki/Hail" title="Hail">\n        hail\n       </a>\n       and high winds.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SquallLines_47-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-SquallLines-47">\n         [47]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       When significant directional wind shear exists in the atmosphere ahead of a cold front in the presence of a strong upper level jet stream,\n       <a href="/wiki/Tornado" title="Tornado">\n        tornado\n       </a>\n       formation is possible.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tornadoes_48-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-Tornadoes-48">\n         [48]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       Although tornadoes can form anywhere on Earth, the greatest number occur in the\n       <a href="/wiki/Great_Plains" title="Great Plains">\n        Great Plains\n       </a>\n       in the United States, because downsloped winds off the north-south oriented\n       <a href="/wiki/Rocky_Mountains" title="Rocky Mountains">\n        Rocky Mountains\n       </a>\n       , which can form a dryline, aid their development at any\n       <a href="/wiki/Fujita_scale" title="Fujita scale">\n        strength\n       </a>\n       .\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       Explosive development of extratropical cyclones can be sudden. The storm known in the UK as the "\n       <a href="/wiki/Great_Storm_of_1987" title="Great Storm of 1987">\n        Great Storm of 1987\n       </a>\n       " deepened to 953 millibars (28.14\xc2\xa0inHg) with a highest recorded wind of 220\xc2\xa0km/h (140\xc2\xa0mph), resulting in the loss of 19 lives, 15\xc2\xa0million trees, widespread damage to homes and an estimated economic cost of\n       <a href="/wiki/Pound_sterling" title="Pound sterling">\n        \xc2\xa3\n       </a>\n       1.2\xc2\xa0billion (\n       <a href="/wiki/United_States_dollar" title="United States dollar">\n        US$\n       </a>\n       2.3\xc2\xa0billion).\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MetoGreatStorm87_49-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-MetoGreatStorm87-49">\n         [49]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       Although most tropical cyclones that become extratropical quickly dissipate or are absorbed by another weather system, they can still retain winds of hurricane or gale force. In 1954,\n       <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Hazel" title="Hurricane Hazel">\n        Hurricane Hazel\n       </a>\n       became extratropical over\n       <a href="/wiki/North_Carolina" title="North Carolina">\n        North Carolina\n       </a>\n       as a strong Category\xc2\xa03 storm. The\n       <a href="/wiki/Columbus_Day_Storm_of_1962" title="Columbus Day Storm of 1962">\n        Columbus Day Storm of 1962\n       </a>\n       , which evolved from the remains of Typhoon Freda, caused heavy damage in\n       <a href="/wiki/Oregon" title="Oregon">\n        Oregon\n       </a>\n       and\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Washington_(U.S._state)" title="Washington (U.S. state)">\n        Washington\n       </a>\n       , with widespread damage equivalent to at least a Category\xc2\xa03. In 2005,\n       <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Wilma" title="Hurricane Wilma">\n        Hurricane Wilma\n       </a>\n       began to lose tropical characteristics while still sporting Category\xc2\xa03-force winds (and became fully extratropical as a Category\xc2\xa01 storm).\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wilma_50-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-Wilma-50">\n         [50]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       In summer, extratropical cyclones are generally weak, but some of the systems can cause significant\n       <a href="/wiki/Flood" title="Flood">\n        floods\n       </a>\n       overland because of torrential rainfall. The\n       <a href="/wiki/July_2016_North_China_cyclone" title="July 2016 North China cyclone">\n        July 2016 North China cyclone\n       </a>\n       never brought\n       <a href="/wiki/Gale" title="Gale">\n        gale\n       </a>\n       -force sustained winds, but it caused devastating floods in\n       <a href="/wiki/Mainland_China" title="Mainland China">\n        mainland China\n       </a>\n       , resulting in at least 184 deaths and\n       <a href="/wiki/Renminbi" title="Renminbi">\n        \xc2\xa5\n       </a>\n       33.19 billion (\n       <a href="/wiki/United_States_dollar" title="United States dollar">\n        US$\n       </a>\n       4.96 billion) of damage.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mca_north_51-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-mca_north-51">\n         [51]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mca_southwest_52-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-mca_southwest-52">\n         [52]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <h3>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Climate_and_general_circulation">\n        Climate and general circulation\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Extratropical_cyclone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Climate and general circulation">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h3>\n      <p>\n       In the classic analysis by\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Edward_Lorenz" title="Edward Lorenz">\n        Edward Lorenz\n       </a>\n       (the Lorenz Energy Cycle),\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53">\n        <a href="#cite_note-53">\n         [53]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       extratropical cyclones (so-called atmospheric transients) acts as a mechanism in converting potential energy that is created by pole to equator temperature gradients to eddy kinetic energy. In the process, the pole-equator temperature gradient is reduced (i.e. energy is transported poleward to warm up the higher latitudes).\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       The existence of such transients are also closely related to the formation of the Icelandic and Aleutian Low\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x94 the two most prominent general circulation features in the mid- to sub-polar northern latitudes.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54">\n        <a href="#cite_note-54">\n         [54]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       The two lows are formed by both the transport of kinetic energy and the latent heating (the energy released when water phase changed from vapor to liquid during precipitation) from the extratropical cyclones.\n      </p>\n      <h2>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Historic_storms">\n        Historic storms\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Extratropical_cyclone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Historic storms">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h2>\n      <div class="thumb tright">\n       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">\n        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Storm_Oratia_30_Oct_2000.jpg">\n         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="600" data-file-width="569" height="232" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/71/Storm_Oratia_30_Oct_2000.jpg/220px-Storm_Oratia_30_Oct_2000.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/71/Storm_Oratia_30_Oct_2000.jpg/330px-Storm_Oratia_30_Oct_2000.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/71/Storm_Oratia_30_Oct_2000.jpg/440px-Storm_Oratia_30_Oct_2000.jpg 2x" width="220"/>\n        </a>\n        <div class="thumbcaption">\n         <div class="magnify">\n          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Storm_Oratia_30_Oct_2000.jpg" title="Enlarge">\n          </a>\n         </div>\n         <a href="/wiki/Cyclone_Oratia" title="Cyclone Oratia">\n          Cyclone Oratia\n         </a>\n         showing typical comma shape of extratropical cyclone over Europe in October 2000.\n        </div>\n       </div>\n      </div>\n      <p>\n       A violent storm during the\n       <a href="/wiki/Crimean_War" title="Crimean War">\n        Crimean War\n       </a>\n       on November 14, 1854, wrecked 30 vessels, and sparked initial investigations into meteorology and forecasting in Europe. In the United States, the\n       <a href="/wiki/Columbus_Day_Storm_of_1962" title="Columbus Day Storm of 1962">\n        Columbus Day Storm of 1962\n       </a>\n       , one of many\n       <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Northwest_windstorm" title="Pacific Northwest windstorm">\n        Pacific Northwest windstorms\n       </a>\n       , led to\n       <a href="/wiki/Oregon" title="Oregon">\n        Oregon\n       </a>\n       \'s lowest measured pressure of 965.5\n       <a href="/wiki/Pascal_(unit)" title="Pascal (unit)">\n        hPa\n       </a>\n       (96.55\xc2\xa0kPa; 28.51\xc2\xa0inHg), violent winds, and US$170\xc2\xa0million in damage (1964\xc2\xa0dollars).\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55">\n        <a href="#cite_note-55">\n         [55]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       The "Wahine storm" was an extratropical cyclone that struck\n       <a href="/wiki/Wellington" title="Wellington">\n        Wellington\n       </a>\n       , New Zealand on April 10, 1968, so named after causing the inter-island ferry\n       <a href="/wiki/TEV_Wahine" title="TEV Wahine">\n        TEV\n        <i>\n         Wahine\n        </i>\n       </a>\n       to strike a reef and founder at the entrance to Wellington Harbour, resulting in 53 deaths. On November 10, 1975, an extratropical storm on\n       <a href="/wiki/Lake_Superior" title="Lake Superior">\n        Lake Superior\n       </a>\n       contributed to the sinking of the\n       <a href="/wiki/SS_Edmund_Fitzgerald" title="SS Edmund Fitzgerald">\n        SS Edmund Fitzgerald\n       </a>\n       near the\n       <a href="/wiki/Canada%E2%80%93United_States_border" title="Canada\xe2\x80\x93United States border">\n        Canada\xe2\x80\x93US border\n       </a>\n       , 15 NM northwest of the entrance to\n       <a href="/wiki/Whitefish_Bay" title="Whitefish Bay">\n        Whitefish Bay\n       </a>\n       .\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56">\n        <a href="#cite_note-56">\n         [56]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       A rapidly strengthening storm struck\n       <a href="/wiki/Vancouver_Island" title="Vancouver Island">\n        Vancouver Island\n       </a>\n       on October 11, 1984, and inspired the development of moored buoys off the western coast of\n       <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">\n        Canada\n       </a>\n       .\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57">\n        <a href="#cite_note-57">\n         [57]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       The\n       <a href="/wiki/Braer_Storm_of_January_1993" title="Braer Storm of January 1993">\n        Braer Storm of January 1993\n       </a>\n       was the strongest extratropical cyclone known to occur across the northern Atlantic Ocean, with a central pressure of 914 millibars (27.0\xc2\xa0inHg).\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Burt_58-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-Burt-58">\n         [58]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       In 2012,\n       <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Sandy" title="Hurricane Sandy">\n        Hurricane Sandy\n       </a>\n       transitioned to a post-tropical cyclone on the night of October 29; a few minutes later it made landfall on the\n       <a href="/wiki/New_Jersey" title="New Jersey">\n        New Jersey\n       </a>\n       coast as an extratropical storm with winds similar to a\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Category_1_hurricane" title="Category 1 hurricane">\n        Category 1\n       </a>\n       hurricane and a wind field of over 1,150 miles (1,850\xc2\xa0km).\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       In the\n       <a href="/wiki/Southern_Hemisphere" title="Southern Hemisphere">\n        Southern Hemisphere\n       </a>\n       , a violent extratropical storm hit\n       <a href="/wiki/Uruguay" title="Uruguay">\n        Uruguay\n       </a>\n       on August 23\xe2\x80\x9324, 2005, killing 10 people.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59">\n        <a href="#cite_note-59">\n         [59]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       The system\'s winds exceeded 100\xc2\xa0mph (160\xc2\xa0km/h) while\n       <a href="/wiki/Montevideo" title="Montevideo">\n        Montevideo\n       </a>\n       , the country\'s capital with 1.5 million inhabitants, was affected by tropical storm-force winds for over 12 hours and by hurricane-force winds for nearly four hours.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gary_Padget_60-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-Gary_Padget-60">\n         [60]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       Peak gusts were registered at\n       <a href="/wiki/Carrasco_International_Airport" title="Carrasco International Airport">\n        Carrasco International Airport\n       </a>\n       as 107\xc2\xa0mph (172\xc2\xa0km/h) and at the Harbour of Montevideo as 116\xc2\xa0mph (187\xc2\xa0km/h). The lowest reported pressure was 991.7\xc2\xa0hPa (99.17\xc2\xa0kPa; 29.28\xc2\xa0inHg). Extratropical cyclones are common in this part of the globe during fall, winter and spring months. The winds usually peak to 80\xe2\x80\x93110\xc2\xa0km/h (50\xe2\x80\x9368\xc2\xa0mph), and winds of 116\xc2\xa0mph (187\xc2\xa0km/h) are very uncommon.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gary_Padget_60-1">\n        <a href="#cite_note-Gary_Padget-60">\n         [60]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <h2>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">\n
