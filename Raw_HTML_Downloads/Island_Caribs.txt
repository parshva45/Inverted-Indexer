       <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="580" data-file-width="295" height="393" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/70/Dibujo_de_mujer_caribe.jpg/200px-Dibujo_de_mujer_caribe.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/7/70/Dibujo_de_mujer_caribe.jpg 1.5x" width="200"/>\n        </a>\n        <div class="thumbcaption">\n         <div class="magnify">\n          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Dibujo_de_mujer_caribe.jpg" title="Enlarge">\n          </a>\n         </div>\n         Drawing of a Carib woman (1888)\n        </div>\n       </div>\n      </div>\n      <p>\n       The\n       <b>\n        Island Caribs\n       </b>\n       , also known as the\n       <b>\n        Kalinago\n       </b>\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">\n        <a href="#cite_note-2">\n         [2]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       or simply\n       <b>\n        Caribs\n       </b>\n       , are an\n       <a href="/wiki/Indigenous_peoples_of_the_Americas" title="Indigenous peoples of the Americas">\n        indigenous people\n       </a>\n       of the\n       <a href="/wiki/Lesser_Antilles" title="Lesser Antilles">\n        Lesser Antilles\n       </a>\n       in the\n       <a href="/wiki/Caribbean" title="Caribbean">\n        Caribbean\n       </a>\n       . They may have descended from the\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mainland_Caribs" title="Mainland Caribs">\n        Mainland Caribs\n       </a>\n       (Kalina) of South America, but they spoke an unrelated language known as\n       <a href="/wiki/Island_Carib_language" title="Island Carib language">\n        Island Carib\n       </a>\n       .\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       At the time of\n       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_colonization_of_the_Americas" title="Spanish colonization of the Americas">\n        Spanish contact\n       </a>\n       , the Caribs were one of the dominant groups in the Caribbean, which owes its name to them. They lived throughout the\n       <a href="/wiki/Windward_Islands" title="Windward Islands">\n        Windward Islands\n       </a>\n       ,\n       <a href="/wiki/Dominica" title="Dominica">\n        Dominica\n       </a>\n       , and possibly the southern\n       <a href="/wiki/Leeward_Islands" title="Leeward Islands">\n        Leeward Islands\n       </a>\n       . Historically, it was thought their ancestors were mainland Caribs, known as the\n       <a href="/wiki/Igneri" title="Igneri">\n        Igneri\n       </a>\n       . The Igneri had conquered the islands from their previous inhabitants, However, linguistic and archaeological evidence disputes the notion of a mass emigration and conquest; the Island Carib language appears not to have been\n       <a href="/wiki/Cariban_languages" title="Cariban languages">\n        Cariban\n       </a>\n       , but\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arawakan" title="Arawakan">\n        Arawakan\n       </a>\n       like that of their neighbors, the\n       <a href="/wiki/Ta%C3%ADno" title="Ta\xc3\xadno">\n        Ta\xc3\xadno\n       </a>\n       . Irving Rouse and others suggest that a smaller group of mainland Caribs conquered the islands without displacing their inhabitants, eventually adopting the local language but retaining their traditions of a South American origin.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">\n        <a href="#cite_note-3">\n         [3]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       In the early colonial period, the Caribs had a reputation as warriors who raided neighboring islands. It was claimed that they practiced\n       <a href="/wiki/Cannibalism" title="Cannibalism">\n        cannibalism\n       </a>\n       \xe2\x80\x93 the word "cannibal" derives from a corruption of their name.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">\n        <a href="#cite_note-4">\n         [4]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       According to the Spanish conquistadores, the Carib Indians were cannibals who regularly ate roasted human flesh. Archaeological evidence indicates that they limited the consumption of humans to ceremonial occasions. There is evidence as to the taking of human trophies and the ritual cannibalism of war captives among both Carib and other Amerindian groups such as the Arawak and Tupinamba. Today, the Caribs and their descendants continue to live in the Antilles. The\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Garifuna_people" title="Garifuna people">\n        Garifuna\n       </a>\n       or "\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Black_Caribs" title="Black Caribs">\n        Black Caribs\n       </a>\n       ," a group of mixed Carib and African ancestry, also live principally in Central America.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n      </p>\n <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Island_Caribs&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">\n         edit\n Over the two centuries leading up to\n       <a href="/wiki/Christopher_Columbus" title="Christopher Columbus">\n        Christopher Columbus\n       </a>\n       \' arrival in the Caribbean archipelago in 1492, the Caribs mostly displaced the\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Maipurean_language" title="Maipurean language">\n        Maipurean\n       </a>\n       -speaking\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ta%C3%ADnos" title="Ta\xc3\xadnos">\n        Ta\xc3\xadnos\n       </a>\n       by warfare, extermination, and assimilation. The Ta\xc3\xadno had settled the island chains earlier in history, migrating from the mainland.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">\n        <a href="#cite_note-5">\n         [5]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <div class="thumb tleft">\n       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">\n        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Tobago_jade_ceremonial_ax.jpg">\n         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1728" data-file-width="2304" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cd/Tobago_jade_ceremonial_ax.jpg/220px-Tobago_jade_ceremonial_ax.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cd/Tobago_jade_ceremonial_ax.jpg/330px-Tobago_jade_ceremonial_ax.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cd/Tobago_jade_ceremonial_ax.jpg/440px-Tobago_jade_ceremonial_ax.jpg 2x" width="220"/>\n        </a>\n        <div class="thumbcaption">\n         <div class="magnify">\n          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Tobago_jade_ceremonial_ax.jpg" title="Enlarge">\n          </a>\n         </div>\n         <a href="/wiki/Greenstone_(archaeology)" title="Greenstone (archaeology)">\n          Greenstone\n         </a>\n         ceremonial axe. From\n         <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shell_midden" title="Shell midden">\n          shell midden\n         </a>\n         , Mt Irvine Bay,\n         <a href="/wiki/Tobago" title="Tobago">\n          Tobago\n         </a>\n         , 1957.\n        </div>\n       </div>\n      </div>\n      <p>\n       Caribs traded with the Eastern\n       <a href="/wiki/Ta%C3%ADno" title="Ta\xc3\xadno">\n        Ta\xc3\xadno\n       </a>\n       of the\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Caribbean_Islands" title="Caribbean Islands">\n        Caribbean Islands\n       </a>\n       .\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       The Caribs produced the\n       <a href="/wiki/Silver" title="Silver">\n        silver\n       </a>\n       products which\n       <a href="/wiki/Juan_Ponce_de_Le%C3%B3n" title="Juan Ponce de Le\xc3\xb3n">\n        Ponce de Leon\n       </a>\n       found in Ta\xc3\xadno communities. None of the insular Amerindians mined for gold but obtained it by trade from the mainland. The Caribs were skilled boat builders and sailors. They appeared to have owed their dominance in the Caribbean basin to their mastery of warfare.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       According to Floyd, "The question arose in\n       <a href="/wiki/Christopher_Columbus" title="Christopher Columbus">\n        Columbus\'s\n       </a>\n       time whether Indians could be enslaved and\n       <a href="/wiki/Isabella_I_of_Castile" title="Isabella I of Castile">\n        Queen Isabel\n       </a>\n       had ruled against it. At about the same time, however,\n       <a href="/wiki/Alonso_de_Ojeda" title="Alonso de Ojeda">\n        Ojeda\n       </a>\n       ,\n       <a href="/wiki/Rodrigo_de_Bastidas" title="Rodrigo de Bastidas">\n        Bastidas\n       </a>\n       , and other explorers voyaging along the\n       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_Main" title="Spanish Main">\n        Spanish Main\n       </a>\n       had been attacked by Indians with poisoned arrows - all such Indians were considered Caribs - which took a considerable toll of Spanish lives. These attacks and the evidence some of the perpetrators, at least, were cannibals, provided the rationale for the decree authorizing enslavement of Caribs." On 3 June 1511, king\n       <a href="/wiki/Ferdinand_II_of_Aragon" title="Ferdinand II of Aragon">\n        Ferdinand\n       </a>\n       declared war on the Caribs.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Floyd_6-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-Floyd-6">\n         [6]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       Island Caribs nevertheless mostly succeeded in keeping their islands unoccupied by Spaniards.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       In the 17th century, Island Caribs were displaced with a great loss of life by a new wave of European invaders: French and English. Most fatalities resulted from\n       <a href="/wiki/Eurasia" title="Eurasia">\n        Eurasian\n       </a>\n       infectious diseases such as\n       <a href="/wiki/Smallpox" title="Smallpox">\n        smallpox\n       </a>\n       , which they had no natural\n       <a href="/wiki/Immunity_(medical)" title="Immunity (medical)">\n        immunity\n       </a>\n       to, as well as warfare.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       In 1660, France and England signed with Island Caribs the\n       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Treaty_of_Saint_Charles&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Treaty of Saint Charles (page does not exist)">\n        Treaty of Saint Charles\n       </a>\n       that stipulated that Caribs would evacuate all the Lesser Antilles except for\n       <a href="/wiki/Dominica" title="Dominica">\n        Dominica\n       </a>\n       and\n       <a href="/wiki/Saint_Vincent_and_the_Grenadines" title="Saint Vincent and the Grenadines">\n        Saint Vincent\n       </a>\n       , which were recognized as reserves. However, the English would later ignore the treaty and ended up annexing both islands in 1763.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">\n        <a href="#cite_note-7">\n         [7]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       To this date, a small population of around 3,000 Caribs survives in the\n       <a href="/wiki/Carib_Territory" title="Carib Territory">\n        Carib Territory\n       </a>\n       in northeast Dominica.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       The \'Black Caribs\' (later known as\n       <i>\n        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Garifuna_people" title="Garifuna people">\n         carifuna\n        </a>\n       </i>\n       ) of\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saint_Vincent_(island)" title="Saint Vincent (island)">\n        St. Vincent\n       </a>\n       (\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saint_Vincent_(island)" title="Saint Vincent (island)">\n        St. Vincent\n       </a>\n       has some "Yellow Caribs" as well) were descended from a group of\n       <a href="/wiki/Slavery" title="Slavery">\n        enslaved\n       </a>\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Africans" title="Africans">\n        Africans\n       </a>\n       who were\n       <a href="/wiki/Maroon_(people)" title="Maroon (people)">\n        marooned\n       </a>\n       from shipwrecks of slave ships, as well as slaves who escaped here.\n       <a href="/wiki/Chief_Kairouane" title="Chief Kairouane">\n        Chief Kairouane\n       </a>\n       and his men from\n       <a href="/wiki/Grenada" title="Grenada">\n        Grenada\n       </a>\n       jumped off of the \xe2\x80\x9cLeapers Hill" rather than face slavery under the French invaders and have served as an iconic representation of the Caribs spirit of resistance.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">\n        <a href="#cite_note-8">\n         [8]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">\n        <a href="#cite_note-9">\n         [9]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">\n        <a href="#cite_note-10">\n         [10]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       They intermarried with the Carib and formed the last native culture to resist the British. It was not until 1795 that\n       <a href="/wiki/British_people" title="British people">\n        British\n       </a>\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Colonists" title="Colonists">\n        colonists\n       </a>\n       deported the Black Caribs to\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Roatan" title="Roatan">\n        Roatan\n       </a>\n       Island, off\n       <a href="/wiki/Honduras" title="Honduras">\n        Honduras\n       </a>\n       . Their descendants continue to live there today and are known as the\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Garifuna_people" title="Garifuna people">\n        Garifuna\n       </a>\n       ethnic group. Carib resistance delayed the settlement of Dominica by Europeans. The Black Carib communities that remained in St. Vincent and Dominica retained a degree of autonomy well into the 19th century.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       The last known speakers of Island Carib died in the 1930s, and the language is\n       <a href="/wiki/Extinct_language" title="Extinct language">\n        extinct\n       </a>\n       .\n      </p>\n      <h2>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="People">\n        People\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Island_Caribs&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: People">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h2>\n      <p>\n       The Kalinago of Dominica maintained their independence for many years by taking advantage of the island\'s rugged terrain. The island\'s east coast includes a 3,700-acre (15\xc2\xa0km\n       <sup>\n        2\n       </sup>\n       ) territory formerly known as the\n       <a href="/wiki/Carib_Territory" title="Carib Territory">\n        Carib Territory\n       </a>\n       that was granted to the people by the British\n       <a href="/wiki/The_Crown" title="The Crown">\n        Crown\n       </a>\n       in 1903. There are only 3,000 Caribs remaining. They elect their own chief. In July 2003, the Kalinago observed 100 Years of Territory. In July 2014, Charles Williams was elected Kalinago Chief,\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">\n        <a href="#cite_note-11">\n         [11]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       who succeeded Chief Garnette Joseph.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       Several hundred ethnic Carib descendants live in Puerto Rico, the\n       <a href="/wiki/United_States_Virgin_Islands" title="United States Virgin Islands">\n        U. S. Virgin Islands\n       </a>\n       ,\n       <a href="/wiki/Saint_Kitts_and_Nevis" title="Saint Kitts and Nevis">\n        St. Kitts &amp; Nevis\n       </a>\n       ,\n       <a href="/wiki/Antigua_and_Barbuda" title="Antigua and Barbuda">\n        Antigua &amp; Barbuda\n       </a>\n       ,\n       <a href="/wiki/Guadeloupe" title="Guadeloupe">\n        Guadeloupe\n       </a>\n       ,\n       <a href="/wiki/Martinique" title="Martinique">\n        Martinique\n       </a>\n       , Dominica, Saint Lucia, Grenada,\n       <a href="/wiki/Trinidad" title="Trinidad">\n        Trinidad\n       </a>\n       and St. Vincent. "\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Black_Caribs" title="Black Caribs">\n        Black Caribs\n       </a>\n       ," the descendants of the mixture of African slaves live in St. Vincent whose total population is unknown. Some ethnic Carib communities remain on the American mainland, in countries such as\n       <a href="/wiki/Venezuela" title="Venezuela">\n        Venezuela\n       </a>\n       ,\n       <a href="/wiki/Colombia" title="Colombia">\n        Colombia\n       </a>\n       ,\n       <a href="/wiki/Brazil" title="Brazil">\n        Brazil\n       </a>\n       ,\n       <a href="/wiki/French_Guiana" title="French Guiana">\n        French Guiana\n       </a>\n       ,\n       <a href="/wiki/Guyana" title="Guyana">\n        Guyana\n       </a>\n       and\n       <a href="/wiki/Suriname" title="Suriname">\n        Suriname\n       </a>\n       in South America, and\n       <a href="/wiki/Belize" title="Belize">\n        Belize\n       </a>\n       in Central America. The size of these communities varies widely.\n      </p>\n      <h2>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Appearance">\n        Appearance\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Island_Caribs&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Appearance">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h2>\n      <table class="plainlinks metadata ambox ambox-style ambox-mos" </i>\n          </small>\n         </div>\n        </td>\n       </tr>\n      </table>\n      <p>\n       The Island Caribs were of a medium height but in general were of a taller stature than the Arawaks. They were described as being stronger than the Arawaks reportedly due to the emphasis placed on training for warfare. Their skin was of a brown hue and they were usually naked and it was very rare for their loins to be covered. The women painted their skin with a red dye called\n       <i>\n        Roucou\n       </i>\n       , which they used to make fantastical decorations of many colours. The eyes of the females were usually circled with black. The men also painted their bodies and on occasion wore feathered headdresses, jewellery through their lips, and jewellery through their noses. They wore the\n       <i>\n        Caracoli\n       </i>\n       which was a necklace of small bones along with the teeth of defeated enemies from which a crescent shaped ornament was suspended. The\n       <i>\n        Caracoli\n       </i>\n       was worn to represent the courage of the wearer.\n      </p>\n      <h2>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Religion">\n        Religion\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Island_Caribs&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Religion">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h2>\n      <p>\n       The Caribs are believed to have been\n       <a href="/wiki/Polytheism" title="Polytheism">\n        polytheists\n       </a>\n       . As the Spanish began to colonise the Caribbean area, they wanted to convert the natives to\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Catholicism" title="Catholicism">\n        Catholicism\n       </a>\n       .\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">\n        <a href="#cite_note-12">\n         [12]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       The religion practised by the Caribs had elements similar to the ancestor worship of the\n       <a href="/wiki/Ta%C3%ADno" title="Ta\xc3\xadno">\n        Ta\xc3\xadno\n       </a>\n       . They believed in an evil spirit called\n       <i>\n        Maybouya,\n       </i>\n       who had to be placated for an individual to avoid harm. The chief function of their\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shamans" title="Shamans">\n        shamans\n       </a>\n       , called\n       <i>\n        buyeis\n       </i>\n       , was to heal the sick with herbs and to cast spells\n       <i>\n        (piai)\n       </i>\n       which would keep\n       <i>\n        Maybouya\n       </i>\n       at bay. The\n       <i>\n        buyeis\n       </i>\n       were very important and underwent special training instead of becoming warriors. As they were held to be the only people who could avert evil, they were treated with great respect. Their ceremonies were accompanied with sacrifices. As with the Arawaks and other\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Indigenous_people_of_the_Americas" title="Indigenous people of the Americas">\n        Native Americans\n       </a>\n       , the Caribs smoked\n       <a href="/wiki/Tobacco" title="Tobacco">\n        tobacco\n       </a>\n       in the rituals of their religion.\n      </p>\n      <h2>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Social_organization">\n        Social organization\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Island_Caribs&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Social organization">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h2>\n      <p>\n       Early Carib culture was\n       <a href="/wiki/Patriarchy" title="Patriarchy">\n        patriarchal\n       </a>\n       . Women carried out the domestic duties of rearing young children, processing and producing food and clothing, and cultivating the land for farming, including sowing and harvesting. Men carried out the tasks of hunting for food and protecting women and children from attack by other warring tribes. In the 17th century, Europeans reported that the women, with their children, lived in separate houses from men, a custom recorded among other South American tribes.\n       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">\n        [\n        <i>\n         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">\n          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2016)">\n           citation needed\n          </span>\n         </a>\n        </i>\n        ]\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       The women were highly revered and held substantial socio-political power.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">\n        <a href="#cite_note-13">\n         [13]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       Island Carib society was reputedly more socially\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Egalitarian" title="Egalitarian">\n        egalitarian\n       </a>\n       than Ta\xc3\xadno society. Although there were village chiefs and war leaders, there were no large states or multi-tiered aristocracy. The local\n       <a href="/wiki/Democracy" title="Democracy">\n        self-government\n       </a>\n       unit may have been the\n       <a href="/wiki/Longhouse" title="Longhouse">\n        longhouse\n       </a>\n       dwellings populated by men or women, typically run by one or more\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chieftain" title="Chieftain">\n        chieftains\n       </a>\n       reporting to an island\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tribal_council" title="Tribal council">\n        council\n       </a>\n       .\n      </p>\n      <h2>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Food">\n        Food\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Island_Caribs&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Food">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h2>\n      <p>\n       The Caribs ate almost the same foods as the Tainos. They did not farm very well so they relied less on maize and cassava but still knew how to grow them. Although they were not very good farmers, the Caribs were excellent fishermen and were not afraid of long voyages. Their diet consisted of lots of protein: they ate manatees, ducks, iguanas, and fish, among other foods. The Caribs had many myths and beliefs about diet - they believed that eating pig would give one small beady eyes, they also believed eating turtle meat made one stupid.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-aspiringminds_14-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-aspiringminds-14">\n         [14]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       In doing so, they deprived themselves of the protein that was easily available in the West Indies. They also believed that eating crab before a voyage would bring storms. The Caribs seasoned their food with pepper but did not use salt.\n       <i>\n        Couii\n       </i>\n       and\n       <i>\n        taumalin\n       </i>\n       were pepper sauces.\n       <i>\n        Taumalin\n       </i>\n       was made from pepper, lemon juice and the green meat of crab. The Caribs made\n       <i>\n        ouicou\n       </i>\n       , a cassava beer with a strong alcoholic content, and got very drunk on festivals and holidays. Although their diet was not quite so varied as that of the Arawaks, it was well balanced, prepared in many different ways and served with different seasonings.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-aspiringminds_14-1">\n        <a href="#cite_note-aspiringminds-14">\n         [14]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <h2>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Music">\n The most famous form is\n       <a href="/wiki/Punta" title="Punta">\n        punta\n       </a>\n       . Its associated musical style, which has the dancers move their hips in a circular motion. An evolved form of traditional music, still usually played using traditional instruments, punta has seen some modernization and electrification in the 1970s; this is called\n       <a href="/wiki/Punta_rock" title="Punta rock">\n        punta rock\n       </a>\n       . Traditional punta dancing is consciously competitive. Artists like\n       <a href="/wiki/Pen_Cayetano" title="Pen Cayetano">\n        Pen Cayetano\n       </a>\n       helped innovate modern punta rock by adding\n       <a href="/wiki/Guitar" title="Guitar">\n        guitars\n       </a>\n       to the traditional music, and paved the way for later artists like\n       <a href="/wiki/Andy_Palacio" title="Andy Palacio">\n        Andy Palacio\n       </a>\n       ,\n       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Children_of_the_Most_High&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Children of the Most High (page does not exist)">\n        Children of the Most High\n       </a>\n       and\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Black_Coral" title="Black Coral">\n        Black Coral\n       </a>\n       . Punta was popular across the region, especially in Belize, by the mid-1980s, culminating in the release of\n       <i>\n        <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Punta_Rockers&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Punta Rockers (page does not exist)">\n         Punta Rockers\n        </a>\n       </i>\n       in 1987, a compilation featuring many of the genre\'s biggest stars.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       Other forms of Garifuna music and dance include: hungu-hungu, combination, wanaragua, abaimahani, matamuerte, laremuna wadaguman, gunjai, sambai, charikanari, eremuna egi, paranda, berusu, punta rock, teremuna ligilisi, arumahani, and Mali-amalihani. Punta is the most popular dance in Garifuna culture. It is performed around holidays and at parties and other social events. Punta lyrics are usually composed by the women.\n       <a href="/wiki/Chumba" title="Chumba">\n        Chumba\n       </a>\n       and\n       <a href="/wiki/Hunguhungu" title="Hunguhungu">\n        hunguhungu\n       </a>\n       are a circular dance in a three-beat rhythm, which is often combined with punta. There are other songs typical to each gender, women having\n       <a href="/wiki/Eremwu_eu" title="Eremwu eu">\n        eremwu eu\n       </a>\n       and\n       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Abaimajani&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Abaimajani (page does not exist)">\n        abaimajani\n       </a>\n       , rhythmic\n       <a href="/wiki/A_cappella" title="A cappella">\n        a cappella\n       </a>\n       songs, and\n       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Laremuna_wadaguman&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Laremuna wadaguman (page does not exist)">\n        laremuna wadaguman\n       </a>\n       , men\'s\n       <a href="/wiki/Work_song" title="Work song">\n        work songs\n       </a>\n       ,\n       <a href="/wiki/Chumba" title="Chumba">\n        chumba\n       </a>\n       and\n       <a href="/wiki/Hunguhungu" title="Hunguhungu">\n        hunguhungu\n       </a>\n       , a circular dance in a three-beat rhythm, which is often combined with punta.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       Drums play a very important role in Garifuna music. There are primarily two types of drums used: the\n       <i>\n        primero\n       </i>\n       (tenor drum) and the\n       <i>\n        segunda\n       </i>\n       (bass drum). These drums are typically made of hollowed-out hardwood such as mahogany or mayflower, with the skins coming from the peccary (wild bush pig), deer, or sheep.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       Also used in combination with the drums are the\n       <i>\n        sisera\n       </i>\n       . These shakers are made from the dried fruit of the gourd tree, filled with seeds, then fitted with hardwood handles.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       Paranda music developed soon after the Garifunas arrival in Central America. The music is instrumental and\n       <a href="/wiki/Percussion_instrument" title="Percussion instrument">\n        percussion\n       </a>\n       -based. The music was barely recorded until the 1990s, when\n       <a href="/wiki/Ivan_Duran" title="Ivan Duran">\n        Ivan Duran\n       </a>\n       of\n       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Stonetree_Records&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Stonetree Records (page does not exist)">\n        Stonetree Records\n       </a>\n       began the\n       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Paranda_Project&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Paranda Project (page does not exist)">\n        Paranda Project\n       </a>\n       .\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       In contemporary Belize there has been a resurgence of Garifuna music, popularized by musicians such as Andy Palacio, Mohobub Flores, &amp; Adrian Martinez. These musicians have taken many aspects from traditional Garifuna music forms and fused them with more modern sounds. Described as a mixture of punta rock and paranda. One great example is Andy Palacio\'s album\n       <i>\n        Watina\n       </i>\n       , and\n       <i>\n        Umalali: The Garifuna Women\'s Project\n       </i>\n       , both released on the Belizean record label Stonetree Records.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       In the Garifuna culture, there is another dance called Dugu. This dance is a ritual done for a death in the family to pay their respect to their loved ones. In 2001, Garifuna music was proclaimed one of the\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Masterpieces_of_the_oral_and_intangible_heritage_of_humanity" title="Masterpieces of the oral and intangible heritage of humanity">\n        masterpieces of the oral and intangible heritage of humanity\n       </a>\n       by\n       <a href="/wiki/UNESCO" title="UNESCO">\n        UNESCO\n       </a>\n       .\n      </p>\n      <h2>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Cannibalism">\n        Cannibalism\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span It became the origin of the English "cannibal".\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">\n        <a href="#cite_note-15">\n         [15]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       Although, among the Caribs, it was apparently associated with rituals related to the eating of war enemies. There is evidence as to the taking of human trophies and the ritual cannibalism of war captives among both Arawak and other Amerindian groups such as the Carib and Tupinamba.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">\n        <a href="#cite_note-16">\n         [16]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       The Caribs had a tradition of keeping bones of their ancestors in their houses.\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Missionaries" title="Missionaries">\n        Missionaries\n       </a>\n       , such as P\xc3\xa8re\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jean_Baptiste_Labat" title="Jean Baptiste Labat">\n        Jean Baptiste Labat\n       </a>\n       and\n       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Cesar_de_Rochefort&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Cesar de Rochefort (page does not exist)">\n        Cesar de Rochefort\n       </a>\n       , described the practice as part of a belief that the ancestral spirits would always look after the bones and protect their descendants. The Caribs have been described as vicious and violent people in the history of the people who battled against other tribes.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       Italian explorer\n       <a href="/wiki/Giovanni_da_Verrazzano" title="Giovanni da Verrazzano">\n        Giovanni da Verrazzano\n       </a>\n       was killed and said to have been eaten by Carib natives on what is now\n       <a href="/wiki/Guadeloupe" title="Guadeloupe">\n        Guadeloupe\n       </a>\n       (French West Indies) in 1528 (before called\n       <i>\n        Karukera\n       </i>\n       by the Amerindian people which means \xe2\x80\x9cthe island of beautiful waters\xe2\x80\x9d), during his third voyage to North America, after exploring\n       <a href="/wiki/Florida" title="Florida">\n        Florida\n       </a>\n       ,\n       <a href="/wiki/The_Bahamas" title="The Bahamas">\n        the Bahamas\n       </a>\n       and the\n       <a href="/wiki/Lesser_Antilles" title="Lesser Antilles">\n        Lesser Antilles\n       </a>\n       . Historian William Riviere\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17">\n        <a href="#cite_note-17">\n         [17]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       has described most of the cannibalism as related to war rituals.\n      </p>\n      <h2>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Kalinago_canoe_project">\n        Kalinago canoe project\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Island_Caribs&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Kalinago canoe project">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h2>\n      <p>\n       In 1997\n       <a href="/wiki/Dominica" title="Dominica">\n        Dominica\n       </a>\n       artist Jacob Frederick and\n       <a href="/wiki/Tortola" title="Tortola">\n        Tortola\n       </a>\n       artist Aragorn Dick Read joined forces and set out to build a traditional canoe based on the fishing canoes still used in Dominica,\n       <a href="/wiki/Guadeloupe" title="Guadeloupe">\n        Guadeloupe\n       </a>\n       and\n       <a href="/wiki/Martinique" title="Martinique">\n        Martinique\n       </a>\n       . The project consisted of a return voyage by canoe to the Orinoco delta to meet up with the Kalinago tribes still living in those parts. On the way a cultural assessment was carried out and ties were reestablished with the remaining communities along the island chain. A documentary,\n       <i>\n        The Quest of the Carib Canoe,\n       </i>\n       was made by the BBC.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18">\n        <a href="#cite_note-18">\n         [18]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       The expedition sent shock waves through the Lesser Antilles as it made the local governments aware of the presence and the struggles for cultural survival of the Kalinago.\n      </p>\n      <h2>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">\n        See also\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span
