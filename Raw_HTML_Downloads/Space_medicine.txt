former\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nazi" title="Nazi">\n        Nazi\n       </a>\n       physician and physiologist, was brought to the\n       <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">\n        United States\n       </a>\n       after\n       <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">\n        World War II\n       </a>\n       as part of\n       <a href="/wiki/Operation_Paperclip" title="Operation Paperclip">\n        Operation Paperclip\n       </a>\n       .\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">\n        <a href="#cite_note-6">\n         [6]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       He first coined the term "space medicine" in 1948 and was the first and only Professor of Space Medicine at the\n       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=School_of_Aviation_Medicine&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="School of Aviation Medicine (page does not exist)">\n        School of Aviation Medicine\n       </a>\n       (SAM) at\n       <a href="/wiki/Randolph_Air_Force_Base" title="Randolph Air Force Base">\n        Randolph Air Force Base\n       </a>\n       ,\n       <a href="/wiki/Texas" title="Texas">\n        Texas\n       </a>\n       . In 1949 Strughold was made director of the\n       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Department_of_Space_Medicine&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Department of Space Medicine (page does not exist)">\n        Department of Space Medicine\n       </a>\n       at the SAM (which is now the\n       <a href="/wiki/United_States_Air_Force_School_of_Aerospace_Medicine" title="United States Air Force School of Aerospace Medicine">\n        US Air Force School of Aerospace Medicine\n       </a>\n       (USAFSAM) at Wright-Patterson Air Force Base, Ohio). He played an important role in developing the\n       <a href="/wiki/Pressure_suit" title="Pressure suit">\n        pressure suit\n       </a>\n       worn by early American astronauts. He was a co-founder of the Space Medicine Branch of the\n       <a href="/wiki/Aerospace_Medical_Association" title="Aerospace Medical Association">\n        Aerospace Medical Association\n       </a>\n       in 1950. The aeromedical library at Brooks AFB was named after him in 1977, but later renamed because documents from the\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nuremberg_Trials" title="Nuremberg Trials">\n        Nuremberg War Crimes Tribunal\n       </a>\n       linked Strughold to medical experiments in which inmates of the\n       <a href="/wiki/Dachau_concentration_camp" title="Dachau concentration camp">\n        Dachau concentration camp\n       </a>\n       were tortured and killed.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">\n        <a href="#cite_note-7">\n         [7]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <h2>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Benefits">\n        Benefits\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Space_medicine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Benefits">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h2>\n      <p>\n       Astronauts are not the only ones who benefit from space medicine research. Several medical products have been developed that are\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Space_spinoffs" title="Space spinoffs">\n        space spinoffs\n       </a>\n       , that is practical applications for the field of medicine arising out of the space program. Because of joint research efforts between NASA, the National Institutes on Aging (a part of the National Institutes of Health), and other aging-related organizations, space exploration has benefited a particular segment of society, seniors. Evidence of aging related medical research conducted in space was most publicly noticeable during STS-95 (See below).\n      </p>\n      <h3>\n       <span This idea also led to the development of the\n        <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Anti-Gravity_Treadmill&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Anti-Gravity Treadmill (page does not exist)">\n         Anti-Gravity Treadmill\n        </a>\n        from NASA technology.\n       </li>\n      </ul>\n      <p>\n       <b>\n        Feasibility of Long Duration Space Flights\n       </b>\n       In the interest of creating the possibility of longer duration space flight, NASA has invested in the research and application of preventative space medicine, not only for medically preventable pathologies, but trauma as well. Although trauma constitutes more of a life-threatening situation, medically preventable pathologies pose more of a threat to astronauts. "The involved crewmember is endangered because of mission stress and the lack of complete treatment capabilities on board the spacecraft, which could result in the manifestation of more severe symptoms than those usually associated with the same disease in the terrestrial environment. Also, the situation is potentially hazardous for the other crewmembers because the small, closed, ecological system of the spacecraft is conducive to disease transmission. Even if the disease is not transmitted, the safety of the other crewmembers may be jeopardized by the loss of the capabilities of the crewmember who is ill. Such an occurrence will be more serious and potentially hazardous as the durations of manned missions increase and as operational procedures become more complex. Not only do the health and safety of the crewmembers become critical, but the probability of mission success is lessened if the illness occurs during flight. Aborting a mission to return an ill crewmember before mission goals are completed is costly and potentially dangerous."\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">\n        <a href="#cite_note-8">\n         [8]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <h3>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Medical_investigations_in_space_during_the_Space_Shuttle_era">\n        Medical investigations in space during the Space Shuttle era\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Space_medicine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Medical investigations in space during the Space Shuttle era">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h3>\n      <p>\n       With the additional lifting capability presented by the Space Shuttle program, NASA designers were able to create a more comprehensive medical readiness kit. The SOMS consists of two separate packages: the Medications and Bandage Kit (MBK) and the Emergency Medical Kit (EMK). While the MBK contained capsulate medications (tablets, capsules, and suppositories), bandage materials, and topical medication, the EMK had medications to be administered by injection, items for performing minor surgeries, diagnostic/therapeutic items, and a microbiological test kit.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">\n        <a href="#cite_note-9">\n         [9]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       <a href="/wiki/John_Glenn" title="John Glenn">\n        John Glenn\n       </a>\n       , the first American astronaut to orbit the Earth, returned with much fanfare to space once again on\n       <a href="/wiki/STS-95" title="STS-95">\n        STS-95\n       </a>\n       at 77 years of age to confront the physiological challenges preventing long-term space travel for astronauts\xe2\x80\x94loss of bone density, loss of muscle mass, balance disorders, sleep disturbances, cardiovascular changes, and immune system depression\xe2\x80\x94all of which are problems confronting aging people as well as astronauts. Once again Glenn stepped forward to play a historic role in the future of space exploration, but this time he would provide new medical research in the field of gerontology as well.\n      </p>\n class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Space_medicine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Cardiac rhythms">\n         edit\n astronauts.\n       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">\n        [\n        <i>\n         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">\n          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2015)">\n           citation needed\n          </span>\n         </a>\n        </i>\n        ]\n       </sup>\n       Most of these have been related to\n       <a href="/wiki/Cardiovascular_disease" title="Cardiovascular disease">\n        cardiovascular disease\n       </a>\n       , but it is not clear whether this was due to\n       <a href="/wiki/Pre-existing_condition" title="Pre-existing condition">\n        pre-existing conditions\n       </a>\n       or\n       <a href="/wiki/Effect_of_spaceflight_on_the_human_body" title="Effect of spaceflight on the human body">\n        effects of space flight\n       </a>\n       . Other heart rhythm problems, such as\n       <a href="/wiki/Atrial_fibrillation" title="Atrial fibrillation">\n        atrial fibrillation\n       </a>\n       , can develop over time, necessitating periodic screening of crewmembers\xe2\x80\x99 heart rhythms. Beyond these terrestrial heart risks, some concern exists that prolonged exposure to\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Microgravity" title="Microgravity">\n        microgravity\n       </a>\n       may lead to heart rhythm disturbances. Although this has not been observed to date, further surveillance is warranted.\n      </p>\n      <h4>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Decompression_illness_in_spaceflight">\n        Decompression illness in spaceflight\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Space_medicine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Decompression illness in spaceflight">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h4>\n      <p>\n       In space, astronauts use a\n       <a href="/wiki/Space_suit" title="Space suit">\n        space suit\n       </a>\n       , essentially a self-contained individual spacecraft, to do spacewalks, or\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Extra-vehicular_activity" title="Extra-vehicular activity">\n        extra-vehicular activities\n       </a>\n       (EVAs). Spacesuits are generally inflated with 100%\n       <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">\n        oxygen\n       </a>\n       at a total pressure that is less than a third of normal\n       <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_pressure" title="Atmospheric pressure">\n        atmospheric pressure\n       </a>\n       . Eliminating inert atmospheric components such as\n       <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen" title="Nitrogen">\n        nitrogen\n       </a>\n       allows the astronaut to breathe comfortably, but also have the mobility to use their hands, arms, and legs to complete required work, which would be more difficult in a higher pressure suit.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       After the astronaut dons the spacesuit, air is replaced by 100% oxygen in a process called a "nitrogen purge". In order to reduce the risk of\n       <a href="/wiki/Decompression_sickness" title="Decompression sickness">\n        decompression sickness\n       </a>\n       , the astronaut must spend several hours "pre-breathing" at an intermediate nitrogen\n       <a href="/wiki/Partial_pressure" title="Partial pressure">\n        partial pressure\n       </a>\n       , in order to let their body tissues\n       <a href="/wiki/Outgassing" title="Outgassing">\n        outgas\n       </a>\n       nitrogen slowly enough that bubbles are not formed. When the astronaut returns to the "shirt sleeve" environment of the spacecraft after an EVA, pressure is restored to whatever the operating pressure of that spacecraft may be, generally normal atmospheric pressure.\n       <a href="/wiki/Decompression_illness" title="Decompression illness">\n        Decompression illness\n       </a>\n       in spaceflight consists of decompression sickness (DCS) and other injuries due to uncompensated changes in pressure, or\n       <a href="/wiki/Barotrauma" title="Barotrauma">\n        barotrauma\n       </a>\n       .\n      </p>\n      <h4>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Decompression_sickness">\n        Decompression sickness\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Space_medicine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Decompression sickness">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h4>\n      <p>\n       <a href="/wiki/Decompression_sickness" title="Decompression sickness">\n        Decompression sickness\n       </a>\n       is the injury to the tissues of the body resulting from the presence of nitrogen bubbles in the tissues and blood. This occurs due to a rapid reduction in ambient pressure causing the dissolved nitrogen to come out of solution as gas bubbles. In space the risk of DCS is significantly reduced by using a technique to wash out the nitrogen in the body\xe2\x80\x99s tissues. This is achieved by breathing 100% oxygen for a specified period of time before donning the spacesuit, and is continued after a nitrogen purge. DCS may result from inadequate or interrupted pre-oxygenation time, or other factors including the astronaut\xe2\x80\x99s level of hydration, physical conditioning, prior injuries and age. Other risks of DCS include inadequate nitrogen purge in the EMU, a strenuous or excessively prolonged EVA, or a loss of suit pressure. Non-EVA crewmembers may also be at risk for DCS if there is a loss of spacecraft cabin pressure.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       Symptoms of DCS in space may include chest pain, shortness of breath, cough or pain with a deep breath, unusual fatigue, lightheadedness, dizziness, headache, unexplained musculoskeletal pain, tingling or numbness, extremities weakness, or visual abnormalities.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       Primary treatment principles consist of in-suit repressurization to re-dissolve nitrogen bubbles, 100% oxygen to re-oxygenate tissues, and hydration to improve the circulation to injured tissues.\n      </p>\n      <h4>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Barotrauma">\n        Barotrauma\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Space_medicine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Barotrauma">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h4>\n      <p>\n       <a href="/wiki/Barotrauma" title="Barotrauma">\n        Barotrauma\n       </a>\n       is the injury to the tissues of air filled spaces in the body as a result of differences in pressure between the body spaces and the ambient atmospheric pressure. Air filled spaces include the middle ears, paranasal sinuses, lungs and gastrointestinal tract. One would be predisposed by a pre-existing upper respiratory infection, nasal allergies, recurrent changing pressures, dehydration, or a poor equalizing technique.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       Positive pressure in the air filled spaces results from reduced barometric pressure during the depressurization phase of an EVA. It can cause abdominal distension, ear or sinus pain, decreased hearing, and dental or jaw pain. Abdominal distension can be treated with extending the abdomen, gentle massage and encourage passing\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Flatus" title="Flatus">\n        flatus\n       </a>\n       . Ear and sinus pressure can be relieved with passive release of positive pressure. Pretreatment for susceptible individuals can include oral and nasal\n       <a href="/wiki/Decongestant" title="Decongestant">\n        decongestants\n       </a>\n       , or oral and nasal\n       <a href="/wiki/Steroid" title="Steroid">\n        steroids\n       </a>\n       .\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       Negative pressure in air fill spaces results from increased\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Barometric_pressure" title="Barometric pressure">\n        barometric pressure\n       </a>\n       during repressurization after an EVA or following a planned restoration of a reduced cabin pressure. Common symptoms include ear or sinus pain, decreased hearing, and tooth or jaw pain.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       Treatment may include active positive pressure equalization of ears and sinuses, oral and nasal decongestants, or oral and nasal steroids, and appropriate pain medication if needed.\n      </p>\n      <h3>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Ultrasound_and_space">\n        Ultrasound and space\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Space_medicine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Ultrasound and space">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h3>\n      <p>\n       <a href="/wiki/Ultrasound" title="Ultrasound">\n        Ultrasound\n       </a>\n       is the main diagnostic imaging tool on ISS and for the foreseeable future missions. X-rays and\n       <a href="/wiki/CT_scan" title="CT scan">\n        CT scans\n       </a>\n       involve radiation which is unacceptable in the space environment. Though\n       <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_resonance_imaging" title="Magnetic resonance imaging">\n        MRI\n       </a>\n       uses magnetics to create images, it is too large at present to consider as a viable option. Ultrasound, which uses sound waves to create images and comes in laptop size packages, provides imaging of a wide variety of tissues and organs. It is currently being used to look at the eyeball and the optic nerve to help determine the cause(s) of changes that NASA has noted mostly in long duration astronauts. NASA is also pushing the limits of ultrasound use regarding musculoskeletal problems as these are some of the most common and most likely problems to occur. Significant challenges to using ultrasounds on space missions is training the astronaut to use the equipment (ultrasound technicians spend years in training and developing the skills necessary to be "good" at their job) as well as interpreting the images that are captured. Much of ultrasound interpretation is done real-time but it is impractical to train astronauts to actually read/interpret ultrasounds. Thus, the data is currently being sent back to\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mission_control" title="Mission control">\n        mission control\n       </a>\n       and forwarded to medical personnel to read and interpret. Future exploration class missions will need to be autonomous due to transmission times taking too long for urgent/emergent medical conditions. Because it has less blood to pump, the heart will\n       <a href="/wiki/Atrophy" title="Atrophy">\n        atrophy\n       </a>\n       . A weakened heart results in low blood pressure and can produce a problem with \xe2\x80\x9corthostatic tolerance,\xe2\x80\x9d or the body\xe2\x80\x99s ability to send enough oxygen to the brain without fainting or becoming dizzy. "Under the effects of the earth\'s\n       <a href="/wiki/Gravity" title="Gravity">\n        gravity\n       </a>\n       , blood and other body fluids are pulled towards the lower body. When gravity is taken away or reduced during space exploration, the blood tends to collect in the upper body instead, resulting in facial\n       <a href="/wiki/Edema" title="Edema">\n        edema\n       </a>\n       and other unwelcome side effects. Upon return to earth, the blood begins to pool in the lower extremities again, resulting in\n       <a href="/wiki/Orthostatic_hypotension" title="Orthostatic hypotension">\n        orthostatic hypotension\n       </a>\n       ."\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34">\n        <a href="#cite_note-34">\n         [34]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <h3>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Radiation_effects">\n        Radiation effects\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Space_medicine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Radiation effects">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h3>\n      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">\n       See also:\n       <a href="/wiki/Spaceflight_radiation_carcinogenesis" title="Spaceflight radiation carcinogenesis">\n        Spaceflight radiation carcinogenesis\n       </a>\n      </div>\n      <p>\n       Soviet\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cosmonaut" title="Cosmonaut">\n        cosmonaut\n       </a>\n       <a href="/wiki/Valentin_Lebedev" title="Valentin Lebedev">\n        Valentin Lebedev\n       </a>\n       , who spent 211 days in orbit during 1982 (an absolute record for stay in Earth\xe2\x80\x99s orbit), lost his eyesight to progressive\n       <a href="/wiki/Cataract" title="Cataract">\n        cataract\n       </a>\n       . Lebedev stated: \xe2\x80\x9cI suffered from a lot of radiation in space. It was all concealed back then, during the Soviet years, but now I can say that I caused damage to my health because of that flight.\xe2\x80\x9d\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wired-20120723_3-4">\n        <a href="#cite_note-Wired-20120723-3">\n         [3]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35">\n        <a href="#cite_note-35">\n         [35]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       On 31 May 2013, NASA scientists reported that a possible\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Manned_mission_to_Mars" title="Manned mission to Mars">\n        manned mission to Mars\n       </a>\n       may involve a great\n       <a href="/wiki/Radiation" title="Radiation">\n        radiation risk\n       </a>\n       based on the amount of\n       <a href="/wiki/Radiation" title="Radiation">\n        energetic particle radiation\n       </a>\n       detected by the\n       <a href="/wiki/Radiation_assessment_detector" title="Radiation assessment detector">\n        RAD\n       </a>\n       on the\n       <a href="/wiki/Mars_Science_Laboratory" title="Mars Science Laboratory">\n        Mars Science Laboratory\n       </a>\n       while traveling from the\n       <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">\n        Earth\n       </a>\n       to\n       <a href="/wiki/Mars" title="Mars">\n        Mars\n       </a>\n       in 2011-2012.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WIRED-20140212_24-1">\n        <a href="#cite_note-WIRED-20140212-24">\n         [24]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SCI-20130531a_30-1">\n        <a href="#cite_note-SCI-20130531a-30">\n         [30]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SCI-20130531b_31-1">\n        <a href="#cite_note-SCI-20130531b-31">\n         [31]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       <sup class="reference" href="#cite_note-SN-20130629-33">\n         [33]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <h3>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Sleep_disorders">\n        Sleep disorders\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Space_medicine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Sleep disorders">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h3>\n      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">\n       Further information:\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Performance_Errors_due_to_Fatigue_and_Sleep_Loss" title="Performance Errors due to Fatigue and Sleep Loss">\n        Performance Errors due to Fatigue and Sleep Loss\n       </a>\n      </div>\n      <p>\n       Fifty percent of space shuttle astronauts take sleeping pills and still get two hours or less of sleep. NASA is researching two areas which may provide the keys to a better night\xe2\x80\x99s sleep, as improved sleep decreases fatigue and increases daytime productivity. A variety of methods for combating this phenomenon are constantly under discussion. A partial list of remedies would include:\n      </p>\n      <ul>\n       <li>\n        Go to sleep at the same time each night. With practice, you will (almost) always be tired and ready for sleep.\n       </li>\n       <li>\n        <a href="/wiki/Melatonin" title="Melatonin">\n         Melatonin\n        </a>\n        , once thought to be an anti-aging wonder drug (this was due to the well-documented observation that as people age they gradually produce less and less of the hormone naturally). The amount of melatonin the body produces decreases linearly over a lifetime. Although the melatonin anti-aging fad was thoroughly debunked following a large number of randomized trials, it was soon in the spotlight once more due to the observation that a healthy person\'s normal melatonin levels varies widely throughout each day: usually, levels rise in the evening and fall in the morning. Ever since the discovery that melatonin levels are highest at bedtime, melatonin has been purported by some to be an effective sleep-aid - it is especially popular for jet-lag. Melatonin\'s efficacy in treating insomnia is hotly debated and therefore in the US it is sold as a dietary supplement. "These statements have not been evaluated by the FDA" is printed on the packaging even though melatonin has been studied very extensively.\n       </li>\n       <li>\n        <a href="/wiki/Ramelteon" title="Ramelteon">\n         Ramelteon\n        </a>\n        , a melatonin receptor\n        <a href="/wiki/Agonist" title="Agonist">\n         agonist\n        </a>\n        , is a relatively new drug designed by using the melatonin molecule and the shapes of melatonin receptors as starting points. Ramelteon binds to the same M1 and M2 receptors in the\n        <a href="/wiki/Suprachiasmatic_nucleus" title="Suprachiasmatic nucleus">\n         suprachiasmatic nucleus\n        </a>\n        (the "biological clock" in the brain) as melatonin (M1 and M2 get their names from melatonin). It also may derive some of its properties from its three-times greater elimination half-life. Ramelteon is not without detractors who claim that it is no more effective than melatonin, and melatonin is less expensive by orders of magnitude. It is unclear whether Ramelteon causes its receptors to behave differently than they do when bound to melatonin, and Ramelteon may have a significantly greater\n        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Affinity_(pharmacology)" title="Affinity (pharmacology)">\n         affinity\n        </a>\n        for these receptors. Better information on Ramelteon\'s effectiveness should be available soon, and despite questions of its efficacy, the general lack of side effects makes Ramelteon one of the very few sleep medications that could potentially be safely used by astronauts.\n       </li>\n       <li>\n        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Barbiturates" title="Barbiturates">\n         Barbiturates\n        </a>\n        and\n        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Benzodiazepines" title="Benzodiazepines">\n         Benzodiazepines\n        </a>\n        are both very strong sedatives. While they certainly would work (at least short term) in helping astronauts sleep, they have side effects that could affect the astronaut\'s ability to perform his/her job, especially in the "morning." This side effect renders barbiturates and benzodiazepines likely unfit as treatments for space insomnia. Narcotics and most tranquilizers also fall into this category.\n       </li>\n       <li>\n        <a href="/wiki/Zolpidem" title="Zolpidem">\n         Zolpidem\n        </a>\n        and\n        <a href="/wiki/Zopiclone" title="Zopiclone">\n         Zopiclone\n        </a>\n        are sedative-hypnotics, better known by their trade names "Ambien" and "Lunesta". These are extremely popular sleep-aids, due in large part to their effectiveness and significantly reduced side-effect profiles vis-a-vis benzodiazepines and barbiturates. Although other drugs may be more effective in inducing sleep Zolpidem and Zopiclone essentially lack the sorts of side effects that disqualify other insomnia drugs for astronauts, for whom being able to wake up easily and quickly can be of paramount importance; astronauts who are not thinking clearly, are groggy, and are disoriented when a sudden emergency wakes them could end up trading their grogginess for the indifference of death in seconds. Zolpidem, Zopiclone, and the like - in most people - are significantly less likely to cause drug-related daytime sleepiness, nor excessive drowsiness if woken abruptly.\n       </li>\n       <li>\n        Practice good\n        <a href="/wiki/Sleep_hygiene" title="Sleep hygiene">\n         sleep hygiene\n        </a>\n        . In other words, the bed is for sleeping only; get out of bed within a few moments of waking up.\n        <i>\n         Do not\n        </i>\n        sit in bed watching TV or using a laptop. When one is acclimated to spending many hours\n        <i>\n         awake\n        </i>\n        in bed, it can disrupt the body\'s natural set of daily cycles, called the\n        <a href="/wiki/Circadian_rhythm" title="Circadian rhythm">\n         circadian rhythm\n        </a>\n        . While this is less of an issue for astronauts who have very limited entertainment options in their sleeping areas, another aspect of sleep hygiene is adhering to a specific pre-sleep routine (shower, brush teeth, fold up clothing, spend 20 minutes with a trashy novel, for example); observing this sort of routine regularly can significantly improve one\'s sleep quality. Of course, sleep hygiene studies have all been conducted at 1G, but it seems possible (if not likely) that observing sleep hygiene would retain at least some efficacy in micro-gravity.\n       </li>\n       <li>\n        <a href="/wiki/Modafinil" title="Modafinil">\n         Modafinil\n        </a>\n        is a drug that is prescribed for narcolepsy and other disorders that involve excessive daytime exhaustion. It has been approved in various military situations and for astronauts thanks to its ability to stave off fatigue. It is unclear whether astronauts sometimes use the drug because they are sleep-deprived - it might only be used on spacewalks and in other high-risk situations.\n       </li>\n       <li>\n        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dexedrine" title="Dexedrine">\n         Dexedrine\n        </a>\n        is an amphetamine which used to be the gold-standard for fighter pilots flying long and multiple sorties in a row, and therefore may have at some point been available if astronauts were in need of a strong stimulant. Today, Modafinil has largely - if not entirely - replaced Dexedrine; reaction time and reasoning among pilots who are sleep-deprived and on Dexedrine suffer, and get worse the longer the pilot stays awake. In one study, helicopter pilots that were given six-hundred milligrams of Modafinil every three hours were able to perform with the same accuracy as they did at pre-deprivation levels for 40 hours without sleep; significant impairment would have been evident had the pilots been given Dexedrine instead.\n        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36">\n         <a href="#cite_note-36">\n          [36]\n         </a>\n        </sup>\n       </li>\n      </ul>\n      <h2>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Spaceflight_analogues">\n        Spaceflight analogues\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Space_medicine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Spaceflight analogues">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h2>\n      <p>\n       Biomedical research in space is expensive and logistically and technically complicated, and thus limited. Conducting medical research in space alone will not provide humans with the depth of knowledge needed to ensure the safety of inter-planetary travellers. Complimentary to research in space is the use of spaceflight analogues. Analogues are particularly useful for the study of immunity, sleep, psychological factors, human performance, habitability, and telemedicine. Examples of spaceflight analogues include confinement chambers (\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mars-500" title="Mars-500">\n        Mars-500\n       </a>\n       ), sub-aqua habitats (\n       <a href="/wiki/NEEMO" title="NEEMO">\n        NEEMO\n       </a>\n       ), and Antarctic (\n       <a href="/wiki/Concordia_Station" title="Concordia Station">\n        Concordia Station\n       </a>\n       ) and Arctic (\n       <a href="/wiki/Haughton%E2%80%93Mars_Project" title="Haughton\xe2\x80\x93Mars Project">\n        Haughton\xe2\x80\x93Mars Project\n       </a>\n       ) stations.\n
