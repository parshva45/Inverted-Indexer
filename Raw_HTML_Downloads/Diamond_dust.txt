</div>\n      <p>\n       Diamond dust is similar to\n       <a href="/wiki/Fog" title="Fog">\n        fog\n       </a>\n       in that it is a cloud based at the surface; however, it differs from fog in two main ways. Generally fog refers to a cloud composed of liquid water (the term\n       <a href="/wiki/Ice_fog" title="Ice fog">\n        ice fog\n       </a>\n       usually refers to a fog that formed as liquid water and then froze, and frequently seems to occur in valleys with airborne pollution such as\n       <a href="/wiki/Fairbanks,_Alaska" title="Fairbanks, Alaska">\n        Fairbanks\n       </a>\n       ,\n       <a href="/wiki/Alaska" title="Alaska">\n        Alaska\n       </a>\n       , while diamond dust forms directly as ice). Also, fog is a dense enough cloud to significantly reduce visibility, while diamond dust is usually very thin and may not have any effect on visibility (there are far fewer crystals in a volume of air than there are droplets in the same volume with fog). However, diamond dust can often reduce the visibility, in some cases to under 600\xc2\xa0m (2,000\xc2\xa0ft).\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       The depth of the diamond dust layer can vary substantially from as little as 20 to 30\xc2\xa0m (66 to 98\xc2\xa0ft) to 300 metres (980\xc2\xa0ft). Because diamond dust does not always reduce visibility it is often first noticed by the brief flashes caused when the tiny crystals, tumbling through the air, reflect sunlight to your eye. This glittering effect gives the phenomenon its name since it looks like many tiny diamonds are flashing in the air.\n      </p>\n      <ul class="gallery mw-gallery-traditional" style="max-width: 446px;_width: 446px;">\n       <li class="gallerycaption">\n        serial photos of Diamond Dust\n       </li>\n       <li class="gallerybox" style="width: 215px">\n        <div style="width: 215px">\n         <div class="thumb" style="width: 210px;">\n          <div style="margin:15px auto;">\n           <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:050207diamond_dust1_Tsukuba_Japan.png">\n            <img alt="" data-file-height="476" data-file-width="308" height="240" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8c/050207diamond_dust1_Tsukuba_Japan.png/155px-050207diamond_dust1_Tsukuba_Japan.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8c/050207diamond_dust1_Tsukuba_Japan.png/233px-050207diamond_dust1_Tsukuba_Japan.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/8/8c/050207diamond_dust1_Tsukuba_Japan.png 2x" width="155"/>\n           </a>\n          </div>\n         </div>\n         <div class="gallerytext">\n          <p>\n           Diamond dust briefly observed at Tsukuba, Japan. </span>\n       </span>\n      </h2>\n      <div class="thumb tright">\n       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">\n        <a class="image" class="thumbcaption">\n         <div class="magnify">\n          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Sun_halo_optical_phenomenon_edit.jpg" title="Enlarge">\n          </a>\n         </div>\n Diamond dust is visible as point-like reflections of individual crystals close to the camera.\n        </div>\n       </div>\n      </div>\n      <div class="thumb tright">\n       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">\n        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Ischgl_Halo.jpg">\n         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1536" data-file-width="2048" width="220"/>\n        </a>\n        <div class="thumbcaption">\n         <div class="magnify">\n          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Ischgl_Halo.jpg" title="Enlarge">\n          </a>\n         </div>\n         Subhorizon halos in\n         <a href="/wiki/Ischgl" title="Ischgl">\n          Ischgl\n         </a>\n         :\n         <a href="/wiki/Subsun" title="Subsun">\n          subsun\n         </a>\n         , subparhelion and partial 22\xc2\xb0 halo, with the glint of ice crystals around the subsun.\n        </div>\n       </div>\n      </div>\n      <p>\n       These ice crystals usually form when a\n       <a href="/wiki/Inversion_(meteorology)" title="Inversion (meteorology)">\n        temperature inversion\n       </a>\n       is present at the surface and the warmer air above the ground mixes with the colder air near the surface.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gloss_1-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-gloss-1">\n         [1]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       Since warmer air frequently contains more\n       <a href="/wiki/Water_vapor" title="Water vapor">\n        water vapor\n       </a>\n       than colder air, this mixing will usually also transport water vapor into the air near the surface, causing the\n       <a href="/wiki/Relative_humidity" title="Relative humidity">\n        relative humidity\n       </a>\n       of the near-surface air to increase. If the relative humidity increase near the surface is large enough then ice crystals may form.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       To form diamond dust the temperature must be below the\n       <a href="/wiki/Melting_point" title="Melting point">\n        freezing point\n       </a>\n       of water, 0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xb0C (32\xc2\xa0\xc2\xb0F), or the ice cannot form or would melt. However, diamond dust is not often observed at temperatures near 0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xb0C (32\xc2\xa0\xc2\xb0F). At temperatures between 0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xb0C (32\xc2\xa0\xc2\xb0F) and about \xe2\x88\x9239\xc2\xa0\xc2\xb0C (\xe2\x88\x9238\xc2\xa0\xc2\xb0F) increasing the relative humidity can cause either fog or diamond dust. This is because very small droplets of water can remain liquid well below the freezing point, a state known as\n       <a href="/wiki/Supercooling" title="Supercooling">\n        supercooled\n       </a>\n       water. In areas with a lot of small particles in the air, from human pollution or natural sources like dust, the water droplets are likely to be able to freeze at a temperature around \xe2\x88\x9210\xc2\xa0\xc2\xb0C (14\xc2\xa0\xc2\xb0F), but in very clean areas, where there are no particles (\n       <a href="/wiki/Ice_nucleus" title="Ice nucleus">\n        ice nuclei\n       </a>\n       ) to help the droplets freeze, they can remain liquid to \xe2\x88\x9239\xc2\xa0\xc2\xb0C (\xe2\x88\x9238\xc2\xa0\xc2\xb0F), at which point even very tiny, pure water droplets will freeze. In the interior of Antarctica diamond dust is fairly common at temperatures below about \xe2\x88\x9225\xc2\xa0\xc2\xb0C (\xe2\x88\x9213\xc2\xa0\xc2\xb0F).\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       Artificial diamond dust can form from snow machines which blow ice crystals into the air. These are found at ski resorts.\n      </p>\n      <h2>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Optical_properties">\n        Optical properties\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Diamond_dust&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Optical properties">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h2>\n      <p>\n       Diamond dust is often associated with\n       <a href="/wiki/Halo_(optical_phenomenon)" title="Halo (optical phenomenon)">\n        halos\n       </a>\n       , such as\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sun_dogs" title="Sun dogs">\n        sun dogs\n       </a>\n       ,\n       <a href="/wiki/Light_pillar" title="Light pillar">\n        light pillars\n       </a>\n       , etc. Like the ice crystals in\n       <a href="/wiki/Cirrus_cloud" title="Cirrus cloud">\n        cirrus\n       </a>\n       or\n       <a href="/wiki/Cirrostratus_cloud" title="Cirrostratus cloud">\n        cirrostratus clouds\n       </a>\n       , diamond dust crystals form directly as simple hexagonal ice crystals \xe2\x80\x94 as opposed to freezing drops \xe2\x80\x94\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">\n        <a href="#cite_note-2">\n         [2]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       and generally form slowly. This combination results in crystals with well defined shapes - usually either hexagonal plates or columns - which, like a prism, can\n       <a href="/wiki/Reflection_(physics)" title="Reflection (physics)">\n        reflect\n       </a>\n       and/or\n       <a href="/wiki/Refraction" title="Refraction">\n        refract\n       </a>\n       light in specific directions.\n      </p>\n      <h2>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Climatology">\n        Climatology\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Diamond_dust&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Climatology">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h2>\n      <p>\n       While diamond dust can be seen in any area of the world that has cold winters, it is most frequent in the interior of Antarctica, where it is common year-round. Schwerdtfeger (1970) shows that diamond dust was observed on average 316 days a year at\n       <a href="/wiki/Plateau_Station" title="Plateau Station">\n        Plateau Station\n       </a>\n       in Antarctica, and Radok and Lile (1977) estimate that over 70% of the precipitation that fell at Plateau Station in 1967 fell in the form of diamond dust. Once melted, the total precipitation for the year was only 25\xc2\xa0mm (0.98\xc2\xa0in).\n      </p>\n      <h2>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Weather_reporting_and_interference">\n        Weather reporting and interference\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Diamond_dust&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Weather reporting and interference">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h2>\n      <p>\n       Diamond dust may sometimes cause a problem for\n       <a href="/wiki/Automated_airport_weather_station" title="Automated airport weather station">\n        automated airport weather stations\n       </a>\n       . The\n       <a href="/wiki/Ceilometer" title="Ceilometer">\n        ceilometer\n       </a>\n       and visibility sensor do not always correctly interpret the falling diamond dust and report the visibility and ceiling as zero (overcast skies). However, a human observer would correctly notice clear skies and unrestricted visibility. The METAR identifier for diamond dust within international hourly weather reports is IC.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-METAR_3-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-METAR-3">\n         [3]\n
