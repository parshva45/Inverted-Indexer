</table>\n      <p>\n       <i>\n        <b>\n         Nature\n        </b>\n       </i>\n       is an English\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Multidisciplinary" title="Multidisciplinary">\n        multidisciplinary\n       </a>\n       <a href="/wiki/Scientific_journal" title="Scientific journal">\n        scientific journal\n       </a>\n       , first published on 4 November 1869.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">\n        <a href="#cite_note-1">\n         [1]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       It was ranked the world\'s most cited scientific journal by the Science Edition of the 2010\n       <i>\n        <a href="/wiki/Journal_Citation_Reports" title="Journal Citation Reports">\n         Journal Citation Reports\n        </a>\n       </i>\n       and is ascribed an\n       <a href="/wiki/Impact_factor" title="Impact factor">\n        impact factor\n       </a>\n       of approximately 38.1, making it one of the world\'s top academic journals.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">\n        <a href="#cite_note-2">\n         [2]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_3-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-:0-3">\n         [3]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       It is one of the few remaining academic journals that publishes original research across a wide range of scientific fields.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_3-1">\n        <a href="#cite_note-:0-3">\n         [3]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">\n        <a href="#cite_note-4">\n         [4]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       Research scientists are the primary audience for the journal, but summaries and accompanying articles are intended to make many of the most important papers understandable to scientists in other fields and the educated public. Towards the front of each issue are\n       <a href="/wiki/Editorial" title="Editorial">\n        editorials\n       </a>\n       , news and feature articles on issues of general interest to scientists, including current affairs, science funding, business, scientific ethics and research breakthroughs. There are also sections on books and arts. The remainder of the journal consists mostly of research papers (articles or letters), which are often dense and highly technical. Because of strict limits on the length of papers, often the printed text is actually a summary of the work in question with many details relegated to accompanying\n       <i>\n        supplementary material\n       </i>\n       on the journal\'s website.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       There are many fields of\n       <a href="/wiki/Research" title="Research">\n        research\n       </a>\n       in which important new advances and original research are published as either articles or letters in\n       <i>\n        Nature.\n       </i>\n       The papers that have been published in this journal are internationally acclaimed for maintaining high research standards. Less than 8% of submitted papers are accepted for publication.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">\n        <a href="#cite_note-5">\n         [5]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n Britain underwent enormous technological and industrial changes and advances particularly in the latter half of the 19th century.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">\n        <a href="#cite_note-8">\n         [8]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       In English the most respected scientific journals of this time were the refereed journals of the\n       <a href="/wiki/Royal_Society" title="Royal Society">\n        Royal Society\n       </a>\n       , which had published many of the great works from\n       <a href="/wiki/Isaac_Newton" title="Isaac Newton">\n        Isaac Newton\n       </a>\n       ,\n       <a href="/wiki/Michael_Faraday" title="Michael Faraday">\n        Michael Faraday\n       </a>\n       through to early works from\n       <a href="/wiki/Charles_Darwin" title="Charles Darwin">\n        Charles Darwin\n       </a>\n       . In addition, during this period, the number of popular science periodicals doubled from the 1850s to the 1860s.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bar3_9-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-Bar3-9">\n         [9]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       According to the editors of these popular science magazines, the publications were designed to serve as "organs of science", in essence, a means of connecting the public to the scientific world.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bar3_9-1">\n        <a href="#cite_note-Bar3-9">\n         [9]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       <i>\n        Nature\n       </i>\n       , first created in 1869, was not the first magazine of its kind in Britain. One journal to precede\n       <i>\n        Nature\n       </i>\n       was\n       <i>\n        <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Recreative_Science:_A_Record_and_Remembrancer_of_Intellectual_Observation&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Recreative Science: A Record and Remembrancer of Intellectual Observation (page does not exist)">\n         Recreative Science: A Record and Remembrancer of Intellectual Observation\n        </a>\n       </i>\n       , which, created in 1859, began as a\n       <a href="/wiki/Natural_history" title="Natural history">\n        natural history\n       </a>\n       magazine and progressed to include more physical observational science and technical subjects and less natural history.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bar7_10-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-Bar7-10">\n         [10]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       The journal\'s name changed from its original title to\n       <i>\n        Intellectual Observer: A Review of Natural History, Microscopic Research, and Recreative Science\n       </i>\n       and then later to the\n       <i>\n        Student and Intellectual Observer of Science, Literature, and Art\n       </i>\n       .\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bar6_11-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-Bar6-11">\n         [11]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       While\n       <i>\n        Recreative Science\n       </i>\n       had attempted to include more physical sciences such as\n       <a href="/wiki/Astronomy" title="Astronomy">\n        astronomy\n       </a>\n       and\n       <a href="/wiki/Archaeology" title="Archaeology">\n        archaeology\n       </a>\n       , the\n       <i>\n        Intellectual Observer\n       </i>\n       broadened itself further to include literature and art as well.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bar6_11-1">\n        <a href="#cite_note-Bar6-11">\n         [11]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       Similar to\n       <i>\n        Recreative Science\n       </i>\n       was the scientific journal\n       <i>\n        <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Popular_Science_Review&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Popular Science Review (page does not exist)">\n         Popular Science Review\n        </a>\n       </i>\n       , created in 1862,\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bar13_12-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-Bar13-12">\n         [12]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       which covered different fields of science by creating subsections titled "Scientific Summary" or "Quarterly Retrospect", with book reviews and commentary on the latest scientific works and publications.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bar13_12-1">\n        <a href="#cite_note-Bar13-12">\n         [12]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       Two other journals produced in England prior to the development of\n       <i>\n        Nature\n       </i>\n       were the\n       <i>\n        <a href="/wiki/Quarterly_Journal_of_Science" title="Quarterly Journal of Science">\n         Quarterly Journal of Science\n        </a>\n       </i>\n       and\n       <i>\n        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Scientific_Opinion" title="Scientific Opinion">\n         Scientific Opinion\n        </a>\n       </i>\n       , established in 1864 and 1868, respectively.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bar6_11-2">\n        <a href="#cite_note-Bar6-11">\n         [11]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       The journal most closely related to\n       <i>\n        Nature\n       </i>\n       in its editorship and format was\n       <i>\n        The Reader\n       </i>\n       , created in 1864; the publication mixed science with literature and art in an attempt to reach an audience outside of the scientific community, similar to\n       <i>\n        Popular Science Review\n       </i>\n       .\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bar6_11-3">\n        <a href="#cite_note-Bar6-11">\n         [11]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       These similar journals all ultimately failed. The\n       <i>\n        Popular Science Review\n       </i>\n       survived longest, lasting 20 years and ending its publication in 1881;\n       <i>\n        Recreative Science\n       </i>\n       ceased publication as the\n       <i>\n        Student and Intellectual Observer\n       </i>\n       in 1871. The\n       <i>\n        Quarterly Journal\n       </i>\n       , after undergoing a number of editorial changes, ceased publication in 1885.\n       <i>\n        The Reader\n       </i>\n       terminated in 1867, and finally,\n       <i>\n        Scientific Opinion\n       </i>\n       lasted a mere 2 years, until June 1870.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bar7_10-1">\n        <a href="#cite_note-Bar7-10">\n         [10]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <h3>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Creation">\n        Creation\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Nature_(journal)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit First title page, 4 November 1869\n        </div>\n       </div>\n      </div>\n      <p>\n       Not long after the conclusion of\n       <i>\n        The Reader\n       </i>\n       , a former editor,\n       <a href="/wiki/Norman_Lockyer" title="Norman Lockyer">\n        Norman Lockyer\n       </a>\n       , decided to create a new scientific journal titled\n       <i>\n        Nature\n       </i>\n       ,\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-B248_13-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-B248-13">\n         [13]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       taking its name from a line by\n       <a href="/wiki/William_Wordsworth" title="William Wordsworth">\n        William Wordsworth\n       </a>\n       : "To the solid ground of nature trusts the Mind that builds for aye".\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">\n        <a href="#cite_note-14">\n         [14]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       First owned and published by\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alexander_Macmillan_(publisher)" title="Alexander Macmillan (publisher)">\n        Alexander Macmillan\n       </a>\n       ,\n       <i>\n        Nature\n       </i>\n       was similar to its predecessors in its attempt to "provide cultivated readers with an accessible forum for reading about advances in scientific knowledge."\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-B248_13-1">\n        <a href="#cite_note-B248-13">\n         [13]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       Janet Browne has proposed that "far more than any other science journal of the period,\n       <i>\n        Nature\n       </i>\n       was conceived, born, and raised to serve polemic purpose."\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-B248_13-2">\n        <a href="#cite_note-B248-13">\n         [13]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       Many of the early editions of\n       <i>\n        Nature\n       </i>\n       consisted of articles written by members of a group that called itself the\n       <a href="/wiki/X_Club" title="X Club">\n        X Club\n       </a>\n       , a group of scientists known for having liberal, progressive, and somewhat controversial scientific beliefs relative to the time period.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-B248_13-3">\n        <a href="#cite_note-B248-13">\n         [13]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       Initiated by\n       <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Henry_Huxley" title="Thomas Henry Huxley">\n        Thomas Henry Huxley\n       </a>\n       , the group consisted of such important scientists as\n       <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Dalton_Hooker" title="Joseph Dalton Hooker">\n        Joseph Dalton Hooker\n       </a>\n       ,\n       <a href="/wiki/Herbert_Spencer" title="Herbert Spencer">\n        Herbert Spencer\n       </a>\n       , and\n       <a href="/wiki/John_Tyndall" title="John Tyndall">\n        John Tyndall\n       </a>\n       , along with another five scientists and mathematicians; these scientists were all avid supporters of Darwin\'s theory of evolution as\n       <a href="/wiki/Common_descent" title="Common descent">\n        common descent\n       </a>\n       , a theory which, during the latter half of the 19th century, received a great deal of criticism among more conservative groups of scientists.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">\n        <a href="#cite_note-15">\n         [15]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       Perhaps it was in part its scientific liberality that made\n       <i>\n        Nature\n       </i>\n       a longer-lasting success than its predecessors.\n       <a href="/wiki/John_Maddox" title="John Maddox">\n        John Maddox\n       </a>\n       , editor of\n       <i>\n        Nature\n       </i>\n       from 1966 to 1973 as well as from 1980 to 1995, suggested at a celebratory dinner for the journal\'s centennial edition that perhaps it was the journalistic qualities of Nature that drew readers in; "journalism" Maddox states, "is a way of creating a sense of community among people who would otherwise be isolated from each other. This is what Lockyer\'s journal did from the start."\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dinner_16-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-Dinner-16">\n         [16]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       In addition, Maddox mentions that the financial backing of the journal in its first years by the Macmillan family also allowed the journal to flourish and develop more freely than scientific journals before it.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dinner_16-1">\n        <a href="#cite_note-Dinner-16">\n         [16]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <h4>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Editors">\n        Editors\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Nature_(journal)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Editors">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h4>\n      <p>\n       <a href="/wiki/Norman_Lockyer" title="Norman Lockyer">\n        Norman Lockyer\n       </a>\n       , the founder of\n       <i>\n        Nature\n       </i>\n       , was a professor at\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Imperial_College" title="Imperial College">\n        Imperial College\n       </a>\n       . He was succeeded as editor in 1919 by\n       <a href="/wiki/Sir_Richard_Gregory,_1st_Baronet" title="Sir Richard Gregory, 1st Baronet">\n        Sir Richard Gregory\n       </a>\n       .\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NPG_17-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-NPG-17">\n         [17]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       Gregory helped to establish\n       <i>\n        Nature\n       </i>\n       in the international scientific community. His obituary by the Royal Society stated: "Gregory was always very interested in the international contacts of science, and in the columns of\n       <i>\n        Nature\n       </i>\n       he always gave generous space to accounts of the activities of the International Scientific Unions."\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18">\n        <a href="#cite_note-18">\n         [18]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       During the years 1945 to 1973, editorship of\n       <i>\n        Nature\n       </i>\n       changed three times, first in 1945 to A. J. V. Gale and\n       <a href="/wiki/L._J._F._Brimble" title="L. J. F. Brimble">\n        L. J. F. Brimble\n       </a>\n       (who in 1958 became the sole editor), then to\n       <a href="/wiki/John_Maddox" title="John Maddox">\n        John Maddox\n       </a>\n       in 1965, and finally to\n       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=David_%27Dai%27_Davies&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="David \'Dai\' Davies (page does not exist)">\n        David Davies\n       </a>\n       in 1973.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NPG_17-1">\n        <a href="#cite_note-NPG-17">\n         [17]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       In 1980, Maddox returned as editor and retained his position until 1995.\n       <a href="/wiki/Philip_Campbell_(scientist)" title="Philip Campbell (scientist)">\n        Philip Campbell\n       </a>\n       has since become Editor-in-chief of all\n       <i>\n        Nature\n       </i>\n       publications.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NPG_17-2">\n        <a href="#cite_note-NPG-17">\n         [17]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <h4>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Expansion_and_development">\n        Expansion and development\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Nature_(journal)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Expansion and development">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h4>\n      <p>\n       In 1970,\n       <i>\n        Nature\n       </i>\n       first opened its Washington office; other branches opened in New York in 1985, Tokyo and Munich in 1987, Paris in 1989, San Francisco in 2001, Boston in 2004, and Hong Kong in 2005. In 1971, under\n       <a href="/wiki/John_Maddox" title="John Maddox">\n        John Maddox\n       </a>\n       \'s editorship, the journal split into\n       <i>\n        Nature Physical Sciences\n       </i>\n       (published on Mondays),\n       <i>\n        Nature New Biology\n       </i>\n       (published on Wednesdays) and\n       <i>\n        Nature\n       </i>\n       (published on Fridays). In 1974, Maddox was no longer editor, and the journals were merged into\n       <i>\n        Nature\n       </i>\n       .\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19">\n        <a href="#cite_note-19">\n         [19]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       Starting in the 1980s, the journal underwent a great deal of expansion, launching over ten new journals. These new journals comprise the Nature Publishing Group, which was created in 1999 and includes\n       <i>\n        Nature\n       </i>\n       , Nature Publishing Group Journals, Stockton Press Specialist Journals and Macmillan Reference (renamed NPG Reference).\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       In 1996,\n       <i>\n        Nature\n       </i>\n       created its own website\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20">\n        <a href="#cite_note-20">\n         [20]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       and in 1999 Nature Publishing Group began its series of\n       <i>\n        Nature Reviews\n       </i>\n       .\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NPG_17-3">\n        <a href="#cite_note-NPG-17">\n         [17]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       Some articles and papers are available for free on the Nature website. Others require the purchase of premium access to the site.\n       <i>\n        Nature\n       </i>\n       claims an online readership of about 3 million unique readers per month.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21">\n        <a href="#cite_note-21">\n         [21]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       On 30 October 2008,\n       <i>\n        Nature\n       </i>\n       endorsed an American presidential candidate for the first time when it supported\n       <a href="/wiki/Barack_Obama" title="Barack Obama">\n        Barack Obama\n       </a>\n       during his campaign in\n       <a href="/wiki/United_States_presidential_election,_2008" title="United States presidential election, 2008">\n        America\'s 2008 presidential election\n       </a>\n       .\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22">\n        <a href="#cite_note-22">\n         [22]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23">\n        <a href="#cite_note-23">\n         [23]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       In October 2012, an\n       <a href="/wiki/Nature_Arabic_Edition" title="Nature Arabic Edition">\n        Arabic edition\n       </a>\n       of the magazine was launched in partnership with\n       <a href="/wiki/King_Abdulaziz_City_for_Science_and_Technology" title="King Abdulaziz City for Science and Technology">\n        King Abdulaziz City for Science and Technology\n       </a>\n       . As of the time it was released, it had about 10,000 subscribers.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NatureME_24-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-NatureME-24">\n         [24]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       On 2 December 2014,\n       <i>\n        Nature\n       </i>\n       announced that it would allow its subscribers and a group of selected media outlets to share links allowing free, "read-only" access to content from its journals. its restrictions on re-use and distribution.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wired-naturefreeview_25-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-wired-naturefreeview-25">\n         [25]\n </p>\n      <p>\n       On 15 January 2015, details of a proposed merger with Springer Science+Business Media were announced.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27">\n        <a Because of these\n       <a href="/wiki/Positive_feedback" title="Positive feedback">\n        positive feedback\n       </a>\n       effects, competition among scientists to publish in high-level journals like\n       <i>\n        Nature\n       </i>\n       and its closest competitor,\n       <i>\n        <a href="/wiki/Science_(journal)" title="Science (journal)">\n         Science\n        </a>\n       </i>\n       , can be very fierce.\n       <i>\n        Nature\n       </i>\n       <span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">\n        \'\n       </span>\n       s\n       <a href="/wiki/Impact_factor" title="Impact factor">\n        impact factor\n       </a>\n       , a measure of how many citations a journal generates in other works, was 38.138 in 2015 (as measured by\n       <a href="/wiki/Institute_for_Scientific_Information" title="Institute for Scientific Information">\n        Thomson ISI\n       </a>\n       ), among the highest of any science journal.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       As with most other professional scientific journals, papers undergo an initial screening by the editor, followed by\n       <a href="/wiki/Peer_review" title="Peer review">\n        peer review\n       </a>\n       (in which other scientists, chosen by the editor for expertise with the subject matter but who have no connection to the research under review, will read and critique articles), before publication. In the case of\n       <i>\n        Nature\n       </i>\n       , they are only sent for review if it is decided that they deal with a topical subject and are sufficiently ground-breaking in that particular field. As a consequence, the majority of submitted papers are rejected without review.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       According to\n       <i>\n        Nature\n       </i>\n       <span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">\n        \'\n       </span>\n       s original\n       <a href="/wiki/Mission_statement" title="Mission statement">\n        mission statement\n       </a>\n       :\n      </p>\n      <blockquote class="templatequote">\n       <p>\n        It is intended, FIRST, to place before the general public the grand results of Scientific Work and Scientific Discovery; and to urge the claims of Science to a more general recognition in Education and in Daily Life; and, SECONDLY, to aid Scientific men themselves, by giving early information of all advances made in any branch of Natural knowledge throughout the world, and by affording them an opportunity of discussing the various Scientific questions which arise from time to time.\n        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nature1869_29-0">\n         <a href="#cite_note-nature1869-29">\n          [29]\n         </a>\n        </sup>\n       </p>\n      </blockquote>\n      <p>\n       This was revised in 2000 to:\n      </p>\n      <blockquote class="templatequote">\n       <p>\n        First, to serve scientists through prompt publication of significant advances in any branch of science, and to provide a forum for the reporting and discussion of news and issues concerning science.       </span>\n       </span>\n      </h3>\n      <p>\n       In 2017, Nature published an editorial entitled "Removing Statues of Historical figures risks whitewashing history: Science must acknowledge mistakes as it marks its past". The article commented on the placement and maintenance of statues honouring scientists with known unethical, abusive and torturous histories. Specifically, the editorial called on examples of\n       <a href="/wiki/J._Marion_Sims" title="J. Marion Sims">\n        J. Marion Sims\n       </a>\n       , the \'Father of gynecology\' who experimented on African American women (slaves) without their consent, and\n       <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Parran_Jr." title="Thomas Parran Jr.">\n        Thomas Parran Jr.\n       </a>\n       who oversaw the\n       <a href="/wiki/Tuskegee_syphilis_experiment" title="Tuskegee syphilis experiment">\n        Tuskegee syphilis experiment\n       </a>\n       . The editorial as written made the case that removing such statues, and erasing names, runs the risk of "whitewashing history", and stated \xe2\x80\x9cInstead of removing painful reminders, perhaps these should be supplemented\xe2\x80\x9d. The article caused a large outcry and was quickly modified by Nature.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31">\n        <a href="#cite_note-31">\n         [31]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       The article was largely seen as offensive, inappropriate, and by many, racist. Nature acknowledged that the article as originally written was "offensive and poorly worded" and published selected letters of response.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32">\n        <a href="#cite_note-32">\n         [32]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       The editorial came just weeks after hundreds of white supremacists marched in\n       <a href="/wiki/Charlottesville,_Virginia" title="Charlottesville, Virginia">\n        Charlottesville, Virginia\n       </a>\n       in the\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Unite_the_Right_Rally" title="Unite the Right Rally">\n        Unite the Right rally\n       </a>\n       to oppose the removal of\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Robert_Edward_Lee_Sculpture" title="Robert Edward Lee Sculpture">\n        a statue of Robert E. Lee\n       </a>\n       , setting off violence in the streets and killing a young woman. When Nature posted a link to the editorial on\n       <a href="/wiki/Twitter" title="Twitter">\n        Twitter\n       </a>\n       , the thread quickly exploded with criticisms. In response, several scientists called for a boycott.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33">\n        <a href="#cite_note-33">\n         [33]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       On 18 September 2017, the editorial was updated and edited by Philip Campbell, the editor of the journal.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34">\n        <a href="#cite_note-34">\n         [34]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       When\n       <a href="/wiki/Paul_Lauterbur" title="Paul Lauterbur">\n        Paul Lauterbur\n       </a>\n       and\n       <a href="/wiki/Peter_Mansfield" title="Peter Mansfield">\n        Peter Mansfield\n       </a>\n       won a\n       <a href="/wiki/Nobel_Prize_in_Physiology_or_Medicine" title="Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine">\n        Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine\n       </a>\n       for research initially rejected by\n       <i>\n        Nature\n       </i>\n       and published only after Lauterbur appealed the rejection,\n       <i>\n        Nature\n       </i>\n       acknowledged more of its own missteps in rejecting papers in an editorial titled, "Coping with Peer Rejection":\n      </p>\n      <blockquote class="templatequote">\n       <p>\n        [T]here are unarguable\n        <i>\n         faux pas\n        </i>\n        in our history.
