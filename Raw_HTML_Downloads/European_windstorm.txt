storms crossing the Atlantic Ocean eastward and striking Europe. It is not to be confused with storms arising within Europe or elsewhere and striking Europe.\n      </div>\n      <div class="thumb tright">\n       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:242px;">\n        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Xynthia_animated_small.gif">\n         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="213" data-file-width="240" height="213" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/b/b9/Xynthia_animated_small.gif" width="240"/>\n        </a>\n        <div class="thumbcaption">\n         <div class="magnify">\n          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Xynthia_animated_small.gif" title="Enlarge">\n          </a>\n         </div>\n         24-hour animation of\n         <a href="/wiki/Cyclone_Xynthia" title="Cyclone Xynthia">\n          Cyclone Xynthia\n         </a>\n         crossing France\n        </div>\n       </div>\n      </div>\n      <div class="thumb tright">\n       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;">\n        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:European_Windstorm_Conceptual_Model.jpg">\n         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1329" data-file-width="1772" height="225" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/03/European_Windstorm_Conceptual_Model.jpg/300px-European_Windstorm_Conceptual_Model.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/03/European_Windstorm_Conceptual_Model.jpg/450px-European_Windstorm_Conceptual_Model.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/03/European_Windstorm_Conceptual_Model.jpg/600px-European_Windstorm_Conceptual_Model.jpg 2x" width="300"/>\n        </a>\n        <div class="thumbcaption">\n         <div class="magnify">\n          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:European_Windstorm_Conceptual_Model.jpg" title="Enlarge">\n          </a>\n         </div>\n         Conceptual model for a European Windstorm and the associated strong wind "footprints". Note that storm track, footprint locations and footprint sizes vary by case, and that all footprints are not always present.\n         <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HewsonNeu_1-0">\n          <a href="#cite_note-HewsonNeu-1">\n           [1]\n          </a>\n         </sup>\n        </div>\n       </div>\n      </div>\n      <p>\n       <b>\n        European windstorm\n       </b>\n       is a name given to the strongest\n       <a href="/wiki/Extratropical_cyclone" title="Extratropical cyclone">\n        extratropical cyclones\n       </a>\n       which occur across the continent of\n       <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">\n        Europe\n       </a>\n       .\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Martinez-Alvarado_2-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-Martinez-Alvarado-2">\n         [2]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       They form as\n       <a href="/wiki/Cyclone" title="Cyclone">\n        cyclonic\n       </a>\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Windstorm" title="Windstorm">\n        windstorms\n       </a>\n       associated with areas of low\n       <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_pressure" title="Atmospheric pressure">\n        atmospheric pressure\n       </a>\n       . They are most common in the autumn and winter months. On average, the month when most windstorms form is January. The seasonal average is 4.6 windstorms.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">\n        <a href="#cite_note-3">\n         [3]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       Deep low pressure areas are relatively common over the North Atlantic, sometimes starting as\n       <a href="/wiki/Nor%27easter" title="Nor\'easter">\n        nor\'easters\n       </a>\n       off the\n       <a href="/wiki/New_England" title="New England">\n        New England\n       </a>\n       coast, and frequently track across the North Atlantic Ocean towards western Europe, past the north coast of\n       <a href="/wiki/Great_Britain" title="Great Britain">\n        Britain\n       </a>\n       and\n       <a href="/wiki/Ireland" title="Ireland">\n        Ireland\n       </a>\n       and into the\n       <a href="/wiki/Norwegian_Sea" title="Norwegian Sea">\n        Norwegian Sea\n       </a>\n       . However, when they track further south they can affect almost any country in Europe. Commonly affected countries include the\n       <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">\n        United Kingdom\n       </a>\n       ,\n       <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Ireland" title="Republic of Ireland">\n        Ireland\n       </a>\n       , the\n       <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">\n        Netherlands\n       </a>\n       ,\n       <a href="/wiki/Norway" title="Norway">\n        Norway\n       </a>\n       , the\n       <a href="/wiki/Faroe_Islands" title="Faroe Islands">\n        Faroe Islands\n       </a>\n       and\n       <a href="/wiki/Iceland" title="Iceland">\n        Iceland\n       </a>\n       , but any country in\n       <a href="/wiki/Central_Europe" title="Central Europe">\n        Central Europe\n       </a>\n       ,\n       <a href="/wiki/Northern_Europe" title="Northern Europe">\n        Northern Europe\n       </a>\n       and especially\n       <a href="/wiki/Western_Europe" title="Western Europe">\n        Western Europe\n       </a>\n       is occasionally struck by such a storm system.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       The strong wind phenomena intrinsic to European windstorms, that give rise to "damage footprints" at the surface, can be placed into three categories, namely the "warm jet", the "cold jet" and the "sting jet". These phenomena vary in terms of physical mechanisms, atmospheric structure, spatial extent, duration, severity level, predictability, and location relative to cyclone and fronts.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HewsonNeu_1-1">\n        <a href="#cite_note-HewsonNeu-1">\n         [1]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       On average these storms cause economic damage \xe2\x82\xac1.9\xc2\xa0billion per year, and insurance losses of \xe2\x82\xac1.4\xc2\xa0billion per year (1990\xe2\x80\x931998). They rank as the second highest cause of global natural catastrophe insurance loss (after U.S.\n       <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone" title="Tropical cyclone">\n        hurricanes\n       </a>\n       ).\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FU_4-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-FU-4">\n         [4]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n      </p>\n      <div class="toc" For instance, the Norwegian weather service also names independently notable storms that affect\n       <a href="/wiki/Norway" title="Norway">\n        Norway\n       </a>\n       ,\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">\n        <a href="#cite_note-6">\n         [6]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       which can result in multiple names being used in different countries they affect, such as:\n      </p>\n      <ul>\n       <li>\n        1999 storm "\n        <b>\n         <a href="/wiki/Cyclone_Anatol" title="Cyclone Anatol">\n          Anatol\n         </a>\n        </b>\n        " in Germany, is known as the "\n        <b>\n         Decemberorkan\n        </b>\n        " or "\n        <b>\n         Adam\n        </b>\n        " in Denmark and as "\n        <b>\n         Carola\n        </b>\n        " in Sweden.\n       </li>\n       <li>\n        2011 storm "\n        <b>\n         <a href="/wiki/Cyclone_Dagmar" title="Cyclone Dagmar">\n          Dagmar\n         </a>\n        </b>\n        " in Norway and Sweden is known as "\n        <b>\n         Patrick\n        </b>\n        " in Germany and "\n        <b>\n         Tapani\n        </b>\n        " in Finland.\n       </li>\n       <li>\n        2013\n        <b>\n         <a href="/wiki/St._Jude_storm" title="St. Jude storm">\n          St. Jude storm\n         </a>\n        </b>\n        in the English media, is known as\n        <b>\n         Christian\n        </b>\n        in German and French (following the Free University of Berlin\'s Adopt-a-Vortex program) it was named\n        <b>\n         Simone\n        </b>\n        by the\n        <a href="/wiki/Swedish_Meteorological_and_Hydrological_Institute" title="Swedish Meteorological and Hydrological Institute">\n         Swedish Meteorological and Hydrological Institute\n        </a>\n        , and as the\n        <b>\n         October storm\n        </b>\n        in Danish and Dutch, it was later given the name\n        <b>\n         Allan\n        </b>\n        by the\n        <a href="/wiki/Danish_Meteorological_Institute" title="Danish Meteorological Institute">\n         Danish Meteorological Institute\n        </a>\n        following the political decision to name strong storms which affect Denmark.\n       </li>\n      </ul>\n      <p>\n       An alternative Scottish naming system arose in 2011 via social media/Twitter which resulted in the humorous naming of\n       <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Bawbag" title="Hurricane Bawbag">\n        Hurricane Bawbag\n       </a>\n       <sup class="reference" referred to as "Hurricane Low Q".\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">\n        <a href="#cite_note-10">\n         [10]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <h4>\n       <span class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">\n        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Storm_Oratia_30_Oct_2000.jpg">\n         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="600" data-file-width="569" class="magnify">\n          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Storm_Oratia_30_Oct_2000.jpg" title="Enlarge">\n          </a>\n         </div>\n         Satellite picture of\n         <a href="/wiki/Cyclone_Oratia" title="Cyclone Oratia">\n          Cyclone Oratia\n         </a>\n         on 30 October 2000\n        </div>\n       </div>\n      </div>\n      <p>\n       During 1954, Karla Wege, a student at the Free University of Berlin\'s meteorological institute suggested that names should be assigned to all areas of low and high pressure that influenced the weather of Central Europe.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EWS_History_16-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-EWS_History-16">\n         [16]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       The university subsequently started to name every area of high or low pressure within its weather forecasts, from a list of 260 male and 260 female names submitted by its students.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EWS_History_16-1">\n        <a href="#cite_note-EWS_History-16">\n         [16]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Germany_Bans_17-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-Germany_Bans-17">\n         [17]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       The female names were assigned to areas of low pressure while male names were assigned to areas of high pressure.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EWS_History_16-2">\n        <a href="#cite_note-EWS_History-16">\n         [16]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Germany_Bans_17-1">\n        <a href="#cite_note-Germany_Bans-17">\n         [17]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       The names were subsequently exclusively used by Berlin\'s media until February 1990, after which the German media started to commonly use the names, however, they were not officially approved by the German Meteorological Service\n       <a href="/wiki/Deutscher_Wetterdienst" title="Deutscher Wetterdienst">\n        Deutscher Wetterdienst\n       </a>\n       .\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EWS_History_16-3">\n        <a href="#cite_note-EWS_History-16">\n         [16]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GEWS_History_18-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-GEWS_History-18">\n         [18]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       The DWD subsequently banned the usage of the names by their offices during July 1991, after complaints had poured in about the naming system.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Germany_Bans_17-2">\n        <a href="#cite_note-Germany_Bans-17">\n         [17]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       However, the order was leaked to the German press agency,\n       <a href="/wiki/Deutsche_Presse-Agentur" title="Deutsche Presse-Agentur">\n        Deutsche Presse-Agentur\n       </a>\n       , who ran it as its lead weather story.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Germany_Bans_17-3">\n        <a href="#cite_note-Germany_Bans-17">\n         [17]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       Germany\'s ZDF television channel subsequently ran a phone in poll on 17 July 1991 and claimed that 72% of the 40,000 responses favored keeping the names.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Germany_Bans_17-4">\n        <a href="#cite_note-Germany_Bans-17">\n         [17]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       This made the DWD pause and think about the naming system and these days the DWD accept the naming system and request that it is maintained.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Germany_Bans_17-5">\n        <a href="#cite_note-Germany_Bans-17">\n         [17]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GEWS_History_18-1">\n        <a href="#cite_note-GEWS_History-18">\n         [18]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       During 1998 a debate started about if it was discrimination to name areas of high pressure with male names and the areas of low pressure with female names.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EWS_History_16-4">\n        <a href="#cite_note-EWS_History-16">\n         [16]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       The issue was subsequently resolved by alternating male and female names each year.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EWS_History_16-5">\n        <a href="#cite_note-EWS_History-16">\n         [16]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       In November 2002 the "Adopt-a-Vortex" scheme began, which allows members of the public or companies to buy naming rights for a letter chosen by the buyer that are then assigned alphabetically to high and low pressure areas in Europe during each year.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cooper_19-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-Cooper-19">\n         [19]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       The naming comes with the slim chance that the system will be notable. The money raised by this is used by the meteorology department to maintain weather observations at the Free University.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FU_4-1">\n        <a href="#cite_note-FU-4">\n         [4]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <h3>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Name_of_phenomena">\n        Name of phenomena\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=European_windstorm&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Name of phenomena">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h3>\n      <p>\n       Several European languages use\n       <a href="/wiki/Cognate" title="Cognate">\n        cognates\n       </a>\n       of the word\n       <i>\n        hurac\xc3\xa1n\n       </i>\n       (\n       <i>\n        ouragan\n       </i>\n       ,\n       <i>\n        orkan\n       </i>\n       ,\n       <i>\n        huragan\n       </i>\n       ,\n       <i>\n        orkaan\n       </i>\n       ,\n       <i>\n        \xd1\x83\xd1\x80\xd0\xb0\xd0\xb3\xd0\xb0\xd0\xbd\n       </i>\n       , which may or may not be differentiated from tropical hurricanes in these languages) to indicate particularly strong cyclonic winds occurring in Europe. The term hurricane as applied to these storms is not in reference to the structurally different\n       <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone" title="Tropical cyclone">\n        tropical cyclone\n       </a>\n       of the same name, but to the\n       <i>\n        hurricane strength\n       </i>\n       of the wind on the\n       <a href="/wiki/Beaufort_scale" title="Beaufort scale">\n        Beaufort scale\n       </a>\n       (winds \xe2\x89\xa5 118\xc2\xa0km/h or \xe2\x89\xa5 73\xc2\xa0mph).\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       In English, use of term\n       <i>\n        hurricane\n       </i>\n       to refer to European windstorms is mostly discouraged, as these storms do not display the structure of tropical storms. Likewise the use of the French term\n       <i>\n        ouragan\n       </i>\n       is similarly discouraged as\n       <i>\n        hurricane\n       </i>\n       is in English, as it is typically reserved for tropical storms only.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20">\n        <a href="#cite_note-20">\n         [20]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21">\n        <a href="#cite_note-21">\n         [21]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       European windstorms in\n       <a href="/wiki/Romance-speaking_Europe" title="Romance-speaking Europe">\n        Latin Europe\n       </a>\n       are generally referred to by derivatives of\n       <i>\n        tempestas\n       </i>\n       (\n       <i>\n        tempest\n       </i>\n       ,\n       <i>\n        temp\xc3\xaate\n       </i>\n       ,\n       <i>\n        tempestado\n       </i>\n       ), meaning storm, weather, or season, from the\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Latin_language" title="Latin language">\n        Latin\n       </a>\n       <i>\n        tempus\n       </i>\n       , meaning time.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22">\n        <a href="#cite_note-22">\n         [22]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       Globally storms of this type forming between 30\xc2\xb0 and 60\xc2\xb0 latitude are known as\n       <i>\n        extratropical cyclones\n       </i>\n       . The name\n       <i>\n        European windstorm\n       </i>\n       reflects that these storms in Europe are primarily notable for their strong winds and associated damage, which can span several nations on the continent. The strongest cyclones are called\n       <i>\n        windstorms\n       </i>\n       within academia and the insurance industry.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Martinez-Alvarado_2-1">\n        <a href="#cite_note-Martinez-Alvarado-2">\n         [2]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       The name\n       <i>\n        European windstorm\n       </i>\n       has not been adopted by the UK Met Office in broadcasts (though it is used in their academic research\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23">\n        <a href="#cite_note-23">\n         [23]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       ), the media or by the general public, and appears to have gained currency in academic and insurance circles as a linguistic and terminologically neutral name for the phenomena.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       In contrast to some other European nations there is a lack of a widely accepted name for these storms in English. The Met Office and UK media generally refer to these storms as\n       <i>\n        severe gales\n       </i>\n       .\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HPA_24-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-HPA-24">\n         [24]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       The current definition of severe gales (which warrants the issue of a weather warning) are repeated gusts of 70\xc2\xa0mph (110\xc2\xa0km/h) or more over inland areas.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HPA_24-1">\n        <a href="#cite_note-HPA-24">\n         [24]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       European windstorms are also described in forecasts variously as\n       <i>\n        winter storms\n       </i>\n       ,\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25">\n        <a href="#cite_note-25">\n         [25]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       <i>\n        winter lows\n       </i>\n       ,\n       <i>\n        autumnal lows\n       </i>\n       ,\n       <i>\n        Atlantic lows\n       </i>\n       and\n       <i>\n        cyclonic systems\n       </i>\n       .\n       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">\n        [\n        <i>\n         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">\n          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2013)">\n           citation needed\n          </span>\n         </a>\n        </i>\n        ]\n       </sup>\n       They are also sometimes referred to as\n       <i>\n        bullseye isobars\n       </i>\n       and\n       <i>\n        dartboard lows\n       </i>\n       in reference to their appearance on weather charts.\n       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">\n        [\n        <i>\n         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">\n          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2014)">\n           citation needed\n          </span>\n         </a>\n        </i>\n        ]\n       </sup>\n       A\n       <a href="/wiki/Royal_Society" title="Royal Society">\n        Royal Society\n       </a>\n       exhibition has used the name\n       <i>\n        European cyclones\n       </i>\n       ,\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Royal_Society_26-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-Royal_Society-26">\n         [26]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       with\n       <i>\n        North-Atlantic cyclone\n       </i>\n       and\n       <i>\n        North-Atlantic windstorms\n       </i>\n       also being used.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Martinez-Alvarado_2-2">\n        <a href="#cite_note-Martinez-Alvarado-2">\n         [2]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       Though with the advent of the "Name our Storms" project, they are generally known as storms.\n      </p>\n      <div class="thumb tright">\n       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;">\n        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Uk-cyclone-2.png">\n         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="451" data-file-width="621" height="182" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c7/Uk-cyclone-2.png/250px-Uk-cyclone-2.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c7/Uk-cyclone-2.png/375px-Uk-cyclone-2.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c7/Uk-cyclone-2.png/500px-Uk-cyclone-2.png 2x" width="250"/>\n        </a>\n        <div class="thumbcaption">\n         <div class="magnify">\n          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Uk-cyclone-2.png" title="Enlarge">\n          </a>\n         </div>\n         A fictitious synoptic chart of an extratropical cyclone affecting Great Britain &amp; Ireland.        <a href="/w/index.php?title=European_windstorm&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: North Atlantic Oscillation">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h3>\n      <p>\n       The state of the\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/North_Atlantic_Oscillation" title="North Atlantic Oscillation">\n        North Atlantic Oscillation\n       </a>\n       relates strongly to the frequency, intensity, and tracks of European windstorms.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27">\n        <a href="#cite_note-27">\n         [27]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       An enhanced number of storms have been noted over the North Atlantic/European region during positive NAO phases (compared to negative NAO phases) and is due to larger areas of suitable growth conditions. The occurrence of extreme North Atlantic cyclones is aligned with the NAO state during the cyclones\' development phase.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Donat_28-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-Donat-28">\n         [28]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       The strongest storms are embedded within, and form in large scale atmospheric flow.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hanley_2012_29-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-Hanley_2012-29">\n         [29]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       It should be kept in mind that, on the other hand, the cyclones themselves play a major role in steering the NAO phase.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Donat_28-1">\n        <a href="#cite_note-Donat-28">\n         [28]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       Aggregate European windstorm losses show a strong dependence on NAO,\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WRN2012_30-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-WRN2012-30">\n         [30]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       with losses increasing/decreasing 10-15% at all return periods.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WRN2012_30-1">\n        <a href="#cite_note-WRN2012-30">\n         [30]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <h3>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Clustering">\n        Clustering\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span 8 consecutive storms hitting Europe during the winter of 1989/90.\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cyclone_Lothar_and_Martin" title="Cyclone Lothar and Martin">\n        Lothar and Martin\n       </a>\n       in 1999 were separated only by 36 hours. In December the same year,\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cyclone_Friedhelm" title="Cyclone Friedhelm">\n        Friedhelm\n       </a>\n       ,\n       <a href="/wiki/Cyclone_Hergen" title="Cyclone Hergen">\n        Hergen\n       </a>\n       ,\n       <a href="/wiki/Cyclone_Joachim" title="Cyclone Joachim">\n        Joachim\n       </a>\n       and\n       <a class="new" Dagmar">\n        /Patrick\n       </a>\n       (Cato/Dagmar) struck northern Europe.\n      </p>\n      <h2>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Economic_impact">\n        Economic impact\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=European_windstorm&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Economic impact">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h2>\n      <h3>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Insurance_losses">\n        Insurance losses\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=European_windstorm&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Insurance losses">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h3>\n      <p>\n       Insurance losses from windstorms are the second greatest source of loss for any natural peril after\n       <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_hurricane" title="Atlantic hurricane">\n        Atlantic hurricanes\n       </a>\n       in the United States.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33">\n        <a href="#cite_note-33">\n         [33]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       Windstorm losses exceed those caused by flooding in Europe.
