 </table>\n      <p>\n       The\n       <b>\n        RAE Hurricane\n       </b>\n       was a single-seat, single-engined light\n       <a href="/wiki/Monoplane" title="Monoplane">\n        monoplane\n       </a>\n       designed and built by the Aero Club of the\n       <a href="/wiki/Royal_Aircraft_Establishment" title="Royal Aircraft Establishment">\n        Royal Aircraft Establishment\n       </a>\n       for the\n       <a href="/wiki/Lympne_light_aircraft_trials" title="Lympne light aircraft trials">\n        1923 Lympne Motor Glider Competition\n       </a>\n       . It was underpowered with an unreliable engine. Re-engined, it flew in many races, with first place in the 1926\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Grosvenor_Challenge_Cup" title="Grosvenor Challenge Cup">\n        Grosvenor Challenge Cup\n       </a>\n       its greatest success.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n      </p>\n      <div class="toc" id="toc">\n       <div class="toctitle">\n        <h2>\n         Contents\n        </h2>\n </div>\n      <p>\n      </p>\n      <h2>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Design_and_development">\n        Design and development\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=RAE_Hurricane&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Design and development">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h2>\n      <p>\n       Amongst the many aircraft designed and built for the\n       <a href="/wiki/Lympne_light_aircraft_trials" title="Lympne light aircraft trials">\n        Lympne\n       </a>\n       light aircraft competitions of the mid-1920s, the RAE Hurricane and the\n       <a href="/wiki/Cranwell_CLA.2" title="Cranwell CLA.2">\n        Cranwell CLA.2\n       </a>\n       had one other thing in common. Both were product of amateur groups formed within government funded aeronautical establishments, the Royal Aircraft Establishment at\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/RAE_Farnborough" title="RAE Farnborough">\n        Farnborough\n       </a>\n       and the\n       <a href="/wiki/RAF_Cranwell" title="RAF Cranwell">\n        Cranwell RAF College\n       </a>\n       . The Hurricane was built for speed and flew in the 1923\n       <a href="/wiki/Lympne_light_aircraft_trials" title="Lympne light aircraft trials">\n        Lympne Motor Glider Competition\n       </a>\n       , the CLA.2 for durability, flying in the two-seater competition the following year.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">\n        <a href="#cite_note-1">\n         [1]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       The Hurricane was a wooden framed, fabric covered shoulder wing monoplane.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OrdH_2-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-OrdH-2">\n         [2]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       The wing was a two spar cantilever structure,\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FlightA_3-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-FlightA-3">\n         [3]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       made possible by the adoption of one of the novel thick G\xc3\xb6ttingen airfoil sections.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OrdH_2-1">\n        <a href="#cite_note-OrdH-2">\n         [2]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       It was tapered, but almost entirely on the trailing edge and with clipped wingtips. Seen in plan, the wing of the Hurricane looked much a late 20th-century light aircraft.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OrdH_2-2">\n        <a href="#cite_note-OrdH-2">\n         [2]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FlightA_3-1">\n        <a href="#cite_note-FlightA-3">\n         [3]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       The competition rules required that aircraft could access suitable fields via a farm gate, and many met this condition with folding wings. The Hurricane\'s designer, Samuel Childs chose instead to make its wings easily detachable after removing eight bolts per side.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OrdH_2-3">\n        <a href="#cite_note-OrdH-2">\n         [2]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FlightA_3-2">\n        <a href="#cite_note-FlightA-3">\n         [3]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       The fuselage of the Hurricane was in contrast very much of its time, built up on two upper longerons and a strongly curved keel, producing a structure that was narrow and almost parallel in plan and a thin triangle in cross section, deepest between engine and the wing leading edge, tapering rapidly aft.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OrdH_2-4">\n        <a href="#cite_note-OrdH-2">\n         [2]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FlightA_3-3">\n        <a href="#cite_note-FlightA-3">\n         [3]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       The single-seat open cockpit was at mid-chord, between the spars. The main undercarriage was a pair of fabric covered mtorcycle type wire wheels mounted at the ends of a horizontal leaf spring fixed to the keel and braced to the fuselage with a pair of V-struts,\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OrdH_2-5">\n        <a href="#cite_note-OrdH-2">\n         [2]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       with the result that the Hurricane sat very close to the ground. At the rear the empennage was conventional, with a broad chord fin bearing an unbalanced rudder that extended between split elevators to the keel. Initially the tailplane leading edge was curved and merged into the curved elevator tips.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FlightA_3-4">\n        <a href="#cite_note-FlightA-3">\n         [3]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       Forward of the wing, the fuselage tapered rapidly, mostly through the upward bend of the keel. Because the 600 cc Douglas flat twin engine used in the 1923 aircraft was geared down 2:1 via a front-mounted chain drive that put the propeller shaft at the top of the motor, it was mounted low and to the rear of the nose, with cylinders exposed for cooling. The output of this engine was only 21.5\xc2\xa0hp (16\xc2\xa0kW).\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OrdH_2-6">\n        <a href="#cite_note-OrdH-2">\n         [2]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       Several changes were made during 1924.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OrdH_2-7">\n        <a href="#cite_note-OrdH-2">\n         [2]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AJJ_4-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-AJJ-4">\n         [4]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       The Douglas engine was replaced with a 32\xc2\xa0hp (24\xc2\xa0kW)\n       <a href="/wiki/Bristol_Cherub" title="Bristol Cherub">\n        Bristol Cherub\n       </a>\n       II flat twin. This more powerful motor was also lighter, lacking the reduction gear of the Douglas and therefore mounted in the extreme nose with propeller and exposed cylinders at wing level. At about the same time the empennage was modified with a squarer tailplane,\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">\n        <a href="#cite_note-5">\n         [5]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       taller, less rounded fin and square tipped rudder. The leaf spring undercarriage was replaced with a lighter split axle unit carrying smaller wheels and braced with conspicuous V-struts to the wing roots.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OrdH_2-8">\n        <a href="#cite_note-OrdH-2">\n         [2]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AJJ_4-1">\n        <a href="#cite_note-AJJ-4">\n         [4]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       There were also modifications to the top of the fuselage,\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OrdH_2-9">\n        <a href="#cite_note-OrdH-2">\n         [2]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       including the installation of a side hinged cockpit cover with an opening just large enough to allow the pilot\'s head to protrude.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AJJ_4-2">\n        <a href="#cite_note-AJJ-4">\n         [4]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <h2>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Operational_history">\n        Operational history\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=RAE_Hurricane&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Operational history">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h2>\n      <p>\n       Only one Hurricane was built, registered\n       <i>\n        G-EBHS\n       </i>\n       .\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">\n        <a href="#cite_note-6">\n         [6]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       Since the Lympne trials were in October, the Hurricane must have made its first light in the late summer of 1923, probably piloted by\n       <a href="/wiki/George_Bulman_(pilot)" title="George Bulman (pilot)">\n        Paul Bulman\n       </a>\n       . At the trials, with Bulman at the controls it flew rather tail down, suggesting trim and wing incidence issues had not been sorted.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OrdH_2-10">\n        <a href="#cite_note-OrdH-2">\n         [2]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FlightB_7-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-FlightB-7">\n         [7]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       Compared with some other competitors it was overweight; like many others it suffered from an unreliable engine. Worst, it was slow; it had been entered specifically for the Abdullah speed prize and there had been talk of speeds near 100\xc2\xa0mph, but the best it could do was 58.5\xc2\xa0mph (94\xc2\xa0km/h)\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FlightB_7-1">\n        <a href="#cite_note-FlightB-7">\n         [7]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       before retiring with a broken rocker arm.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">\n        <a href="#cite_note-8">\n         [8]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       The winner of this \xc2\xa3500 prize was the\n       <a href="/wiki/Parnall_Pixie" title="Parnall Pixie">\n        Parnall Pixie II\n       </a>\n       at 76.1\xc2\xa0mph.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">\n        <a href="#cite_note-9">\n         [9]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       As a single seater, the Hurricane did not qualify for the 1924\n       <a href="/wiki/Lympne" title="Lympne">\n        Lympne Two Seater Light Plane Competition\n       </a>\n       , but flew in the Grosvenor Challenge Cup immediately afterwards, now powered with the Cherub engine. The extra power produced a speed of about 80\xc2\xa0mph, but it failed at just past the half way stage.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OrdH_2-11">\n        <a href="#cite_note-OrdH-2">\n         [2]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">\n        <a href="#cite_note-10">\n         [10]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       Its most successful meeting was at Lympne in August 1925, flown by F/Lt J. F. Chick where it won the light Plane Holiday Handicap, the Private Owners Race and, most prestigiously the eight lap, 100 mile (161\xc2\xa0km) Grosvenor Challenge Cup,\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">\n        <a href="#cite_note-11">\n         [11]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       averaging 81.2\xc2\xa0mph (131\xc2\xa0km/h).\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">\n        <a href="#cite_note-12">\n         [12]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       In total, the Hurricane picked up \xc2\xa3300 in prizes at that meeting.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AJJ_4-3">\n        <a href="#cite_note-AJJ-4">\n         [4]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       It was flown by Chick in its last Grosvenor Cup in 1926; although it was faster than before (84.8\xc2\xa0mph or 136\xc2\xa0km/h), it only managed third place.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">\n        <a href="#cite_note-13">\n         [13]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       It was broken up later that year.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AJJ_4-4">\n        <a href="#cite_note-AJJ-4">\n         [4]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n href="#CITEREFOrd-Hume2000">\n        Ord-Hume 2000\n       </a>\n       , p.\xc2\xa0455\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       <span style="font-size: 120%;">\n        <b>\n         General characteristics\n        </b>\n       </span>\n      </p>\n      <ul>\n       <li>\n        <b>\n         Crew:\n        </b>\n        1\n       </li>\n       <li>\n        <b>\n         Length:\n        </b>\n
