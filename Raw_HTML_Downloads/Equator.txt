<a href="/wiki/Degree_(angle)" title="Degree (angle)">\n        degrees\n       </a>\n       ) of arc. The Equator is one of the five notable\n       <a href="/wiki/Circle_of_latitude" title="Circle of latitude">\n        circles of latitude\n       </a>\n       on Earth; the other four are the two\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Polar_Circle" title="Polar Circle">\n        Polar Circles\n       </a>\n       (the\n       <a href="/wiki/Arctic_Circle" title="Arctic Circle">\n        Arctic Circle\n       </a>\n       and the\n       <a href="/wiki/Antarctic_Circle" title="Antarctic Circle">\n        Antarctic Circle\n       </a>\n       ) and the\n       <a href="/wiki/Tropics" title="Tropics">\n        two Tropical Circles\n       </a>\n       (the\n       <a href="/wiki/Tropic_of_Cancer" title="Tropic of Cancer">\n        Tropic of Cancer\n       </a>\n       and the\n       <a href="/wiki/Tropic_of_Capricorn" title="Tropic of Capricorn">\n        Tropic of Capricorn\n       </a>\n       ). The Equator is the only line of latitude which is also a\n       <a href="/wiki/Great_circle" title="Great circle">\n        great circle\n       </a>\n       \xe2\x80\x94 that is, one whose plane passes through the center of the globe.       <a href="/wiki/Sunrise" title="Sunrise">\n        sunrises\n       </a>\n       and\n       <a href="/wiki/Sunset" title="Sunset">\n        sunsets\n       </a>\n       , because the sun moves nearly perpendicular to the horizon for most of the year. The length of daylight (sunrise to sunset) is almost constant throughout the year; it is about fourteen minutes longer than nighttime due to\n       <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_refraction" title="Atmospheric refraction">\n        atmospheric refraction\n       </a>\n       and the fact that sunrise is begun (or sunset is ended) as the upper edge, not the centre, of the Sun\'s disk crosses the\n       <a href="/wiki/Horizon" title="Horizon">\n        horizon\n       </a>\n       .\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       The Earth\n       <a href="/wiki/Equatorial_bulge" title="Equatorial bulge">\n        bulges slightly at the Equator\n       </a>\n       ; the "average" diameter of the Earth is 12,750 kilometres (7,922\xc2\xa0mi), but the diameter at the Equator is about 43 kilometres (27\xc2\xa0mi) greater than at the poles.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-National_1-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-National-1">\n         [1]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       Sites near the Equator, such as the\n       <a href="/wiki/Guiana_Space_Centre" title="Guiana Space Centre">\n        Guiana Space Centre\n       </a>\n       in\n       <a href="/wiki/Kourou" title="Kourou">\n        Kourou\n       </a>\n       ,\n       <a href="/wiki/French_Guiana" title="French Guiana">\n        French Guiana\n       </a>\n       , are good locations for\n       <a href="/wiki/Spaceport" title="Spaceport">\n        spaceports\n       </a>\n       as they have a faster rotational speed than other latitudes; the added velocity reduces the fuel needed to launch spacecraft. Because the Earth spins to the east, spacecraft must also launch to the east (or to the southeast or northeast) to take advantage of this Earth-boost of speed.\n       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">\n        [\n        <i>\n         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">\n          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2017)">\n           citation needed\n          </span>\n         </a>\n        </i>\n        ]\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <h2>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Geodesy">\n        Geodesy\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Equator&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Geodesy">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h2>\n      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">\n       Further information:\n       <a href="/wiki/Earth_ellipsoid" title="Earth ellipsoid">\n        Earth ellipsoid\n       </a>\n       and\n       <a href="/wiki/Reference_ellipsoid" title="Reference ellipsoid">\n        Reference ellipsoid\n       </a>\n      </div>\n      <h3>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Precise_location">\n        Precise location\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Equator&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Precise location">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h3>\n      <p>\n       The precise location of the Equator is not truly fixed; the true equatorial plane is perpendicular to the Earth\'s spin axis, which\n       <a href="/wiki/Polar_motion" title="Polar motion">\n        drifts\n       </a>\n       about 9 metres (30\xc2\xa0ft) during a year. This effect must be accounted for in detailed\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Geophysical" title="Geophysical">\n        geophysical\n       </a>\n       measurements.\n       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">\n        [\n        <i>\n         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">\n          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2017)">\n           citation needed\n          </span>\n         </a>\n        </i>\n        ]\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <h3>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Exact_length">\n        Exact length\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Equator&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Exact length">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h3>\n      <p>\n       In two widely used\n       <a href="/wiki/Geodesy" title="Geodesy">\n        geodetic\n       </a>\n       standards, the Equator is modelled as a circle whose radius is a\n       <a href="/wiki/Integer" title="Integer">\n        whole number\n       </a>\n       of metres. In 1976 the\n       <a href="/wiki/International_Astronomical_Union" title="International Astronomical Union">\n        IAU\n       </a>\n       chose a radius of 6,378.140 kilometres (3,963.192\xc2\xa0mi) (codified as the IAU-1976 value), later refined by the\n       <a href="/wiki/International_Union_of_Geodesy_and_Geophysics" title="International Union of Geodesy and Geophysics">\n        IUGG\n       </a>\n       to 6,378.137 kilometres (3,963.191\xc2\xa0mi) and adopted in\n       <a href="/wiki/World_Geodetic_System" title="World Geodetic System">\n        WGS-84\n       </a>\n       . Despite this change, the yet more recent IAU-2000 retained the old IAU-1976 value. If it were really circular, the length of the Equator would be exactly 2\n       <span class="texhtml">\n        \xcf\x80\n       </span>\n       times the radius, namely 40,075.036 kilometres (24,901.473\xc2\xa0mi). However, due to the effect of\n       <a href="/wiki/Earth_tide" title="Earth tide">\n        tidal force\n       </a>\n       on the Earth\'s surface the actual circumference at the equator is about 40,075.16 kilometres (24,901.55\xc2\xa0mi).\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       The\n       <a href="/wiki/Geographical_mile" title="Geographical mile">\n        geographical mile\n       </a>\n       is defined as one arc minute of the Equator, so it has different values depending on which radius is assumed. For example, by WSG-84, the distance is 1,855.3248 metres (6,087.024\xc2\xa0ft), while by IAU-2000, it is 1,855.3257 metres (6,087.027\xc2\xa0ft). This is a difference of less than one millimeter over the total distance (approximately 1.86 kilometres (1.16\xc2\xa0mi)).\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       The earth is commonly modeled as a sphere flattened 0.336% along its axis. This makes the Equator 0.16% longer than a\n       <a href="/wiki/Meridian_(geography)" title="Meridian (geography)">\n        meridian\n       </a>\n       (a great circle passing through the two poles). The IUGG standard meridian is, to the nearest millimetre, 40,007.862917 kilometres (24,859.733480\xc2\xa0mi), one arc minute of which is 1,852.216 metres (6,076.82\xc2\xa0ft), explaining the\n       <a href="/wiki/International_System_of_Units" title="International System of Units">\n        SI\n       </a>\n       standardization of the\n       <a href="/wiki/Nautical_mile" title="Nautical mile">\n        nautical mile\n       </a>\n       as 1,852 metres (6,076\xc2\xa0ft), more than 3 metres (9.8\xc2\xa0ft) less than the\n       <a href="/wiki/Geographical_mile" title="Geographical mile">\n        geographical mile\n       </a>\n       .\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       The sea-level surface of the Earth (the\n       <a href="/wiki/Geoid" title="Geoid">\n        geoid\n       </a>\n       ) is irregular, so the actual length of the Equator is not so easy to determine.\n       <i>\n        Aviation Week and Space Technology\n       </i>\n       on 9 October 1961 reported that measurements using the Transit IV-A satellite had shown the equatorial "diameter" from longitude 11\xc2\xb0 West to 169\xc2\xb0 East to be 1,000 feet (300\xc2\xa0m) greater than its "diameter" ninety degrees away.\n       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">\n        [\n        <i>\n         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">\n          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2012)">\n           citation needed\n          </span>\n         </a>\n        </i>\n        ]\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <h2>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Crossing-the-Equator_ceremonies">\n        Crossing-the-Equator ceremonies\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Equator&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Crossing-the-Equator ceremonies">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h2>\n      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">\n       Main article:\n       <a href="/wiki/Line-crossing_ceremony" title="Line-crossing ceremony">\n        Line-crossing ceremony\n       </a>\n      </div>\n      <p>\n       Certain navies, such as the\n       <a href="/wiki/Royal_Navy" title="Royal Navy">\n        Royal Navy\n       </a>\n       ,\n       <a href="/wiki/Russian_Navy" title="Russian Navy">\n        Russian Navy\n       </a>\n       , and\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/US_Navy" title="US Navy">\n        US Navy\n       </a>\n       , have a tradition of holding ceremonies aboard ships to mark sailors\' first crossing of the Equator. (April 2017)">\n           citation needed\n          </span>\n         </a>\n        </i>\n        ]\n       </sup>\n       Milder line-crossing ceremonies, typically featuring\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Neptune_(god)" title="Neptune (god)">\n        King Neptune\n       </a>\n       , are also held for passengers\' entertainment on some civilian ocean liners and cruise ships.\n       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">\n        [\n        <i>\n         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">\n          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources.    ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h2>\n      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">\n       Main articles:\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Seasons" title="Seasons">\n        Seasons\n       </a>\n       ,\n       <a href="/wiki/Tropics" title="Tropics">\n        Tropics\n       </a>\n       , and\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Effect_of_sun_angle_on_climate" title="Effect of sun angle on climate">\n        Effect of sun angle on climate\n       </a>\n      </div>\n      <div class="thumb tright">\n       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:268px;">\n        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Seasons.svg">\n         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="170" data-file-width="266" height="170" seasons, depicting the situation at the December solstice. Regardless of the time of day (i.e. the\n         <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">\n          Earth\n         </a>\n         \xe2\x80\x99s rotation on its axis), the\n         <a href="/wiki/North_Pole" title="North Pole">\n          North Pole\n         </a>\n         will be dark, and the\n         <a href="/wiki/South_Pole" title="South Pole">\n          South Pole\n         </a>\n         will be illuminated; see also\n         <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arctic_winter" title="Arctic winter">\n          arctic winter\n         </a>\n         . In addition to the density of\n         <a href="/wiki/Angle_of_incidence_(optics)" title="Angle of incidence (optics)">\n          incident\n         </a>\n         light, the\n         <a href="/wiki/Dissipation" title="Dissipation">\n          dissipation\n         </a>\n         of light in the\n         <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Earth%27s_atmosphere" title="Earth\'s atmosphere">\n          atmosphere\n         </a>\n         is greater when it falls at a shallow angle.\n        </div>\n       </div>\n      </div>\n      <p>\n       Seasons result from the yearly revolution of the Earth around the Sun and the tilt of the Earth\'s axis relative to the plane of revolution. During the year the northern and southern hemispheres are inclined toward or away from the sun according to Earth\'s position in its orbit. The hemisphere inclined toward the sun receives more sunlight and is in summer, while the other hemisphere receives less sun and is in winter (see\n       <a href="/wiki/Solstice" title="Solstice">\n        solstice\n       </a>\n       ).\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       At the\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Equinoxes" title="Equinoxes">\n        equinoxes\n       </a>\n       , the Earth\'s axis is not tilted toward the sun, instead it is perpendicular to the sun meaning that the day is about 12 hours long, as is the night, across the whole of the Earth.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       The Equator lies mostly on the three largest\n       <a href="/wiki/Ocean" title="Ocean">\n        oceans\n       </a>\n       : the\n       <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Ocean" title="Atlantic Ocean">\n        Atlantic Ocean\n       </a>\n       , the\n       <a href="/wiki/Indian_Ocean" title="Indian Ocean">\n        Indian Ocean\n       </a>\n       , and the\n       <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Ocean" title="Pacific Ocean">\n        Pacific Ocean\n       </a>\n       . Near the Equator there is little temperature fluctuation throughout the year, though there may be dramatic differences in rainfall and humidity. The terms summer, autumn, winter and spring do not generally apply. Lowlands around the Equator generally have a\n       <a href="/wiki/Tropical_rainforest_climate" title="Tropical rainforest climate">\n        tropical rainforest climate\n       </a>\n       , also known as an equatorial climate, though cold currents cause some regions to have\n       <a href="/wiki/Tropical_monsoon_climate" title="Tropical monsoon climate">\n        tropical monsoon climates\n       </a>\n       with a\n       <a href="/wiki/Dry_season" title="Dry season">\n        dry season\n       </a>\n       in the middle of the year, and the\n       <a href="/wiki/Somali_Current" title="Somali Current">\n        Somali Current\n       </a>\n       generated by\n       <a href="/wiki/Monsoon_of_South_Asia" title="Monsoon of South Asia">\n        the Asian monsoon\n       </a>\n       due to continental heating via the high\n       <a href="/wiki/Tibetan_Plateau" title="Tibetan Plateau">\n        Tibetan Plateau\n       </a>\n       causes\n       <a href="/wiki/Greater_Somalia" title="Greater Somalia">\n        Greater Somalia\n       </a>\n       to have an arid climate despite its equatorial location.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       Average annual temperatures in equatorial lowlands are around 30\xc2\xa0\xc2\xb0C (86\xc2\xa0\xc2\xb0F) during the day and 23\xc2\xa0\xc2\xb0C (73\xc2\xa0\xc2\xb0F) at night. Rainfall is away from cold current upwelling zones very high, from 2,500 to 3,500\xc2\xa0mm (98 to 138\xc2\xa0in) per year. Average rainy days are around 200 per year and average annual sunshine hours around 2,000. Despite high year-round sea level temperatures, some higher altitudes such as the\n       <a href="/wiki/Andes" title="Andes">\n        Andes\n       </a>\n       and\n       <a href="/wiki/Mount_Kilimanjaro" title="Mount Kilimanjaro">\n        Mount Kilimanjaro\n       </a>\n       sustain glaciers.
