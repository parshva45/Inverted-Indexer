</a>\n    </div>\n    <div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en">\n     <div class="mw-parser-output">\n      <p>\n       The\n       <b>\n        NanoRacks CubeSat Deployer\n       </b>\n       (\n       <b>\n        NRCSD\n       </b>\n       ) is a device to deploy\n       <a href="/wiki/CubeSat" title="CubeSat">\n        CubeSats\n       </a>\n       into orbit from the\n       <a href="/wiki/International_Space_Station" title="International Space Station">\n        International Space Station\n       </a>\n       .\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       Currently,\n       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">\n        [\n        <i>\n         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">\n          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2014)">\n           citation needed\n          </span>\n         </a>\n        </i>\n        ]\n       </sup>\n       there are two\n       <a href="/wiki/CubeSat" title="CubeSat">\n        CubeSat\n       </a>\n       deployers on board the\n       <a href="/wiki/International_Space_Station" title="International Space Station">\n        International Space Station\n       </a>\n       (ISS): The\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Japanese_Experiment_Module" title="Japanese Experiment Module">\n        Japanese Experiment Module\n       </a>\n       (JEM) Small Satellite Orbital Deployer (J-SSOD) and the\n       <a href="/wiki/NanoRacks" title="NanoRacks">\n        NanoRacks\n       </a>\n       CubeSat Deployer (NRCSD). The J-SSOD is the first of its kind to deploy small satellites from the\n       <a href="/wiki/International_Space_Station" title="International Space Station">\n        International Space Station\n       </a>\n       . The NRCSD is the first commercially operated small satellite deployer from the ISS, maximizing full capabilities of each airlock cycle\n       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">\n        [\n        <i>\n         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify">\n          <span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (December 2014)">\n           clarification needed\n          </span>\n         </a>\n        </i>\n        ]\n       </sup>\n       of deployments.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       CubeSats belong to a class of research spacecraft called\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nanosatellite" title="Nanosatellite">\n        nanosatellites\n       </a>\n       . The basic cube-shaped satellites measure 10 centimeters (3.9\xc2\xa0in) on each side, weigh less than 3 pounds (1.4\xc2\xa0kg), and have a volume of about 1 liter (0.22\xc2\xa0imp\xc2\xa0gal; 0.26\xc2\xa0U.S.\xc2\xa0gal), although CubeSats are built, and deployed, that are multiples of 10\xc2\xa0cm in length.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       As of 2014\n       <sup class="plainlinks noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;">\n        <a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=NanoRacks_CubeSat_Deployer&amp;action=edit">\n         [update]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       , one method of getting CubeSats to\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Low-Earth_orbit" title="Low-Earth orbit">\n        orbit\n       </a>\n       is to\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Space_transport" title="Space transport">\n        transport\n       </a>\n       them aboard a larger spacecraft as part of\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Automated_cargo_spacecraft" title="Automated cargo spacecraft">\n        a cargo load\n       </a>\n       to a larger\n       <a href="/wiki/Space_station" title="Space station">\n        space station\n       </a>\n       . When this is done, deploying the CubeSats into orbit as a separate\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Artificial_satellite" title="Artificial satellite">\n        artificial satellite\n       </a>\n       requires a special apparatus, such as the NanoRacks CubeSat Deployer. The NRCSD is put into position to be grabbed by one of the ISS\xe2\x80\x99s robotic arms, which then places the CubeSat deployer into the correct position externally mounted to the ISS to be able to release the miniature satellites into proper orbit.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n      </p>\n      <div class="toc" id="toc">\n       <div class="toctitle">\n        <h2>\n         Contents\n <span class="mw-headline" id="Background">\n        Background\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=NanoRacks_CubeSat_Deployer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Background">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h2>\n      <p>\n       The\n       <a href="/wiki/International_Space_Station" title="International Space Station">\n        International Space Station\n       </a>\n       was designed to be used as both a microgravity laboratory, as well as a launch pad for low-Earth-orbit services. The Japanese Space Agency\'s (\n       <a href="/wiki/JAXA" title="JAXA">\n        JAXA\n       </a>\n       )\n       <a href="/wiki/Kibo_(ISS_module)" title="Kibo (ISS module)">\n        Kibo\n       </a>\n       ISS module includes a small satellite-deployment system called the J-SSOD.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">\n        <a href="#cite_note-1">\n         [1]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       <a href="/wiki/NanoRacks" title="NanoRacks">\n        NanoRacks\n       </a>\n       , via its Space Act Agreement with\n       <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">\n        NASA\n       </a>\n       , deployed a CubeSat using the J-SSOD. Seeing the emerging market demand for CubeSats, NanoRacks self-funded its own ISS deployer, with the permission of both NASA and JAXA. NanoRacks evolved away from the J-SSOD due to the small number of satellites that could be deployed in one airlock cycle and their desire to maximize the capacity of each airlock cycle. The J-SSOD used a full airlock cycle to only launch 6U. The NanoRacks CubeSat Deployer uses two airlock cycles, each holding 8 deployers. Each deployer is capable of holding 6U, allowing a total of 48U per airlock cycle.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">\n        <a href="#cite_note-2">\n         [2]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template noprint Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">\n        [\n        <i>\n         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:No_original_research#Primary.2C_secondary_and_tertiary_sources" title="Wikipedia:No original research">\n          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable secondary sources. (December 2014)">\n           non-primary source needed\n          </span>\n         </a>\n        </i>\n        ]\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       Deploying CubeSats from ISS has a number of benefits. Launching the vehicles aboard the logistics carrier of ISS visiting vehicle reduces the vibration and loads they have to encounter during launch. In addition, they can be packed in protective materials so that the probability of CubeSat damage during launch is reduced significantly. In addition, for earth observation satellites, such as those of\n       <a href="/wiki/Planet_Labs" title="Planet Labs">\n        Planet Labs\n       </a>\n       , the lower orbit of the ISS orbit, at roughly 400\xc2\xa0km, is an advantage. In addition, the lower orbit allows a natural decay of the satellites, thus reducing the build-up of orbital debris.\n       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">\n        [\n        <i>\n         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">\n          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2014)">\n           citation needed\n          </span>\n         </a>\n        </i>\n        ]\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <h2>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="History">\n        History\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=NanoRacks_CubeSat_Deployer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h2>\n      <h3>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="JEM_Small_Satellite_Orbital_Deployer">\n        JEM Small Satellite Orbital Deployer\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=NanoRacks_CubeSat_Deployer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: JEM Small Satellite Orbital Deployer">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h3>\n      <p>\n       The Japanese Experiment Module Small Satellite Orbital Deployer (J-SSOD) is the first of its kind to deploy small satellites from the International Space Station. The facility provides a unique satellite install case to the Japanese Experiment Module (JEM) Remote Manipulator System (RMS) for deploying small, CubeSat, satellites from the ISS.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JAXA_3-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-JAXA-3">\n         [3]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       The J-SSOD holds up to 3 small one-unit (1U, 10 x 10 x 10 centimeters) small CubeSats per satellite install case, 6 in total, though other sizes up to 55 x 55 x 35\xc2\xa0cm may also be used. Each pre-packed satellite install case is loaded by crewmembers onto the Multi-Purpose Experiment Platform (MPEP) within the JEM habitable volume.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JAXA_3-1">\n        <a href="#cite_note-JAXA-3">\n         [3]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       The MPEP platform is then attached to the JEM Slide Table inside the JEM airlock for transfer to the JEMRMS and space environment. The JEMRMS grapples and maneuvers the MPEP and J-SSOD to a predefined deployment orientation and then jettisons the small CubeSat satellites.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JAXA_3-2">\n        <a href="#cite_note-JAXA-3">\n         [3]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       The MPEP is a platform that acts as an interface between operations inside and outside the ISS, and the J-SSOD mechanism is installed on this platform. On July 21, 2012, JAXA launched the\n       <a href="/wiki/Kounotori_3" title="Kounotori 3">\n        Kounotori 3\n       </a>\n       (HTV-3) cargo spacecraft to the ISS on\n       <a href="/wiki/Expedition_33" title="Expedition 33">\n        Expedition 33\n       </a>\n       . The J-SSOD was a payload on this flight along with five CubeSats that were planned to be deployed by the J-SSOD mounted on the JEMRMS (JEM- Remote Manipulator System), a robotic arm, later in 2012. The five CubeSats were deployed successfully on Oct. 4, 2012 by the JAXA astronaut\n       <a href="/wiki/Akihiko_Hoshide" title="Akihiko Hoshide">\n        Akihiko Hoshide\n       </a>\n       using the newly installed J-SSOD. This represented the first deployment service of J-SSOD.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">\n        <a href="#cite_note-4">\n         [4]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <h3>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="NanoRacks_CubeSat_Deployer_Development">\n        NanoRacks CubeSat Deployer Development\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=NanoRacks_CubeSat_Deployer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: NanoRacks CubeSat Deployer Development">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h3>\n      <p>\n       In October 2013, NanoRacks became the first company to coordinate the deployment of small satellites (CubeSats/nanosatellites) from the ISS via the airlock in the Japanese KIBO module. This deployment was done by NanoRacks using J-SSOD. NanoRacks\xe2\x80\x99 first customer was FPT University of Hanoi, Vietnam. Their F-1 CubeSat was developed by young engineers and students at FSpace laboratory at FPT University of Hanoi. The mission of F-1 was to \xe2\x80\x9csurvive\xe2\x80\x9d the space environment for one month, measuring temperature and magnetic data while taking low-resolution photos of Earth.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">\n        <a href="#cite_note-5">\n         [5]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       In 2013, NanoRacks sought permission from NASA to develop their own hardware and CubeSat/SmallSat deployer to use over the JEM- Small Satellite Deployer. NanoRacks brought leadership to the American small satellite industry by building a larger deployer capable of deploying 48U of satellites. NanoRacks designed, manufactured, and tested the deployer for NASA and JAXA approval to reach the International Space Station.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       The NanoRacks CubeSat Deployer was launched on January 9, 2014, on the Orbital Sciences Orb-1 Cygnus Mission along with 33 small satellites.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">\n        <a href="#cite_note-6">\n         [6]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <h2>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="NRCSD_technical_details">\n        NRCSD technical details\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=NanoRacks_CubeSat_Deployer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: NRCSD technical details">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h2>\n      <p>\n       The NanoRacks CubeSat Deployer (NRCSD) is a self-contained CubeSat deployer system that mechanically and electrically isolates CubeSats from the ISS, cargo resupply vehicles, and ISS crew. The NRCSD design is compliant with NASA ISS flight safety requirements and is space-qualified.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nanoracks_7-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-Nanoracks-7">\n         [7]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       The NRCSD is a rectangular tube that consists of anodized aluminum plates, base-plate assembly, access panels, and deployer doors. The NRCSD deployer doors are located on the forward end, the base-plate assembly is located on the aft end, and access panels are provided on the top.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nanoracks_7-1">\n        <a href="#cite_note-Nanoracks-7">\n         [7]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       The inside walls of the NRCSD are a smooth-bore design to minimize and/or preclude hang-up or jamming of CubeSat appendages during deployment, should these become released prematurely. However, deployable systems shall be designed such that there is no intentional contact with the inside walls of the NRCSD.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nanoracks_7-2">\n        <a href="#cite_note-Nanoracks-7">\n         [7]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       For a deployment, the platform is moved outside via the Kibo Module\xe2\x80\x99s Airlock and slide table that allows the JEMRMS to move the deployers to the correct orientation for the satellite release and also provides command and control to the deployers. Each NRCSD (NanoRacks CubeSat Deployer) is capable of holding six CubeSat Units, allowing it to launch two 3U satellites or a number of 2U and 1U satellites.\n      </p>\n      <h2>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Manufacturing_the_NRCSD">\n        Manufacturing the NRCSD\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=NanoRacks_CubeSat_Deployer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Manufacturing the NRCSD">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h2>\n      <p>\n       Quad-M, Inc. developed the CubeSat Deployer to be compliant with the Cal Poly standard. It was redesigned and manufactured to NanoRacks\xe2\x80\x99 specification for use on the International Space Station.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       Quad-M performed an initial design analysis to ensure a compliant design. The structural analysis included a modal analysis to evaluate vibration response, and the thermal analysis included calculations to evaluate different door coating options and an initial transient thermal analysis to estimate. In addition, Quad-M performed development tests for: the door release, the CSD/CubeSat Deployment test, random vibration test, and temperature cycling.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">\n        <a href="#cite_note-8">\n         [8]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <h2>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Mission_profile">\n        Mission profile\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=NanoRacks_CubeSat_Deployer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Mission profile">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h2>\n      <h3>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Integrating_the_CubeSats">\n        Integrating the CubeSats\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=NanoRacks_CubeSat_Deployer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Integrating the CubeSats">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h3>\n      <p>\n       CubeSat integration begins with unpacking the CSD from the shipping container and then removing the Base Plate Assembly from the rear of the CSD. Next, the CubeSat is inserted from the rear and is slid up snug against the doors. Additional CubeSats are then inserted from the rear in the same progress. The Base Plate Assembly is then reinstalled. Four jack screws are then adjusted with the Pusher Plate and locked. The Containment Bolt is then removed, and the deployer is packed for shipment.\n      </p>\n      <h3>\n       <span id="Orbital_Sciences,_Orb-1">\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-headline"
