class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">\n        <a href="#cite_note-2">\n         [2]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">\n        <a href="#cite_note-3">\n         [3]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       While it is often weaker than most of its land counterparts, stronger versions spawned by\n       <a href="/wiki/Mesocyclone" title="Mesocyclone">\n        mesocyclones\n       </a>\n       do occur.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">\n        <a href="#cite_note-4">\n         [4]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">\n        <a href="#cite_note-5">\n         [5]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       Most waterspouts do not suck up water; they are small and weak rotating columns of air over water.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Glossary_1-2">\n        <a href="#cite_note-Glossary-1">\n         [1]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">\n        <a href="#cite_note-6">\n         [6]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       While waterspouts form mostly in the tropics and\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Subtropical" title="Subtropical">\n        subtropical\n       </a>\n       areas,\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Glossary_1-3">\n        <a href="#cite_note-Glossary-1">\n         [1]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       other areas also report waterspouts, including\n       <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">\n        Europe\n       </a>\n       ,\n       <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand" title="New Zealand">\n        New Zealand\n       </a>\n       , the\n       <a href="/wiki/Great_Lakes" title="Great Lakes">\n        Great Lakes\n       </a>\n       ,\n       <a href="/wiki/Antarctica" title="Antarctica">\n        Antarctica\n       </a>\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lake_michigan_7-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-lake_michigan-7">\n         [7]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">\n        <a href="#cite_note-8">\n         [8]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       and on rare occasions, the\n       <a href="/wiki/Great_Salt_Lake" title="Great Salt Lake">\n        Great Salt Lake\n       </a>\n       .\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-A_Great_Salt_Lake_Waterspout_9-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-A_Great_Salt_Lake_Waterspout-9">\n         [9]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       Although rare, waterspouts have been observed in connection with\n       <a href="/wiki/Lake-effect_snow" title="Lake-effect snow">\n        lake-effect snow\n       </a>\n       <a href="/wiki/Rainband" title="Rainband">\n        precipitation bands\n       </a>\n       .\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       Waterspouts have a five-part life cycle: formation of a dark spot on the water surface, spiral pattern on the water surface, formation of a spray ring, development of the visible\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Condensation_funnel" title="Condensation funnel">\n        condensation funnel\n       </a>\n       , and ultimately decay.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n      </p>\n      <div class="toc" id="toc">\n       <div class="toctitle">\n        <h2>\n         Contents\n        </h2>\n       </div>\n       <ul>\n </span>\n       </span>\n      </h2>\n      <div class="thumb tleft">\n       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">\n        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:The-philosophy-of-storms-1841-James-Pollard-Espy.jpg">\n         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="456" data-file-width="543" height="185" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e1/The-philosophy-of-storms-1841-James-Pollard-Espy.jpg/220px-The-philosophy-of-storms-1841-James-Pollard-Espy.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e1/The-philosophy-of-storms-1841-James-Pollard-Espy.jpg/330px-The-philosophy-of-storms-1841-James-Pollard-Espy.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e1/The-philosophy-of-storms-1841-James-Pollard-Espy.jpg/440px-The-philosophy-of-storms-1841-James-Pollard-Espy.jpg 2x" width="220"/>\n        </a>\n        <div class="thumbcaption">\n         <div class="magnify">\n          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:The-philosophy-of-storms-1841-James-Pollard-Espy.jpg" title="Agrandir">\n          </a>\n         </div>\n         Illustration from the book\n         <i>\n          The Philosophy of Storms\n         </i>\n         , published in 1841.\n        </div>\n       </div>\n      </div>\n      <p>\n       Waterspouts exist on a microscale, where their environment is less than two kilometers in width. The cloud from which they develop can be as innocuous as a moderate cumulus, or as great as a\n       <a href="/wiki/Supercell" title="Supercell">\n        supercell\n       </a>\n       . While some waterspouts are strong and tornadic in nature, most are much weaker and caused by different atmospheric dynamics. They normally develop in moisture-laden environments as their parent clouds are in the process of development, and it is theorized they spin as they move up the surface boundary from the horizontal shear near the surface, and then stretch upwards to the cloud once the low level shear vortex aligns with a developing cumulus cloud or thunderstorm. Weak tornadoes, known as\n       <a href="/wiki/Landspout" title="Landspout">\n        landspouts\n       </a>\n       , have been shown to develop in a similar manner.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Choy_10-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-Choy-10">\n         [10]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       More than one waterspout can occur in the same vicinity at the same time. As many as nine simultaneous waterspouts have been reported on\n       <a href="/wiki/Lake_Michigan" title="Lake Michigan">\n        Lake Michigan\n       </a>\n       .\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lake_michigan_7-1">\n        <a href="#cite_note-lake_michigan-7">\n         [7]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       The illustration on the left, by\n       <a href="/wiki/James_Pollard_Espy" title="James Pollard Espy">\n        James Pollard Espy\n       </a>\n       in 1841, shows the manner in which one spout, while forming, tends to generate others in its neighborhood.\n      </p>\n      <h2>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Types">\n        Types\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Waterspout&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Types">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h2>\n      <h3>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Non-tornadic">\n        Non-tornadic\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Waterspout&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Non-tornadic">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h3>\n      <div class="thumb tleft">\n       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">\n        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Three_waterspouts_Kijkduin.jpg">\n         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="750" data-file-width="994" height="166" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/22/Three_waterspouts_Kijkduin.jpg/220px-Three_waterspouts_Kijkduin.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/22/Three_waterspouts_Kijkduin.jpg/330px-Three_waterspouts_Kijkduin.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/22/Three_waterspouts_Kijkduin.jpg/440px-Three_waterspouts_Kijkduin.jpg 2x" width="220"/>\n        </a>\n        <div class="thumbcaption">\n         <div class="magnify">\n          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Three_waterspouts_Kijkduin.jpg" title="Agrandir">\n          </a>\n         </div>\n         Non-tornadic waterspouts seen from the beach at Kijkduin near\n         <a href="/wiki/The_Hague" title="The Hague">\n          The Hague\n         </a>\n         , the\n         <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">\n          Netherlands\n         </a>\n         on 27 August 2006.\n        </div>\n       </div>\n      </div>\n      <div class="thumb tleft">\n       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">\n        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Punta_Gorda_waterspout.jpg">\n         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="336" data-file-width="448" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8d/Punta_Gorda_waterspout.jpg/220px-Punta_Gorda_waterspout.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8d/Punta_Gorda_waterspout.jpg/330px-Punta_Gorda_waterspout.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8d/Punta_Gorda_waterspout.jpg/440px-Punta_Gorda_waterspout.jpg 2x" width="220"/>\n        </a>\n        <div class="thumbcaption">\n         <div class="magnify">\n          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Punta_Gorda_waterspout.jpg" title="Agrandir">\n          </a>\n         </div>\n         Tornadic waterspout on 15 July 2005 off the coast of\n         <a href="/wiki/Punta_Gorda,_Florida" title="Punta Gorda, Florida">\n          Punta Gorda, Florida\n         </a>\n         , caused by a severe thunderstorm.\n        </div>\n       </div>\n      </div>\n      <p>\n       Waterspouts that are not associated with a rotating updraft of a\n       <a href="/wiki/Supercell" title="Supercell">\n        supercell\n       </a>\n       <a href="/wiki/Thunderstorm" title="Thunderstorm">\n        thunderstorm\n       </a>\n       are known as "non-tornadic" or "fair-weather waterspouts", and are by far the most common type. Fair-weather waterspouts occur in coastal waters and are associated with dark, flat-bottomed, developing convective\n       <a href="/wiki/Cumulus_cloud" title="Cumulus cloud">\n        cumulus\n       </a>\n       towers. Waterspouts of this type rapidly develop and dissipate, having life cycles shorter than 20\xc2\xa0minutes.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Choy_10-1">\n        <a href="#cite_note-Choy-10">\n         [10]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       They usually rate no higher than EF0 on the\n       <a href="/wiki/Enhanced_Fujita_scale" title="Enhanced Fujita scale">\n        Enhanced Fujita scale\n       </a>\n       , generally exhibiting winds of less than 30\xc2\xa0m/s (67\xc2\xa0mph).\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">\n        <a href="#cite_note-11">\n         [11]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       They are most frequently seen in tropical and sub-tropical climates, with upwards of 400 per year observed in the\n       <a href="/wiki/Florida_Keys" title="Florida Keys">\n        Florida Keys\n       </a>\n       .\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NWS_12-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-NWS-12">\n         [12]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       They typically move slowly, if at all, since the cloud to which they are attached is horizontally static, being formed by vertical\n       <a href="/wiki/Convection" title="Convection">\n        convective action\n       </a>\n       instead of the subduction/adduction interaction between colliding\n       <a href="/wiki/Weather_front" title="Weather front">\n        fronts\n       </a>\n       .\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NWS_12-1">\n        <a href="#cite_note-NWS-12">\n         [12]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bruce_13-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-Bruce-13">\n         [13]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       Fair-weather waterspouts are very similar in both appearance and mechanics to\n       <a href="/wiki/Landspout" title="Landspout">\n        landspouts\n       </a>\n       , and largely behave as such if they move ashore.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NWS_12-2">\n        <a href="#cite_note-NWS-12">\n         [12]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <h3>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Tornadic">\n        Tornadic\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Waterspout&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Tornadic">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h3>\n      <p>\n       "Tornadic waterspouts", also accurately referred to as "tornadoes over water", are formed from\n       <a href="/wiki/Mesocyclone" title="Mesocyclone">\n        mesocyclonic action\n       </a>\n       in a manner essentially identical to traditional land-based tornadoes in connection with\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Severe_thunderstorm" title="Severe thunderstorm">\n        severe thunderstorms\n       </a>\n       , but simply occurring over water.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">\n        <a href="#cite_note-14">\n         [14]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       A tornado which travels from land to a body of water would also be considered a tornadic waterspout.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">\n        <a href="#cite_note-15">\n         [15]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       Since the vast majority of mesocyclonic thunderstorms occur in land-locked areas of the United States, true tornadic waterspouts are correspondingly rarer than their fair-weather counterparts in that country. However, in some areas, such as the\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Adriatic_sea" title="Adriatic sea">\n        Adriatic\n       </a>\n       ,\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aegean_sea" title="Aegean sea">\n        Aegean\n       </a>\n       and\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ionian_sea" title="Ionian sea">\n        Ionian seas\n       </a>\n       , tornadic waterspouts can make up half of the total number.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">\n        <a href="#cite_note-16">\n         [16]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <h3>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Snowspout">\n        <span id="Snowspout">\n         Snowspout\n        </span>\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Waterspout&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Snowspout">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h3>\n      <div class="thumb tright">\n       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">\n        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Winter_waterspout.jpg">\n         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="202" data-file-width="385" height="115" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/4/4f/Winter_waterspout.jpg/220px-Winter_waterspout.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/4/4f/Winter_waterspout.jpg/330px-Winter_waterspout.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/4/4f/Winter_waterspout.jpg 2x" width="220"/>\n        </a>\n        <div class="thumbcaption">\n         <div class="magnify">\n          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Winter_waterspout.jpg" title="Agrandir">\n          </a>\n         </div>\n         A large winter waterspout over\n         <a href="/wiki/Lake_Ontario" title="Lake Ontario">\n          Lake Ontario\n         </a>\n         , just off the shore of\n         <a href="/wiki/Whitby,_Ontario" title="Whitby, Ontario">\n          Whitby, Ontario\n         </a>\n         on 26 January 1994.\n        </div>\n       </div>\n      </div>\n      <p>\n       A\n       <i>\n        winter waterspout\n       </i>\n       , also known as a\n       <i>\n        snow devil\n       </i>\n       , an\n       <i>\n        icespout\n       </i>\n       , an\n       <i>\n        ice devil\n       </i>\n       , a\n       <i>\n        snownado\n       </i>\n       , or a\n       <i>\n        snowspout\n       </i>\n       , is an extremely rare instance of a waterspout forming under the base of a\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Snow_squall" title="Snow squall">\n        snow squall\n       </a>\n       .\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17">\n        <a href="#cite_note-17">\n         [17]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18">\n        <a href="#cite_note-18">\n         [18]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       The term "winter waterspout" is used to differentiate between the common warm season waterspout and this rare winter season event. Very little is known about this phenomenon and only six known pictures of this event exist to date, four of which were taken in\n       <a href="/wiki/Ontario" title="Ontario">\n        Ontario\n       </a>\n       , Canada. There are a couple of critical criteria for the formation of a winter waterspout. Very cold temperatures need to be present over a body of water warm enough to produce\n       <a href="/wiki/Fog" title="Fog">\n        fog\n       </a>\n       resembling\n       <a href="/wiki/Steam" title="Steam">\n        steam\n       </a>\n       above the water\'s surface. Like the more efficient\n       <a href="/wiki/Lake-effect_snow" title="Lake-effect snow">\n        lake-effect snow\n       </a>\n       events, winds focusing down the axis of long lakes enhance wind convergence and likely enhance their development.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19">\n        <a href="#cite_note-19">\n         [19]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n    </a>\n         </div>\n         Four waterspouts seen in the Florida Keys on 5 June 2009.\n        </div>\n       </div>\n      </div>\n      <p>\n       Though the majority of waterspouts occur in the tropics, they can seasonally appear in temperate areas throughout the world, and are common across the western coast of Europe as well as the British Isles and several areas of the Mediterranean and Baltic Sea. They are not restricted to saltwater; many have been reported on lakes and rivers including the\n       <a href="/wiki/Great_Lakes" title="Great Lakes">\n        Great Lakes\n       </a>\n       and the\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/St._Lawrence_River" title="St. Lawrence River">\n        St. Lawrence River\n       </a>\n       .\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20">\n        <a href="#cite_note-20">\n         [20]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       Waterspouts are fairly common on the Great Lakes during late summer and early fall, with a record 66+ waterspouts reported over just a seven-day period in 2003.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21">\n        <a href="#cite_note-21">\n         [21]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       They are more frequent within 100\xc2\xa0kilometers (60\xc2\xa0mi) from the coast than farther out at sea. Waterspouts are common along the southeast U.S. coast, especially off southern Florida and the\n       <a href="/wiki/Florida_Keys" title="Florida Keys">\n        Keys\n       </a>\n       and can happen over seas, bays, and lakes worldwide. Approximately 160 waterspouts are currently reported per year across Europe, with the Netherlands reporting the most at 60, followed by Spain and Italy at 25, and the United Kingdom at 15. They are most common in late summer. In the Northern Hemisphere, September has been pinpointed as the prime month of formation.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22">\n        <a href="#cite_note-22">\n         [22]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       Waterspouts are frequently observed off the east coast of Australia,\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23">\n        <a href="#cite_note-23">\n         [23]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24">\n        <a href="#cite_note-24">\n         [24]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       with several being described by\n       <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Banks" title="Joseph Banks">\n        Joseph Banks\n       </a>\n       during the voyage of the\n       <a href="/wiki/First_voyage_of_James_Cook" title="First voyage of James Cook">\n        Endeavour\n       </a>\n       in 1770.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25">\n        <a href="#cite_note-25">\n         [25]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <h2>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Life_cycle">\n        Life cycle\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Waterspout&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Life cycle">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h2>\n      <p>\n       There are five stages to the waterspout life cycle. Initially, a prominent circular, light-colored disk appears on the surface of the water, surrounded by a larger dark area of indeterminate shape. After the formation of these colored disks on the water, a pattern of light and dark-colored spiral bands develop from the dark spot on the water surface. Then, a dense\n       <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/annulus" title="wikt:annulus">\n        annulus\n       </a>\n       of sea spray, called a cascade, appears around the dark spot with what appears to be an\n       <a href="/wiki/Eye_(cyclone)" title="Eye (cyclone)">\n        eye\n       </a>\n       . Eventually, the waterspout becomes a visible funnel from the water surface to the overhead cloud. The spray vortex can rise to a height of several hundred feet or more and often creates a visible wake and an associated wave train as it moves. Eventually, the funnel and spray vortex begin to dissipate as the inflow of warm air into the vortex weakens, ending the waterspout\'s life cycle.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bruce_13-1">\n        <a href="#cite_note-Bruce-13">\n         [13]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <div class="thumb tright">\n       <div class="thumbinner" November 2010 by an RAF Search and Rescue crew\n        </div>\n       </div>\n      </div>\n      <h2>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Marine_hazard">\n        Marine hazard\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Waterspout&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Marine hazard">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h2>\n      <p>\n       Waterspouts have long been recognized as serious marine hazards. Stronger waterspouts pose threats to watercraft, aircraft and people.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26">\n        <a href="#cite_note-26">\n         [26]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       It is recommended to keep a considerable distance from these phenomena, and to always be on alert through weather reports. The United States\n       <a href="/wiki/National_Weather_Service" title="National Weather Service">\n        National Weather Service\n       </a>\n       will often issue\n       <a href="/wiki/Severe_weather_terminology_(United_States)" title="Severe weather terminology (United States)">\n        special marine warnings\n       </a>\n       when waterspouts are likely or have been sighted over coastal waters, or\n       <a href="/wiki/Tornado_warning" title="Tornado warning">\n        tornado warnings\n       </a>\n       when waterspouts are expected to move onshore.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27">\n        <a href="#cite_note-27">\n         [27]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       Incidents of waterspouts causing severe damage and casualties are rare. However, there have been several notable examples. The\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Valletta,_Malta_tornado" title="Valletta, Malta tornado">\n        Malta tornado\n       </a>\n       in 1555 was the earliest record of a deadly waterspout. It struck the\n       <a href="/wiki/Grand_Harbour" title="Grand Harbour">\n        Grand Harbour\n       </a>\n       of Valletta, sinking four\n       <a href="/wiki/Galley" title="Galley">\n        galleys\n       </a>\n       , numerous boats, and claiming hundreds of lives.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28">\n        <a href="#cite_note-28">\n         [28]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       The 1851\n       <a href="/wiki/Sicily_Tornadoes" title="Sicily Tornadoes">\n        Sicily Tornadoes\n       </a>\n       were twin waterspouts that made landfall in western Sicily, ravaging the coast and countryside before ultimately dissipating back again over the sea. A waterspout has been proposed as a possible reason for the inexplicable abandonment of the\n       <i>\n        <a href="/wiki/Mary_Celeste" title="Mary Celeste">\n         Mary Celeste\n        </a>\n       </i>\n       .\n      </p>\n      <h2>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Threat_to_marine_animals">\n        Threat to marine animals\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Waterspout&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Threat to marine animals">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h2>\n      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">\n       See also:\n       <a href="/wiki/Rain_of_animals#Fish" title="Rain of animals">\n        Rain of animals \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa0Fish\n       </a>\n      </div>\n      <p>\n       Depending on how fast the winds from a waterspout are whipping, anything that is within about one yard of the surface of the water, including fish of different sizes, frogs, and even turtles, can be lifted into the air.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cosier_29-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-Cosier-29">\n         [29]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       A waterspout can sometimes suck small animals such as fish out of the water and all the way up into the cloud. Even if the waterspout stops spinning, the fish in the cloud can be carried over land, buffeted up and down and around with the cloud\xe2\x80\x99s winds until its currents no longer keep the flying fish in the atmosphere. Depending on how far they travel and how high they are taken into the atmosphere, the fish are sometimes dead by the time they rain down. People as far as 100 miles (160\xc2\xa0km) inland have experienced raining fish.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cosier_29-1">\n        <a href="#cite_note-Cosier-29">\n         [29]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       Fish can also be sucked up from rivers, but\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Raining_fish" title="Raining fish">\n        raining fish\n       </a>\n       is not a common weather phenomenon.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cosier_29-2">\n        <a href="#cite_note-Cosier-29">\n         [29]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       On September 28, 2017, according to the\n       <a href="/wiki/Associated_Press" title="Associated Press">\n        Associated Press\n       </a>\n       , rain in\n       <a href="/wiki/Tampico" title="Tampico">\n        Tampico\n       </a>\n       , on northeastern\n       <a href="/wiki/Mexico" title="Mexico">\n        Mexico\n       </a>\n       \'s\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gulf_coast" title="Gulf coast">\n        Gulf coast\n       </a>\n       , included fish.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30">\n        <a href="#cite_note-30">\n         [30]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <h2>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Research_and_forecasting">\n        Research and forecasting\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Waterspout&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Research and forecasting">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h2>\n      <h3>\n       <span id="Szilagyi_Waterspout_Index_(SWI)">\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Szilagyi_Waterspout_Index_.28SWI.29">\n        Szilagyi Waterspout Index (SWI)\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Waterspout&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Szilagyi Waterspout Index (SWI)">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h3>\n      <p>\n       The Szilagyi Waterspout Index (SWI), developed by Canadian meteorologist Wade Szilagyi, is used to predict conditions favorable for waterspout development. The SWI ranges from \xe2\x88\x9210 to +10, where values greater than or equal to zero represent conditions favorable for waterspout development.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31">\n        <a href="#cite_note-31">\n         [31]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32">\n        <a href="#cite_note-32">\n         [32]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <h3>\n       <span id="International_Centre_for_Waterspout_Research_(ICWR)">\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="International_Centre_for_Waterspout_Research_.28ICWR.29">\n        International Centre for Waterspout Research (ICWR)\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Waterspout&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: International Centre for Waterspout Research (ICWR)">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h3>\n      <p>\n       The ICWR is a non governmental organization of individuals from around the world who are interested in the field of waterspouts from a research, operational and safety perspective.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33">\n        <a href="#cite_note-33">\n         [33]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       Originally a forum for researchers and meteorologists, the ICWR has expanded interest and contribution from storm chasers, the media, the marine and aviation communities and from private individuals.\n      </p>\n      <h2>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">\n        See
