A cloudburst can suddenly dump large amounts of water e.g. 25\xc2\xa0mm of precipitation corresponds to 25000 metric tons/km\n       <sup>\n        2\n       </sup>\n       (1 inch corresponds to 72,300\xc2\xa0short tons over one square mile). However, cloudbursts are infrequent as they occur only via\n       <a href="/wiki/Orographic_lift" title="Orographic lift">\n        orographic lift\n       </a>\n       or occasionally when a warm air parcel mixes with cooler air, resulting in sudden\n       <a href="/wiki/Condensation" title="Condensation">\n        condensation\n       </a>\n       . At times, a large amount of runoff from higher elevations is mistakenly conflated with a cloudburst. The term "cloudburst" arose from the notion that clouds were akin to water balloons and could burst, resulting in rapid precipitation. Though this idea has since been disproven, the term remains in use.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n      </p>\n      <div class="toc" id="toc">\n       <div class="toctitle">\n        <h2>\n </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Cloudburst&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Properties">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h2>\n      <p>\n       Rainfall rate equal to or greater than 100 millimetres (3.9\xc2\xa0in) per hour is a cloudburst.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-The_Saakal_Times_2-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-The_Saakal_Times-2">\n         [2]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">\n        <a href="#cite_note-3">\n         [3]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       However, different definitions are used, e.g. the Swedish weather service SMHI defines the corresponding Swedish term "skyfall" as 1\xc2\xa0mm/min for short bursts and 50\xc2\xa0mm/h for longer rainfalls. The associated convective\n       <a href="/wiki/Cloud" title="Cloud">\n        cloud\n       </a>\n       can extend up to a height of 15 kilometres (9.3\xc2\xa0mi) above the ground.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">\n        <a href="#cite_note-4">\n         [4]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       During a cloudburst, more than 20 millimetres (0.79\xc2\xa0in) of rain may fall in a few minutes. The results of cloudbursts can be disastrous. Cloudbursts are also responsible for\n       <a href="/wiki/Flash_flood" title="Flash flood">\n        flash flood\n       </a>\n       creation.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       Rapid precipitation from\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cumulonimbus" title="Cumulonimbus">\n        cumulonimbus\n       </a>\n       clouds is possible due to the\n       <a href="/wiki/Langmuir_circulation" title="Langmuir circulation">\n        Langmuir precipitation process\n       </a>\n       in which large droplets can grow rapidly by coagulating with smaller droplets which fall down slowly. It is not essential that cloudbursts occur only when a cloud clashes with a solid body like a mountain. They can also occur when hot water vapor mingles into the cold resulting in sudden condensation.\n      </p>\n      <h2>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Record_cloudbursts">\n        Record
