id="bodyContent">\n    <div class="noprint" id="siteSub">\n     From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia\n    </div>\n    <div id="contentSub">\n    </div>\n    <div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">\n     Jump to:\n     <a href="#mw-head">\n      navigation\n     </a>\n     ,\n     <a href="#p-search">\n      search\n     </a>\n    </div>\n    <div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en">\n     <div class="mw-parser-output">\n      <p>\n       In\n       <a href="/wiki/Physics" title="Physics">\n        physics\n       </a>\n       ,\n       <a href="/wiki/Engineering" title="Engineering">\n        engineering\n       </a>\n       , and\n       <a href="/wiki/Earth_science" title="Earth science">\n        earth sciences\n       </a>\n       ,\n       <b>\n        advection\n       </b>\n       is the\n       <a href="/wiki/Transport" title="Transport">\n        transport\n       </a>\n       of a substance by bulk motion. The properties of that substance are carried with it. Generally the majority of the advected substance is a fluid. The properties that are carried with the advected substance are\n       <a href="/wiki/Conservation_of_energy" title="Conservation of energy">\n        conserved\n       </a>\n       properties such as\n       <a href="/wiki/Energy" title="Energy">\n        energy\n       </a>\n       . An example of advection is the transport of\n       <a href="/wiki/Pollutant" title="Pollutant">\n        pollutants\n       </a>\n       or\n       <a href="/wiki/Silt" title="Silt">\n        silt\n       </a>\n       in a\n       <a href="/wiki/River" title="River">\n        river\n       </a>\n       by bulk water flow downstream. Another commonly advected quantity is energy or\n       <a href="/wiki/Enthalpy" title="Enthalpy">\n        enthalpy\n       </a>\n       . Here the fluid may be any material that contains thermal energy, such as\n       <a href="/wiki/Water" title="Water">\n        water\n       </a>\n       or\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Air" title="Air">\n        air\n       </a>\n       . In general, any substance or conserved,\n       <a href="/wiki/Intensive_and_extensive_properties" title="Intensive and extensive properties">\n        extensive\n       </a>\n       quantity can be advected by a\n       <a href="/wiki/Fluid" title="Fluid">\n        fluid\n       </a>\n       that can hold or contain the quantity or substance.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       During advection, a fluid transports some conserved quantity or material via bulk motion. The fluid\'s motion is described\n       <a href="/wiki/Mathematics" title="Mathematics">\n        mathematically\n       </a>\n       as a\n       <a href="/wiki/Vector_field" title="Vector field">\n        vector field\n       </a>\n       , and the transported material is described by a\n       <a href="/wiki/Scalar_field" title="Scalar field">\n        scalar field\n       </a>\n       showing its distribution over space. Advection requires currents in the fluid, and so cannot happen in rigid solids. It does not include transport of substances by\n       <a href="/wiki/Molecular_diffusion" title="Molecular diffusion">\n        molecular diffusion\n       </a>\n       .\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       Advection is sometimes confused with the more encompassing process of\n       <a href="/wiki/Convection" title="Convection">\n        convection\n       </a>\n       which is the combination of advective transport and diffusive transport.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       In\n       <a href="/wiki/Meteorology" title="Meteorology">\n        meteorology\n       </a>\n       and\n       <a href="/wiki/Physical_oceanography" title="Physical oceanography">\n        physical oceanography\n       </a>\n       , advection often refers to the transport of some property of the atmosphere or\n       <a href="/wiki/Ocean" title="Ocean">\n        ocean\n       </a>\n       , such as\n       <a href="/wiki/Heat" title="Heat">\n        heat\n       </a>\n       , humidity (see\n       <a href="/wiki/Water_vapor" title="Water vapor">\n        moisture\n       </a>\n       ) or\n       <a href="/wiki/Salinity" title="Salinity">\n        salinity\n       </a>\n       . Advection is important for the formation of\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Orographic" title="Orographic">\n        orographic\n       </a>\n       clouds and the precipitation of water from clouds, as part of the\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydrological_cycle" title="Hydrological cycle">\n        hydrological cycle\n       </a>\n       .\n      </p>\n      <p>\n      <span class="toctext">\n           References\n          </span>\n         </a>\n        </li>\n       </ul>\n      </div>\n      <p>\n      </p>\n      <h2>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Distinction_between_advection_and_convection">\n        Distinction between advection and convection\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Advection&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Distinction between advection and convection">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h2>\n      <p>\n       The term\n       <i>\n        advection\n       </i>\n       sometimes serves as a synonym for\n       <i>\n        <a href="/wiki/Convection" title="Convection">\n         convection\n        </a>\n       </i>\n       , but technically,\n       <i>\n        convection\n       </i>\n       covers the sum of transport both by\n       <a href="/wiki/Diffusion" title="Diffusion">\n        diffusion\n       </a>\n       and by advection. Advective transport describes the movement of some quantity via the bulk flow of a fluid (as in a river or pipeline).\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">\n        <a href="#cite_note-1">\n         [1]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">\n        <a href="#cite_note-2">\n         [2]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <h2>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Meteorology">\n        Meteorology\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Advection&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Meteorology">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h2>\n      <p>\n       In\n       <a href="/wiki/Meteorology" title="Meteorology">\n        meteorology\n       </a>\n       and\n       <a href="/wiki/Physical_oceanography" title="Physical oceanography">\n        physical oceanography\n       </a>\n       , advection often refers to the horizontal transport of some property of the atmosphere or\n       <a href="/wiki/Ocean" title="Ocean">\n        ocean\n       </a>\n       , such as\n       <a href="/wiki/Heat" title="Heat">\n        heat\n       </a>\n       , humidity or salinity, and convection generally refers to vertical transport (vertical advection). Advection is important for the formation of\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Orographic_cloud" title="Orographic cloud">\n        orographic clouds\n       </a>\n       (terrain-forced convection) and the precipitation of water from clouds, as part of the\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydrological_cycle" title="Hydrological cycle">\n        hydrological cycle\n       </a>\n       .\n      </p>\n      <h2>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Other_quantities">\n        Other quantities\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Advection&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Other quantities">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h2>\n      <p>\n       The advection equation also applies if the quantity being advected is represented by a\n       <a href="/wiki/Probability_density_function" title="Probability density function">\n        probability density function\n       </a>\n       at each point, although accounting for diffusion is more difficult.\n       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">\n        [\n        <i>\n         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">\n          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2013)">\n           citation needed\n          </span>\n         </a>\n        </i>\n        ]\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <h2>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Mathematics_of_advection">\n        Mathematics of advection\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Advection&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Mathematics of advection">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h2>\n      <p>\n       The\n       <b>\n        advection equation\n       </b>\n       is the\n       <a href="/wiki/Partial_differential_equation" title="Partial differential equation">\n        partial differential equation\n       </a>\n       that governs the motion of a conserved\n       <a href="/wiki/Scalar_field" title="Scalar field">\n        scalar field\n       </a>\n       as it is advected by a known\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Velocity_field" title="Velocity field">\n        velocity vector field\n       </a>\n       . It is derived using the scalar field\'s\n       <a href="/wiki/Conservation_law" title="Conservation law">\n        conservation law\n       </a>\n       , together with\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gauss%27s_theorem" title="Gauss\'s theorem">\n        Gauss\'s theorem\n       </a>\n       , and taking the\n       <a href="/wiki/Infinitesimal" title="Infinitesimal">\n        infinitesimal\n       </a>\n       limit.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       One easily visualized example of advection is the transport of ink dumped into a river. As the river flows, ink will move downstream in a "pulse" via advection, as the water\'s movement itself transports the ink. If added to a lake without significant bulk water flow, the ink would simply disperse outwards from its source in a\n       <a href="/wiki/Diffusion" title="Diffusion">\n        diffusive\n       </a>\n       manner, which is not advection. Note that as it moves downstream, the "pulse" of ink will also spread via diffusion.
