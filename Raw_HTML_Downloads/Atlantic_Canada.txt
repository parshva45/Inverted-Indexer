<p>\n      </p>\n      <h2>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="History">\n        History\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Atlantic_Canada&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h2>\n      <p>\n       The first\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Premier_of_Newfoundland" title="Premier of Newfoundland">\n        Premier of Newfoundland\n       </a>\n       ,\n       <a href="/wiki/Joey_Smallwood" title="Joey Smallwood">\n        Joey Smallwood\n       </a>\n       , coined the term "Atlantic Canada" when\n       <a href="/wiki/Dominion_of_Newfoundland" title="Dominion of Newfoundland">\n        Newfoundland\n       </a>\n       joined the\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dominion_of_Canada" title="Dominion of Canada">\n        Dominion of Canada\n       </a>\n       in 1949. He believed that it would have been presumptuous for Newfoundland to assume that it could include itself within the existing term "\n       <a href="/wiki/The_Maritimes" title="The Maritimes">\n        Maritime Provinces\n       </a>\n       ", used to describe the cultural similarities shared by\n       <a href="/wiki/New_Brunswick" title="New Brunswick">\n        New Brunswick\n       </a>\n       ,\n       <a href="/wiki/Prince_Edward_Island" title="Prince Edward Island">\n        Prince Edward Island\n       </a>\n       , and\n       <a href="/wiki/Nova_Scotia" title="Nova Scotia">\n        Nova Scotia\n       </a>\n       . The three Maritime provinces entered into confederation during the 19th century: New Brunswick and Nova Scotia were founding members of the Dominion of Canada in 1867, and Prince Edward Island joined in 1873. Today Atlantic Canada is a culturally distinct region of Canada, with the original founding cultures of Celtic, English and French remaining strong and vibrant to this day.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n
