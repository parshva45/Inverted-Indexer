A\n       <b>\n        South Pacific tropical cyclone\n       </b>\n       is a non\n       <a href="/wiki/Weather_front" title="Weather front">\n        frontal\n       </a>\n       ,\n       <a href="/wiki/Low-pressure_area" title="Low-pressure area">\n        low pressure system\n       </a>\n       that has developed, within an environment of warm\n       <a href="/wiki/Sea_surface_temperature" title="Sea surface temperature">\n        sea surface temperatures\n       </a>\n       and little vertical\n       <a href="/wiki/Wind_shear" title="Wind shear">\n        wind shear\n       </a>\n       aloft in the South\n       <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Ocean" title="Pacific Ocean">\n        Pacific Ocean\n       </a>\n       .\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SPAC_TCOP_1-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-SPAC_TCOP-1">\n         [1]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       Within the Southern Hemisphere there are officially three areas where\n       <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone" title="Tropical cyclone">\n        tropical cyclones\n       </a>\n       develop on a regular basis, these areas are the South-West Indian Ocean between Africa and 90\xc2\xb0E, the Australian region between 90\xc2\xb0E and 160\xc2\xb0E and the South Pacific basin between 160\xc2\xb0E and 120\xc2\xb0W. The South Pacific basin between 160\xc2\xb0E and 120\xc2\xb0W is officially monitored by the\n       <a href="/wiki/Fiji_Meteorological_Service" title="Fiji Meteorological Service">\n        Fiji Meteorological Service\n       </a>\n       and New Zealand\'s\n       <a href="/wiki/MetService" title="MetService">\n        MetService\n       </a>\n       , while others like the Australian\n       <a href="/wiki/Bureau_of_Meteorology" title="Bureau of Meteorology">\n        Bureau of Meteorology\n       </a>\n       and the United States\n       <a href="/wiki/National_Oceanic_and_Atmospheric_Administration" title="National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration">\n        National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration\n       </a>\n       also monitor the basin. Each tropical cyclone year within this basin starts on July 1 and runs throughout the year, encompassing the tropical cyclone season which runs from November 1 and lasts until April 30 each season. Within the basin, most tropical cyclones have their origins within the\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/South_Pacific_Convergence_Zone" title="South Pacific Convergence Zone">\n        South Pacific Convergence Zone\n       </a>\n       or within the Northern Australian monsoon trough, both of which form an extensive area of cloudiness and are dominant features of the season. Within this region a tropical disturbance is classified as a tropical cyclone, when it has 10-minute sustained wind speeds of more than 65\xc2\xa0km/h (35\xc2\xa0mph), that wrap halfway around the low level circulation centre, while a severe tropical cyclone is classified when the maximum 10-minute sustained wind speeds are greater than 120\xc2\xa0km/h (75\xc2\xa0mph).\n      </p>\n      <p>\n     <span class="toctext">\n           External links\n          </span>\n         </a>\n        </li>\n       </ul>\n      </div>\n      <p>\n      </p>\n      <h2>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Basin_history">\n        Basin history\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=South_Pacific_tropical_cyclone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Basin history">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h2>\n      <p>\n       The ancient mariners of the South Seas who roamed the tropical Pacific before the arrival of the Europeans, knew of and feared the hurricanes of the South Pacific.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NZ_TC_39-69_2-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-NZ_TC_39-69-2">\n         [2]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       They were keen and accurate observers of nature with traditional myths and legends, reflecting their knowledge of these systems.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NZ_TC_39-69_2-1">\n        <a href="#cite_note-NZ_TC_39-69-2">\n         [2]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       On his voyages around the Pacific, it is thought that Captain\n       <a href="/wiki/James_Cook" title="James Cook">\n        James Cook\n       </a>\n       did not collect any information about cyclones in the Pacific.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NZ_TC_39-69_2-2">\n        <a href="#cite_note-NZ_TC_39-69-2">\n         [2]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       The European whalers, traders and missionaries that followed Cook soon realised that the South Pacific was not free of hurricanes, and were the first to publish accounts about the systems.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NZ_TC_39-69_2-3">\n        <a href="#cite_note-NZ_TC_39-69-2">\n         [2]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       During 1853, Thomas Dobson subsequently became the first person to collect information about these systems, in order to attempt to understand and explain the characteristics of 24 tropical cyclones.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NZ_TC_39-69_2-4">\n        <a href="#cite_note-NZ_TC_39-69-2">\n         [2]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       However, these descriptions were vague and of little value, because he only had a small amount of data and no synoptic weather charts.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NZ_TC_39-69_2-5">\n        <a href="#cite_note-NZ_TC_39-69-2">\n         [2]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       Over the next 40 years various reports, journals and log books on the storms were published, before E Knipping consolidated these reports and extended Dobson\'s list out to 120 tropical cyclones during 1893.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NZ_TC_39-69_2-6">\n        <a href="#cite_note-NZ_TC_39-69-2">\n         [2]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       During the 1920s\n       <a href="/wiki/Stephen_Sargent_Visher" title="Stephen Sargent Visher">\n        Stephen Sargent Visher\n       </a>\n       did some research into tropical cyclones in the Pacific and visited several island nations; including\n       <a href="/wiki/Fiji" title="Fiji">\n        Fiji\n       </a>\n       ,\n       <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">\n        Japan\n       </a>\n       and the\n       <a href="/wiki/Philippines" title="Philippines">\n        Philippines\n       </a>\n       to obtain information on potential systems.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Visher_3-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-Visher-3">\n         [3]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       He also consulted various journals and reports as well as Dobson\'s and Knipping\'s work, before he authored a number of papers on tropical cyclones in the Pacific.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Visher_3-1">\n        <a href="#cite_note-Visher-3">\n         [3]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       These papers contained information about 259 tropical storms in the South Pacific between 160\xc2\xb0E and 140\xc2\xb0W, two of which occurred during 1789 and 1819, while the rest occurred between 1830 and 1923.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NZ_TC_39-69_2-7">\n        <a href="#cite_note-NZ_TC_39-69-2">\n         [2]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       Visher also tried to estimate how many systems were occurring on an annual basis in each area, but overcompensated for his incomplete records and came up with a figure of 12 severe tropical cyclones per year.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NZ_TC_39-69_2-8">\n        <a href="#cite_note-NZ_TC_39-69-2">\n         [2]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Visher_3-2">\n        <a href="#cite_note-Visher-3">\n         [3]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       During Visher\'s time and until the start of\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/World_War_Two" title="World War Two">\n        World War Two\n       </a>\n       , there was insufficient information available to allow for an accurate deception of tropical cyclone tracks.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NZ_TC_39-69_2-9">\n        <a href="#cite_note-NZ_TC_39-69-2">\n         [2]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       However, in the buildup to and during\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/World_War_Two" title="World War Two">\n        World War Two\n       </a>\n       , meteorological operations in the Pacific were greatly expanded, to meet the needs of international aviation and military operations.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NZ_TC_39-69_2-10">\n        <a href="#cite_note-NZ_TC_39-69-2">\n         [2]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hutchings_1953_4-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-Hutchings_1953-4">\n         [4]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       As a result, J W Hutchings decided to write a paper on 43 tropical cyclones between 1940 and 1951, using data that had been collected from the\n       <a href="/wiki/Tropics" title="Tropics">\n        tropics\n       </a>\n       by the\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_Zealand_Meteorological_Service" title="New Zealand Meteorological Service">\n        New Zealand Meteorological Service\n       </a>\n       in the area between the 150\xc2\xb0E and 150\xc2\xb0W.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hutchings_1953_4-1">\n        <a href="#cite_note-Hutchings_1953-4">\n         [4]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       In the paper, systems were only included if they had a wind speed on the\n       <a href="/wiki/Beaufort_scale" title="Beaufort scale">\n        Beaufort scale\n       </a>\n       of Force 9 or above, while located between the Equator and 30\xc2\xb0S.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hutchings_1953_4-2">\n        <a href="#cite_note-Hutchings_1953-4">\n         [4]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       Hutchings also examined where tropical cyclones originated from in the South Pacific and claimed that the place where most tropical cyclones develop could be accurately determined.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NZ_TC_39-69_2-11">\n        <a href="#cite_note-NZ_TC_39-69-2">\n         [2]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hutchings_1953_4-3">\n        <a href="#cite_note-Hutchings_1953-4">\n         [4]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       The paper also drew attention to a marked difference in the tracks of South Pacific tropical cyclones and systems in other basins.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hutchings_1953_4-4">\n        <a href="#cite_note-Hutchings_1953-4">\n         [4]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       This work was subsequently extended in 1956, by the then director of the New Zealand Meteorological Service:\n       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=John_Fletcher_Gabites&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="John Fletcher Gabites (page does not exist)">\n        John Fletcher Gabites\n       </a>\n       , to cover the seasons between 1952\xe2\x80\x9353 and 1955\xe2\x80\x9356.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gabites_5-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-Gabites-5">\n         [5]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       Gabites subsequently wrote a series of papers during 1963 on various aspects of South Pacific tropical cyclones including on the wide variety of tracks that occur over the Pacific.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NZ_TC_39-69_2-12">\n        <a href="#cite_note-NZ_TC_39-69-2">\n         [2]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <h2>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Warning_centers">\n        Warning centers\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=South_Pacific_tropical_cyclone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Warning centers">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h2>\n      <p>\n       During June 1995, the Fiji Meteorological Service\'s Nadi\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x94 Tropical Cyclone Centre, was designated as a Regional Specialized Meteorological Center by the World Meteorological Organization.\n      </p>\n      <h2>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Seasons">\n
