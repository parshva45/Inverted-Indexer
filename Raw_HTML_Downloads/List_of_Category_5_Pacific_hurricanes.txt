Category\xc2\xa05 Pacific hurricanes east of the\n         <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/International_Dateline" title="International Dateline">\n          International Dateline\n         </a>\n         .\n        </div>\n       </div>\n      </div>\n      <p>\n       Category\xc2\xa05 hurricanes are\n       <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone" title="Tropical cyclone">\n        tropical cyclones\n       </a>\n       that reach Category\xc2\xa05 intensity on the\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saffir%E2%80%93Simpson_Hurricane_Scale" title="Saffir\xe2\x80\x93Simpson Hurricane Scale">\n        Saffir\xe2\x80\x93Simpson Hurricane Scale\n       </a>\n       . They are by definition the strongest hurricanes that can form on planet Earth. They are rare in the eastern\n       <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Ocean" title="Pacific Ocean">\n        Pacific Ocean\n       </a>\n       and generally form only once every several years. In general, Category\xc2\xa05s form in clusters in single years. Landfalls by such storms are rare due to the generally westerly path of\n       <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone" title="Tropical cyclone">\n        tropical cyclones\n       </a>\n       in the\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Northern_hemisphere" title="Northern hemisphere">\n        northern hemisphere\n       </a>\n       .\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       The term "hurricane" is used for tropical cyclones in the Pacific Ocean, north of the\n       <a href="/wiki/Equator" title="Equator">\n        equator\n       </a>\n       and east of the\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/International_date_line" title="International date line">\n        international date line\n       </a>\n       . A Category\xc2\xa05 Pacific hurricane is therefore a tropical cyclone in the north Pacific Ocean that reached Category\xc2\xa05 intensity east of the\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/International_dateline" title="International dateline">\n        international dateline\n       </a>\n       . Identical phenomena in the north Pacific Ocean west of the dateline are called "typhoons" or "super typhoons". Category\xc2\xa05 super typhoons generally happen several times per season, so cyclones of that intensity are not exceptional for that region. This difference in terminology therefore excludes storms such as\n       <a href="/wiki/Typhoon_Paka" title="Typhoon Paka">\n        Super Typhoon Paka\n       </a>\n       and\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Typhoon_Oliwa_(1997)" title="Typhoon Oliwa (1997)">\n        Super Typhoon Oliwa\n       </a>\n       of\n       <a href="/wiki/1997_Pacific_typhoon_season" title="1997 Pacific typhoon season">\n        1997\n       </a>\n       , and\n       <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Genevieve_(2014)" title="Hurricane Genevieve (2014)">\n        Super Typhoon Genevieve\n       </a>\n       of\n       <a href="/wiki/2014_Pacific_hurricane_season" title="2014 Pacific hurricane season">\n        2014\n       </a>\n       , which formed east of the dateline but did not reach Category\xc2\xa05 intensity until after crossing the dateline.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n      </p>\n      <div class="toc" id="toc">\n       <div class="toctitle">\n        <h2>\n         Contents\n <p>\n       A Category\xc2\xa05 hurricane is defined as having sustained windspeeds of at least 137\n       <a href="/wiki/Knot_(unit)" title="Knot (unit)">\n        knots\n       </a>\n       (158\xc2\xa0mph; 254\xc2\xa0km/h) over a one-minute period 10 metres (33\xc2\xa0ft) above the ground.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SSHS_1-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-SSHS-1">\n         [1]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SustainedWinds_2-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-SustainedWinds-2">\n         [2]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       As a tropical cyclone is moving, its wind field is asymmetric. In the northern hemisphere, the strongest winds are on the right side of the storm (relative to the direction of motion). The highest winds given in advisories are those from the right side.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Semicircles_3-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-Semicircles-3">\n         [3]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       Since the\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/1950-1969_Pacific_hurricane_seasons#1959_Season" title="1950-1969 Pacific hurricane seasons">\n        1959\n       </a>\n       season, only 15 hurricanes are known to have reached Category\xc2\xa05 intensity. There are no known Category\xc2\xa05 storms occurring before 1959. It is possible that some earlier storms reached Category\xc2\xa05 over open waters, but they were never recognized because they never affected land and remained at sea.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-best_track_4-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-best_track-4">\n         [4]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <h2>\n       <span class="mw-headline"     <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h2>\n      <div class="thumb tright">\n       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">\n        <a   <p>\n       This lists all of the Category\xc2\xa05 hurricanes in the order in which they formed. Only 1994\'s Hurricane Emilia and 2006\'s Hurricane Ioke have reached Category\xc2\xa05 intensity more than once; that is, by weakening into a Category\xc2\xa04 or weaker storm and later re-strengthening to a Category\xc2\xa05 storm.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       Before the advent of reliable geostationary\n       <a href="/wiki/Weather_satellite" title="Weather satellite">\n        satellite\n       </a>\n       coverage in 1966, the number of eastern Pacific tropical cyclones was significantly underestimated.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SatelliteCoverage_5-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-SatelliteCoverage-5">\n         [5]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       It is therefore very possible that there are additional Category\xc2\xa05 hurricanes other than those listed, but they were not reported and therefore not recognized. However, the lack of Pacific Category\xc2\xa05 hurricanes during the late 1970s, 1980s, and early 1990s, is certain.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-best_track_4-1">\n        <a href="#cite_note-best_track-4">\n         [4]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       The minimum\n       <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_pressure" title="Atmospheric pressure">\n        central pressure\n       </a>\n       of these storms is, for the most part, estimated from satellite imagery using the\n       <a href="/wiki/Dvorak_technique" title="Dvorak technique">\n        Dvorak technique\n       </a>\n       . In the case of Kenna,\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kenna_6-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-Kenna-6">\n         [6]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       Ava,\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AvaRecon_7-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-AvaRecon-7">\n         [7]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       and Patricia, the central pressure was measured by\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hurricane_hunter" title="Hurricane hunter">\n        hurricane hunter\n       </a>\n       aircraft flying into the storm.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-best_track_4-2">\n        <a href="#cite_note-best_track-4">\n         [4]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       Because of the estimation of central pressures, it is possible that other storms more intense than these have formed.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Guillermo_8-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-Guillermo-8">\n         [8]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       The reason for estimating the pressure (in lieu of direct measurements) is the fact that most of these storms did not threaten land.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Recon_9-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-Recon-9">\n         [9]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       As Kenna and Patricia were threatening land, their pressures were measured by\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hurricane_Hunters" title="Hurricane Hunters">\n        Hurricane Hunters\n       </a>\n       using\n       <a href="/wiki/Dropsonde" title="Dropsonde">\n        dropsondes\n       </a>\n       .\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kenna_6-1">\n        <a href="#cite_note-Kenna-6">\n         [6]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       While Hurricane Ava never threatened land,\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-best_track_4-3">\n        <a href="#cite_note-best_track-4">\n         [4]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       it too was flown into by Hurricane Hunters to test equipment and conduct research.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AvaRecon_7-1">\n        <a href="#cite_note-AvaRecon-7">\n         [7]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       Older storms have incomplete pressure readings, since there were no satellite-based estimates; the only observations were taken by ships, land-based observations, or reconnaissance aircraft when available. Ava\'s minimum known pressure was measured when it was a Category\xc2\xa04 hurricane, for example.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-best_track_4-4">\n        <a href="#cite_note-best_track-4">\n         [4]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       John and Gilma have incomplete pressures because the\n       <a href="/wiki/Central_Pacific_Hurricane_Center" title="Central Pacific Hurricane Center">\n        Central Pacific Hurricane Center\n       </a>\n       , in general, did not publish pressure on systems in the central Pacific (140\xc2\xb0W to the dateline) at the time.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dalilia_10-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-Dalilia-10">\n         [10]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       However, it should be noted that this list is not identical to the list of most intense Pacific hurricanes. The most intense known Category\xc2\xa04 storm in the eastern Pacific was\n       <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Odile" title="Hurricane Odile">\n        2014\'s Odile\n       </a>\n       . The lowest pressure of this storm was 918 millibars, lower than that of some Category 5\'s, such as Guillermo.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-best_track_4-5">\n        <a href="#cite_note-best_track-4">\n         [4]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       Hurricanes have reached Category\xc2\xa05 intensity during every month from June to October. The earliest Category\xc2\xa05 to form in a season is\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hurricane_Ava_(1973)" title="Hurricane Ava (1973)">\n        1973\'s Hurricane Ava\n       </a>\n       , which formed on June\xc2\xa07. The latest Category\xc2\xa05 to form in a season is\n       <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Kenna" title="Hurricane Kenna">\n        Hurricane Kenna\n       </a>\n       , which reached peak intensity on October 24. Hurricanes Ava,\n       <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Gilma_(1994)" title="Hurricane Gilma (1994)">\n        Gilma\n       </a>\n       ,\n       <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Ioke" title="Hurricane Ioke">\n        Ioke\n       </a>\n       ,\n       <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Linda_(1997)" title="Hurricane Linda (1997)">\n        Linda\n       </a>\n       , and\n       <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Patricia" title="Hurricane Patricia">\n        Patricia\n       </a>\n       are the most intense storms to form in their respective months. There have been no May, November, or off-season Category\xc2\xa05 hurricanes.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-best_track_4-6">\n        <a href="#cite_note-best_track-4">\n         [4]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       Two Pacific hurricanes are known to have reached Category\xc2\xa05 intensity multiple times:\n       <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Emilia_(1994)" title="Hurricane Emilia (1994)">\n        Emilia\n       </a>\n       and\n       <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Ioke" title="Hurricane Ioke">\n        Ioke\n       </a>\n       . Both did it twice, and Ioke reached Category\xc2\xa05 status a third time as a typhoon while in the western Pacific.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-best_track_4-7">\n        <a href="#cite_note-best_track-4">\n         [4]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       Hurricane Ioke was tied for the longest-lasting Category\xc2\xa05 hurricane recorded, spending 42\xc2\xa0hours at that strength,\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ioke_11-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-Ioke-11">\n         [11]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       while hurricanes John and Linda had the longest time spent\n       <i>\n        consecutively\n       </i>\n       at that intensity.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-best_track_4-8">\n        <a href="#cite_note-best_track-4">\n         [4]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <center>\n       <div class="thumb tright">\n        <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">\n </div>\n      <h2>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Climatology">\n        Climatology\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=List_of_Category_5_Pacific_hurricanes&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Climatology">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h2>\n      <div class="thumb tright">\n       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">\n        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:HurricaneGuillermo1997.JPG">\n         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="550" data-file-width="800" height="151" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/08/HurricaneGuillermo1997.JPG/220px-HurricaneGuillermo1997.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/08/HurricaneGuillermo1997.JPG/330px-HurricaneGuillermo1997.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/08/HurricaneGuillermo1997.JPG/440px-HurricaneGuillermo1997.JPG 2x" width="220"/>\n        </a>\n        <div class="thumbcaption">\n         <div class="magnify">\n          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:HurricaneGuillermo1997.JPG" title="Enlarge">\n          </a>\n         </div>\n         Hurricane Guillermo, the first of two Category\xc2\xa05s during the 1997 season.\n        </div>\n       </div>\n      </div>\n      <p>\n       In the eastern Pacific, Category\xc2\xa05 hurricanes usually occur only in\n       <a href="/wiki/El_Ni%C3%B1o" title="El Ni\xc3\xb1o">\n        El Ni\xc3\xb1o\n       </a>\n       years. During El Ni\xc3\xb1o years, conditions are more favorable for tropical cyclones because of warmer\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sea_surface_temperatures" title="Sea surface temperatures">\n        sea surface temperatures\n       </a>\n       and reduced\n       <a href="/wiki/Wind_shear" title="Wind shear">\n        wind shear\n       </a>\n       . This is why Category\xc2\xa05s cluster in single seasons. The effects of El Ni\xc3\xb1o are most significant in the central Pacific (140\xc2\xb0W to the dateline).\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ElNino1_13-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-ElNino1-13">\n         [13]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       The general lack of Category\xc2\xa05s in non warm-ENSO years is because of there being limited space for development. The prevailing\n       <a href="/wiki/Ocean_current" title="Ocean current">\n        ocean currents\n       </a>\n       of the area carry warm water to the west. As there is no large piece of land to block the water and cause it to "pile up" like in the Atlantic, the area suitable for all tropical cyclones is small. Farther out to sea, while waters are still warm, wind shear limits the development of tropical cyclones in the waters south of Hawaii. This makes an otherwise ideal region unfavorable for tropical cyclones.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       This does not mean that a Category\xc2\xa05 hurricane cannot form outside of an El Ni\xc3\xb1o event. The entire year of 1959 was neither an El Ni\xc3\xb1o or a La Ni\xc3\xb1a, but it had one Category\xc2\xa05 hurricane (Patsy). Most of 1973 was during a La Ni\xc3\xb1a, which reduces tropical cyclone activity in the eastern Pacific, yet Hurricane Ava, a Category 5 hurricane, formed in June of that year.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ElNino2_14-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-ElNino2-14">\n         [14]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       The 2009, 2010, 2014, and 2015 seasons are the only pairs of seasons in which a Category 5 hurricane formed in consecutive years (Rick and Celia, and Marie and Patricia, respectively).\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EPAC_HURDAT_15-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-EPAC_HURDAT-15">\n         [15]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <div style="clear:right;">\n      </div>\n      <h2>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Landfalls">\n        Landfalls\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=List_of_Category_5_Pacific_hurricanes&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Landfalls">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h2>\n      <div class="thumb tleft">\n       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">\n        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:HurricaneKenna2002.jpg">\n         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="8000" data-file-width="8000" height="220" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c4/HurricaneKenna2002.jpg/220px-HurricaneKenna2002.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c4/HurricaneKenna2002.jpg/330px-HurricaneKenna2002.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c4/HurricaneKenna2002.jpg/440px-HurricaneKenna2002.jpg 2x" width="220"/>\n        </a>\n        <div class="thumbcaption">\n         <div class="magnify">\n          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:HurricaneKenna2002.jpg" title="Enlarge">\n          </a>\n         </div>\n         <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Kenna" title="Hurricane Kenna">\n          Hurricane Kenna\n         </a>\n         , one of only three Category\xc2\xa05 Pacific hurricanes to make landfall at any intensity.\n        </div>\n       </div>\n      </div>\n      <p>\n       Of all of the Category\xc2\xa05 Pacific hurricanes, the only ones to make landfall at any intensity were\n       <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Kenna" title="Hurricane Kenna">\n        Hurricane Kenna\n       </a>\n       ,\n       <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Rick_(2009)" title="Hurricane Rick (2009)">\n        Hurricane Rick\n       </a>\n       , and\n       <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Patricia" title="Hurricane Patricia">\n        Hurricane Patricia\n       </a>\n       . None made landfall as Category\xc2\xa05 hurricanes; Patricia and Kenna had weakened to Category\xc2\xa04 at the time of their landfalls, and Rick was a tropical storm at its landfall. Patricia was the strongest landfall among Pacific hurricanes;\n       <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Madeline_(1976)" title="Hurricane Madeline (1976)">\n        1976\'s Hurricane Madeline\n       </a>\n       and\n       <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Iniki" title="Hurricane Iniki">\n        1992\'s Hurricane Iniki\n       </a>\n       are tied as the second strongest storms at landfall, both of which didn\'t reach Category\xc2\xa05 strength, but made landfall as stronger Category 4 storms than Kenna.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kenna_6-2">\n        <a href="#cite_note-Kenna-6">\n         [6]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       Originally, a\n       <a href="/wiki/1959_Mexico_hurricane" title="1959 Mexico hurricane">\n        hurricane in 1959\n       </a>\n       was thought to have struck Manzanillo at Category\xc2\xa05 intensity, but a reanalysis in 2016 indicated the storm had peaked as a Category\xc2\xa04 hurricane, and made landfall with the same winds as Kenna.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">\n        <a href="#cite_note-16">\n         [16]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       Patricia was also originally thought to have made landfall as a Category\xc2\xa05 hurricane near Manzanillo before post-season analysis in 2016.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       In addition to these three systems, Hurricanes\n       <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_John_(1994)" title="Hurricane John (1994)">\n        John\n       </a>\n       ,\n       <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Linda_(1997)" title="Hurricane Linda (1997)">\n        Linda\n       </a>\n       , and\n       <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Ioke" title="Hurricane Ioke">\n        Ioke\n       </a>\n       all threatened land for a while. John and Ioke had minimal impact on\n       <a href="/wiki/Johnston_Atoll" title="Johnston Atoll">\n        Johnston Atoll\n       </a>\n       and John caused heavy surf in\n       <a href="/wiki/Hawaii" title="Hawaii">\n        Hawaii\n       </a>\n       .\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EmiliaJohn_12-1">\n        <a href="#cite_note-EmiliaJohn-12">\n         [12]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       Linda was briefly forecast to\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_California_tropical_storms" title="List of California tropical storms">\n        approach\n       </a>\n       <a href="/wiki/Southern_California" title="Southern California">\n        southern California\n       </a>\n       , and it passed close to\n       <a href="/wiki/Socorro_Island" title="Socorro Island">\n        Socorro Island\n       </a>\n       near peak intensity.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ioke_11-1">\n        <a href="#cite_note-Ioke-11">\n         [11]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Linda_17-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-Linda-17">\n         [17]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       The reason for the lack of landfalls is that tropical cyclones in the northern hemisphere usually travel to the west.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-G8_18-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-G8-18">\n         [18]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       In the Atlantic, this sends hurricanes towards North America. In the eastern Pacific, this sends tropical cyclones out into the open ocean to dissipate over waters too cool to support them or in environments with high wind shear. Hawaii, the only heavily populated island chain in the eastern Pacific, is protected from most hurricanes by a\n       <a href="/wiki/Subtropical_ridge" title="Subtropical ridge">\n        subtropical ridge\n       </a>\n       and is small enough to avoid being hit simply due to low odds.\n      </p>\n      <div style="clear:both;">\n      </div>\n      <h2>\n       <span class="mw-headline"
