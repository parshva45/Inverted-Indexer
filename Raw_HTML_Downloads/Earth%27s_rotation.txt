  Earth\'s rotation\n       </b>\n       is the\n       <a href="/wiki/Rotation" title="Rotation">\n        rotation\n       </a>\n       of\n       <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">\n        Planet Earth\n       </a>\n       around its own\n       <a href="/wiki/Axial_tilt" title="Axial tilt">\n        axis\n       </a>\n       . Earth rotates\n       <a href="/wiki/East" title="East">\n        eastward\n       </a>\n       , in\n       <a href="/wiki/Retrograde_and_prograde_motion" title="Retrograde and prograde motion">\n        prograde motion\n       </a>\n       . As viewed from the north\n       <a href="/wiki/Pole_star" title="Pole star">\n        pole star\n       </a>\n       <a href="/wiki/Polaris" title="Polaris">\n        Polaris\n       </a>\n       , Earth turns counter\n       <a href="/wiki/Clockwise" title="Clockwise">\n        clockwise\n       </a>\n       .\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       The\n       <a href="/wiki/North_Pole" title="North Pole">\n        North Pole\n       </a>\n       , also known as the Geographic North Pole or Terrestrial North Pole, is the point in the Northern Hemisphere where Earth\'s axis of rotation meets its surface. This point is distinct from Earth\'s\n       <a href="/wiki/North_Magnetic_Pole" title="North Magnetic Pole">\n        North Magnetic Pole\n       </a>\n       . The\n       <a href="/wiki/South_Pole" title="South Pole">\n        South Pole\n       </a>\n       is the other point where Earth\'s axis of rotation intersects its surface, in\n       <a href="/wiki/Antarctica" title="Antarctica">\n        Antarctica\n       </a>\n       .\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       Earth rotates once in about 24 hours with respect to the sun and once every 23 hours, 56 minutes and 4 seconds with respect to the stars (\n       <a href="#Stellar_and_sidereal_day">\n        see below\n       </a>\n       ). Earth\'s rotation is slowing slightly with time; thus, a day was shorter in the past. This is due to the\n       <a href="/wiki/Tidal_acceleration" title="Tidal acceleration">\n        tidal effects\n       </a>\n       the\n       <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">\n        Moon\n       </a>\n       has on Earth\'s rotation.\n       <a href="/wiki/Atomic_clock" title="Atomic clock">\n        Atomic clocks\n       </a>\n       show that a modern-day is longer by about 1.7\n       <a href="/wiki/Millisecond" title="Millisecond">\n        milliseconds\n       </a>\n       than a century ago,\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-McCarthySeidelmann2009_1-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-McCarthySeidelmann2009-1">\n         [1]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       slowly increasing the rate at which\n       <a href="/wiki/Coordinated_Universal_Time" title="Coordinated Universal Time">\n        UTC\n       </a>\n       is adjusted by\n       <a href="/wiki/Leap_second" title="Leap second">\n        leap seconds\n       </a>\n       . Analysis of historical astronomical records shows a slowing trend of 2.3 milliseconds per century since the 8th century BCE.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">\n        <a href="#cite_note-2">\n         [2]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n      </p>\n      <div class="toc" id="toc">\n       <div class="toctitle">\n        <h2>\n         Contents\n id="History">\n        History\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Earth%27s_rotation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h2>\n      <p>\n       Among the ancient Greeks, several of the\n       <a href="/wiki/Pythagoreanism" title="Pythagoreanism">\n        Pythagorean\n       </a>\n       school believed in the rotation of the earth rather than the apparent diurnal rotation of the heavens. Perhaps the first was\n       <a href="/wiki/Philolaus" title="Philolaus">\n        Philolaus\n       </a>\n       (470\xe2\x80\x93385 BCE), though his system was complicated, including a\n       <a href="/wiki/Counter-Earth" title="Counter-Earth">\n        counter-earth\n       </a>\n       rotating daily about a central fire.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">\n        <a href="#cite_note-3">\n         [3]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       A more conventional picture was that supported by\n       <a href="/wiki/Hicetas" title="Hicetas">\n        Hicetas\n       </a>\n       ,\n       <a href="/wiki/Heraclides_Ponticus" title="Heraclides Ponticus">\n        Heraclides\n       </a>\n       and\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ecphantus" title="Ecphantus">\n        Ecphantus\n       </a>\n       in the fourth century BCE who assumed that the earth rotated but did not suggest that the earth revolved about the sun. In the third century BCE,\n       <a href="/wiki/Aristarchus_of_Samos" title="Aristarchus of Samos">\n        Aristarchus of Samos\n       </a>\n       suggested the\n       <a href="/wiki/Heliocentrism" title="Heliocentrism">\n        sun\'s central place\n       </a>\n       .\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       However,\n       <a href="/wiki/Aristotle" title="Aristotle">\n        Aristotle\n       </a>\n       in the fourth century BCE criticized the ideas of Philolaus as being based on theory rather than observation. He established the idea of a sphere of fixed stars that rotated about the earth.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">\n        <a href="#cite_note-4">\n         [4]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       This was accepted by most of those who came after, in particular\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Claudius_Ptolemy" title="Claudius Ptolemy">\n        Claudius Ptolemy\n       </a>\n       (2nd century CE), who thought the earth would be devastated by gales if it rotated.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">\n        <a href="#cite_note-5">\n         [5]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       In 499 CE, the\n       <a href="/wiki/Indian_astronomy" title="Indian astronomy">\n        Indian astronomer\n       </a>\n       <a href="/wiki/Aryabhata" title="Aryabhata">\n        Aryabhata\n       </a>\n       wrote that the spherical earth rotates about its axis daily, and that the apparent movement of the stars is a relative motion caused by the rotation of the Earth. He provided the following analogy: "Just as a man in a boat going in one direction sees the stationary things on the bank as moving in the opposite direction, in the same way to a man at\n       <a href="/wiki/Lanka" title="Lanka">\n        Lanka\n       </a>\n       the \xef\xac\x81xed stars appear to be going westward."\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">\n        <a href="#cite_note-6">\n         [6]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">\n        <a href="#cite_note-7">\n         [7]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       In the 10th century, some\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Astronomy_in_medieval_Islam" title="Astronomy in medieval Islam">\n        Muslim astronomers\n       </a>\n       accepted that the Earth rotates around its axis.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">\n        <a href="#cite_note-8">\n         [8]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       According to\n       <a href="/wiki/Al-Biruni" title="Al-Biruni">\n        al-Biruni\n       </a>\n       ,\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Abu_Sa%27id_al-Sijzi" title="Abu Sa\'id al-Sijzi">\n        Abu Sa\'id al-Sijzi\n       </a>\n       (d. circa 1020) invented an\n       <a href="/wiki/Astrolabe" title="Astrolabe">\n        astrolabe\n       </a>\n       called\n       <i>\n        al-z\xc5\xabraq\xc4\xab\n       </i>\n       based on the idea believed by some of his contemporaries "that the motion we see is due to the Earth\'s movement and not to that of the sky."\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-young_9-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-young-9">\n         [9]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">\n        <a href="#cite_note-10">\n         [10]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       The prevalence of this view is further confirmed by a reference from the 13th century which states: "According to the geometers [or engineers] (\n       <i>\n        muhandis\xc4\xabn\n       </i>\n       ), the earth is in constant circular motion, and what appears to be the motion of the heavens is actually due to the motion of the earth and not the stars."\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-young_9-1">\n        <a href="#cite_note-young-9">\n         [9]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       Treatises were written to discuss its possibility, either as refutations or expressing doubts about Ptolemy\'s arguments against it.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">\n        <a href="#cite_note-11">\n         [11]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       At the\n       <a href="/wiki/Maragheh_observatory" title="Maragheh observatory">\n        Maragha\n       </a>\n       and\n       <a href="/wiki/Ulugh_Beg_Observatory" title="Ulugh Beg Observatory">\n        Samarkand observatories\n       </a>\n       , the Earth\'s rotation was discussed by\n       <a href="/wiki/Nasir_al-Din_al-Tusi" title="Nasir al-Din al-Tusi">\n        Tusi\n       </a>\n       (b. 1201) and\n       <a href="/wiki/Ali_Qushji" title="Ali Qushji">\n        Qushji\n       </a>\n       (b. 1403); the arguments and evidence they used resemble those used by Copernicus.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ragep_12-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-Ragep-12">\n         [12]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       In medieval Europe,\n       <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Aquinas" title="Thomas Aquinas">\n        Thomas Aquinas\n       </a>\n       accepted Aristotle\'s view\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">\n        <a href="#cite_note-13">\n         [13]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       and so, reluctantly, did\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/John_Buridan" title="John Buridan">\n        John Buridan\n       </a>\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">\n        <a href="#cite_note-14">\n         [14]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       and\n       <a href="/wiki/Nicole_Oresme" title="Nicole Oresme">\n        Nicole Oresme\n       </a>\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">\n        <a href="#cite_note-15">\n         [15]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       in the fourteenth century. Not until\n       <a href="/wiki/Nicolaus_Copernicus" title="Nicolaus Copernicus">\n        Nicolaus Copernicus\n       </a>\n       in 1543 adopted a\n       <a href="/wiki/Heliocentrism" title="Heliocentrism">\n        heliocentric\n       </a>\n       world system did the contemporary understanding of earth\'s rotation begin to be established. Copernicus pointed out that if the movement of the earth is violent, then the movement of the stars must be very much more so. He acknowledged the contribution of the Pythagoreans and pointed to examples of relative motion. For Copernicus this was the first step in establishing the simpler pattern of planets circling a central sun.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">\n        <a href="#cite_note-16">\n         [16]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       <a href="/wiki/Tycho_Brahe" title="Tycho Brahe">\n        Tycho Brahe\n       </a>\n       , who produced accurate observations on which\n       <a href="/wiki/Johannes_Kepler" title="Johannes Kepler">\n        Kepler\n       </a>\n       based his\n       <a href="/wiki/Kepler%27s_laws_of_planetary_motion" title="Kepler\'s laws of planetary motion">\n        laws\n       </a>\n       , used Copernicus\'s work as the basis of a\n       <a href="/wiki/Tychonic_system" title="Tychonic system">\n        system\n       </a>\n       assuming a stationary earth. In 1600,\n       <a href="/wiki/William_Gilbert_(astronomer)" title="William Gilbert (astronomer)">\n        William Gilbert\n       </a>\n       strongly supported the earth\'s rotation in his treatise on the earth\'s magnetism\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17">\n        <a href="#cite_note-17">\n         [17]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       and thereby influenced many of his contemporaries.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18">\n        <a href="#cite_note-18">\n         [18]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       Those like Gilbert who did not openly support or reject the motion of the earth about the sun are often called "semi-Copernicans".\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19">\n        <a href="#cite_note-19">\n         [19]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       A century after Copernicus,\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Riccioli" title="Riccioli">\n        Riccioli\n       </a>\n       disputed the model of a rotating earth due to the lack of then-observable eastward deflections in falling bodies;\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20">\n        <a href="#cite_note-20">\n         [20]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       such deflections would later be called the\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coriolis_effect" title="Coriolis effect">\n        Coriolis effect\n       </a>\n       . However, the contributions of Kepler,\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Galileo" title="Galileo">\n        Galileo\n       </a>\n       and\n       <a href="/wiki/Isaac_Newton" title="Isaac Newton">\n        Newton\n       </a>\n       gathered support for the theory of the rotation of the Earth.\n      </p>\n      <h3>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Empirical_tests">\n        Empirical tests\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Earth%27s_rotation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Empirical tests">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h3>\n      <p>\n       The earth\'s rotation implies that the equator bulges and the poles are flattened. In his\n       <i>\n        <a href="/wiki/Philosophi%C3%A6_Naturalis_Principia_Mathematica" title="Philosophi\xc3\xa6 Naturalis Principia Mathematica">\n         Principia\n        </a>\n       </i>\n       , Newton predicted this flattening would occur in the ratio of 1:230, and pointed to the 1673\n       <a href="/wiki/Pendulum" title="Pendulum">\n        pendulum\n       </a>\n       measurements by\n       <a href="/wiki/Jean_Richer" title="Jean Richer">\n        Richer\n       </a>\n       as corroboration of the change in gravity,\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21">\n        <a href="#cite_note-21">\n         [21]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       but\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/History_of_Geodesy#Europe" title="History of Geodesy">\n        initial measurements\n       </a>\n       of\n       <a href="/wiki/Meridian_arc" title="Meridian arc">\n        meridian lengths\n       </a>\n       by\n       <a href="/wiki/Jean_Picard" title="Jean Picard">\n        Picard\n       </a>\n       and\n       <a href="/wiki/Jacques_Cassini" title="Jacques Cassini">\n        Cassini\n       </a>\n       at the end of the 17th century suggested the opposite. However measurements by\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Maupertuis" title="Maupertuis">\n        Maupertuis\n       </a>\n       and the\n       <a href="/wiki/French_Geodesic_Mission" title="French Geodesic Mission">\n        French Geodesic Mission\n       </a>\n       in the 1730s established the\n       <a href="/wiki/Figure_of_the_Earth" title="Figure of the Earth">\n        flattening\n       </a>\n       , thus confirming both Newton and the Copernican position.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22">\n        <a href="#cite_note-22">\n         [22]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       In the Earth\'s rotating frame of reference, a freely moving body follows an apparent path that deviates from the one it would follow in a fixed frame of reference. Because of the\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coriolis_effect" title="Coriolis effect">\n        Coriolis effect\n       </a>\n       , falling bodies veer slightly eastward from the vertical plumb line below their point of release, and projectiles veer right in the northern hemisphere (and left in the southern) from the direction in which they are shot. The Coriolis effect is mainly observable at a meteorological scale, where it is responsible for the differing rotation direction of\n       <a href="/wiki/Cyclone" title="Cyclone">\n        cyclones\n       </a>\n       in the northern and southern hemispheres.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       Hooke, following a 1679 suggestion from Newton, tried unsuccessfully to verify the predicted eastward deviation of a body dropped from a height of\n       <span class="nowrap">\n        8.2 meters\n       </span>\n       , but definitive results were only obtained later, in the late 18th and early 19th century, by\n       <a href="/wiki/Giovanni_Battista_Guglielmini" title="Giovanni Battista Guglielmini">\n        Giovanni Battista Guglielmini\n       </a>\n       in\n       <a href="/wiki/Bologna" title="Bologna">\n        Bologna\n       </a>\n       ,\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Johann_Friedrich_Benzenberg" title="Johann Friedrich Benzenberg">\n        Johann Friedrich Benzenberg\n       </a>\n       in\n       <a href="/wiki/Hamburg" title="Hamburg">\n        Hamburg\n       </a>\n       and\n       <a href="/wiki/Ferdinand_Reich" title="Ferdinand Reich">\n        Ferdinand Reich\n       </a>\n       in\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Freiberg,_Saxony" title="Freiberg, Saxony">\n        Freiberg\n       </a>\n       , using taller towers and carefully released weights.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23">\n        <a href="#cite_note-23">\n         [n 1]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       A ball dropped from a height of 158.5\xc2\xa0m (520\xc2\xa0ft) departed by 27.4\xc2\xa0mm (1.08\xc2\xa0in) from the vertical compared with a calculated value of 28.1\xc2\xa0mm (1.11\xc2\xa0in).\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       The most celebrated test of Earth\'s rotation is the\n       <a href="/wiki/Foucault_pendulum" title="Foucault pendulum">\n        Foucault pendulum\n       </a>\n       first built by physicist\n       <a href="/wiki/L%C3%A9on_Foucault" title="L\xc3\xa9on Foucault">\n        L\xc3\xa9on Foucault\n       </a>\n       in 1851, which consisted of a lead-filled brass sphere suspended\n       <span class="nowrap">\n        67 m\n       </span>\n       from the top of the\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Panth%C3%A9on_(Paris)" title="Panth\xc3\xa9on (Paris)">\n        Panth\xc3\xa9on\n       </a>\n       in Paris. Because of the Earth\'s rotation under the swinging pendulum, the pendulum\'s plane of oscillation appears to rotate at a rate depending on latitude. At the latitude of Paris the predicted and observed shift was about\n       <span class="nowrap">\n        11 degrees\n       </span>\n       <a href="/wiki/Clockwise" title="Clockwise">\n        clockwise\n       </a>\n       per hour. Foucault pendulums now swing in\n       <a href="/wiki/List_of_Foucault_pendulums" title="List of Foucault pendulums">\n        museums around the world\n       </a>\n       .\n      </p>\n </div>\n      </div>\n      <h3>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="True_solar_day">\n        True solar day\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Earth%27s_rotation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: True solar day">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h3>\n      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">\n       Main article:\n       <a href="/wiki/Solar_time" title="Solar time">\n        Solar time\n       </a>\n      </div>\n      <p>\n       Earth\'s\n       <a href="/wiki/Rotation_period" title="Rotation period">\n        rotation period\n       </a>\n       relative to the Sun (\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solar_noon" title="Solar noon">\n        solar noon\n       </a>\n       to solar noon) is its\n       <i>\n        true solar day\n       </i>\n       or\n       <i>\n        apparent solar day\n       </i>\n       . It depends on the Earth\'s orbital motion and is thus affected by changes in the\n       <a href="/wiki/Orbital_eccentricity" title="Orbital eccentricity">\n        eccentricity\n       </a>\n       and\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Inclination" title="Inclination">\n        inclination\n       </a>\n       of Earth\'s orbit. Both vary over thousands of years, so the annual variation of the true solar day also varies. Generally, it is longer than the mean solar day during two periods of the year and shorter during another two.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26">\n        <a href="#cite_note-26">\n         [n 2]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       The true solar day tends to be longer near\n       <a href="/wiki/Apsis" title="Apsis">\n        perihelion\n       </a>\n       when the Sun apparently moves along the\n       <a href="/wiki/Ecliptic" title="Ecliptic">\n        ecliptic\n       </a>\n       through a greater angle than usual, taking about\n       <span class="nowrap">\n        10 seconds\n       </span>\n       longer to do so. Conversely, it is about\n       <span class="nowrap">\n        10 seconds\n       </span>\n       shorter near\n       <a href="/wiki/Apsis" title="Apsis">\n        aphelion\n       </a>\n       . It is about\n       <span class="nowrap">\n        20 seconds\n       </span>\n       longer near a\n       <a href="/wiki/Solstice" title="Solstice">\n        solstice\n       </a>\n       when the projection of the Sun\'s apparent motion along the ecliptic onto the\n       <a href="/wiki/Celestial_equator" title="Celestial equator">\n        celestial equator\n       </a>\n       causes the Sun to move through a greater angle than usual. Conversely, near an\n       <a href="/wiki/Equinox" title="Equinox">\n        equinox\n       </a>\n       the projection onto the equator is shorter by about\n       <span class="nowrap">\n        20 seconds\n       </span>\n       . Currently, the perihelion and solstice effects combine to lengthen the true solar day near\n       <span class="nowrap">\n        22 December\n       </span>\n       by\n       <span class="nowrap">\n        30 mean\n       </span>\n       solar seconds, but the solstice effect is partially cancelled by the aphelion effect near\n       <span class="nowrap">\n        19 June\n       </span>\n       when it is only\n       <span class="nowrap">\n        13 seconds\n       </span>\n       longer. The effects of the equinoxes shorten it near\n       <span class="nowrap">\n        26 March\n       </span>\n       and\n       <span class="nowrap">\n        16 September\n       </span>\n       by\n       <span class="nowrap">\n        18 seconds\n       </span>\n       and\n       <span class="nowrap">\n        21 seconds\n       </span>\n       , respectively.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Meeus_25-1">\n        <a href="#cite_note-Meeus-25">\n         [24]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27">\n        <a href="#cite_note-27">\n         [25]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28">\n        <a href="#cite_note-28">\n         [26]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <h3>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Mean_solar_day">\n        Mean solar day\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Earth%27s_rotation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Mean solar day">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h3>\n      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">\n       Main article:\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mean_solar_day" title="Mean solar day">\n        Mean solar day\n       </a>\n      </div>\n      <p>\n       The average of the true solar day during the course of an entire year is the\n       <i>\n        mean solar day\n       </i>\n       , which contains\n       <span class="nowrap">\n        86,400 mean\n       </span>\n       solar seconds. Currently, each of these seconds is slightly longer than an\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SI" title="SI">\n        SI\n       </a>\n       second because Earth\'s mean solar day is now slightly longer than it was during the 19th century due to\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tidal_friction" title="Tidal friction">\n        tidal friction\n       </a>\n       . The average length of the mean solar day since the introduction of the leap second in 1972 has been about 0 to 2 ms longer than 86,400 SI seconds.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29">\n        <a href="#cite_note-29">\n         [27]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30">\n        <a href="#cite_note-30">\n         [28]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31">\n        <a href="#cite_note-31">\n         [29]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       Random fluctuations due to core-mantle coupling have an amplitude of about 5 ms.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32">\n        <a href="#cite_note-32">\n         [30]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33">\n        <a href="#cite_note-33">\n         [31]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       The mean solar second between 1750 and 1892 was chosen in 1895 by\n       <a href="/wiki/Simon_Newcomb" title="Simon Newcomb">\n        Simon Newcomb\n       </a>\n       as the independent unit of time in his\n       <a href="/wiki/Newcomb%27s_Tables_of_the_Sun" title="Newcomb\'s Tables of the Sun">\n        Tables of the Sun\n       </a>\n       . These tables were used to calculate the world\'s\n       <a href="/wiki/Ephemeris" title="Ephemeris">\n        ephemerides\n       </a>\n       between 1900 and 1983, so this second became known as the\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ephemeris_second" title="Ephemeris second">\n        ephemeris second\n       </a>\n       . In 1967 the SI second was made equal to the ephemeris second.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34">\n        <a href="#cite_note-34">\n         [32]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       The\n       <a href="/wiki/Solar_time#Apparent_solar_time" title="Solar time">\n        apparent solar time\n       </a>\n       is a measure of the Earth\'s rotation and the difference between it and the mean solar time is known as the\n       <a href="/wiki/Equation_of_time" title="Equation of time">\n        equation of time\n       </a>\n       .\n      </p>\n <div class="magnify">\n          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Sidereal_day_(prograde).png" title="Enlarge">\n          </a>\n         </div>\n         On a\n         <a class="mw-redirect" relative to the\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fixed_star" title="Fixed star">\n        fixed stars\n       </a>\n       , called its\n       <i>\n        stellar day\n       </i>\n       by the\n       <a href="/wiki/International_Earth_Rotation_and_Reference_Systems_Service" title="International Earth Rotation and Reference Systems Service">\n        International Earth Rotation and Reference Systems Service\n       </a>\n       (IERS), is\n       <span class="nowrap">\n        86,164.098 903 691\n       </span>\n       seconds of mean solar time (UT1)\n       <span class="nowrap">\n        (23\n        <sup>\n         <small>\n          h\n         </small>\n        </sup>\n        56\n        <sup>\n         <small>\n          m\n         </small>\n     </span>\n       mean solar days).\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IERS_35-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-IERS-35">\n         [33]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37">\n        <a href="#cite_note-37">\n         [n 3]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       Earth\'s rotation period relative to the\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Precession_(astronomy)" title="Precession (astronomy)">\n        precessing\n       </a>\n       or moving mean vernal equinox, named\n       <i>\n        <a href="/wiki/Sidereal_time" title="Sidereal time">\n         sidereal day\n        </a>\n       </i>\n       , is\n       <span class="nowrap">\n        86,164.090 530 832 88\n       </span>\n       seconds of mean solar time (UT1)\n       <span class="nowrap">\n        (23\n        <sup>\n         <small>\n          h\n         </small>\n        </sup>\n        56\n        <sup>\n         <small>\n          m\n         </small>\n        </sup>\n        4.090 530 832 88\n        <sup>\n         <small>\n          s\n         </small>\n        </sup>\n       </span>\n       ,\n       <span class="nowrap">\n </sup>\n       Thus the sidereal day is shorter than the stellar day by about\n       <span class="nowrap">\n        8.4 ms\n       </span>\n       .\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38">\n        <a href="#cite_note-38">\n         [35]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       Both the stellar day and the sidereal day are shorter than the mean solar day by about\n       <span class="nowrap">\n        3 minutes\n       </span>\n       <span class="nowrap">\n        56 seconds\n       </span>\n       . The mean solar day in SI seconds is available from the IERS for the periods\n       <span class="nowrap">\n        1623\xe2\x80\x932005\n       </span>\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39">\n        <a href="#cite_note-39">\n         [36]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       and\n       <span class="nowrap">\n        1962\xe2\x80\x932005\n       </span>\n       .\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40">\n        <a href="#cite_note-40">\n         [37]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       Recently (1999\xe2\x80\x932010) the average annual length of the mean solar day in excess of 86,400 SI seconds has varied between\n       <span class="nowrap">\n        0.25 ms\n       </span>\n       and\n       <span class="nowrap">\n        1 ms\n       </span>\n       , which must be added to both the stellar and sidereal days given in mean solar time above to obtain their lengths in SI seconds (see\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fluctuations_in_the_length_of_day" title="Fluctuations in the length of day">\n        Fluctuations in the length of day\n       </a>\n       ).\n      </p>\n      <h3>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Angular_speed">\n        Angular speed\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Earth%27s_rotation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Angular speed">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h3>\n      <div class="thumb tright">\n       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">\n        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Earth_rotation_tangential_speed.svg">\n         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="384" data-file-width="512" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/db/Earth_rotation_tangential_speed.svg/220px-Earth_rotation_tangential_speed.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/db/Earth_rotation_tangential_speed.svg/330px-Earth_rotation_tangential_speed.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/db/Earth_rotation_tangential_speed.svg/440px-Earth_rotation_tangential_speed.svg.png 2x" width="220"/>\n        </a>\n        <div class="thumbcaption">\n         <div class="magnify">\n          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Earth_rotation_tangential_speed.svg" title="Enlarge">\n          </a>\n         </div>\n         Plot of latitude vs tangential speed. The dashed line shows that the\n         <a href="/wiki/Kennedy_Space_Center" title="Kennedy Space Center">\n          Kennedy Space Center\n         </a>\n         example. The dot-dash line denotes typical airliner\n         <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cruise_speed" title="Cruise speed">\n          cruise speed\n         </a>\n         .\n        </div>\n       </div>\n      </div>\n      <p>\n       The angular speed of Earth\'s rotation in inertial space is\n       <span class="nowrap">\n        (7.2921150 \xc2\xb1 0.0000001)\n        <span style="margin:0 .15em 0 .25em">\n         \xc3\x97\n        </span>\n        10\n        <sup>\n         \xe2\x88\x925\n        </sup>\n       </span>\n       <a href="/wiki/Radian" title="Radian">\n        radians\n       </a>\n       per SI second (mean solar second).\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IERS_35-2">\n        <a href="#cite_note-IERS-35">\n         [33]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       Multiplying by (180\xc2\xb0/\xcf\x80 radians)\xc3\x97(86,400 seconds/mean solar day) yields 360.9856\xc2\xb0/mean solar day, indicating that Earth rotates more than 360\xc2\xb0 relative to the fixed stars in one solar day. Earth\'s movement along its nearly circular orbit while it is rotating once around its axis requires that Earth rotate slightly more than once relative to the fixed stars before the mean Sun can pass overhead again, even though it rotates only once (360\xc2\xb0) relative to the mean Sun.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41">\n        <a href="#cite_note-41">\n         [n 4]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       Multiplying the value in rad/s by Earth\'s equatorial radius of\n       <span class="nowrap">\n        6,378,137 m\n       </span>\n       (\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/WGS84" title="WGS84">\n        WGS84\n       </a>\n       ellipsoid) (factors of 2\xcf\x80 radians needed by both cancel) yields an\n       <b>\n        equatorial speed of 465.1\xc2\xa0m/s (1,526\xc2\xa0ft/s)\n       </b>\n       , or 1,674.4\xc2\xa0km/h (1,040.4\xc2\xa0mph).\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42">\n        <a href="#cite_note-42">\n         [38]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       Some sources state that Earth\'s equatorial speed is slightly less, or\n       <span class="nowrap">\n        1,669.8 km/h\n       </span>\n       .\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43">\n        <a href="#cite_note-43">\n         [39]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       This is obtained by dividing Earth\'s equatorial circumference by\n       <span class="nowrap">\n        24 hours\n       </span>\n       . However, the use of only one circumference unwittingly implies only one rotation in inertial space, so the corresponding time unit must be a sidereal hour. This is confirmed by multiplying by the number of sidereal days in one mean solar day,\n       <span class="nowrap">\n        1.002 737 909 350 795\n       </span>\n       ,\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IERS_35-3">\n        <a href="#cite_note-IERS-35">\n         [33]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       which yields the equatorial speed in mean solar hours given above of\n       <span class="nowrap">\n        1,674.4 km/h\n       </span>\n       .\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       The tangential speed of Earth\'s rotation at a point on Earth can be approximated by multiplying the speed at the equator by the cosine of the latitude.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44">\n        <a href="#cite_note-44">\n         [40]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       For example, the\n       <a href="/wiki/Kennedy_Space_Center" title="Kennedy Space Center">\n        Kennedy Space Center\n       </a>\n       is located at latitude 28.59\xc2\xb0 N, which yields a speed of: cos 28.59\xc2\xb0 \xc3\x97 1,674.4\xc2\xa0km/h (1,040.4\xc2\xa0mph; 465.1\xc2\xa0m/s) = 1,470.23\xc2\xa0km/h (913.56\xc2\xa0mph; 408.40\xc2\xa0m/s)\n      </p>\n      <h2>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Changes_in_rotation">\n        Changes in rotation\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Earth%27s_rotation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Changes in rotation">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h2>\n      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">\n       Main articles:\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fluctuations_in_the_length_of_day" title="Fluctuations in the length of day">\n        Fluctuations in the length of day\n       </a>\n       and\n       <a href="/wiki/%CE%94T" title="\xce\x94T">\n        \xce\x94T\n       </a>\n      </div>\n      <div class="thumb tright">\n       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">\n        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:AxialTiltObliquity.png">\n         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="590" data-file-width="760" height="171" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/61/AxialTiltObliquity.png/220px-AxialTiltObliquity.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/61/AxialTiltObliquity.png/330px-AxialTiltObliquity.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/61/AxialTiltObliquity.png/440px-AxialTiltObliquity.png 2x" width="220"/>\n        </a>\n        <div class="thumbcaption">\n         <div class="magnify">\n          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:AxialTiltObliquity.png" title="Enlarge">\n          </a>\n         </div>\n         Earth\'s\n         <a href="/wiki/Axial_tilt" title="Axial tilt">\n          axial tilt\n         </a>\n         is about 23.4\xc2\xb0. It oscillates between 22.1\xc2\xb0 and 24.5\xc2\xb0 on a 41,000-year cycle and is currently decreasing.\n        </div>\n       </div>\n      </div>\n      <h3>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Change_in_rotational_axis">\n        Change in rotational axis\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Earth%27s_rotation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Change in rotational axis">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h3>\n      <p>\n       The Earth\'s rotation axis moves with respect to the fixed stars (\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Inertial_space" title="Inertial space">\n        inertial space\n       </a>\n       ); the components of this motion are\n       <a href="/wiki/Precession" title="Precession">\n        precession\n       </a>\n       and\n       <a href="/wiki/Nutation" title="Nutation">\n        nutation\n       </a>\n       . It also moves with respect to the Earth\'s crust; this is called\n       <a href="/wiki/Polar_motion" title="Polar motion">\n        polar motion\n       </a>\n       .\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       Precession is a rotation of the Earth\'s rotation axis, caused primarily by external torques from the gravity of the\n       <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">\n        Sun\n       </a>\n       , Moon and other bodies. The polar motion is primarily due to\n       <a href="/wiki/Nutation" title="Nutation">\n        free core nutation\n       </a>\n       and the\n       <a href="/wiki/Chandler_wobble" title="Chandler wobble">\n        Chandler wobble\n       </a>\n       .\n      </p>\n      <h3>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Change_in_rotational_velocity">\n        Change in rotational velocity\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Earth%27s_rotation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Change in rotational velocity">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h3>\n      <h4>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Tidal_interactions">\n        Tidal interactions\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Earth%27s_rotation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Tidal interactions">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h4>\n      <p>\n       Over millions of years, the Earth\'s rotation slowed significantly by\n       <a href="/wiki/Tidal_acceleration" title="Tidal acceleration">\n        tidal acceleration\n       </a>\n       through gravitational interactions with the Moon. In this process,\n       <a href="/wiki/Angular_momentum" title="Angular momentum">\n        angular momentum\n       </a>\n       is slowly transferred to the Moon at a rate proportional to\n       <span class="mwe-math-element">\n        <span class="mwe-math-mathml-inline mwe-math-mathml-a11y" style="display: none;">\n         <math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">\n          <semantics>\n           <mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">\n            <mstyle displaystyle="true" scriptlevel="0">\n             <msup>\n              <mi>\n               r\n              </mi>\n              <mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">\n               <mo>\n                \xe2\x88\x92\n                <!-- \xe2\x88\x92 -->\n               </mo>\n               <mn>\n                6\n               </mn>\n              </mrow>\n             </msup>\n            </mstyle>\n           </mrow>\n           <annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\n            {\\displaystyle r^{-6}}\n           </annotation>\n          </semantics>\n         </math>\n        </span>\n        <img alt="r^{{-6}}" aria-hidden="true" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline" src="https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5562d12471ec1ca2053ab569f0e5c1451f315d62" style="vertical-align: -0.338ex; width:3.407ex; height:2.676ex;"/>\n       </span>\n       , where\n       <span class="mwe-math-element">\n        <span class="mwe-math-mathml-inline mwe-math-mathml-a11y" style="display: none;">\n         <math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">\n          <semantics>\n           <mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">\n            <mstyle displaystyle="true" scriptlevel="0">\n             <mi>\n              r\n             </mi>\n            </mstyle>\n           </mrow>\n           <annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\n            {\\displaystyle r}\n           </annotation>\n          </semantics>\n         </math>\n        </span>\n        <img alt="r" aria-hidden="true" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline" src="https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0d1ecb613aa2984f0576f70f86650b7c2a132538" style="vertical-align: -0.338ex; width:1.059ex; height:1.676ex;"/>\n       </span>\n       is the orbital radius of the Moon. This process gradually increased the length of day to its current value and resulted in the Moon being\n       <a href="/wiki/Tidal_locking" title="Tidal locking">\n        tidally locked\n       </a>\n       with the Earth.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       This gradual rotational deceleration is empirically documented with estimates of day lengths obtained from observations of\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tidal_rhythmites" title="Tidal rhythmites">\n        tidal rhythmites\n       </a>\n       and\n       <a href="/wiki/Stromatolite" title="Stromatolite">\n        stromatolites\n       </a>\n       ; a compilation of these measurements\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_45-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-:0-45">\n         [41]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       found the length of day to increase steadily from about 21 hours at 600Myr ago\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Zahnle_46-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-Zahnle-46">\n         [42]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       to the current 24 hour value. By counting the microscopic lamina that form at higher tides, tidal frequencies (and thus day lengths) can be estimated, much like counting tree rings, though these estimates can be increasingly unreliable at older ages.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47">\n        <a href="#cite_note-47">\n         [43]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <h4>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Resonant_stabilization">\n        Resonant stabilization\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Earth%27s_rotation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Resonant stabilization">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h4>\n      <p>\n       The current rate of tidal deceleration is anomalously high, implying the Earth\'s rotational velocity must have decreased more slowly in the past. Empirical data\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_45-1">\n        <a href="#cite_note-:0-45">\n         [41]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       tentatively shows a sharp increase in rotational deceleration about 600Myr ago. Some models suggest that the Earth maintained a constant day length of 21 hours throughout much of the Precambrian.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Zahnle_46-1">\n        <a href="#cite_note-Zahnle-46">\n         [42]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       This day length corresponds to the semidiurnal\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Resonance_frequency" title="Resonance frequency">\n        resonant period\n       </a>\n       of the thermally-driven\n       <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_tide" title="Atmospheric tide">\n        atmospheric tide\n       </a>\n       ; at this day length, the decelerative lunar torque could have been canceled by an accelerative torque from the atmospheric tide, resulting in no net torque and a constant rotational period. This stabilizing effect could have been broken by a sudden change in global temperature. Recent computational simulations support this hypothesis and suggest the\n       <a href="/wiki/Marinoan_glaciation" title="Marinoan glaciation">\n        Marinoan\n       </a>\n       or\n       <a href="/wiki/Sturtian_glaciation" title="Sturtian glaciation">\n        Sturtian glaciations\n       </a>\n       broke this stable configuration about 600Myr ago, citing the resemblance of simulated results and existing paleorotational data.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48">\n        <a href="#cite_note-48">\n         [44]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <div class="thumb tright">\n       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">\n        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Deviation_of_day_length_from_SI_day.svg">\n         <img alt=""   Deviation of day length from SI based day\n        </div>\n       </div>\n      </div>\n      <h4>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Global_events">\n        Global events\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Earth%27s_rotation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" <a href="#cite_note-49">\n         [45]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       <a href="/wiki/Post-glacial_rebound" title="Post-glacial rebound">\n        Post-glacial rebound\n       </a>\n       ,        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h3>\n      <p>\n       The permanent monitoring of the Earth\'s rotation is performed with\n       <a href="/wiki/Very-long-baseline_interferometry" These observations, as well as further astronomical historical records from\n       <a href="/wiki/Astronomy_in_the_medieval_Islamic_world" title="Astronomy in the medieval Islamic world">\n        astronomy in the medieval Islamic world\n       </a>\n       and elsewhere, can be used to determine the actual changes in the rotation of the Earth over the last 27 centuries: The calculation to describe the place and time of eclipse is dependent on the rotation of the Earth. The ancient observations are consistent with the Earth having rotated a significant fraction of a total turn than would result from today\'s value of the speed of rotation would indicate: The Earth was turning ever faster throughout the past. The cumulative effect of the faster turn, of a magnitude of milliseconds per day per century, 36,525 days per century, shows up in a magnitude of hours and thousands of kilometers in observations. Scholars have combed the ancient records and calculated by modern models the magnitude of the long term historical slowing down of the Earth\'s rotation.\n       <sup class="reference" original rotation was a vestige of the original\n       <a href="/wiki/Angular_momentum" title="Angular momentum">\n        angular momentum\n       </a>\n       of the cloud of\n       <a href="/wiki/Dust" title="Dust">\n        dust\n       </a>\n       ,\n       <a href="/wiki/Rock_(geology)" title="Rock (geology)">\n        rocks\n       </a>\n       , and\n       <a This primordial cloud was composed of\n       <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">\n        hydrogen\n       </a>\n       and\n       <a href="/wiki/Helium" title="Helium">\n        helium\n       </a>\n       produced in the\n       <a href="/wiki/Big_Bang" title="Big Bang">\n        Big Bang\n       </a>\n       , as well as heavier\n       <a href="/wiki/Chemical_element" title="Chemical element">\n        elements\n       </a>\n       ejected by\n       <a href="/wiki/Supernova" title="Supernova">\n        supernovas\n       </a>\n       . As this\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Interstellar_dust" title="Interstellar dust">\n        interstellar dust\n       </a>\n       is heterogeneous, any asymmetry during gravitational accretion resulted in the angular momentum of the eventual planet.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54">\n        <a href="#cite_note-54">\n         [50]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       However, if the\n       <a href="/wiki/Giant-impact_hypothesis" title="Giant-impact hypothesis">\n        giant-impact hypothesis\n       </a>\n       for the origin of the Moon is correct, this primordial rotation rate would have been reset by the Theia impact 4.5 billion years ago. Regardless of the speed and tilt of the Earth\'s rotation before the impact, it would have experienced a day some five hours long after the impact.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-areps15_55-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-areps15-55">\n         [51]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       Tidal effects would then have slowed this rate to its modern value.\n
