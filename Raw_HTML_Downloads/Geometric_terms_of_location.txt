These terms are used in descriptions of engineering, physics, and other sciences, as well as ordinary day to day discourse.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       Though these terms by themselves may be somewhat ambiguous, they are usually used in a context in which their meaning is clear. For example, when referring to a\n       <a href="/wiki/Drive_shaft" title="Drive shaft">\n        drive shaft\n       </a>\n       it is clear what is meant by axial or radial directions. Or, in a\n       <a href="/wiki/Free_body_diagram" title="Free body diagram">\n        free body diagram\n       </a>\n       , one may similarly infer a sense of orientation by the forces or other vectors represented.\n      </p>\n      <h2>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Examples">\n        Examples\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Geometric_terms_of_location&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Examples">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h2>\n      <p>\n       Common geometric terms of location are:\n      </p>\n      <ul>\n       <li>\n        <b>\n         Adjacent\n        </b>\n        - next to\n       </li>\n       <li>\n        <b>\n         Axial\n        </b>\n        \xe2\x80\x93 along the center of a round body, or the\n        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Axis_of_rotation" title="Axis of rotation">\n         axis of rotation\n        </a>\n        of a body\n       </li>\n       <li>\n        <b>\n         Azimuthal\n        </b>\n        or\n        <b>\n         circumferential\n        </b>\n        \xe2\x80\x93 following around a curve or\n        <a href="/wiki/Circumference" title="Circumference">\n         circumference\n        </a>\n        of an object. For instance, the pattern of cells in\n        <a href="/wiki/Taylor%E2%80%93Couette_flow" title="Taylor\xe2\x80\x93Couette flow">\n         Taylor\xe2\x80\x93Couette flow\n        </a>\n        varies along the azimuth of the experiment.\n       </li>\n       <li>\n        <b>\n         Collinear\n        </b>\n        - in the same line\n       </li>\n       <li>\n        <b>\n         Degree of freedom\n        </b>\n        - axis direction, see\n        <a href="/wiki/Six_degrees_of_freedom" title="Six degrees of freedom">\n         six degrees of freedom\n        </a>\n       </li>\n       <li>\n        <b>\n         Lateral\n        </b>\n        \xe2\x80\x93 spanning the width of a body.\n       </li>\n       <li>\n        <b>\n         Lineal\n        </b>\n        \xe2\x80\x93 following along a given path. The shape of the path is not necessarily straight (compare to\n        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Linear" title="Linear">\n         linear\n        </a>\n        ). For instance, a length of rope might be measured in lineal\n        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Meters" title="Meters">\n         meters\n        </a>\n        or\n        <a href="/wiki/Foot_(unit)" title="Foot (unit)">\n         feet\n        </a>\n        . See\n        <a href="/wiki/Arc_length" title="Arc length">\n         arc length\n        </a>\n        .\n       </li>\n       <li>\n        <b>\n         Longitudinal\n        </b>\n        \xe2\x80\x93 spanning the length of a body. The distinction between width and length may be unclear out of context.\n       </li>\n       <li>\n        <b>\n         Orthogonal\n        </b>\n        \xe2\x80\x93 at right angles to a line, or more generally, on a different axis.\n       </li>\n       <li>\n        <b>\n         Parallel\n        </b>\n        - in the same direction\n       </li>\n       <li>\n        <b>\n         Perpendicular\n        </b>\n        - at right angles to, synonym to orthogonal\n       </li>\n       <li>\n        <b>\n         Radial\n        </b>\n        \xe2\x80\x93 along a direction pointing along a\n        <a href="/wiki/Radius" title="Radius">\n         radius\n        </a>\n        from the center of an object, or perpendicular to a curved path.\n       </li>\n       <li>\n        <b>\n         Tangential\n        </b>\n        \xe2\x80\x93 intersecting a curve at a point and parallel to the curve at that point.\n       </li>\n       <li>\n        <b>\n         Transverse\n        </b>\n        \xe2\x80\x93 orthogonal to a specified direction, such as a particle trajectory or an axis of rotation.\n       </li>\n      </ul>\n      <h2>\n       <span class="mw-headline"
