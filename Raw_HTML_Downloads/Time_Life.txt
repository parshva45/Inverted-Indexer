<b>\n        Direct Holdings Global LLC\n       </b>\n       , through its subsidiaries\n       <b>\n        StarVista Live, Lifestyle Products Group\n       </b>\n       and\n       <b>\n        Time Life,\n       </b>\n       is a creator and\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Direct_marketer" title="Direct marketer">\n        direct marketer\n       </a>\n       that is known for selling books, music, video/DVD, and multimedia products. The current focus of the company is music, video and entertainment experiences (such as cruises) as the book division closed in 2003. Its products have been sold throughout North America, Europe, Australia, and Asia through television, print, retail, the Internet, telemarketing, and direct sales. Current operations are focused in the US and Canada with limited retail distribution overseas.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       Time Life was founded in 1961 as the book division of\n       <a href="/wiki/Time_Inc." title="Time Inc.">\n        Time Inc.\n       </a>\n       . It took its name from Time Inc.\'s cornerstone magazines,\n       <i>\n        <a href="/wiki/Time_(magazine)" title="Time (magazine)">\n         Time\n        </a>\n       </i>\n       and\n       <i>\n        <a href="/wiki/Life_(magazine)" title="Life (magazine)">\n         Life\n        </a>\n       </i>\n       , but remained independent of both. Starting in 1967, Time Life combined its book offerings with music collections (two to five records) and packaged them as a sturdy box set. Throughout the 1970s and 1980s, the selection of books, music and videos grew and was diversified into more genres. When record labels stopped producing vinyl albums in 1990, Time Life switched to CD only. In the mid-1990s, Time Life acquired\n       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Heartland_Music&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Heartland Music (page does not exist)">\n        Heartland Music\n       </a>\n       , with the Heartland Music label now appearing as a brand. This company was subsequently sold off and is no longer attached to Time Life.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       At the end of 2003 Time Life was acquired by Ripplewood Holdings L.L.C. and ZelnickMedia Corporation to become part of Direct Holdings Worldwide L.L.C. Direct Holdings Americas Inc. operates as a leader in the sale of music and video products under the Time Life brand. Since 2003,\n       <b>\n        Direct Holdings US Corp\n       </b>\n       is the legal name of Time Life, and is no longer owned by its former parent\n       <a href="/wiki/Time_Warner" title="Time Warner">\n        Time Warner\n       </a>\n       , later\n       <a href="/wiki/Time_Inc." title="Time Inc.">\n        Time Inc.\n       </a>\n       in June 9, 2014. In March 2007, Ripplewood led a group that took\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Reader%27s_Digest_Association" title="Reader\'s Digest Association">\n        The Reader\'s Digest Association\n       </a>\n       private and treated Time Life as a division of RDA. By 2003 onward, a disclaimer on the copyright stated that it is "not affiliated with Time Warner Inc. or\n       <a href="/wiki/Time_Inc." title="Time Inc.">\n        Time Inc.\n       </a>\n       ", who owns the Time and Life magazines which this company name came from.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       In 2013, Reader\'s Digest Association sold Time Life to Mosaic Media Investment Partners.\n <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Time_Life&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit was founded by\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Time,_Incorporated" title="Time, Incorporated">\n        Time, Incorporated\n       </a>\n       in 1961, as a book marketing division. Its name is derived from\n       <i>\n        <a href="/wiki/Time_(magazine)" title="Time (magazine)">\n         Time\n        </a>\n       </i>\n       and\n       <i>\n        <a href="/wiki/Life_(magazine)" title="Life (magazine)">\n         Life\n        </a>\n       </i>\n       magazines, two of the most popular magazines of the era, both of which were owned by the company. During the 1960s and 1970s, the collections released by Time\xe2\x80\x93Life Records catered to a conservative adult audience, with genres including\n       <a href="/wiki/Classical_music" title="Classical music">\n        classical\n       </a>\n       ,\n       <a href="/wiki/Jazz" title="Jazz">\n        jazz\n       </a>\n       ,\n       <a href="/wiki/Swing_music" title="Swing music">\n        swing\n       </a>\n       and\n       <a href="/wiki/Orchestra" title="Orchestra">\n        orchestral\n       </a>\n       music; and the music of\n       <a href="/wiki/Opera" title="Opera">\n        operas\n       </a>\n       and\n       <a href="/wiki/Broadway_theatre" title="Broadway theatre">\n        Broadway theatre\n       </a>\n       . On occasion, Time Life offered\n       <a href="/wiki/Popular_music" title="Popular music">\n        popular music\n       </a>\n       (generally pre-1955 music, as opposed to\n       <a href="/wiki/Pop_music" title="Pop music">\n        pop\n       </a>\n       and\n       <a href="/wiki/Rock_music" title="Rock music">\n        rock\n       </a>\n       music airing on\n       <a href="/wiki/Contemporary_hit_radio" title="Contemporary hit radio">\n        contemporary hit radio\n       </a>\n       stations in the United States at the time) in box sets. Although there were television advertisements, Time Life advertised most of these sets in magazines, specialty catalogs and\n       <a href="/wiki/Advertising_mail" title="Advertising mail">\n        direct mail\n       </a>\n       .\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       In the early 1980s, Time Life began branching out, offering a series of albums focusing on\n       <a href="/wiki/Country_music" title="Country music">\n        country music\n       </a>\n       . The first series was 1981\'s "Country Music," with volumes focusing on a particular artist and featuring eight or nine tracks per album. Twenty volumes were issued, with many of country\'s greatest artists of the time (\n       <a href="/wiki/Charley_Pride" title="Charley Pride">\n        Charley Pride\n       </a>\n       was the first artist featured) getting their own album. But until the mid-1980s, Time\xe2\x80\x93Life did not feature a rock music-intensive series for customers, preferring to cater to older adults with conservative music tastes.\n      </p>\n      <h3>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Pop_music_enters_the_picture">\n        Pop music enters the picture\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Time_Life&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Pop music enters the picture">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h3>\n      <p>\n       Time Life\'s first successful foray into rock music came in 1986, with a series called "The Rock \'n\' Roll Era." Each volume in that series\xe2\x80\x94like similar series that followed\xe2\x80\x94focused on a particular year (in this case, 1955 through 1964\xe2\x80\x94the early, pre-Beatles years of rock music), a stylistic trend or particular artist influential in rock music. Each volume had 22 tracks, and was said to contain the original hit recording by the original artist (although this wasn\'t always true on early pressings of the early albums in the series). The songs themselves represented the most important and popular songs from the period or subject featured. An essay published by\n       <i>\n        Both Sides Now Publications\n       </i>\n       noted that Time-Life\'s move into rock music came at a time when much of the adult audience Time-Life catered to grew up during the rock-and-roll era and, as such, the new series was consistent with its goal of catering to an adult audience.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">\n        <a href="#cite_note-4">\n         [4]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       "\n       <a href="/wiki/The_Rock%27n%27Roll_Era_(Time-Life_Music)" title="The Rock\'n\'Roll Era (Time-Life Music)">\n        The Rock \'n\' Roll Era\n       </a>\n       " series was a big success, and by the time the final volume was issued in the early 1990s, more than 50 different volumes (including two Christmas albums) had been released. This paved the way for more country and pop music-intensive series, including "\n       <a href="/wiki/Country_USA_(Time-Life_Music)" title="Country USA (Time-Life Music)">\n        Country USA\n       </a>\n       ," "\n       <a href="/wiki/Classic_Rock_(Time-Life_Music)" title="Classic Rock (Time-Life Music)">\n        Classic Rock\n       </a>\n       ," "\n       <a href="/wiki/Sounds_of_the_Seventies_(Time-Life_Music)" title="Sounds of the Seventies (Time-Life Music)">\n        Sounds of the Seventies\n       </a>\n       ," "Sounds of the Eighties," "\n       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Your_Hit_Parade_(Time-Life_Music)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Your Hit Parade (Time-Life Music) (page does not exist)">\n        Your Hit Parade\n       </a>\n       " (a series featuring popular music of the 1940s through early 1960s) and "\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/AM_Gold_(Time-Life_Music)" title="AM Gold (Time-Life Music)">\n        Super Hits\n       </a>\n       ." Like the earlier series, each volume issued had its own paperback booklet containing liner notes and information about the songs, with the addition of placement on various\n       <i>\n        <a href="/wiki/Billboard_(magazine)" title="Billboard (magazine)">\n         Billboard\n        </a>\n       </i>\n       magazine charts.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       Like the earlier box sets featuring other musical styles and genres, the country and pop music series were advertised in magazines, catalogs and direct mail. By this time, some of these collections were advertised on television: either commercials or 30-minute\n       <a href="/wiki/Infomercial" title="Infomercial">\n        infomercials\n       </a>\n       . The television advertisements used slogans (e.g., "Relive your high school days ..."), clips of songs included in each volume (along with a scrolling list of other titles), a commercial spokesman (usually a performer or legendary disc jockey relevant to a given series, such as\n       <a href="/wiki/Rick_Dees" title="Rick Dees">\n        Rick Dees\n       </a>\n       for a 1970s-intensive collection and\n       <a href="/wiki/Ralph_Emery" title="Ralph Emery">\n        Ralph Emery\n       </a>\n       for a country music series) and testimonials from customers attesting to the quality and value of the albums, to pitch a given series. Key selling points of these collections are that each track was digitally transferred to the desired format using the original master recordings, as opposed to being "re-records"; and that the most popular and requested songs by customers could be found in a single collection (as opposed to a customer having to purchase many albums to obtain just a few desired tracks).\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       Customers were given a choice of which format they wanted their box set: either\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vinyl_record" title="Vinyl record">\n        vinyl albums\n       </a>\n       (through 1990),\n       <a href="/wiki/8-track_tape" title="8-track tape">\n        8-track\n       </a>\n       or\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Compact_audio_cassette" title="Compact audio cassette">\n        cassette tape\n       </a>\n       , or\n       <a href="/wiki/Compact_disc" title="Compact disc">\n        compact disc\n       </a>\n       ; today\'s box sets are offered only as compact discs.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       While most of Time Life\'s box sets and releases were critically hailed, there were also some minor faults pointed out by critics. For instance, several early pressings of the early volumes in "The Rock\'n\'Roll Era" series contained stereo re-recordings of the original hits (something that would be corrected on later pressings, either with the correct original recording or a replacement track). Sometimes, the most popular songs of a given time period were omitted, frequently due to licensing issues. Examples included\n       <a href="/wiki/The_Beatles" title="The Beatles">\n        The Beatles\n       </a>\n       and\n       <a href="/wiki/The_Rolling_Stones" title="The Rolling Stones">\n        The Rolling Stones\n       </a>\n       for the\n       <i>\n        <a href="/wiki/Classic_Rock_(Time-Life_Music)" title="Classic Rock (Time-Life Music)">\n         Classic Rock\n        </a>\n       </i>\n       and "Super Hits"/"AM Gold" series; and\n       <a href="/wiki/Prince_(musician)" title="Prince (musician)">\n        Prince\n       </a>\n       ,\n       <a href="/wiki/Madonna_(entertainer)" title="Madonna (entertainer)">\n        Madonna\n       </a>\n       ,\n       <a href="/wiki/Whitney_Houston" title="Whitney Houston">\n        Whitney Houston\n       </a>\n       and\n       <a href="/wiki/Michael_Jackson" title="Michael Jackson">\n        Michael Jackson\n       </a>\n       on the main\n       <i>\n        Sounds of the Eighties\n       </i>\n       series.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       Through 2010, several different series Time Life had offered were available on a subscription basis, either by calling a\n       <a href="/wiki/Toll-free_telephone_number" title="Toll-free telephone number">\n        1-800 number\n       </a>\n       or sending a completed postcard-sized card and payment to Time\xe2\x80\x93Life. Purportedly, the customer would get a specific volume (as advertised on TV or in a magazine) first, before receiving a new volume roughly every other month (on the format of their choice); customers and had the option of keeping just the volumes they wanted. In time, each volume was also offered for individual sale.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       Several of the series \xe2\x80\x93 especially the pop, rock, country and rhythm and blues series \xe2\x80\x93 had retail versions for sale, released after the entire series was issued. Typically, these were sold at discount stores, often grouped in three-CD sets of 12 tracks each and having the most popular of the series\' tracks, and cover artwork and naming loosely based off the subscription/catalog-exclusive titles. Additionally, the "Classic Country" series had special 15-track single-CD versions of several of its volumes issued for retail sale (in addition to budget 3-CD sets).\n      </p>\n      <h3>\n
