</tr>\n      </table>\n      <p>\n       "\n       <b>\n        Space Oddity\n       </b>\n       " is a song written and recorded by\n       <a href="/wiki/David_Bowie" title="David Bowie">\n        David Bowie\n       </a>\n       . It was first released as a\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gramophone_record" title="Gramophone record">\n        7-inch single\n       </a>\n       on 11 July 1969. It was also the opening track of his second studio album,\n       <i>\n        <a href="/wiki/David_Bowie_(1969_album)" title="David Bowie (1969 album)">\n         David Bowie\n        </a>\n       </i>\n       . It became one of Bowie\'s\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Signature_song" title="Signature song">\n        signature songs\n       </a>\n       and one of four of his songs to be included in\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Rock_and_Roll_Hall_of_Fame%27s_500_Songs_that_Shaped_Rock_and_Roll" title="The Rock and Roll Hall of Fame\'s 500 Songs that Shaped Rock and Roll">\n        The Rock and Roll Hall of Fame\'s 500 Songs that Shaped Rock and Roll\n       </a>\n       .\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-655_Songs_2-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-655_Songs-2">\n         [2]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       The song is about the launch of\n       <a href="/wiki/Major_Tom" title="Major Tom">\n        Major Tom\n       </a>\n       , a fictional\n       <a href="/wiki/Astronaut" title="Astronaut">\n        astronaut\n       </a>\n       , and was released during a period of great interest in space flight. The United States\'\n       <a href="/wiki/Apollo_11" title="Apollo 11">\n        Apollo 11\n       </a>\n       mission would launch five days later and would become the first manned moon landing another five days after that.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">\n        <a href="#cite_note-3">\n         [3]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       The lyrics have also been seen to lampoon the\n       <a href="/wiki/British_space_programme" title="British space programme">\n        British space programme\n       </a>\n       ,\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">\n        <a href="#cite_note-4">\n         [4]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       which was and still is an unmanned project. Bowie would later revisit his Major Tom character in the songs "\n       <a href="/wiki/Ashes_to_Ashes_(David_Bowie_song)" title="Ashes to Ashes (David Bowie song)">\n        Ashes to Ashes\n       </a>\n       ", "\n       <a href="/wiki/Hallo_Spaceboy" title="Hallo Spaceboy">\n        Hallo Spaceboy\n       </a>\n       " and possibly the music video for "\n       <a href="/wiki/Blackstar_(song)" title="Blackstar (song)">\n        Blackstar\n       </a>\n       ".\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       "Space Oddity" was David Bowie\'s first single to chart in the UK. It reached the top five on its initial release and received the 1970\n       <a href="/wiki/Ivor_Novello_Awards" title="Ivor Novello Awards">\n        Ivor Novello\n       </a>\n       Special Award for Originality.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">\n        <a href="#cite_note-5">\n         [5]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       His second album, originally released as\n       <i>\n        David Bowie\n       </i>\n       in the UK, was renamed after the track for its 1972 re-release by\n       <a href="/wiki/RCA_Records" title="RCA Records">\n        RCA Records\n       </a>\n       and became known by this name. In 1975, upon re-release as part of a maxi-single, the song became Bowie\'s first\n       <a href="/wiki/List_of_UK_Singles_Chart_number_ones_of_the_1970s" title="List of UK Singles Chart number ones of the 1970s">\n        UK No. 1 single\n       </a>\n       .\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">\n        <a href="#cite_note-6">\n         [6]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       In 2013, the song gained renewed popularity after it was covered by Canadian astronaut\n       <a href="/wiki/Chris_Hadfield" title="Chris Hadfield">\n        Chris Hadfield\n       </a>\n       , who performed the song while aboard the\n       <a href="/wiki/International_Space_Station" title="International Space Station">\n        International Space Station\n       </a>\n       , and therefore became the first music video shot in space. In January 2016, the song re-entered singles charts around the world following\n       <a href="/wiki/Death_of_David_Bowie" title="Death of David Bowie">\n        Bowie\'s death\n       </a>\n       , which included becoming Bowie\'s first single to top the\n       <a href="/wiki/List_of_number-one_hits_of_2016_(France)" title="List of number-one hits of 2016 (France)">\n        French Singles Chart\n       </a>\n       . The song also ranked as third on iTunes on January 12, 2016.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">\n        <a href="#cite_note-7">\n         [7]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n </div>\n      <p>\n      </p>\n      <h2>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Recording_and_release">\n        Recording and release\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Space_Oddity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Recording and release">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h2>\n      <p>\n       Three primary studio versions of "Space Oddity" exist: an early version recorded in February 1969, the album version recorded that June (edited for release as a single), and a 1979 re-recording.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       The early version of "Space Oddity" was recorded on 2 February 1969 for Bowie\'s promotional film\n       <i>\n        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Love_You_Till_Tuesday_(film)" title="Love You Till Tuesday (film)">\n         Love You Till Tuesday\n        </a>\n       </i>\n       .\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Any_Day_Now_pp.147.2C_145_8-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-Any_Day_Now_pp.147.2C_145-8">\n         [8]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       This recording became commercially available in 1984, on a belated VHS release of the film and accompanying\n       <a href="/wiki/Love_You_till_Tuesday_(album)" title="Love You till Tuesday (album)">\n        soundtrack album\n       </a>\n       . It also appeared on the compilation album\n       <i>\n        <a href="/wiki/The_Deram_Anthology_1966%E2%80%931968" title="The Deram Anthology 1966\xe2\x80\x931968">\n         The Deram Anthology 1966\xe2\x80\x931968\n        </a>\n       </i>\n       .\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       In June 1969, after Bowie\'s split from record label\n       <a href="/wiki/Deram_Records" title="Deram Records">\n        Deram\n       </a>\n       , his manager, Kenneth Pitt, negotiated a one-album deal (with options for a further one or two albums) with\n       <a href="/wiki/Mercury_Records" title="Mercury Records">\n        Mercury Records\n       </a>\n       and its UK subsidiary,\n       <a href="/wiki/Philips_Records" title="Philips Records">\n        Philips\n       </a>\n       .\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Alias_David_Bowie_p._172_9-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-Alias_David_Bowie_p._172-9">\n         [9]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       Mercury executives had heard an audition tape that included a demo of "Space Oddity", recorded by Bowie and his then musical partner John Hutchinson in spring 1969. Next Bowie tried to find a\n       <a href="/wiki/Record_producer" title="Record producer">\n        producer\n       </a>\n       .\n       <a href="/wiki/George_Martin" title="George Martin">\n        George Martin\n       </a>\n       turned the project down,\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Alias_David_Bowie_p._172_9-1">\n        <a href="#cite_note-Alias_David_Bowie_p._172-9">\n         [9]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       while\n       <a href="/wiki/Tony_Visconti" title="Tony Visconti">\n        Tony Visconti\n       </a>\n       liked the album demo-tracks, but considered the planned lead-off single, "Space Oddity", a \'cheap shot\' at the impending\n       <a href="/wiki/Apollo_11" title="Apollo 11">\n        Apollo 11\n       </a>\n       space mission. Visconti decided to delegate its production to\n       <a href="/wiki/Gus_Dudgeon" title="Gus Dudgeon">\n        Gus Dudgeon\n       </a>\n       .\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">\n        <a href="#cite_note-10">\n         [10]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       The album version of "Space Oddity" was recorded at\n       <a href="/wiki/Trident_Studios" title="Trident Studios">\n        Trident Studios\n       </a>\n       on 20 June 1969 (with overdubs a few days later) and used the in-house\n       <a href="/wiki/Session_musician" title="Session musician">\n        session player\n       </a>\n       <a href="/wiki/Rick_Wakeman" title="Rick Wakeman">\n        Rick Wakeman\n       </a>\n       (\n       <a href="/wiki/Mellotron" title="Mellotron">\n        Mellotron\n       </a>\n       ), who was later to achieve fame with the progressive rock band\n       <a href="/wiki/Yes_(band)" title="Yes (band)">\n        Yes\n       </a>\n       , as well as\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mick_Wayne" title="Mick Wayne">\n        Mick Wayne\n       </a>\n       (guitar),\n       <a href="/wiki/Herbie_Flowers" title="Herbie Flowers">\n        Herbie Flowers\n       </a>\n       (bass), and\n       <a href="/wiki/Terry_Cox" title="Terry Cox">\n        Terry Cox\n       </a>\n       (drums).\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Any_Day_Now_pp.153-154.2C_155_11-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-Any_Day_Now_pp.153-154.2C_155-11">\n         [11]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       Differing edits of the album version were released as singles in the UK and US.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       The song was promoted in advertisements for the\n       <a href="/wiki/Stylophone" title="Stylophone">\n        Stylophone\n       </a>\n       , played by Bowie on the record and heard in the background during the opening verse. The single was not played by the BBC until after the\n       <a href="/wiki/Apollo_11" title="Apollo 11">\n        Apollo 11\n       </a>\n       crew had safely returned;\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">\n        <a href="#cite_note-12">\n         [12]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       after this slow start, the song reached No. 5 in the\n       <a href="/wiki/UK_Singles_Chart" title="UK Singles Chart">\n        UK Singles Chart\n       </a>\n       . In the US, it stalled at 124.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       Besides its title, which alludes to the film\n       <i>\n        <a href="/wiki/2001:_A_Space_Odyssey_(film)" title="2001: A Space Odyssey (film)">\n         2001: A Space Odyssey\n        </a>\n       </i>\n       , the introduction to the song is a barely audible instrumental build-up that is analogous to the deep bass tone in\n       <i>\n        <a href="/wiki/Also_sprach_Zarathustra_(Strauss)" title="Also sprach Zarathustra (Strauss)">\n         Also sprach Zarathustra\n        </a>\n       </i>\n       that is prominently used in the film.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       On 2 October 1969, he performed the song for an episode of Top of the Pops. However, this was recorded separate from the main audience. The performance was shown on 9 October the following week, and repeated on 16 October. At present, the performance is \'missing\' due to the BBC\'s late junking policy.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       <a href="/wiki/Mogol_(lyricist)" title="Mogol (lyricist)">\n        Mogol\n       </a>\n       wrote Italian lyrics for the song, and Bowie recorded a new vocal in December 1969, releasing the single "\n       <a href="/wiki/Ragazzo_solo,_ragazza_sola" title="Ragazzo solo, ragazza sola">\n        Ragazzo solo, ragazza sola\n       </a>\n       " ("Lonely Boy, Lonely Girl") in Italy.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Any_Day_Now_p.174_13-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-Any_Day_Now_p.174-13">\n         [13]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       Upon its re-release as a single in 1973, "Space Oddity" reached No. 15 on the\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Billboard_Chart" title="Billboard Chart">\n        <i>\n         Billboard\n        </i>\n        Chart\n       </a>\n       and became Bowie\'s first hit single in the United States; in Canada, it reached No. 16.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">\n        <a href="#cite_note-14">\n         [14]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       This was then used to support RCA\'s 1975 UK reissue, which gave Bowie his first No. 1 single in the\n       <a href="/wiki/UK_Singles_Chart" title="UK Singles Chart">\n        UK Singles Chart\n       </a>\n       in November that year. It spent two weeks at the top of that chart.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-British_Hit_Singles_.26_Albums_15-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-British_Hit_Singles_.26_Albums-15">\n         [15]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       Bowie recorded a stripped-down, acoustic version of the song in late 1979,\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rebel_Rebel_pp._99.2C_107_16-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-Rebel_Rebel_pp._99.2C_107-16">\n         [16]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       which was issued in February 1980 as the\n       <a href="/wiki/A-side_and_B-side" title="A-side and B-side">\n        B-side\n       </a>\n       of "\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alabama_Song_(Bowie)" title="Alabama Song (Bowie)">\n        Alabama Song\n       </a>\n       ". The 1979 recording was released, in a remixed form, in 1992 on the\n       <a href="/wiki/Rykodisc" title="Rykodisc">\n        Rykodisc\n       </a>\n       reissue of Bowie\'s\n       <i>\n        <a href="/wiki/Scary_Monsters_(And_Super_Creeps)" title="Scary Monsters (And Super Creeps)">\n         Scary Monsters (And Super Creeps)\n        </a>\n       </i>\n       album, and it was rereleased on\n       <i>\n        Re:Call 3\n       </i>\n       , part of the\n       <i>\n        <a href="/wiki/A_New_Career_in_a_New_Town_(1977%E2%80%931982)" title="A New Career in a New Town (1977\xe2\x80\x931982)">\n         A New Career in a New Town (1977\xe2\x80\x931982)\n        </a>\n       </i>\n       compilation, in 2017.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17">\n        <a href="#cite_note-17">\n         [17]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       The B-side of the original single, "\n       <a href="/wiki/Wild_Eyed_Boy_from_Freecloud" title="Wild Eyed Boy from Freecloud">\n        Wild Eyed Boy from Freecloud\n       </a>\n       ", first appeared on CD on 1989\'s\n       <i>\n        <a href="/wiki/Sound_%2B_Vision_(box_set)" title="Sound + Vision (box set)">\n         Sound + Vision\n        </a>\n       </i>\n       . This compilation also included, as its opening track, the spring 1969 demo of "Space Oddity" featuring Bowie and Hutchinson. (An earlier Bowie/Hutchinson demo appears on the 2009 two-CD special edition of the album\n       <i>\n        <a href="/wiki/David_Bowie_(1969_album)" title="David Bowie (1969 album)">\n         David Bowie\n        </a>\n       </i>\n       .)\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       On 20 July 2009, the single was reissued on a digital EP that featured four previously released versions of the song and\n       <a href="/wiki/Stem_mixing_and_mastering" title="Stem mixing and mastering">\n        stems\n       </a>\n       that allow listeners to remix the song. This release coincided with the 40th anniversary of the song and the Apollo 11 moon landing.\n      </p>\n      <h2>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Accolades">\n        Accolades\n </div>\n      <h2>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Cover_versions_and_samples">\n        Cover versions and samples\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Space_Oddity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Cover versions and samples">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h2>\n      <ul>\n       <li>\n        In May 2013, Canadian astronaut\n        <a href="/wiki/Chris_Hadfield" title="Chris Hadfield">\n         Chris Hadfield\n        </a>\n        , commander of\n        <a href="/wiki/Expedition_35" title="Expedition 35">\n         Expedition 35\n        </a>\n        to the\n        <a href="/wiki/International_Space_Station" title="International Space Station">\n         International Space Station\n        </a>\n        , recorded a video of the song on the space station which went\n        <a href="/wiki/Viral_video" title="Viral video">\n         viral\n        </a>\n        and generated a great deal of media exposure.\n        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44">\n         <a href="#cite_note-44">\n          [44]\n         </a>\n        </sup>\n        The lyrics were somewhat altered; instead of losing communication with ground control and presumably being lost in space as a result, Major Tom successfully receives his orders to land and does so safely, reflecting Hadfield\'s imminent return from his final mission on the Station. Hadfield announced the video on his\n        <a href="/wiki/Twitter" title="Twitter">\n         Twitter\n        </a>\n        account, writing, "With deference to the genius of David Bowie, here\'s Space Oddity, recorded on Station. A last glimpse of the World." Bowie was also thanked in the ending credits.\n        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45">\n         <a href="#cite_note-45">\n          [45]\n         </a>\n        </sup>\n        This was the first music video ever shot in space.\n        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46">\n         <a href="#cite_note-46">\n          [46]\n         </a>\n        </sup>\n        Bowie responded to the video, tweeting back to Hadfield, "\n        <a href="/wiki/Hallo_Spaceboy" title="Hallo Spaceboy">\n         Hallo Spaceboy\n        </a>\n        ..."\n        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47">\n         <a href="#cite_note-47">\n          [47]\n         </a>\n        </sup>\n        and would later call the cover "possibly the most poignant version of the song ever created".\n        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48">\n         <a href="#cite_note-48">\n          [48]\n         </a>\n        </sup>\n        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49">\n         <a href="#cite_note-49">\n          [49]\n         </a>\n        </sup>\n        The video has had over 35,000,000 views on\n        <a href="/wiki/YouTube" title="YouTube">\n         YouTube\n        </a>\n        . The performance was the subject of a piece by Glenn Fleishman in\n        <i>\n         <a href="/wiki/The_Economist" title="The Economist">\n          The Economist\n         </a>\n        </i>\n        on 22 May 2013 analyzing the legal implications of publicly performing a copyrighted work of music while in\n        <a href="/wiki/Geocentric_orbit" title="Geocentric orbit">\n         earth orbit\n        </a>\n        .\n        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50">\n         <a href="#cite_note-50">\n          [50]\n         </a>\n        </sup>\n        The song is the only one of Bowie\'s for which Bowie did not own the copyright. Bowie\'s publisher granted Hadfield a license to the song for only one year.\n        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ottawa_citizen_apology_51-0">\n         <a href="#cite_note-ottawa_citizen_apology-51">\n          [51]\n         </a>\n        </sup>\n        Due to the expiry of the one year licence, the official video was taken offline on 13 May 2014,\n        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52">\n         <a href="#cite_note-52">\n          [52]\n         </a>\n        </sup>\n        despite Bowie\'s explicit wishes that the publisher grant Hadfield a license at no charge to record the song and produce the video.\n        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ottawa_citizen_apology_51-1">\n         <a href="#cite_note-ottawa_citizen_apology-51">\n          [51]\n         </a>\n        </sup>\n        Following a period of negotiations, the video was restored to YouTube on 2 November 2014 with a
