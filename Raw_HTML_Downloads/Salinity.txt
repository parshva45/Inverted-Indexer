</span>\n       </span>\n      </h2>\n      <p>\n       Salinity in rivers, lakes, and the ocean is conceptually simple, but technically challenging to define and measure precisely. Conceptually the salinity is the quantity of dissolved salt content of the water. Salts are compounds like\n       <a href="/wiki/Sodium_chloride" title="Sodium chloride">\n        sodium chloride\n       </a>\n       ,\n       <a href="/wiki/Magnesium_sulfate" title="Magnesium sulfate">\n        magnesium sulfate\n       </a>\n       ,\n       <a href="/wiki/Potassium_nitrate" title="Potassium nitrate">\n        potassium nitrate\n       </a>\n       , and\n       <a href="/wiki/Sodium_bicarbonate" title="Sodium bicarbonate">\n        sodium bicarbonate\n       </a>\n       which dissolve into ions. The concentration of dissolved chloride ions is sometimes referred to as chlorinity. Operationally, dissolved matter is defined as that which can pass through a very fine filter (historically a filter with a pore size of 0.45 \xce\xbcm, but nowadays usually 0.2 \xce\xbcm).\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-key_2-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-key-2">\n         [2]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       Salinity can be expressed in the form of a\n       <a href="/wiki/Mass_fraction_(chemistry)" title="Mass fraction (chemistry)">\n        mass fraction\n       </a>\n       , i.e. the mass of the dissolved material in a unit mass of solution.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       Seawater typically has a mass salinity of around 35\xc2\xa0g/kg, although lower values are typical near coasts where rivers enter the ocean. Rivers and lakes can have a wide range of salinities, from less than 0.01\xc2\xa0g/kg\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dilute_3-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-dilute-3">\n         [3]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       to a few g/kg, although there are many places where higher salinities are found. The\n       <a href="/wiki/Dead_Sea" title="Dead Sea">\n        Dead Sea\n       </a>\n       has a salinity of more than 200\xc2\xa0g/kg.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-anati_4-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-anati-4">\n         [4]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       Whatever pore size is used in the definition, the resulting salinity value of a given sample of natural water will not vary by more than a few\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Percent" title="Percent">\n        percent\n       </a>\n       (%). Physical oceanographers working in the\n       <a href="/wiki/Abyssal_zone" title="Abyssal zone">\n        abyssal ocean\n       </a>\n       , however, are often concerned with precision and intercomparability of measurements by different researchers, at different times, to almost five significant digits.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-teos10_5-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-teos10-5">\n         [5]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       A bottled seawater product known as IAPSO Standard Seawater is used by oceanographers to standardize their measurements with enough precision to meet this requirement.\n      </p>\n      <h3>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Composition">\n        Composition\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Salinity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Composition">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h3>\n      <p>\n       Measurement and definition difficulties arise because natural waters contain a complex mixture of many different elements from different sources (not all from dissolved salts) in different molecular forms. The chemical properties of some of these forms depend on temperature and pressure. Many of these forms are difficult to measure with high accuracy, and in any case complete chemical analysis is not practical when analyzing multiple samples. Different practical definitions of salinity result from different attempts to account for these problems, to different levels of precision, while still remaining reasonably easy to use.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       For practical reasons salinity is usually related to the sum of masses of a subset of these dissolved chemical constituents (so-called\n       <i>\n        solution salinity\n       </i>\n       ), rather than to the unknown mass of salts that gave rise to this composition (an exception is when\n       <a href="/wiki/Artificial_seawater" title="Artificial seawater">\n        artificial seawater\n       </a>\n       is created). For many purposes this sum can be limited to a set of eight major ions in natural waters,\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wetzel_6-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-wetzel-6">\n         [6]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-limteos_7-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-limteos-7">\n         [7]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       although for seawater at highest precision an additional seven minor ions are also included.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-teos10_5-1">\n        <a href="#cite_note-teos10-5">\n         [5]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       The major ions dominate the inorganic composition of most (but by no means all) natural waters. Exceptions include some\n       <a href="/wiki/Cenote" title="Cenote">\n        pit lakes\n       </a>\n       and waters from some\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydrothermal_spring" title="Hydrothermal spring">\n        hydrothermal springs\n       </a>\n       .\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       The concentrations of dissolved gases like\n       <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">\n        oxygen\n       </a>\n       and\n       <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen" title="Nitrogen">\n        nitrogen\n       </a>\n       are not usually included in descriptions of salinity.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-key_2-1">\n        <a href="#cite_note-key-2">\n         [2]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       However,\n       <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">\n        carbon dioxide\n       </a>\n       gas, which when dissolved is partially converted into\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carbonates" title="Carbonates">\n        carbonates\n       </a>\n       and\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bicarbonates" title="Bicarbonates">\n        bicarbonates\n       </a>\n       , is often included.\n       <a href="/wiki/Silicon" title="Silicon">\n        Silicon\n       </a>\n       in the form of\n       <a href="/wiki/Silicic_acid" title="Silicic acid">\n        silicic acid\n       </a>\n       , which usually appears as a neutral molecule in the\n       <a href="/wiki/PH" title="PH">\n        pH\n       </a>\n       range of most natural waters, may also be included for some purposes (e.g., when salinity/density relationships are being investigated).\n      </p>\n      <h3>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Seawater">\n        Seawater\n       </span>\n       <span Red colors represent areas of high salinity, while blue shades represent areas of low salinity.\n        </div>\n       </div>\n      </div>\n      <p>\n       The term \'salinity\' is, for oceanographers, usually associated with one of a set of specific measurement techniques. As the dominant techniques evolve, so do different descriptions of salinity. The distinctions between these different descriptions are important to physical oceanographers but are obscure and confusing to nonspecialists.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       Salinities were largely measured using\n       <a href="/wiki/Titration" title="Titration">\n        titration\n       </a>\n       -based techniques before the 1980s. Titration with\n       <a href="/wiki/Silver_nitrate" title="Silver nitrate">\n        silver nitrate\n       </a>\n       could be used to determine the concentration of\n       <a href="/wiki/Halide" title="Halide">\n        halide\n       </a>\n       ions (mainly\n       <a href="/wiki/Chlorine" title="Chlorine">\n        chlorine\n       </a>\n       and\n       <a href="/wiki/Bromine" title="Bromine">\n        bromine\n       </a>\n       ) to give a\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chlorinity" title="Chlorinity">\n        chlorinity\n       </a>\n       . The chlorinity was then multiplied by a factor to account for all other constituents. The resulting \'Knudsen salinities\' are expressed in units of parts per thousand (ppt or\n       <a href="/wiki/Per_mille" title="Per mille">\n        \xe2\x80\xb0\n       </a>\n       ).\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       The use of\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electrical_conductivity" title="Electrical conductivity">\n        electrical conductivity\n       </a>\n       measurements to estimate the ionic content of seawater led to the development of the scale called the\n       <i>\n        practical salinity scale 1978\n       </i>\n       (PSS-78).\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pss78a_8-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-pss78a-8">\n         [8]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pss78b_9-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-pss78b-9">\n         [9]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       Salinities measured using PSS-78 do not have units.\n       <span id="PSU">\n       </span>\n       The suffix\n       <b>\n        psu\n       </b>\n       or\n       <b>\n        PSU\n       </b>\n       (denoting\n       <i>\n        practical salinity unit\n       </i>\n       ) is sometimes added to PSS-78 measurement values.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rant_10-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-rant-10">\n         [10]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       In 2010 a new standard for the properties of seawater called the\n       <i>\n        thermodynamic equation of seawater 2010\n       </i>\n       (TEOS-10) was introduced, advocating absolute salinity as a replacement for practical salinity, and\n       <a href="/wiki/Conservative_temperature" title="Conservative temperature">\n        conservative temperature\n       </a>\n       as a replacement for\n       <a href="/wiki/Potential_temperature" title="Potential temperature">\n        potential temperature\n       </a>\n       .\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-teos10_5-2">\n        <a href="#cite_note-teos10-5">\n         [5]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       This standard includes a new scale called the\n       <i>\n        reference composition salinity scale\n       </i>\n       . Absolute salinities on this scale are expressed as a mass fraction, in grams per kilogram of solution. Salinities on this scale are determined by combining electrical conductivity measurements with other information that can account for regional changes in the composition of seawater. They can also be determined by making direct density measurements.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       A sample of seawater from most locations with a chlorinity of 19.37\xc2\xa0ppt will have a Knudsen salinity of 35.00\xc2\xa0ppt, a PSS-78 practical salinity of about 35.0, and a TEOS-10 absolute salinity of about 35.2\xc2\xa0g/kg. The electrical conductivity of this water at a temperature of 15\xc2\xa0\xc2\xb0C is 42.9 mS/cm.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-teos10_5-3">\n        <a href="#cite_note-teos10-5">\n         [5]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-culk_11-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-culk-11">\n         [11]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <h3>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Lakes_and_rivers">\n        Lakes and rivers\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Salinity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Lakes and rivers">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h3>\n      <p>\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Limnologist" title="Limnologist">\n        Limnologists\n       </a>\n       and chemists often define salinity in terms of mass of salt per unit volume, expressed in units of mg per litre or g per litre.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wetzel_6-1">\n        <a href="#cite_note-wetzel-6">\n         [6]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       It is implied, although often not stated, that this value applies accurately only at some reference temperature. Values presented in this way are typically accurate to the order of 1%. Limnologists also use\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electrical_conductivity" title="Electrical conductivity">\n        electrical conductivity\n       </a>\n       , or "reference conductivity", as a proxy for salinity. This measurement may be corrected for temperature effects, and is usually expressed in units of \xce\xbcS/cm.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       A river or lake water with a salinity of around 70\xc2\xa0mg/L will typically have a specific conductivity at 25\xc2\xa0\xc2\xb0C of between 80 and 130\xc2\xa0\xce\xbcS/cm. The actual ratio depends on the ions present.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">\n        <a href="#cite_note-12">\n         [12]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       The actual conductivity usually changes by about 2% per degree Celsius, so the measured conductivity at 5\xc2\xa0\xc2\xb0C might only be in the range of 50\xe2\x80\x9380\xc2\xa0\xce\xbcS/cm.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       Direct density measurements are also used to estimate salinities, particularly in highly\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saline_lake" title="Saline lake">\n        saline lakes\n       </a>\n       .\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-anati_4-1">\n        <a href="#cite_note-anati-4">\n         [4]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       Sometimes density at a specific temperature is used as a proxy for salinity. At other times an empirical salinity/density relationship developed for a particular body of water is used to estimate the salinity of samples from a measured density.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       <br/>\n      </p>\n      <table style="margin-left:auto; These waters are all regarded as\n       <i>\n        thalassic\n       </i>\n       because their salinity is derived from the ocean and defined as\n       <i>\n        homoiohaline\n       </i>\n       if salinity does not vary much over time (essentially constant). The table on the right, modified from Por (1972),\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">\n        <a href="#cite_note-13">\n         [13]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       follows the "Venice system" (1959).\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">\n        <a href="#cite_note-14">\n         [14]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       In contrast to homoiohaline environments are certain\n       <i>\n        poikilohaline\n       </i>\n       environments (which may also be\n       <i>\n        thalassic\n       </i>\n       ) in which the salinity variation is biologically significant.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">\n        <a href="#cite_note-15">\n         [15]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       <i>\n        Poikilohaline\n       </i>\n       water salinities may range anywhere from 0.5 to greater than 300. The important characteristic is that these waters tend to vary in salinity over some biologically meaningful range seasonally or on some other roughly comparable time scale. Put simply, these are bodies of water with quite variable salinity.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       Highly saline water, from which salts crystallize (or are about to), is referred to as\n       <a href="/wiki/Brine" title="Brine">\n        brine\n       </a>\n       .\n      </p>\n      <h3>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Environmental_considerations">\n        Environmental considerations\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Salinity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Environmental considerations">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h3>\n      <p>\n       Salinity is an ecological factor of considerable importance, influencing the types of organisms that live in a body of water. As well, salinity influences the kinds of\n       <a href="/wiki/Plant" title="Plant">\n        plants\n       </a>\n       that will grow either in a water body, or on land fed by a water (or by a\n       <a href="/wiki/Groundwater" title="Groundwater">\n        groundwater\n       </a>\n       ).\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">\n        <a href="#cite_note-16">\n         [16]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       A plant adapted to saline conditions is called a\n       <a href="/wiki/Halophyte" title="Halophyte">\n        halophyte\n       </a>\n       . A halophyte which is tolerant to\n       <a href="/wiki/Residual_sodium_carbonate_index" title="Residual sodium carbonate index">\n        residual sodium carbonate\n       </a>\n       salinity are called\n       <a href="/wiki/Glasswort" title="Glasswort">\n        glasswort\n       </a>\n       or\n       <a href="/wiki/Saltwort" title="Saltwort">\n        saltwort\n       </a>\n       or\n       <a href="/wiki/Barilla" title="Barilla">\n        barilla\n       </a>\n       plants. Organisms (mostly bacteria) that can live in very salty conditions are classified as\n       <a href="/wiki/Extremophile" title="Extremophile">\n        extremophiles\n       </a>\n       , or\n       <a href="/wiki/Halophile" title="Halophile">\n        halophiles\n       </a>\n       specifically. An organism that can withstand a wide range of salinities is\n       <a href="/wiki/Euryhaline" title="Euryhaline">\n        euryhaline\n       </a>\n       .\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       Salt is expensive to remove from water, and salt content is an important factor in water use (such as\n       <a href="/wiki/Drinking_water" title="Drinking water">\n        potability\n       </a>\n       ).\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       The degree of salinity in oceans is a driver of the\n       <a href="/wiki/Thermohaline_circulation" title="Thermohaline circulation">\n        world\'s ocean circulation\n       </a>\n       , where density changes due to both salinity changes and temperature changes at the surface of the ocean produce changes in buoyancy, which cause the sinking and rising of water masses.\n       <a href="/wiki/Paleosalinity" title="Paleosalinity">\n        Changes in the salinity of the oceans\n       </a>\n       are thought to contribute to global changes in carbon dioxide as more saline waters are less soluble to carbon dioxide. In addition, during glacial periods, the\n       <a href="/wiki/Hydrography" title="Hydrography">\n        hydrography\n       </a>\n       is such that a possible cause of reduced circulation is the production of stratified oceans. Hence it is difficult in this case to subduct water through the thermohaline circulation.\n      </p>\n      <h2>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">\n        See also\n       </span>\n
