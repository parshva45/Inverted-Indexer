     <p>\n       The\n       <b>\n        Integrated Truss Structure\n       </b>\n       (\n       <b>\n        ITS\n       </b>\n       ) of the\n       <a href="/wiki/International_Space_Station" It supplies the ISS with a\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spacecraft_bus" title="Spacecraft bus">\n        bus\n       </a>\n       architecture.         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h2>\n      <p>\n       All\n       <a href="/wiki/Truss" title="Truss">\n        truss\n       </a>\n       components were named after their The S0 truss might be considered a misnomer, as it is mounted centrally on the zenith position of\n       <i>\n        Destiny\n       </i>\n       and is neither starboard nor port side.\n <div class="thumbcaption">\n         <div class="magnify">\n          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:ISS_Unity_and_Z1_truss_structure_from_STS-92.jpg" title="Enlarge">\n          </a>\n         </div>\n         Z1 Truss is above the module\n        </div>\n       </div>\n      </div>\n      <p>\n       The first truss piece, the Z1 truss, launched aboard\n       <a href="/wiki/STS-92" title="STS-92">\n        STS-92\n       </a>\n       in October 2000. It contains the\n       <a href="/wiki/Control_moment_gyroscope" title="Control moment gyroscope">\n        control moment gyroscope\n       </a>\n       (CMG) assemblies, electrical wiring, communications equipment, and two\n       <a href="/wiki/Plasma_contactor" title="Plasma contactor">\n        plasma contactors\n       </a>\n       designed to neutralize the static electrical charge of the space station.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       Another objective of the Z1 truss was to serve as a temporary mounting position for the "P6 truss and solar array" until its relocation to the end of the P5 truss during STS-120. Though not a part of the main truss, the Z1 truss was the first permanent lattice-work structure for the ISS, very much like a girder, setting the stage for the future addition of the station\'s major trusses or backbones.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       While the bulk of the Z1 truss is unpressurized, it features a\n       <a href="/wiki/Common_Berthing_Mechanism" title="Common Berthing Mechanism">\n        Common Berthing Mechanism\n       </a>\n       (CBM) port that connects its nadir to the zenith port of\n       <i>\n        Unity\n       </i>\n       and contains a small pressurized dome that allowed astronauts to connect electrical ground straps between\n       <i>\n        Unity\n       </i>\n       and the truss without an EVA.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">\n        <a href="#cite_note-1">\n         [1]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">\n        <a href="#cite_note-2">\n         [2]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       In addition, the dome inside the CBM of Z1 can be used as storage space.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">\n        <a href="#cite_note-3">\n         [3]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       The Z1 truss also features a forward-facing Manual Berthing Mechanism (MBM) ring.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">\n        <a href="#cite_note-4">\n         [4]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       This MBM is not a port and is not pressurized or electrically powered, but it can be operated with a handheld tool to berth any passive\n       <a href="/wiki/Common_Berthing_Mechanism" title="Common Berthing Mechanism">\n        CBM\n       </a>\n       to it.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">\n        <a href="#cite_note-5">\n         [5]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       The Z1 truss\'s MBM was used only once, to temporarily hold\n       <a class="mw-redirect" the\n       <i>\n        Unity\n       </i>\n       node during\n       <a href="/wiki/STS-98" title="STS-98">\n        STS-98\n       </a>\n       . Since the installation of the nearby S0 truss in April 2002, access to the MBM has been blocked.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       In October 2007, the P6 was moved to its permanent position next to P5\xe2\x80\x94the Z1 truss is now solely used to house the CMGs, communications equipment and the plasma contactors; Z1 no longer connects other space station elements to\n       <i>\n        Unity\n       </i>\n       (Node 1).\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       In December 2008, the\n       <a href="/wiki/Ad_Astra_Rocket_Company" title="Ad Astra Rocket Company">\n        Ad Astra Rocket Company\n       </a>\n       announced an agreement with NASA to place a flight test version of its\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/VASIMR" title="VASIMR">\n        VASIMR\n       </a>\n       ion thruster on the station to take over reboost duties. In 2013, Ad Astra announced specific plans to place the thruster module, named Aurora, on top of the Z1 truss in 2015.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">\n        <a href="#cite_note-6">\n         [6]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       NASA and Ad Astra signed a contract for development of the VASIMR engine for up to three years in 2015.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">\n        <a href="#cite_note-7">\n         [7]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       This testing of the VX-200SSTM VASMIR engine for up to 100 hours and 100 kilowatts.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">\n        <a href="#cite_note-8">\n         [8]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       An example of a spacecraft that used an ion engine to maintain its orbit was the\n       <a href="/wiki/Gravity_Field_and_Steady-State_Ocean_Circulation_Explorer" title="Gravity Field and Steady-State Ocean Circulation Explorer">\n        Gravity Field It was attached on the top of the\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Destiny_Laboratory_Module" title="Destiny Laboratory Module">\n        Destiny Laboratory Module\n       </a>\n       during\n       <a href="/wiki/STS-110" title="STS-110">\n        STS-110\n       </a>\n       in April 2002. S0 is used to route power to the pressurized station modules and conduct heat away from the modules to the S1 and P1 Trusses. The S0 truss is not docked to the ISS, but is connected with four Module to Truss Structure (MTS) struts.\n      </p>\n      <h3>\n       <span id="P1,_S1_trusses">\n       </span>\n       <span Trusses\n       </i>\n       ) are attached to the S0 truss, and contain carts to transport the\n       <a href="/wiki/Mobile_Servicing_System" title="Mobile Servicing System">\n        Canadarm2\n       </a>\n       and astronauts to worksites along the space station. They each flow 290\xc2\xa0kg (637\xc2\xa0lb) of\n       <a href="/wiki/Anhydrous" title="Anhydrous">\n        anhydrous\n       </a>\n       <a href="/wiki/Ammonia" title="Ammonia">\n        ammonia\n       </a>\n       through three heat rejection radiators. The S1 truss was launched on\n       <a href="/wiki/STS-112" title="STS-112">\n        STS-112\n       </a>\n       in October 2002 and the P1 truss was launched on\n       <a href="/wiki/STS-113" title="STS-113">\n        STS-113\n       </a>\n       in November 2002. Detailed design, test and construction of the S1 and P1 structures was conducted by McDonnell Douglas (now Boeing) in Huntington Beach, CA. First parts were cut for the structure in 1996, and delivery of the first truss occurred in 1999.\n      </p>\n      <h3>\n       <span id="P2,_S2_trusses">\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="P2.2C_S2_trusses">\n        P2, S2 trusses\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Integrated_Truss_Structure&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: P2, S2 trusses">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h3>\n      <p>\n       The P2 and S2 trusses were planned as locations for rocket thrusters in the original design for\n       <a href="/wiki/Space_Station_Freedom" title="Space Station Freedom">\n        Space Station Freedom\n       </a>\n       . Since the Russian parts of the ISS also provided that capability, the\n       <a href="/wiki/Reboost" title="Reboost">\n        reboost\n       </a>\n       capability of the Space Station Freedom design was no longer needed at that location. The P3/P4 truss assembly was installed by the\n       <a href="/wiki/Space_Shuttle" title="Space Shuttle">\n        Space Shuttle\n       </a>\n       <a href="/wiki/Space_Shuttle_Atlantis" September 9, 2006, and attached to the P1 segment. The P3 and P4 segments together contain a pair of\n       <a href="/wiki/Solar_panels_on_spacecraft" title="Solar panels on spacecraft">\n        solar arrays\n       </a>\n       , a radiator and a\n       <a href="#Solar_alpha_rotary_joint">\n        rotary joint\n       </a>\n       that will aim the solar arrays, and connects P3 to P4. Upon its installation, no power was flowing across the rotary joint, so the electricity generated by the P4 solar array wings was only being used on the P4 segment, and not the rest of the station. Then in December 2006 a major electrical rewiring of the station by\n       <a href="/wiki/STS-116" title="STS-116">\n        STS-116\n       </a>\n       routed this power to the entire grid. The S3/S4 truss assembly\xe2\x80\x94a mirror-image of P3/P4\xe2\x80\x94was installed on June 11, 2007 also by\n       <a href="/wiki/Space_Shuttle" title="Space Shuttle">\n        Space Shuttle\n       </a>\n       <a href="/wiki/Space_Shuttle_Atlantis" title="Space Shuttle Atlantis">\n        <i>\n         Atlantis\n        </i>\n       </a>\n       during flight\n       <a href="/wiki/STS-117" title="STS-117">\n        STS-117\n       </a>\n       , mission\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ISS_assembly_sequence" title="ISS assembly sequence">\n        13A\n       </a>\n       and mounted to the S1 truss segment.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       Major P3 and S3 subsystems include the Segment-to-Segment Attach System (SSAS),\n       <a href="#Solar_alpha_rotary_joint">\n        Solar Alpha Rotary Joint\n       </a>\n       (SARJ), and Unpressurized Cargo Carrier Attach System (UCCAS). The primary functions of the P3 truss segment are to provide mechanical, power and data interfaces to payloads attached to the two UCCAS platforms; axial indexing for solar tracking, or rotating of the arrays to follow the sun, via the SARJ; movement and work site accommodations for the\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Canadarm2#Mobile_Base_System" title="Canadarm2">\n        Mobile Transporter\n       </a>\n       .    .\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Presskit_10-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-Presskit-10">\n         [10]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       The S3 truss also supports\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/EXPRESS_Logistics_Carrier" title="EXPRESS Logistics Carrier">\n        EXPRESS Logistics Carrier\n       </a>\n       locations, first to be launched and installed in the 2009 time frame.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       Major subsystems of the P4 and S4 Photovoltaic Modules (PVM) include the two\n       <a href="#Solar_arrays">\n        Solar Array Wings\n       </a>\n       (SAW), the\n       <a href="#Radiators">\n        Photovoltaic Radiator\n       </a>\n       (PVR), the Alpha Joint Interface Structure (AJIS), and Modified Rocketdyne Truss Attachment </div>\n      <p>\n       The P6 truss was the second truss segment to be added, because it contains a large\n       <a href="#Solar_arrays">\n        Solar Array Wing\n       </a>\n       (SAW) that generated essential electricity for the station, prior to activation of the SAW on the P4 truss. It was originally mounted to the Z1 truss and had its SAW extended during\n       <a href="/wiki/STS-97" title="STS-97">\n        STS-97\n       </a>\n       , but the SAW was folded, one half at a time, to make room for the SAWs on the P4 and S4 trusses, during\n       <a href="/wiki/STS-116" title="STS-116">\n        STS-116\n       </a>\n       and\n       <a href="/wiki/STS-117" title="STS-117">\n        STS-117\n       </a>\n       respectively. Shuttle mission\n       <a href="/wiki/STS-120" title="STS-120">\n        STS-120\n       </a>\n       (assembly mission\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ISS_assembly_sequence" title="ISS assembly sequence">\n        10A\n       </a>\n       ) detached the P6 truss from Z1, remounted it on the P5 truss, redeployed its radiator panels and attempted to redeploy its SAWs. One SAW (2B) was deployed successfully but the second SAW (4B) developed a significant tear that temporarily stopped deployment at around 80%. This was subsequently fixed and the array is now fully deployed. A later assembly mission (the out of sequence\n       <a href="/wiki/STS-119" title="STS-119">\n        STS-119\n       </a>\n       ) mounted the S6 truss on the S5 truss, which provided a fourth and href="/wiki/International_Space_Station" title="International Space Station">\n        International Space Station\n       </a>\n       \'s main source of\n       <a href="/wiki/Energy" title="Energy">\n        energy\n       </a>\n       is from three of the four large U.S.-made\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Photovoltaic_array" title="Photovoltaic array">\n The second pair of arrays was launched and installed in September 2006 during\n       <a href="/wiki/STS-115" title="STS-115">\n        STS-115\n       </a>\n       , but they didn\'t provide electricity until\n       <a href="/wiki/STS-116" title="STS-116">\n        STS-116\n       </a>\n       in December 2006 when the station got an electrical rewiring. A third pair of arrays was installed during\n       <a href="/wiki/STS-117" title="STS-117">\n        STS-117\n       </a>\n       in June 2007. A final pair arrived mid March 2009 on\n       <a href="/wiki/STS-119" title="STS-119">\n        STS-119\n       </a>\n       . More solar power was to have been available via the\n       <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">\n        Russian\n       </a>\n       -built\n       <a href="/wiki/Science_Power_Platform" title="Science Power Platform">\n        Science Power Platform\n       </a>\n       , but it was canceled.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Presskit_10-1">\n        <a href="#cite_note-Presskit-10">\n         [10]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       Each of the Solar Array Wings are 34\xc2\xa0m (112\xc2\xa0ft) long by 12\xc2\xa0m (39\xc2\xa0ft) wide, and are capable of generating nearly 32.8\n       <a href="/wiki/Watt" title="Watt">\n        kW\n       </a>\n       of\n       <a href="/wiki/Direct_current" title="Direct current">\n        DC\n       </a>\n       power.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">\n        <a href="#cite_note-11">\n         [11]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       They are split into two photovoltaic blankets, with the deployment mast in between. Each blanket has 16,400\n       <a href="/wiki/Silicon" title="Silicon">\n        silicon\n       </a>\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Photovoltaic_cell" title="Photovoltaic cell">\n        photovoltaic cells\n       </a>\n       , each cell measuring 8\xc2\xa0cm x 8\xc2\xa0cm, grouped into 82 active panels, each consisting of 200 cells, with 4,100\n       <a href="/wiki/Diode" title="Diode">\n        diodes\n       </a>\n       .\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Presskit_10-2">\n        <a href="#cite_note-Presskit-10">\n         [10]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       Each pair of blankets is folded like an\n       <a href="/wiki/Accordion" title="Accordion">\n        accordion\n       </a>\n       for compact delivery to space. Once in orbit, the deployment mast between each pair of blankets unfolds the array to its full length.\n       <a href="/wiki/Gimbal" title="Gimbal">\n        Gimbals\n       </a>\n       , known as the\n       <i>\n        Beta Gimbal Assembly\n       </i>\n       (BGA) are used to\n       <a href="/wiki/Night_Glider_mode" title="Night Glider mode">\n        rotate\n       </a>\n       the arrays so that they face the Sun to provide maximum power to the International Space Station.\n      </p>\n      <h3>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Solar_alpha_rotary_joint">\n        Solar alpha rotary joint\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Integrated_Truss_Structure&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Solar alpha rotary joint">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h3>\n      <p>\n       The\n       <b>\n        Alpha\n       </b>\n       joint is the main rotary joint allowing the solar arrays to track the sun; in nominal operation the alpha joint rotates by 360\xc2\xb0 each orbit (however, see also\n       <a href="/wiki/Night_Glider_mode" title="Night Glider mode">\n        Night Glider mode\n       </a>\n       ). One Solar Alpha Rotary Joint (SARJ) is located between the P3 and P4 truss segments and the other is located between the S3 and S4 truss segments. When in operation, these joints continuously rotate to keep the solar array wings on the outboard truss segments oriented towards the Sun. Each SARJ is 10 feet in diameter, weighs approximately 2,500 pounds and can be rotated continuously using bearing assemblies and a servo control system. On both the port and starboard sides, all of the power flows through the Utility Transfer Assembly (UTA) in the SARJ.\n       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Roll_ring&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Roll ring (page does not exist)">\n        Roll ring\n       </a>\n       assemblies allow transmission of data and power across the rotating interface so it never has to unwind. The SARJ was designed, built and tested by\n       <a href="/wiki/Lockheed_Martin" title="Lockheed Martin">\n        Lockheed Martin\n       </a>\n       and its subcontractors.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Presskit_10-3">\n        <a href="#cite_note-Presskit-10">\n         [10]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       The Solar Alpha Rotary Joints contain Drive Lock Assemblies which allow the outer segments of the ITS to rotate and track the\n       <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">\n        Sun\n       </a>\n       . A component of the DLA is a\n       <a href="/wiki/Pinion" title="Pinion">\n        pinion\n       </a>\n       which engages with the race ring that serves as a\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bull_gear" title="Bull gear">\n        bull gear\n       </a>\n       . There are two race rings and two DLAs in each SARJ providing on-orbit redundancy, however a series of\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Space_walks" title="Space walks">\n        space walks\n       </a>\n       would be required to reposition the DLAs and the\n       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Trundle_Bearing_Assembly&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Trundle Bearing Assembly (page does not exist)">\n        Trundle Bearing Assemblies\n       </a>\n       (TBAs) to utilize the alternate race ring. A spare DLA was brought to the ISS on\n       <a href="/wiki/STS-122" title="STS-122">\n        STS-122\n       </a>\n       .\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dla_12-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-dla-12">\n         [12]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       In 2007, a problem was detected in the starboard SARJ and in one of the two beta gimbal assemblies (BGA).\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">\n        <a href="#cite_note-13">\n         [13]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       Damage had occurred due to excessive and premature wear of a track in the joint mechanism. The SARJ was frozen during problem diagnosis, and in 2008 lubrication was applied to the track to address the issue.\n       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">\n        [\n        <i>\n         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">\n          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2011)">\n           citation needed\n          </span>\n         </a>\n        </i>\n        ]\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <h3>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Power_conditioning_and_storage">\n        Power conditioning and storage\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Integrated_Truss_Structure&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Power conditioning and storage">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h3>\n      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">\n       See also:\n       <a href="/wiki/Electrical_system_of_the_International_Space_Station" title="Electrical system of the International Space Station">\n        Electrical system of the International Space Station\n       </a>\n      </div>\n      <p>\n       The sequential shunt unit (SSU) is designed to coarsely regulate the solar power collected during periods of insolation\xe2\x80\x94when the arrays collect power during sun-pointing periods. A sequence of 82 separate strings, or power lines, leads from the solar array to the SSU. Shunting, or controlling, the output of each string regulates the amount of power transferred. The regulated voltage setpoint is controlled by a computer located on the IEA and is normally set to around 140 volts. The SSU has an overvoltage protection feature to maintain the output voltage below 200 V DC maximum for all operating conditions. This power is then passed through the BMRRM to the DCSU located in the IEA. The SSU measures 32\xe2\x80\x9d by 20\xe2\x80\x9d by 12\xe2\x80\x9d and weighs 185 pounds.\n       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">\n        [\n        <i>\n         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">\n          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2013)">\n           citation needed\n          </span>\n         </a>\n        </i>\n        ]\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       The power storage system consists of a battery charge/discharge unit (BCDU) and two\n       <a href="/wiki/Nickel%E2%80%93hydrogen_battery" title="Nickel\xe2\x80\x93hydrogen battery">\n        nickel\xe2\x80\x93hydrogen battery\n       </a>\n       assemblies.\n       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">\n        [\n        <i>\n         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">\n          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2013)">\n           citation needed\n          </span>\n         </a>\n        </i>\n        ]\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       Each battery assembly consist of 38 lightweight Nickel Hydrogen cells and associated electrical and mechanical equipment. Each battery assembly has a nameplate capacity of 81\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ampere-hour" title="Ampere-hour">\n        Ah\n       </a>\n       and 4\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Watt-hour" title="Watt-hour">\n        kWh\n       </a>\n       .\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">\n        <a href="#cite_note-14">\n         [14]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       This power is fed to the ISS via the BCDU and DCSU respectively. The batteries have a design life of 6.5 years and can exceed 38,000 charge/discharge cycles at 35% depth of discharge. Each battery measures 40\xe2\x80\x9d by 36\xe2\x80\x9d by 18\xe2\x80\x9d and weighs 375 pounds.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">\n        <a href="#cite_note-15">\n         [15]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <h3>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Mobile_Base_System">\n        Mobile Base System\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Integrated_Truss_Structure&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Mobile Base System">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h3>\n      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">\n       Main article:\n       <a href="/wiki/Mobile_Servicing_System#Mobile_Base_System" title="Mobile Servicing System">\n        Mobile Servicing System \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa0Mobile Base System\n       </a>\n      </div>\n      <p>\n       The Mobile Base System(MBS) is a platform (mounted on the\n       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Mobile_Transporter&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Mobile Transporter (page does not exist)">\n        Mobile Transporter\n       </a>\n       ) for the robotic arms\n       <a href="/wiki/Mobile_Servicing_System#Canadarm2" title="Mobile Servicing System">\n        Canadarm2\n       </a>\n       and\n       <a href="/wiki/Dextre" title="Dextre">\n        Dextre\n       </a>\n       carrying them 108 metres down rails between the S3 and P3 truss.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">\n        <a href="#cite_note-16">\n         [16]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       Beyond the rails Canadarm2 can step over the alpha rotary joint and relocate to\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Grapple_Fixture" title="Grapple Fixture">\n        grapple fixtures\n       </a>\n       on the S6 and P6 truss. During STS-120 Astronaut Scott Parazynski rode the\n       <a href="/wiki/Orbiter_Boom_Sensor_System" title="Orbiter Boom Sensor System">\n        Orbiter Boom Sensor\n       </a>\n       to repair a tear in the 4B solar array.\n      </p>\n      <h2>\n       <span id="Truss_and_solar_array_assembly_sequence_[17]">\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-headline"
