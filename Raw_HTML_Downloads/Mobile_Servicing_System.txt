Launched to the ISS in 2001, it plays a key role in station assembly and maintenance; it moves equipment and supplies around the station, supports astronauts working in space, and services instruments and other payloads attached to the ISS and is used for external maintenance. Astronauts receive specialized training to enable them to perform these functions with the various systems of the MSS.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       The MSS is composed of three components - the Space Station Remote Manipulator System (SSRMS), known as Canadarm2, the Mobile Remote Servicer Base System (MBS) and the\n       <a href="/wiki/Dextre" title="Dextre">\n        Special Purpose Dexterous Manipulator\n       </a>\n       (SPDM, also known as "Dextre" or "Canada hand"). The system can move along rails on the\n       <a href="/wiki/Integrated_Truss_Structure" title="Integrated Truss Structure">\n        Integrated Truss Structure\n       </a>\n       on top of the US provided\n       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Mobile_Transporter&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Mobile Transporter (page does not exist)">\n        Mobile Transporter\n       </a>\n       cart which hosts the MRS Base System. The system\'s control software was written in the\n       <a href="/wiki/Ada_(programming_language)" title="Ada (programming language)">\n        Ada 95 programming language\n       </a>\n       .\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">\n        <a href="#cite_note-1">\n         [1]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       The MSS was designed and manufactured by\n       <a href="/wiki/MDA_Space_Missions" title="MDA Space Missions">\n        MDA Space Missions\n       </a>\n       (previously called MD Robotics; previously called SPAR Aerospace) for the\n       <a href="/wiki/Canadian_Space_Agency" title="Canadian Space Agency">\n        Canadian Space Agency\n       </a>\n       \'s contribution to the International Space Station.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n in late 2016\n        </div>\n       </div>\n      </div>\n      <p>\n       Officially known as the\n       <b>\n        Space Station Remote Manipulator System\n       </b>\n       (SSRMS). Launched on\n       <a href="/wiki/STS-100" title="STS-100">\n        STS-100\n       </a>\n       in April 2001, this second generation arm is a larger, more advanced version of the\n       <a href="/wiki/Space_Shuttle" title="Space Shuttle">\n        Space Shuttle\n       </a>\n       \'s original\n       <a href="/wiki/Canadarm" title="Canadarm">\n        Canadarm\n       </a>\n       . Canadarm2 is 17.6\xc2\xa0m (58\xc2\xa0ft) when fully extended and has seven motorized joints (an \'elbow\' hinge in the middle, and three rotary joints at each of the \'wrist/shoulder\' ends). It has a mass of 1,800\xc2\xa0kg (4,000\xc2\xa0lb) and a diameter of 35\xc2\xa0cm (14\xc2\xa0in). The arm is capable of handling large payloads of up to 116,000\xc2\xa0kg (256,000\xc2\xa0lb) and was able to assist with docking the space shuttle. It is self-relocatable and can move end-over-end to reach many parts of the Space Station in an\n       <a href="/wiki/Geometer_moth#Caterpillars" title="Geometer moth">\n        inchworm\n       </a>\n       -like movement. In this movement, it is limited only by the number of\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Power_Data_Grapple_Fixture" title="Power Data Grapple Fixture">\n        Power Data Grapple Fixtures\n       </a>\n       (PDGFs) on the station. PDGFs located around the station provide power, data and video to the arm through either of its two\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Latching_End_Effector" title="Latching End Effector">\n        Latching End Effectors\n       </a>\n       (LEEs). The arm can also travel the entire length of the space station truss using the Mobile Base System.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       In addition to moving itself around the station, the arm can move any object with a\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Grapple_Fixture" title="Grapple Fixture">\n        grapple fixture\n       </a>\n       . In construction of the station the arm was used to move large segments into place. It can also be used to capture unpiloted ships like the\n       <a href="/wiki/SpaceX" title="SpaceX">\n        SpaceX\n       </a>\n       Dragon, the\n       <a href="/wiki/Cygnus_(spacecraft)" title="Cygnus (spacecraft)">\n        <i>\n         Cygnus\n        </i>\n        spacecraft\n       </a>\n       , and Japanese\n       <a href="/wiki/H-II_Transfer_Vehicle" title="H-II Transfer Vehicle">\n        H-II Transfer Vehicle\n       </a>\n       (HTV) which are equipped with a standard grapple fixture which the Canadarm2 uses to capture and dock the spacecraft. The arm is also used to undock and release the spacecraft after use.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       On-board operators see what they are doing by looking at the three Robotic Work Station (RWS) LCD screens. The MSS has two RWS units: one located in the\n       <a href="/wiki/Destiny_(ISS_module)" title="Destiny (ISS module)">\n        Destiny module\n       </a>\n       (US Lab module) and the other in the\n       <a href="/wiki/Cupola_(ISS_module)" title="Cupola (ISS module)">\n        Cupola\n       </a>\n       . Only one RWS controls the MSS at a time. The RWS has two sets of control joysticks: one Rotational Hand Controller (RHC) and one Translational Hand Controller (THC). In addition to this is the Display and Control Panel (DCP) and the Portable Computer System (PCS) laptop.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       In recent years, the majority of robotic operations are commanded remotely by flight controllers on the ground at\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mission_Control_Center_(NASA)" title="Mission Control Center (NASA)">\n        Mission Control Center (NASA)\n       </a>\n       , or from the\n       <a href="/wiki/Canadian_Space_Agency" title="Canadian Space Agency">\n        Canadian Space Agency\n       </a>\n       . Operators can work in shifts to accomplish objectives with more flexibility than when done by on-board crew operators, albeit at a slower pace. Astronaut operators are used for time-critical operations such as visiting vehicle captures and robotics-supported\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Extra-vehicular_activity" The arms and its power tools are capable of handling the delicate assembly tasks and changing\n       <a href="/wiki/Orbital_replacement_unit" title="Orbital replacement unit">\n        Orbital Replacement Units (ORUs)\n       </a>\n       currently handled by astronauts during space walks. Although Canadarm2 can move around the station in an "inchworm motion", it\'s unable to carry anything with it unless Dextre is attached. Testing was done in the space simulation chambers of the Canadian Space Agency\'s\n       <a href="/wiki/David_Florida_Laboratory" title="David Florida Laboratory">\n        David Florida Laboratory\n       </a>\n       in\n       <a href="/wiki/Ottawa" title="Ottawa">\n        Ottawa\n       </a>\n       . It was added to the station during\n       <a href="/wiki/STS-111" title="STS-111">\n        STS-111\n       </a>\n       in June 2002. The platform rests atop the\n       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Mobile_Transporter&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Mobile Transporter (page does not exist)">\n        Mobile Transporter\n       </a>\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nst_3-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-nst-3">\n         [3]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       (installed on\n       <a href="/wiki/STS-110" title="STS-110">\n        STS-110\n       </a>\n       , designed by\n       <a href="/wiki/Northrop_Grumman#Aerospace_Systems" title="Northrop Grumman">\n        Northrop Grumman\n       </a>\n       in\n       <a href="/wiki/Carpinteria,_California" title="Carpinteria, California">\n        Carpinteria, CA\n       </a>\n       ), which allows it to glide 108 metres down rails on the\n       <a href="/wiki/Integrated_Truss_Structure" title="Integrated Truss Structure">\n        station\'s main truss\n       </a>\n       .\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">\n        <a href="#cite_note-4">\n         [4]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       Canadarm2 can relocate by itself, but can\'t carry at the same time, Dextre can\'t relocate by itself. The MBS gives the two robotic arms the ability to travel to work sites all along the truss structure and to step off onto grapple fixtures along the way. When\n       <i>\n        Canadarm2\n       </i>\n       and\n       <i>\n        Dextre\n       </i>\n       are attached to the MBS, they have a combined mass of 4,900\xc2\xa0kg (10,800\xc2\xa0lb).\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MSS_5-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-MSS-5">\n         [5]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       Like\n       <i>\n        Canadarm2\n       </i>\n       it was built by\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/MD_Robotics" title="MD Robotics">\n        MD Robotics\n       </a>\n       and it has a minimum service life of 15 years.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MBS_features_6-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-MBS_features-6">\n         [6]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MBS_background_7-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-MBS_background-7">\n         [7]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       The MBS is equipped with 4\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Power_Data_Grapple_Fixture" title="Power Data Grapple Fixture">\n        Power Data Grapple Fixtures\n       </a>\n       , one at each of its four top corners. The first is the\n       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Payload/Orbital_Replacement_Unit_Accommodations&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Payload/Orbital Replacement Unit Accommodations (page does not exist)">\n        Payload/Orbital Replacement Unit Accommodations\n       </a>\n       (POA). It can be used to park, power and command any payload with a grapple fixture, while keeping Canadarm2 free to do something else. The other attachment location is the\n       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=MBS_Common_Attachment_System&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="MBS Common Attachment System (page does not exist)">\n        MBS Common Attachment System\n       </a>\n       (MCAS). This is another type of attachment system that is used to host scientific experiments.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MBS_features_6-1">\n        <a href="#cite_note-MBS_features-6">\n         [6]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       The MBS also supports astronauts during\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Extra-vehicular_activity" title="Extra-vehicular activity">\n        extra-vehicular activities\n       </a>\n       . It has locations to store tools and equipment, foot-restraints, handrails and safety tether attachment points as well as a camera assembly. If needed, it is even possible for an astronaut to "ride" the MBS while it moves at a top speed of about 1.5 meters per minute.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nst_3-1">\n        <a href="#cite_note-nst-3">\n         [3]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       On either side of the MBS are the Crew and Equipment Translation Aids. These carts ride on the same rails as the MBS. Astronauts ride them manually during EVAs to transport equipment and to facilitate their movements around the station.\n      </p>\n      <div class="thumb tnone" style="margin-left: auto;
