For the 2007 film, see\n       <a href="/wiki/Decaying_Orbit_(film)" title="Decaying Orbit (film)">\n        Decaying Orbit (film)\n       </a>\n       .\n      </div>\n      <p>\n       In\n       <a href="/wiki/Orbital_mechanics" title="Orbital mechanics">\n        orbital mechanics\n       </a>\n       ,\n       <b>\n        decay\n       </b>\n       is a process that leads to gradual decrease of the\n       <a href="/wiki/Distance" title="Distance">\n        distance\n       </a>\n       between two\n       <a href="/wiki/Orbit" title="Orbit">\n        orbiting\n       </a>\n       bodies at their closest approach (the\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Periapsis" title="Periapsis">\n        periapsis\n       </a>\n       ) over many orbital periods. These orbiting bodies can be a\n       <a href="/wiki/Planet" title="Planet">\n        planet\n       </a>\n       and its\n       <a href="/wiki/Satellite" title="Satellite">\n        satellite\n       </a>\n       , a\n       <a href="/wiki/Star" title="Star">\n        star\n       </a>\n       and any object orbiting it, or components of any\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Binary_system_(astronomy)" title="Binary system (astronomy)">\n        binary system\n       </a>\n       . Orbits do not decay without some friction-like mechanism which robs energy from the orbital motion. This can be any of a number of\n       <a href="/wiki/Mechanics" title="Mechanics">\n        mechanical\n       </a>\n       ,\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gravitation" title="Gravitation">\n        gravitational\n       </a>\n       , or\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electromagnetic_interaction" title="Electromagnetic interaction">\n        electromagnetic\n       </a>\n       effects. For bodies in a\n       <a href="/wiki/Low_Earth_orbit" title="Low Earth orbit">\n        low Earth orbit\n       </a>\n       , the most significant effect is the\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atmospheric_drag" title="Atmospheric drag">\n        atmospheric drag\n       </a>\n       .\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       If left unchecked, the decay eventually results in termination of the orbit when the smaller object\n       <a href="/wiki/Collision" title="Collision">\n        strikes\n       </a>\n       the surface of the primary; or for objects where the primary has an atmosphere, the smaller object\n       <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_entry" title="Atmospheric entry">\n        burns, explodes, or otherwise breaks up\n       </a>\n       in the larger object\'s\n       <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere" title="Atmosphere">\n        atmosphere\n       </a>\n       ; or for objects where the primary is a star, ends with incineration by the star\'s radiation (such as for\n       <a href="/wiki/Comet" title="Comet">\n        comets\n       </a>\n       ), and so on.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       Collisions and mergers of two stellar-mass objects usually produce cataclysmic effects;\n       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">\n        [\n        <i>\n         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">\n          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources in the body of the article. (November 2014)">\n           not verified in body\n          </span>\n         </a>\n        </i>\n        ]\n       </sup>\n       see\n       <a href="/wiki/Stellar_collision" title="Stellar collision">\n        stellar collision\n       </a>\n       and\n       <a href="/wiki/Gamma-ray_burst" title="Gamma-ray burst">\n        gamma-ray burst\n       </a>\n       .\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       Due to atmospheric drag, the lowest altitude above the\n       <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">\n        Earth\n       </a>\n       at which an object in a circular orbit can complete at least one full revolution without propulsion is approximately 150\xc2\xa0km (90\xc2\xa0mi).\n      </p>\n      <p>\n      </p>\n      <div class="toc" id="toc">\n       <div class="toctitle">\n </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h2>\n      <h3>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Atmospheric_drag">\n        Atmospheric drag\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Orbital_decay&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Atmospheric drag">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h3>\n      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">\n       Further information:\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atmospheric_drag" title="Atmospheric drag">\n        Atmospheric drag\n       </a>\n      </div>\n      <p>\n       Atmospheric drag at orbital altitude is caused by frequent collisions of gas\n       <a href="/wiki/Molecule" title="Molecule">\n        molecules\n       </a>\n       with the satellite. It is the major cause of orbital decay for satellites in\n       <a href="/wiki/Low_Earth_orbit" title="Low Earth orbit">\n        low Earth orbit\n       </a>\n       . It results in the reduction in the\n       <a href="/wiki/Altitude" title="Altitude">\n        altitude\n       </a>\n       of a satellite\'s orbit. For the case of Earth, atmospheric drag resulting in satellite re-entry can be described by the following sequence:\n      </p>\n      <dl>\n       <dd>\n        lower altitude \xe2\x86\x92 denser atmosphere \xe2\x86\x92 increased drag \xe2\x86\x92 increased heat \xe2\x86\x92 usually burns on re-entry\n       </dd>\n      </dl>\n      <p>\n       Orbital decay thus involves a\n       <a href="/wiki/Positive_feedback" title="Positive feedback">\n        positive feedback\n       </a>\n       effect, where the more the orbit decays, the lower its altitude drops, and the lower the altitude, the faster the decay. Decay is also particularly sensitive to external factors of the space environment such as solar activity, which are not very predictable. During\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sunspots" title="Sunspots">\n        solar maxima\n       </a>\n       the Earth\'s atmosphere causes significant drag up to a hundred kilometers higher than during\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solar_minima" title="Solar minima">\n        solar minima\n       </a>\n       .\n       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">\n        [\n        <i>\n         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">\n          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2010)">\n           citation needed\n          </span>\n         </a>\n        </i>\n        ]\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       Atmospheric drag exerts a significant effect at the altitudes of\n       <a href="/wiki/Space_station" title="Space station">\n        space stations\n       </a>\n       ,\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Space_shuttle" title="Space shuttle">\n        space shuttles\n       </a>\n       and other manned Earth-orbit spacecraft, and satellites with relatively high "low earth orbits" such as the\n       <a href="/wiki/Hubble_Space_Telescope" title="Hubble Space Telescope">\n        Hubble Space Telescope\n       </a>\n       . Space stations typically require a regular altitude boost to counteract orbital decay (see also\n       <a href="/wiki/Orbital_station-keeping" title="Orbital station-keeping">\n        orbital station-keeping\n       </a>\n       ). Uncontrolled orbital decay brought down the\n       <a href="/wiki/Skylab" title="Skylab">\n        Skylab\n       </a>\n       space station, and (relatively) controlled orbital decay was used to de-orbit the\n       <a href="/wiki/Mir" title="Mir">\n        Mir\n       </a>\n       space station.\n       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">\n        [\n        <i>\n         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">\n          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2016)">\n           citation needed\n          </span>\n         </a>\n        </i>\n        ]\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       Regular orbital boosts are also needed by the Hubble Space Telescope, though on a longer time scale, due to its much higher altitude. However, orbital decay is also a limiting factor to the length of time the Hubble can go without a maintenance rendezvous, the most recent performed successfully by\n       <a href="/wiki/STS-125" title="STS-125">\n        STS-125\n       </a>\n       , with space shuttle\n       <i>\n        Atlantis\n       </i>\n       launching May 11, 2009, though newer telescopes are in much higher orbits or in some cases in solar orbit, so orbital boosting may not be needed.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">\n        <a href="#cite_note-1">\n         [1]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n Ideally, energy absorbed would equal\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Blackbody" title="Blackbody">\n        blackbody\n       </a>\n       energy emitted at any given point, resulting in no net force. However, the Yarkovsky effect is the phenomenon that, because absorption and radiation of heat are not instantaneous, objects which are not terminally locked absorb sunlight energy on surfaces exposed to the Sun, but those surfaces do not re-emit much of that energy until after the object has rotated, so that the emission is parallel to the object\'s orbit. This results in a very small acceleration parallel to the orbital path, yet one which can be significant for small objects over millions of years. The Poynting-Robertson effect is a force opposing the object\'s velocity caused by asymmetric incidence of light, i.e.,\n       <a href="/wiki/Aberration_of_light" title="Aberration of light">\n        aberration of light\n       </a>\n       . For an object with prograde rotation, these two effects will apply opposing, but generally unequal, forces.\n      </p>\n      <h3>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Gravitational_radiation">\n        Gravitational radiation\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Orbital_decay&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Gravitational radiation">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h3>\n      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">\n       Main article:\n       <a href="/wiki/Two-body_problem_in_general_relativity" title="Two-body problem in general relativity">\n        Two-body problem in general relativity\n       </a>\n      </div>\n      <p>\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gravitational_radiation" title="Gravitational radiation">\n        Gravitational radiation\n       </a>\n       is another mechanism of orbital decay. It is negligible for orbits of planets and planetary satellites, but is noticeable for systems of\n       <a href="/wiki/Compact_star" title="Compact star">\n        compact objects\n       </a>\n       , as seen in observations of neutron star orbits.\n      </p>\n      <h2>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Stellar_collision">\n        Stellar collision\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Orbital_decay&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Stellar collision">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h2>\n      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">\n       Further information:\n       <a href="/wiki/Stellar_collision" title="Stellar collision">\n        Stellar collision\n       </a>\n      </div>\n      <p>\n       The coming together of two\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Binary_stars" title="Binary stars">\n        binary stars\n       </a>\n       when they lose energy and approach each other. Several things can cause the loss of energy including\n       <a href="/wiki/Tidal_force" title="Tidal force">\n        tidal forces\n       </a>\n       ,\n       <a href="/wiki/Roche_lobe" title="Roche lobe">\n        mass transfer\n       </a>\n       , and\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gravitational_radiation" title="Gravitational radiation">\n        gravitational radiation\n       </a>\n       . The stars describe the path of a\n       <a href="/wiki/Spiral" title="Spiral">\n        spiral\n       </a>\n       as they approach each other. This sometimes results in a merger of the two stars or the creation of a\n       <a href="/wiki/Black_hole" title="Black hole">\n        black hole\n       </a>\n       . The last several revolutions of the stars around each other take only a few seconds in the latter case.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">\n        <a href="#cite_note-2">\n         [2]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <h2>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Mass_concentration">\n        Mass concentration\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Orbital_decay&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Mass concentration">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h2>\n      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">\n       Further information:\n       <a href="/wiki/Mass_concentration_(astronomy)" title="Mass concentration (astronomy)">\n        Mass concentration (astronomy)\n       </a>\n      </div>\n      <p>\n       While not a direct cause of orbital decay, uneven mass distributions (known as\n       <i>\n        mascons\n       </i>\n       ) of the body being orbited can perturb orbits over time, and extreme distributions can cause orbits to be highly unstable. The resulting unstable orbit can mutate into an orbit where one of the direct causes of orbital decay can take place.\n      </p>\n      <h2>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="References">\n        References\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Orbital_decay&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: References">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h2>\n      <div class="reflist" style="list-style-type: decimal;">\n       <div class="mw-references-wrap">\n        <ol class="references">\n         <li id="cite_note-1">\n          <span class="mw-cite-backlink">\n           <b>\n            <a href="#cite_ref-1">\n             ^\n            </a>\n           </b>\n          </span>\n          <span class="reference-text">\n           <a class="external text" href="http://hubble.nasa.gov/missions/sm4.html" rel="nofollow">\n            The Hubble Program - Servicing Missions - SM4\n           </a>\n          </span>\n         </li>\n         <li id="cite_note-2">\n          <span class="mw-cite-backlink">\n           <b>\n            <a href="#cite_ref-2">\n             ^\n            </a>\n           </b>\n          </span>\n          <span class="reference-text">\n           <cite class="citation web">\n            <a class="external text" href="http://www.ligo.org/science/GW-Inspiral.php" rel="nofollow">\n             "INSPIRAL GRAVITATIONAL WAVES"\n            </a>\n            .\n            <i>\n             LIGO\n            </i>\n            <span class="reference-accessdate">\n             .
