"Billy" Tyne Jr.\n       </a>\n       headed east to the\n       <a href="/wiki/Flemish_Cap" title="Flemish Cap">\n        Flemish Cap\n       </a>\n       where he believed they would have better luck. Despite weather reports warning of dangerous conditions, Tyne set course for home on October 26\xe2\x80\x9327.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cg_2-1">\n        <a href="#cite_note-cg-2">\n         [2]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       The ship\'s ice machine was malfunctioning and would not have been able to maintain the catch for much longer.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">\n        <a href="#cite_note-3">\n         [3]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <h3>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Disappearance">\n        Disappearance\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Andrea_Gail&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Disappearance">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h3>\n      <p>\n       The last reported transmission from\n       <i>\n        Andrea Gail\n       </i>\n       was at about 6:00\xc2\xa0p.m. on October 28, 1991. Captain Tyne radioed Linda Greenlaw, Captain of the\n       <i>\n        Hannah Boden\n       </i>\n       , owned by the same company, and gave his coordinates as\n       <span class="plainlinks nourlexpansion">\n        <a class="external text" href="//tools.wmflabs.org/geohack/geohack.php?pagename=Andrea_Gail&amp;params=44_00_N_56_40_W_">\n         <span class="geo-default">\n          <span class="geo-dms" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location">\n           <span class="latitude">\n            44\xc2\xb000\xe2\x80\xb2N\n           </span>\n           <span class="longitude">\n            56\xc2\xb040\xe2\x80\xb2W\n           </span>\n          </span>\n         </span>\n         <span class="geo-multi-punct">\n          \xef\xbb\xbf / \xef\xbb\xbf\n         </span>\n         <span class="geo-nondefault">\n          <span class="geo-dec" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location">\n           44.000\xc2\xb0N 56.667\xc2\xb0W\n          </span>\n          <span style="display:none">\n           \xef\xbb\xbf /\n           <span class="geo">\n            44.000; -56.667\n           </span>\n          </span>\n         </span>\n        </a>\n       </span>\n       ,\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cg_2-2">\n        <a href="#cite_note-cg-2">\n         [2]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       or about 162\xc2\xa0mi (261\xc2\xa0km) east of\n       <a href="/wiki/Sable_Island" title="Sable Island">\n        Sable Island\n       </a>\n       . He also gave a weather report indicating 30-foot (9.1\xc2\xa0m) seas and wind gusts up to 80\n       <a href="/wiki/Knot_(unit)" title="Knot (unit)">\n        knots\n       </a>\n       (150\xc2\xa0km/h (93\xc2\xa0mph)). Tyne\'s final recorded words were "She\'s comin\' on, boys, and she\'s comin\' on strong." Junger reported that the storm created waves in excess of 100\xc2\xa0ft (30\xc2\xa0m) in height, but ocean buoy monitors recorded a peak wave height of 39 feet (12\xc2\xa0m), and so waves of 100\xc2\xa0ft (30\xc2\xa0m) were deemed "unlikely" by\n       <i>\n        <a href="/wiki/Science_Daily" title="Science Daily">\n         Science Daily\n        </a>\n       </i>\n       .\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">\n        <a href="#cite_note-4">\n         [4]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       However, data from a series of weather buoys in the general vicinity of the vessel\'s last known location recorded peak wave action exceeding 60\xc2\xa0ft (18\xc2\xa0m) in height from October 28 through 30, 1991.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cg_2-3">\n        <a href="#cite_note-cg-2">\n         [2]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <h3>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Search">\n        Search\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Andrea_Gail&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Search">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h3>\n      <p>\n       On October 30, 1991, the vessel was reported overdue. An extensive air and land search was launched by the 106th Rescue Wing from the\n       <a href="/wiki/New_York_Air_National_Guard" title="New York Air National Guard">\n        New York Air National Guard\n       </a>\n       ,\n       <a href="/wiki/United_States_Coast_Guard" title="United States Coast Guard">\n        United States Coast Guard\n       </a>\n       and\n       <a href="/wiki/Canadian_Coast_Guard" title="Canadian Coast Guard">\n        Canadian Coast Guard\n       </a>\n       forces. The search would eventually cover over 186,000 square nautical miles (640,000\xc2\xa0km\n       <sup>\n        2\n       </sup>\n       ).\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">\n        <a href="#cite_note-5">\n         [5]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       On November 6, 1991,\n       <i>\n        Andrea Gail\'s\n       </i>\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/EPIRB" title="EPIRB">\n        emergency position-indicating radio beacon\n       </a>\n       (EPIRB) was discovered washed up on the shore of\n       <a href="/wiki/Sable_Island" title="Sable Island">\n        Sable Island\n       </a>\n       in\n       <a href="/wiki/Nova_Scotia" title="Nova Scotia">\n        Nova Scotia\n       </a>\n       . The EPIRB was designed to automatically send out a distress signal upon contact with sea water, but the Canadian Coast Guard personnel who found the beacon "did not conclusively verify whether the control switch was in the on or off position".\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cg_2-4">\n        <a href="#cite_note-cg-2">\n         [2]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       Authorities called off the search for the missing vessel on November 9, 1991, due to the low probability of crew survival.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cg_2-5">\n        <a href="#cite_note-cg-2">\n         [2]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       Fuel drums, a fuel tank, the\n       <a href="/wiki/Emergency_position-indicating_radiobeacon_station" title="Emergency position-indicating radiobeacon station">\n        EPIRB\n       </a>\n       , an empty\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Life_raft" title="Life raft">\n        life raft\n       </a>\n       , and some other\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Flotsam" title="Flotsam">\n        flotsam\n       </a>\n       were the only wreckage ever found.
