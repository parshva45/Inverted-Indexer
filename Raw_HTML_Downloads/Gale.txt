  </div>\n      <p>\n       The U.S.\n       <a href="/wiki/National_Weather_Service" title="National Weather Service">\n        National Weather Service\n       </a>\n       defines a gale as 34\xe2\x80\x9347\n       <a href="/wiki/Knot_(unit)" title="Knot (unit)">\n        knots\n       </a>\n       (\n       <span class="nowrap">\n        63\xe2\x80\x9387 km/h\n       </span>\n       ,\n       <span class="nowrap">\n        17.5\xe2\x80\x9324.2 m/s\n       </span>\n       or\n       <span class="nowrap">\n        39\xe2\x80\x9354 miles/hour\n       </span>\n       ) of sustained surface winds.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-noaa_glossary_1-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-noaa_glossary-1">\n         [1]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       Forecasters typically issue\n       <a href="/wiki/Gale_warning" title="Gale warning">\n        gale warnings\n       </a>\n       when winds of this strength are expected.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       Other sources use minima as low as 28 knots (52\xc2\xa0km/h, 32\xc2\xa0mph) and maxima as high as 90 knots (170\xc2\xa0km/h, 100\xc2\xa0mph). Through 1986, the\n       <a href="/wiki/National_Hurricane_Center" title="National Hurricane Center">\n        National Hurricane Center\n       </a>\n       used the term gale to refer to winds of\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tropical" title="Tropical">\n        tropical\n       </a>\n       force for coastal areas, between 33 knots (61\xc2\xa0km/h, 38\xc2\xa0mph) and 63 knots (117\xc2\xa0km/h, 72\xc2\xa0mph). The 90 knots (170\xc2\xa0km/h, 100\xc2\xa0mph) definition is very non-standard. A common alternative definition of the maximum is 55 knots (102\xc2\xa0km/h, 63\xc2\xa0mph).\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">\n        <a href="#cite_note-2">\n         [2]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       The most common way of measuring winds is with the\n       <a href="/wiki/Beaufort_scale" title="Beaufort scale">\n        Beaufort scale\n       </a>\n       ,\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">\n        <a href="#cite_note-3">\n         [3]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       which defines a gale as wind from 50 to 102\xc2\xa0km/h. It is an empirical measure for describing wind speed based mainly on observed sea conditions. Its full name is the Beaufort Wind Force Scale.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       On the\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Beaufort_Wind_Scale" title="Beaufort Wind Scale">\n        Beaufort Wind Scale\n       </a>\n       , a gale is classified as: 7: Moderate\xc2\xa0Gale (32\xe2\x80\x9338 miles per hour), 8: Fresh\xc2\xa0Gale (39-46\xc2\xa0mph), 9: Strong\xc2\xa0Gale (47-54\xc2\xa0mph) and 10: Storm/Whole\xc2\xa0Gale (55-63\xc2\xa0mph). A gale is a type of Wind Description preceded by 0: Calm, 1: Light Air, 2: Light Breeze, 3: Gentle Breeze, 4: Moderate Breeze, 5: Fresh Breeze, 6: Strong Breeze and succeeded by 11: Violent Storm and 12: Hurricane on a Beaufort Wind Scale. There is a unique Beaufort Scale number and a unique Arrow Indication for each type of Wind Description mentioned above.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       The word\n       <i>\n        gale\n       </i>\n       is derived from the older\n       <i>\n        gail\n       </i>\n       , but its origin is uncertain.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">\n        <a href="#cite_note-4">\n
