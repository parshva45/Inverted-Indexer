      </div>\n      </div>\n      <p>\n       A\n       <b>\n        launch pad\n       </b>\n       is an above-ground platform from which a\n       <a href="/wiki/Rocket" title="Rocket">\n        rocket\n       </a>\n       -powered\n       <a href="/wiki/Missile" title="Missile">\n        missile\n       </a>\n       or\n       <a href="/wiki/Space_vehicle" title="Space vehicle">\n        space vehicle\n       </a>\n       is vertically launched. A\n       <a href="/wiki/Spaceport" title="Spaceport">\n        spaceport\n       </a>\n       (or\n       <b>\n        launch complex\n       </b>\n       ) is a facility which includes, and provides required support for, one or more launch pads. A launch pad generally contains a fixed or mobile\n       <a href="/wiki/Service_structure" title="Service structure">\n        service structure\n       </a>\n       , which provides one or more access platforms to inspect and maintain the vehicle, and an\n       <i>\n        umbilical structure\n       </i>\n       which provides the vehicle with\n       <a href="/wiki/Liquid-propellant_rocket" title="Liquid-propellant rocket">\n        propellants\n       </a>\n       ,\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cryogenic" title="Cryogenic">\n        cryogenic\n       </a>\n       fluids, electrical power, communications, and\n       <a href="/wiki/Telemetry" title="Telemetry">\n        telemetry\n       </a>\n       prior to launch. The service structure also provides access to the crew cabin for vehicles carrying humans. The pad may contain a flame deflection structure to prevent the intense heat of the rocket exhaust from damaging the vehicle or pad structures, and a sound suppression system spraying large quantities of water may be employed. The pad may also be protected by\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lightning_arrestor" title="Lightning arrestor">\n        lightning arrestors\n       </a>\n       .\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       A launch pad is distinct from a\n       <a href="/wiki/Missile_launch_facility" title="Missile launch facility">\n        missile launch facility\n       </a>\n       (or\n       <b>\n        missile silo\n       </b>\n       ), which also launches a missile vertically but is located underground in order to help harden it against enemy attack, or conceal it from surveillance.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cryogenic_propellant" title="Cryogenic propellant">\n        Cryogenic propellants\n       </a>\n       (\n       <a href="/wiki/Liquid_oxygen" title="Liquid oxygen">\n        liquid oxygen\n       </a>\n       oxidizer, and\n       <a href="/wiki/Liquid_hydrogen" title="Liquid hydrogen">\n        liquid hydrogen\n       </a>\n       fuel) need to be continuously topped off (i.e., boil-off replaced) during the launch sequence (\n       <a href="/wiki/Countdown" title="Countdown">\n        countdown\n       </a>\n       ), as the vehicle awaits liftoff. This becomes particularly important as complex sequences may be interrupted by planned or unplanned holds to fix problems.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       Most rockets need stable support for a few seconds after ignition while the engines build up to stable, full\n       <a href="/wiki/Thrust" title="Thrust">\n        thrust\n       </a>\n       . Therefore, the vehicle is commonly held on the pad by hold-down arms or\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Explosive_bolt" title="Explosive bolt">\n        explosive bolts\n       </a>\n       , which are triggered when the vehicle is stable and ready to fly, at which point all umbilical connections with the pad are released.\n      </p>\n      <h2>\n       <span class="mw-headline" </div>\n       </div>\n      </div>\n      <p>\n       There are several different types of launch site, determined by the means by which the\n       <a href="/wiki/Space_vehicle" title="Space vehicle">\n        space vehicle\n       </a>\n       gets to the pad.\n      </p>\n      <ul>\n       <li>\n        The first large rocket, the\n        <a href="/wiki/V-2_rocket" title="V-2 rocket">\n         V-2\n        </a>\n        , travelled horizontally with its tail forward to the launch site at\n        <a href="/wiki/Peenem%C3%BCnde" title="Peenem\xc3\xbcnde">\n         Peenem\xc3\xbcnde\n        </a>\n        . This method was used for all large Soviet rockets, including the\n        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/N1_rocket" title="N1 rocket">\n         N1\n        </a>\n        and\n        <a href="/wiki/Energia" title="Energia">\n         Energia\n        </a>\n        .\n       </li>\n       <li>\n        In a similar manner, at the Soviet launch site near\n        <a href="/wiki/Volgograd" title="Volgograd">\n         Volgograd\n        </a>\n        , a silo used to launch test rockets would have its top opened and a second stage and payload would be driven in horizontally and tilted on top of a first stage already in the silo, the nose cone and some of the second stage remaining visible above ground. Hence no surface pad is used; Russian silos are reusable. This method was only used for the Cosmos series of small satellite launching vehicles.\n       </li>\n       <li>\n        A method of assembling a large space vehicle vertically in a\n        <a href="/wiki/Vehicle_Assembly_Building" title="Vehicle Assembly Building">\n         Vehicle Assembly Building\n        </a>\n        on a\n        <a href="/wiki/Mobile_Launcher_Platform" title="Mobile Launcher Platform">\n         Mobile Launcher Platform\n        </a>\n        (MLP), which contained the umbilical structure, was invented for the\n        <a href="/wiki/Apollo_(spacecraft)" title="Apollo (spacecraft)">\n         Apollo\n        </a>\n        /\n        <a href="/wiki/Saturn_V" title="Saturn V">\n         Saturn V\n        </a>\n        manned Moon landing vehicle. The MLP is then carried by a\n        <a href="/wiki/Crawler-transporter" title="Crawler-transporter">\n         Crawler-transporter\n        </a>\n        , which slowly drives to one of two pads at\n        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Launch_Complex_39" title="Launch Complex 39">\n         Launch Complex 39\n        </a>\n        at the\n        <a href="/wiki/Kennedy_Space_Center" title="Kennedy Space Center">\n         Kennedy Space Center\n        </a>\n        . This facility was later used for the smaller Apollo/\n        <a href="/wiki/Saturn_IB" title="Saturn IB">\n         Saturn IB\n        </a>\n        , and then the\n        <a href="/wiki/Space_Shuttle" title="Space Shuttle">\n         Space Shuttle\n        </a>\n        .\n        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">\n         <a href="#cite_note-1">\n          [1]\n         </a>\n        </sup>\n        A similar system is used to launch\n        <a href="/wiki/Ariane_5" title="Ariane 5">\n         Ariane 5\n        </a>\n        rockets at\n        <a href="/wiki/ELA-3" title="ELA-3">\n         ELA-3\n        </a>\n        at\n        <a href="/wiki/Guiana_Space_Centre" title="Guiana Space Centre">\n         Guiana Space Centre\n        </a>\n        , a French spaceport near\n        <a href="/wiki/Kourou" title="Kourou">\n         Kourou\n        </a>\n        in\n        <a href="/wiki/French_Guiana" title="French Guiana">\n         French Guiana\n        </a>\n        .\n       </li>\n       <li>\n        The\n        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Titan_III" title="Titan III">\n         Titan III\n        </a>\n        and\n        <a href="/wiki/Titan_IV" title="Titan IV">\n         Titan IV\n        </a>\n        launch vehicles were transported with a mobile launcher platform on two parallel standard gauge railroad tracks from the integration building to launch areas at\n        <a href="/wiki/Cape_Canaveral_Air_Force_Station_Space_Launch_Complex_40" title="Cape Canaveral Air Force Station Space Launch Complex 40">\n         Cape Canaveral Air Force Station Space Launch Complex 40\n        </a>\n        and 41.
