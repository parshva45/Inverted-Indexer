<h2>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Geography">\n        Geography\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lake_Ontario&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Geography">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h2>\n      <div class="thumb tright">\n       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">\n        <a class="image"    </a>\n         </div>\n         Lake Ontario Basin\n        </div>\n       </div>\n      </div>\n      <div class="thumb tright">\n       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">\n        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Fort_Ontario_5.jpg">\n         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2608" data-file-width="3515" height="163" </div>\n      <div class="thumb tright">\n       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">\n        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Fort_Niagara_aiming_at_Toronto.jpg">\n         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1221" data-file-width="1831" height="147" //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3c/Fort_Niagara_aiming_at_Toronto.jpg/440px-Fort_Niagara_aiming_at_Toronto.jpg 2x" width="220"/>\n        </a>\n        <div class="thumbcaption">\n         <div class="magnify">\n          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Fort_Niagara_aiming_at_Toronto.jpg" title="Enlarge">\n          </a>\n         </div>\n         Historic cannon at\n         <a href="/wiki/Fort_Niagara" title="Fort Niagara">\n          Fort Niagara\n         </a>\n         ; Toronto across the lake\n        </div>\n       </div>\n      </div>\n      <p>\n       Lake Ontario is the easternmost of the Great Lakes and the smallest in surface area (7,340\xc2\xa0sq\xc2\xa0mi, 18,960\xc2\xa0km\n       <sup>\n        2\n       </sup>\n       ),\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EPAphysical_1-2">\n        <a href="#cite_note-EPAphysical-1">\n         [1]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       although it exceeds\n       <a href="/wiki/Lake_Erie" title="Lake Erie">\n        Lake Erie\n       </a>\n       in volume (393 cu\xc2\xa0mi, 1,639\xc2\xa0km\n       <sup>\n        3\n       </sup>\n       ). It is the\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Largest_lakes" title="Largest lakes">\n        14th largest lake in the world\n       </a>\n       . When its islands are included, the lake has a shoreline that is 712 miles (1,146\xc2\xa0km) long. As the last lake in the Great Lakes\' hydrologic chain, Lake Ontario has the lowest mean surface elevation of the lakes at 243 feet (74\xc2\xa0m)\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EPA_2-6">\n        <a href="#cite_note-EPA-2">\n         [2]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       above sea level; 326 feet (99\xc2\xa0m) lower than its\n       <a href="/wiki/Lake_Erie" title="Lake Erie">\n        neighbor upstream\n       </a>\n       . Its maximum length is 193\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Statute_mile" title="Statute mile">\n        statute miles\n       </a>\n       (311\n       <a href="/wiki/Kilometre" title="Kilometre">\n        kilometres\n       </a>\n       ; 168\n       <a href="/wiki/Nautical_mile" title="Nautical mile">\n        nautical miles\n       </a>\n       ) and its maximum width is 53 statute miles (85\xc2\xa0km; 46\xc2\xa0nmi).\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EPA_2-7">\n        <a href="#cite_note-EPA-2">\n         [2]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       The lake\'s average depth is 47\n       <a href="/wiki/Fathom" title="Fathom">\n        fathoms\n       </a>\n       1 foot (283\xc2\xa0ft; 86 m), with a maximum depth of 133 fathoms 4 feet (802\xc2\xa0ft; 244 m).\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EPA_2-8">\n        <a href="#cite_note-EPA-2">\n         [2]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWright200664_3-3">\n        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWright200664-3">\n         [3]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       The lake\'s primary source is the\n       <a href="/wiki/Niagara_River" title="Niagara River">\n        Niagara River\n       </a>\n       , draining Lake Erie, with the St. Lawrence River serving as the outlet. The drainage basin covers 24,720\xc2\xa0square\xc2\xa0miles (64,030\xc2\xa0km\n       <sup>\n        2\n       </sup>\n       ).\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EPAphysical_1-3">\n        <a href="#cite_note-EPAphysical-1">\n         [1]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">\n        <a href="#cite_note-5">\n         [5]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       As with all the Great Lakes, water levels change both within the year (owing to seasonal changes in water input) and among years (owing to longer term trends in precipitation). These water level fluctuations are an integral part of lake ecology, and produce and maintain extensive wetlands.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">\n        <a href="#cite_note-6">\n         [6]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">\n        <a href="#cite_note-7">\n         [7]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       The lake also has an important freshwater fishery, although it has been negatively affected by factors including over-fishing, water pollution and invasive species.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Christie.2C_W._J._1974_8-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-Christie.2C_W._J._1974-8">\n         [8]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       <a href="/wiki/Baymouth_bar" title="Baymouth bar">\n        Baymouth bars\n       </a>\n       built by prevailing winds and currents have created a significant number of\n       <a href="/wiki/Lagoon" title="Lagoon">\n        lagoons\n       </a>\n       and sheltered harbors, mostly near (but not limited to)\n       <a href="/wiki/Prince_Edward_County,_Ontario" title="Prince Edward County, Ontario">\n        Prince Edward County, Ontario\n       </a>\n       and the easternmost shores. Perhaps the best-known example is\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Toronto_Bay" title="Toronto Bay">\n        Toronto Bay\n       </a>\n       , chosen as the site of the\n       <a href="/wiki/Upper_Canada" title="Upper Canada">\n        Upper Canada\n       </a>\n       (Ontario) capital for its strategic harbour. Other prominent examples include\n       <a href="/wiki/Hamilton_Harbour" title="Hamilton Harbour">\n        Hamilton Harbour\n       </a>\n       ,\n       <a href="/wiki/Irondequoit_Bay" title="Irondequoit Bay">\n        Irondequoit Bay\n       </a>\n       ,\n       <a href="/wiki/Presqu%27ile_Provincial_Park" title="Presqu\'ile Provincial Park">\n        Presqu\'ile Bay\n       </a>\n       , and\n       <a href="/wiki/Sodus_Bay" title="Sodus Bay">\n        Sodus Bay\n       </a>\n       . The bars themselves are the sites of long beaches, such as\n       <a href="/wiki/Sandbanks_Provincial_Park" title="Sandbanks Provincial Park">\n        Sandbanks Provincial Park\n       </a>\n       and\n       <a href="/wiki/Sandy_Island_Beach_State_Park" title="Sandy Island Beach State Park">\n        Sandy Island Beach State Park\n       </a>\n       . These sand bars are often associated with large\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wetlands" title="Wetlands">\n        wetlands\n       </a>\n       , which support large numbers of plant and animal species, as well as providing important rest areas for migratory birds.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">\n        <a href="#cite_note-9">\n         [9]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">\n        <a href="#cite_note-10">\n         [10]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       Presqu\'ile, on the north shore of Lake Ontario, is particularly significant in this regard. One unique feature of the lake is the Z-shaped\n       <a href="/wiki/Bay_of_Quinte" title="Bay of Quinte">\n        Bay of Quinte\n       </a>\n       which separates Prince Edward County from the Ontario mainland, save for a 2-mile (3.2\xc2\xa0km) isthmus near Trenton; this feature also supports many wetlands and aquatic plants, as well as associated fisheries.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       Major rivers draining into Lake Ontario include the Niagara River,\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Don_River_(Toronto)" title="Don River (Toronto)">\n        Don River\n       </a>\n (Ontario)">\n        Trent River\n       </a>\n       ,\n       <a href="/wiki/Cataraqui_River" title="Cataraqui River">\n        Cataraqui River\n       </a>\n       ,\n       <a href="/wiki/Genesee_River" title="Genesee River">\n        Genesee River\n       </a>\n       ,\n       <a href="/wiki/Oswego_River_(New_York)" title="Oswego River (New York)">\n        Oswego River\n       </a>\n       ,\n       <a href="/wiki/Black_River_(New_York)" title="Black River (New York)">\n        Black River\n       </a>\n       ,\n       <a title="Salmon River (New York)">\n        Salmon River\n       </a>\n       .\n      </p>\n      <h2>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Geology">\n        Geology\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lake_Ontario&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Geology">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h2>\n      <div class="thumb tright">\n       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">\n        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Lake_Ontario_bathymetry_map.png">\n         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="656" data-file-width="1024" height="141" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/32/Lake_Ontario_bathymetry_map.png/220px-Lake_Ontario_bathymetry_map.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/32/Lake_Ontario_bathymetry_map.png/330px-Lake_Ontario_bathymetry_map.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/32/Lake_Ontario_bathymetry_map.png/440px-Lake_Ontario_bathymetry_map.png 2x" width="220"/>\n        </a>\n        <div class="thumbcaption">\n         <div class="magnify">\n          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Lake_Ontario_bathymetry_map.png" title="Enlarge">\n          </a>\n         </div>\n         Lake Ontario\n         <a href="/wiki/Bathymetry" title="Bathymetry">\n          bathymetric\n         </a>\n         map.\n         <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GLBathOnt_11-0">\n          <a href="#cite_note-GLBathOnt-11">\n           [11]\n          </a>\n         </sup>\n         <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GLBathEr_12-0">\n          <a href="#cite_note-GLBathEr-12">\n           [12]\n          </a>\n         </sup>\n         <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GLOBE_13-0">\n          <a href="#cite_note-GLOBE-13">\n           [13]\n          </a>\n         </sup>\n         The deepest point is marked with "\xc3\x97".\n         <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NOAA_GLERL_14-0">\n          <a href="#cite_note-NOAA_GLERL-14">\n           [14]\n          </a>\n         </sup>\n        </div>\n       </div>\n      </div>\n      <p>\n       The lake basin was carved out of soft, weak\n       <a href="/wiki/Silurian" title="Silurian">\n        Silurian\n       </a>\n       -age rocks by the\n       <a href="/wiki/Wisconsin_glaciation" title="Wisconsin glaciation">\n        Wisconsin\n       </a>\n       ice sheet during the last\n       <a href="/wiki/Quaternary_glaciation" title="Quaternary glaciation">\n        ice age\n       </a>\n       . The action of the ice occurred along the pre-glacial\n       <a href="/wiki/Ontarian_River" title="Ontarian River">\n        Ontarian River\n       </a>\n       valley which had approximately the same orientation as today\'s basin.\n       <a href="/wiki/Till" title="Till">\n        Material\n       </a>\n       that was pushed southward by the ice sheet left landforms such as\n       <a href="/wiki/Drumlin" title="Drumlin">\n        drumlins\n       </a>\n       ,\n       <a href="/wiki/Kame" title="Kame">\n        kames\n       </a>\n       , and\n       <a href="/wiki/Moraine" title="Moraine">\n        moraines\n       </a>\n       , both on the modern land surface and the lake bottom,\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">\n        <a href="#cite_note-15">\n         [15]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       reorganizing the region\'s entire drainage system. As the ice sheet retreated toward the north, it still dammed the St. Lawrence valley outlet, so that the lake surface was at a higher level. This stage is known as\n       <a href="/wiki/Glacial_Lake_Iroquois" title="Glacial Lake Iroquois">\n        Lake Iroquois\n       </a>\n       . During that time the lake drained through present-day Syracuse, New York into the\n       <a href="/wiki/Mohawk_River" title="Mohawk River">\n        Mohawk River\n       </a>\n       , thence to the Hudson River and the Atlantic. The shoreline created during this stage can be easily recognized by the (now dry)\n       <a href="/wiki/Beach" title="Beach">\n        beaches\n       </a>\n       and wave-cut hills 10 to 25 miles (15 to 40\xc2\xa0km) from the present shoreline.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       When the ice finally receded from the St. Lawrence valley, the outlet was below\n       <a href="/wiki/Sea_level" title="Sea level">\n        sea level\n       </a>\n       , and for a short time the lake became a\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Epicontinental_sea" title="Epicontinental sea">\n        bay\n       </a>\n       of the Atlantic Ocean, in association with the\n       <a href="/wiki/Champlain_Sea" title="Champlain Sea">\n        Champlain Sea\n       </a>\n       . Gradually the land\n       <a href="/wiki/Post-glacial_rebound" title="Post-glacial rebound">\n        rebounded\n       </a>\n       from the release of the weight of about 6,500 feet (2,000\xc2\xa0m) of ice that had been stacked on it. It is still rebounding about 12\xc2\xa0inches (30\xc2\xa0cm) per century in the St. Lawrence area. Since the ice receded from the area last, the most rapid rebound still occurs there. This means that the lake bed is gradually tilting southward, inundating the south shore and\n       <a href="/wiki/Ria" title="Ria">\n        turning river valleys into bays\n       </a>\n       . Both north and south shores experience shoreline erosion, but the tilting amplifies this effect on the south shore, causing loss to property owners.\n      </p>\n      <h2>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="History">\n        History\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lake_Ontario&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: History">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h2>\n      <div class="thumb tright">\n       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;">\n        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Upper_Canada_and_the_Iroquois_Confederacy.jpg">\n         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1531" data-file-width="1050" height="248" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Upper_Canada_and_the_Iroquois_Confederacy.jpg/170px-Upper_Canada_and_the_Iroquois_Confederacy.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Upper_Canada_and_the_Iroquois_Confederacy.jpg/255px-Upper_Canada_and_the_Iroquois_Confederacy.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Upper_Canada_and_the_Iroquois_Confederacy.jpg/340px-Upper_Canada_and_the_Iroquois_Confederacy.jpg 2x" width="170"/>\n        </a>\n        <div class="thumbcaption">\n         <div class="magnify">\n          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Upper_Canada_and_the_Iroquois_Confederacy.jpg" title="Enlarge">\n          </a>\n         </div>\n         Map of Lac de Frontenac (now Lake Ontario), showing\n         <a href="/wiki/Teiaiagon" title="Teiaiagon">\n          Teiaiagon\n         </a>\n         and Lac Taronto (now\n         <a href="/wiki/Lake_Simcoe" title="Lake Simcoe">\n          Lake Simcoe\n         </a>\n         )\n        </div>\n       </div>\n      </div>\n      <p>\n       The name Ontario is derived from the\n       <a href="/wiki/Wyandot_language" title="Wyandot language">\n        Huron\n       </a>\n       word\n       <i>\n        Ontar\xc3\xad\'io\n       </i>\n       , which means "great lake". The lake was a border between the\n       <a href="/wiki/Wyandot_people" title="Wyandot people">\n        Huron\n       </a>\n       people and the\n       <a href="/wiki/Iroquois" title="Iroquois">\n        Iroquois\n       </a>\n       Confederacy in the\n       <a href="/wiki/Pre-Columbian_era" title="Pre-Columbian era">\n        pre-Columbian era\n       </a>\n       . In the 1600s, the Iroquois drove out the Huron from southern Ontario and\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Iroquois_settlement_of_the_northern_shores_of_Lake_Ontario" title="Iroquois settlement of the northern shores of Lake Ontario">\n        settled the northern shores of Lake Ontario\n       </a>\n       . When the Iroquois withdrew and the\n       <a href="/wiki/Anishinaabe" title="Anishinaabe">\n        Anishnabeg / Ojibwa / Mississaugas\n       </a>\n       moved in from the north to southern Ontario, they retained the Iroquois name.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESmith198710_16-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESmith198710-16">\n         [16]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       It is believed that the first European to reach the lake was\n       <a href="/wiki/%C3%89tienne_Br%C3%BBl%C3%A9" title="\xc3\x89tienne Br\xc3\xbbl\xc3\xa9">\n        \xc3\x89tienne Br\xc3\xbbl\xc3\xa9\n       </a>\n       in 1615. As was their practice, the French explorers introduced other names for the lake. In 1632 and 1656, the lake was referred to as Lac de St. Louis or Lake St. Louis by\n       <a href="/wiki/Samuel_de_Champlain" title="Samuel de Champlain">\n        Samuel de Champlain\n       </a>\n       and\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cartographer" title="Cartographer">\n        cartographer\n       </a>\n       <a href="/wiki/Nicolas_Sanson" title="Nicolas Sanson">\n        Nicolas Sanson\n       </a>\n       respectively (likely for\n       <a href="/wiki/Louis_XIV_of_France" title="Louis XIV of France">\n        Louis XIV of France\n       </a>\n       )\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17">\n        <a href="#cite_note-17">\n         [17]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       In 1660\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jesuit" title="Jesuit">\n        Jesuit\n       </a>\n       historian\n       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Francis_Creuxius&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Francis Creuxius (page does not exist)">\n        Francis Creuxius\n       </a>\n       coined the name\n       <i>\n        Lacus Ontarius\n       </i>\n       . In a map drawn in the\n       <i>\n        Relation des J\xc3\xa9suites\n       </i>\n       (1662\xe2\x80\x931663), the lake bears the legend "Lac Ontario ou des Iroquois" with the name "Ondiara" in smaller type. A French map produced in 1712 (currently in the\n       <a href="/wiki/Canadian_Museum_of_History" title="Canadian Museum of History">\n        Canadian Museum of History\n       </a>\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18">\n        <a href="#cite_note-18">\n         [18]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       ), created by military engineer\n       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Jean-Baptiste_de_Couagne&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Jean-Baptiste de Couagne (page does not exist)">\n        Jean-Baptiste de Couagne\n       </a>\n       , identified Lake Ontario as "Lac Frontenac" named after Louis de Buade, Comte de Frontenac et de Palluau. He was a French soldier, courtier, and Governor General of New France from 1672 to 1682 and from 1689 to his death in 1698.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       Artifacts which are believed to be of\n       <a href="/wiki/Nordic_countries" title="Nordic countries">\n        Norse\n       </a>\n       origin have been found in the area of\n       <a href="/wiki/Sodus_Bay" title="Sodus Bay">\n        Sodus Bay\n       </a>\n       , indicating the possibility of trading by the indigenous peoples with Norse explorers on the east coast of North America.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19">\n        <a href="#cite_note-19">\n         [19]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       A series of trading posts were established by both the British and French, such as\n       <a href="/wiki/Fort_Frontenac" title="Fort Frontenac">\n        Fort Frontenac\n       </a>\n       (Kingston) in 1673,\n       <a href="/wiki/Fort_Oswego" title="Fort Oswego">\n        Fort Oswego\n       </a>\n       in 1722,\n       <a href="/wiki/Fort_Rouill%C3%A9" title="Fort Rouill\xc3\xa9">\n        Fort Rouill\xc3\xa9\n       </a>\n       (Toronto) in 1750. After the\n       <a href="/wiki/French_and_Indian_War" title="French and Indian War">\n        French and Indian War\n       </a>\n       , all forts around the lake were under British control. The United States did not take possession of forts on present-day American territory until the signing of the\n       <a href="/wiki/Jay_Treaty" title="Jay Treaty">\n        Jay Treaty\n       </a>\n       in 1794. Permanent, non-military European settlement began during the\n       <a href="/wiki/American_Revolution" title="American Revolution">\n        American Revolution\n       </a>\n       . As the easternmost and nearest lake to the Atlantic seaboard of Canada and the United States, population centres here are among the oldest in the Great Lakes basin, with\n       <a href="/wiki/Kingston,_Ontario" title="Kingston, Ontario">\n        Kingston, Ontario\n       </a>\n       , formerly the\n       <a href="/wiki/Province_of_Canada" title="Province of Canada">\n        capital of Canada\n       </a>\n       , dating to the 1670s (Fort Frontenac). The lake became a hub of commercial activity following the\n       <a href="/wiki/War_of_1812" title="War of 1812">\n        War of 1812\n       </a>\n       with canal building on both sides of the border and heavy travel by lake steamers. Steamer activity peaked in the mid-19th century before competition from railway lines.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       In the late 19th and early 20th centuries, a type of scow known as a\n       <i>\n        stone hooker\n       </i>\n       was in operation on the north-west shore, particularly around\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Port_Credit,_Ontario" title="Port Credit, Ontario">\n        Port Credit\n       </a>\n       and\n       <a href="/wiki/Bronte,_Ontario" title="Bronte, Ontario">\n        Bronte\n       </a>\n       .\n       <a href="/wiki/Stonehooking" title="Stonehooking">\n        Stonehooking\n       </a>\n       was the practice of raking flat fragments of Dundas shale from the shallow lake floor of the area for use in construction, particularly in the growing city of Toronto.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20">\n        <a href="#cite_note-20">\n         [20]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <h2>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Ecology_and_environmental_concerns">\n        Ecology and environmental concerns\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lake_Ontario&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Ecology and environmental concerns">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h2>\n      <div class="thumb tright">\n       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">\n        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:USGS_image_cropped.jpg">\n         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="759" data-file-width="1073" height="156" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a1/USGS_image_cropped.jpg/220px-USGS_image_cropped.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a1/USGS_image_cropped.jpg/330px-USGS_image_cropped.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a1/USGS_image_cropped.jpg/440px-USGS_image_cropped.jpg 2x" width="220"/>\n        </a>\n        <div class="thumbcaption">\n         <div class="magnify">\n          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:USGS_image_cropped.jpg" title="Enlarge">\n          </a>\n         </div>\n         Wetlands on a lakeshore\n        </div>\n       </div>\n      </div>\n      <p>\n       The Great Lakes watershed is a region of high biodiversity, and Lake Ontario is important for its diversity of birds, fish, reptiles amphibians and plants. Many of these special species are associated with shorelines, particularly sand dunes, lagoons, and wetlands. The importance of wetlands to the lake has been appreciated, and many of the larger wetlands have protected status. However, these wetlands are changing in part because the natural water level fluctuations have been reduced. Many wetland plants are dependent upon low water levels to reproduce.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21">\n        <a href="#cite_note-21">\n         [21]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       When water levels are stabilized, the area and diversity of the marsh is reduced. This is particularly true of meadow marsh (also known as wet meadow wetlands); for example, in Eel Bay near Alexandria Bay, regulation of lake levels has resulted in large losses of wet meadow.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22">\n        <a href="#cite_note-22">\n         [22]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       Often this is accompanied by the invasion of\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cattail" title="Cattail">\n        cattails\n       </a>\n       , which displace many of the native plant species and reduce plant diversity.\n       <a href="/wiki/Eutrophication" title="Eutrophication">\n        Eutrophication\n       </a>\n       may accelerate this process by providing nitrogen and phosphorus for the more rapid growth of competitively dominant plants.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23">\n        <a href="#cite_note-23">\n         [23]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       Similar effects are occurring on the north shore, in wetlands such as\n       <a href="/wiki/Presqu%27ile_Provincial_Park" title="Presqu\'ile Provincial Park">\n        Presqu\'ile\n       </a>\n       , which have interdunal wetlands called\n       <a href="/wiki/Help:Disambiguation" title="Help:Disambiguation">\n        pannes\n       </a>\n       , with high plant diversity and many unusual plant species.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24">\n        <a href="#cite_note-24">\n         [24]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       Most of the forests around the lake are\n       <a href="/wiki/Temperate_deciduous_forest" title="Temperate deciduous forest">\n        deciduous forests\n       </a>\n       dominated by trees including maple, oak, beech, ash and basswood. These are classified as part of the\n       <a href="/wiki/Mixedwood_Plains_Ecozone_(Canada)" title="Mixedwood Plains Ecozone (Canada)">\n        Mixedwood Plains Ecozone\n       </a>\n       by Environment Canada, or as the\n       <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Great_Lakes_and_Hudson_Lowlands_(ecoregion)" title="Eastern Great Lakes and Hudson Lowlands (ecoregion)">\n        Eastern Great Lakes and Hudson Lowlands\n       </a>\n       by the United States Environmental Protection Agency, or as the Great Lakes Ecoregion by The Nature Conservancy.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25">\n        <a href="#cite_note-25">\n         [25]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       Deforestation in the vicinity of the lake has had many negative impacts,\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26">\n        <a href="#cite_note-26">\n         [26]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       including loss of forest birds, extinction of native salmon, and increased amounts of sediment flowing into the lake. In some areas more than 90 percent of the forest cover has been removed and replaced by agriculture. Certain tree species, such as hemlock, have also been particularly depleted by past logging activity.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27">\n        <a href="#cite_note-27">\n         [27]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       Guidelines for restoration stress the importance of maintaining and restoring forest cover, particularly along streams and wetlands.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28">\n        <a href="#cite_note-28">\n         [28]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29">\n        <a href="#cite_note-29">\n         [29]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       The open water is less-affected by shoreline features, such as wetlands, and more affected by nutrient levels that control the production of algae. Algae are the basis of the open water food web, and the source of primary production that ends up as Lake Trout and Walleye at the top of the open water food web.\n      </p>\n      <div class="thumb tright">\n       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">\n        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Acipenser_fulvescens_GLERL.jpg">\n         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="196" data-file-width="800" height="54" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c6/Acipenser_fulvescens_GLERL.jpg/220px-Acipenser_fulvescens_GLERL.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c6/Acipenser_fulvescens_GLERL.jpg/330px-Acipenser_fulvescens_GLERL.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c6/Acipenser_fulvescens_GLERL.jpg/440px-Acipenser_fulvescens_GLERL.jpg 2x" width="220"/>\n        </a>\n        <div class="thumbcaption">\n         <div class="magnify">\n          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Acipenser_fulvescens_GLERL.jpg" title="Enlarge">\n          </a>\n         </div>\n         Lake Sturgeon were abundant in 1860 and had been driven to near extinction by 1960.\n        </div>\n       </div>\n      </div>\n      <p>\n       Like the other Great Lakes, Lake Ontario used to have an important commercial fishery. It has been largely destroyed, mostly by over-fishing. Consider the\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lake_Sturgeon" title="Lake Sturgeon">\n        Lake Sturgeon\n       </a>\n       as but one example. Lake sturgeon are huge fish\xe2\x80\x94they can grow up to three meters long and exceed 190\xc2\xa0kg in weight. The females mature slowly and require decades to reach sexual maturity. It was once an abundant species in Lake Ontario. "In 1860, this species, taken on incidental catches of other fishes, was killed and dumped back in the lake, piled up on shore to dry and be burned, fed to pigs, or dug into the earth as fertilizer."\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Scott.2C_W.B_1972._p._88_30-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-Scott.2C_W.B_1972._p._88-30">\n         [30]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       It was even stacked like cordwood and used to fuel steamboats. Once its value was realized, "They were taken by every available means from spearing and jigging to set lines of baited or unbaited hooks laid on the bottom to trapnets, poundnets and gillnets."\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Scott.2C_W.B_1972._p._88_30-1">\n        <a href="#cite_note-Scott.2C_W.B_1972._p._88-30">\n         [30]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       Over 5 million pounds were taken from adjoining Lake Erie in a single year. The fishery collapsed, largely by 1900. They have never recovered. Like most sturgeons, the lake sturgeon is rare now, and is protected in many areas. Populations in the Oswego River are being actively managed for recovery.\n      </p>\n      <div class="thumb tright">\n       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">\n        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Lake_Ontario_food_web.pdf">\n         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1275" data-file-width="1650" height="170" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/39/Lake_Ontario_food_web.pdf/page1-220px-Lake_Ontario_food_web.pdf.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/39/Lake_Ontario_food_web.pdf/page1-330px-Lake_Ontario_food_web.pdf.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/39/Lake_Ontario_food_web.pdf/page1-440px-Lake_Ontario_food_web.pdf.jpg 2x" width="220"/>\n        </a>\n        <div class="thumbcaption">\n         <div class="magnify">\n          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Lake_Ontario_food_web.pdf" title="Enlarge">\n          </a>\n         </div>\n         Lake Ontario food web\n        </div>\n       </div>\n      </div>\n      <p>\n       This food web has been damaged not only by over-fishing, and changes in nutrient levels, but also by other types of pollution from industrial chemicals, agricultural fertilizers, untreated sewage,\n       <a href="/wiki/Phosphate" title="Phosphate">\n        phosphates\n       </a>\n       from laundry detergents, and pesticides. Some pollutant chemicals that have been found in the lake include\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/DDT" title="DDT">\n        DDT\n       </a>\n       ,\n       <a href="/wiki/Benzo(a)pyrene" title="Benzo(a)pyrene">\n        benzo[\n        <i>\n         a\n        </i>\n        ]pyrene\n       </a>\n       and other pesticides;\n       <a href="/wiki/Polychlorinated_biphenyl" title="Polychlorinated biphenyl">\n        PCBs\n       </a>\n       ,\n       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Aramite&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Aramite (page does not exist)">\n        aramite\n       </a>\n       ,\n       <a href="/wiki/Chromium" title="Chromium">\n        chromium\n       </a>\n       ,\n       <a href="/wiki/Lead" title="Lead">\n        lead\n       </a>\n       ,\n       <a href="/wiki/Mirex" title="Mirex">\n        mirex\n       </a>\n       ,\n       <a href="/wiki/Mercury_(element)" title="Mercury (element)">\n        mercury\n       </a>\n       , and\n       <a href="/wiki/Carbon_tetrachloride" title="Carbon tetrachloride">\n        carbon tetrachloride\n       </a>\n       . The International Joint Commission has identified areas where pollution is particularly intense (point sources) and mapped them as\n       <a href="/wiki/Great_Lakes_Areas_of_Concern" title="Great Lakes Areas of Concern">\n        Areas of Concern\n       </a>\n       . A Remedial Action Plan has been developed for each area. Some Lake Ontario areas of concern include the\n       <a href="/wiki/Oswego_River_(New_York)" title="Oswego River (New York)">\n        Oswego River\n       </a>\n       and Rochester Embayment on the American side, and\n       <a href="/wiki/Hamilton_Harbour" title="Hamilton Harbour">\n        Hamilton Harbour\n       </a>\n       and Toronto on the Canadian Side.\n       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">\n        [\n        <i>\n         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">\n          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2017)">\n           citation needed\n          </span>\n         </a>\n        </i>\n        ]\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       By the 1960s and 1970s, the increased pollution caused frequent\n       <a href="/wiki/Algal_bloom" title="Algal bloom">\n        algal blooms\n       </a>\n       to occur in the summer.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Christie.2C_W._J._1974_8-1">\n        <a href="#cite_note-Christie.2C_W._J._1974-8">\n         [8]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       These blooms killed large numbers of\n       <a href="/wiki/Fish" title="Fish">\n        fish\n       </a>\n       , and left decomposing piles of\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Filamentous_algae" title="Filamentous algae">\n        filamentous algae\n       </a>\n       and dead fish along the shores.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31">\n        <a href="#cite_note-31">\n         [31]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       At times the blooms became so thick that waves could not break. Fish eating birds such as osprey, bald eagle and cormorant were being poisoned by contaminated fish. Since the 1960s and 1970s, environmental concerns have forced a cleanup of industrial and municipal wastes. Cleanup has been accomplished through better treatment plants, tighter environmental regulations,\n       <a href="/wiki/Deindustrialization" title="Deindustrialization">\n        deindustrialization\n       </a>\n       and increased public awareness. Today, Lake Ontario has recovered some of its pristine quality; for example,\n       <a href="/wiki/Walleye" title="Walleye">\n        walleye\n       </a>\n       , a fish species considered as a marker of clean water, are now found. However, regional airshed pollution remains a concern. The lake has also become an important sport\n       <a href="/wiki/Fishery" title="Fishery">\n        fishery\n       </a>\n       , although with introduced species (\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coho" title="Coho">\n        Coho\n       </a>\n       and\n       <a href="/wiki/Chinook_salmon" title="Chinook salmon">\n        Chinook salmon\n       </a>\n       ) rather than the native species. Bald eagle and osprey populations are also beginning to recover.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       <a href="/wiki/Invasive_species" title="Invasive species">\n        Invasive species\n       </a>\n       are a problem for Lake Ontario, particularly\n       <a href="/wiki/Lamprey" title="Lamprey">\n        lamprey\n       </a>\n       and\n       <a href="/wiki/Zebra_mussel" title="Zebra mussel">\n        zebra mussels\n       </a>\n       . Lamprey are being controlled by poisoning in the juvenile stage in the streams where they breed. Zebra mussels in particular are difficult to control, and pose major challenges for the lake and its waterways.\n      </p>\n      <h2>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Climate">\n        Climate\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lake_Ontario&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Climate">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h2>\n      <p>\n       The lake has a natural\n       <a href="/wiki/Seiche" title="Seiche">\n        seiche\n       </a>\n       rhythm of eleven minutes. The seiche effect normally is only about\n       <span class="nowrap">\n       </span>\n       <span class="frac nowrap">\n        <sup>\n         3\n        </sup>\n        \xe2\x81\x84\n        <sub>\n         4\n        </sub>\n       </span>\n       inches (2\xc2\xa0cm) but can be greatly amplified by earth movement, winds, and atmospheric pressure changes.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       Because of its great depth, the lake as a whole never freezes in winter, but an ice sheet covering between 10% and 90% of the lake area typically develops, depending on the severity of the winter. Ice sheets typically form along the shoreline and in slack water bays, where the lake is not as deep. During the winters of 1877 and 1878, the ice sheet coverage was up to 95\xe2\x80\x93100% in most of the lake. In the\n       <a href="/wiki/Engagements_on_Lake_Ontario#Operations_in_1812" title="Engagements on Lake Ontario">\n        winter of 1812\n       </a>\n       , the ice cover was stable enough that the American naval commander stationed at Sackets Harbor feared a British attack from\n       <a href="/wiki/Kingston,_Ontario" title="Kingston, Ontario">\n        Kingston\n       </a>\n       , over the ice.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       When the cold winds of winter pass over the warmer water of the lake, they pick up moisture and drop it as\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lake_effect_snow" title="Lake effect snow">\n        <i>\n         lake-effect\n        </i>\n       </a>\n       snow. Since the prevailing winter winds are from the northwest, the southern and southeastern shoreline of the lake is referred to as the\n       <i>\n        <a href="/wiki/Snowbelt" title="Snowbelt">\n         snowbelt\n        </a>\n       </i>\n       . In some winters the area between Oswego and\n       <a href="/wiki/Pulaski,_New_York" title="Pulaski, New York">\n        Pulaski\n       </a>\n       may receive twenty or more feet (600\xc2\xa0cm) of snowfall. Also impacted by lake-effect snow is the\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tug_Hill_Plateau" title="Tug Hill Plateau">\n        Tug Hill Plateau\n       </a>\n       , an area of elevated land that is about 20 miles (32\xc2\xa0km) east of Lake Ontario, creating ideal conditions for lake-effect snowfall. The "Hill", as it is often referred to, typically receives more snow than any other region in the eastern United States. As a result, Tug Hill is a popular location for winter enthusiasts, such as snow-mobilers and cross-country skiers. Lake-effect snow often extends inland as far as\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Syracuse_(United_States)" title="Syracuse (United States)">\n        Syracuse\n       </a>\n       , with that city often recording the most winter snowfall accumulation of any large city in the United States. Other cities in the world receive more snow annually, such as\n       <a href="/wiki/Quebec_City" title="Quebec City">\n        Quebec City\n       </a>\n       , which averages 135 inches (340\xc2\xa0cm), and\n       <a href="/wiki/Sapporo" title="Sapporo">\n        Sapporo\n       </a>\n       ,\n       <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">\n        Japan\n       </a>\n       , which receives 250 inches (640\xc2\xa0cm) each year and is often regarded as the snowiest city in the world. Foggy conditions (particularly in fall) can be created by thermal contrasts and can be an impediment for recreational boaters. In a normal winter, Lake Ontario will be at most one quarter ice-covered, in a mild winter almost completely unfrozen. Lake Ontario has completely frozen over on five recorded occasions: from about January 20 to about March 20, 1830;\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32">\n        <a href="#cite_note-32">\n         [32]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       in 1874;\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMay2008_33-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMay2008-33">\n         [33]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       in 1893;\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMay2008_33-1">\n        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMay2008-33">\n         [33]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       in 1912;\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMay2008_33-2">\n        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMay2008-33">\n         [33]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       and in February 1934.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMay2008_33-3">\n        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMay2008-33">\n         [33]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       Lake breezes in spring tend to retard fruit bloom until the frost danger is past, and in the autumn delay the onset of fall frost, particularly on the south shore. Cool onshore winds also retard early bloom of plants and flowers until later in the spring season, protecting them from possible frost damage. Such\n       <a href="/wiki/Microclimate" title="Microclimate">\n        microclimatic\n       </a>\n       effects have enabled tender fruit production in a continental climate, with the southwest shore supporting a major fruit-growing area.\n       <a href="/wiki/Apple" title="Apple">\n        Apples\n       </a>\n       ,\n       <a href="/wiki/Cherry" title="Cherry">\n        cherries\n       </a>\n       ,\n       <a href="/wiki/Pear" title="Pear">\n        pears\n       </a>\n       ,\n       <a href="/wiki/Plum" title="Plum">\n        plums\n       </a>\n       , and\n       <a href="/wiki/Peach" title="Peach">\n        peaches\n       </a>\n       are grown in many commercial\n       <a href="/wiki/Orchard" title="Orchard">\n        orchards\n       </a>\n       around Rochester. Between\n       <a href="/wiki/Stoney_Creek,_Ontario" title="Stoney Creek, Ontario">\n        Stoney Creek\n       </a>\n       and\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Niagara-on-the-Lake,_Ontario" title="Niagara-on-the-Lake, Ontario">\n        Niagara-on-the-Lake\n       </a>\n       on the Niagara Peninsula is a\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Niagara_Peninsula_wineries" title="Niagara Peninsula wineries">\n        major fruit-growing and wine-making area\n       </a>\n       . The\n       <a href="/wiki/Niagara_Escarpment_AVA" title="Niagara Escarpment AVA">\n        wine-growing region\n       </a>\n       extends over the international border into\n       <a href="/wiki/Niagara_County,_New_York" title="Niagara County, New York">\n        Niagara\n       </a>\n       and\n       <a href="/wiki/Orleans_County,_New_York" title="Orleans County, New York">\n        Orleans\n       </a>\n       counties. Apple varieties that tolerate a more extreme climate are grown on the lake\'s north shore, around\n       <a href="/wiki/Cobourg" title="Cobourg">\n        Cobourg\n       </a>\n       .\n      </p>\n The park opened in 1984, and is located just to the east of the spot where Bell completed her swim.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39">\n        <a href="#cite_note-39">\n         [39]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       In 1974,\n       <a href="/wiki/Diana_Nyad" title="Diana Nyad">\n        Diana Nyad\n       </a>\n       became the first person who swam across the lake against the current (from north to south).\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40">\n        <a href="#cite_note-40">\n         [40]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       On August 28, 2007, 14-year-old Natalie Lambert from Kingston, Ontario, made the swim, leaving Sackets Harbor, New York, and reaching Kingston\'s Confederation basin less than 24 hours after she entered the lake.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41">\n        <a href="#cite_note-41">\n         [41]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       On August 19, 2012, 14-year-old\n       <a href="/wiki/Annaleise_Carr" title="Annaleise Carr">\n        Annaleise Carr\n       </a>\n       became the youngest person to swim across the lake. She completed the 32-mile (52-km) crossing from\n       <a href="/wiki/Niagara-on-the-Lake" title="Niagara-on-the-Lake">\n        Niagara-on-the-Lake\n       </a>\n       to Marilyn Bell Park in just under 27 hours.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42">\n        <a href="#cite_note-42">\n         [42]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <h2>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Industrialisation">\n        Industrialisation\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lake_Ontario&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Industrialisation">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h2>\n      <p>\n       The\n       <a href="/wiki/Government_of_Ontario" title="Government of Ontario">\n        government of Ontario\n       </a>\n       , which holds the lakebed rights of the Canadian portion of the lake under the Beds of Navigable Waters Act,\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-trilliumsco_43-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-trilliumsco-43">\n         [43]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       currently does not permit off-shore wind power to be generated offshore.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tsoffshore_44-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-tsoffshore-44">\n         [44]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       In\n       <a href="/wiki/Trillium_Power_Wind_1" title="Trillium Power Wind 1">\n        Trillium Power Wind Corporation\n       </a>\n       v. Ontario (Natural Resources),\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-trilliumsco_43-1">\n        <a href="#cite_note-trilliumsco-43">\n         [43]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       the Superior Court of Justice held that Trillium Power\xe2\x80\x94since 2004 an "Applicant of Record" who had invested $35,000 in fees and, when in 2011 the Crown made a policy decision against offshore windfarms, claimed an injury of $2.25\xc2\xa0billion\xe2\x80\x94disclosed no reasonable cause of action.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       While the Great Lakes once supported an industrial-scale fishery, with record hauls in 1899, overfishing later blighted the industry.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-glearb_45-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-glearb-45">\n         [45]\n
