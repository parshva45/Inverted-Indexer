These fluctuations in\n       <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_temperature" title="Atmospheric temperature">\n        atmospheric temperature\n       </a>\n       ,\n       <a href="/wiki/Sea_surface_temperature" title="Sea surface temperature">\n        sea surface temperature\n       </a>\n       ,\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Precipitation_(meteorology)" title="Precipitation (meteorology)">\n        precipitation\n       </a>\n       or other parameters can be\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Quasi-periodic" title="Quasi-periodic">\n        quasi-periodic\n       </a>\n       , often occurring on inter-annual, multi-annual, decadal, multidecadal, century-wide, millennial or longer\n       <a href="/wiki/Dynamical_time_scale" title="Dynamical time scale">\n        timescales\n       </a>\n       .   </p>\n      <p>\n       A prominent example is the\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/El_Ni%C3%B1o_Southern_Oscillation" title="El Ni\xc3\xb1o Southern Oscillation">\n        El Ni\xc3\xb1o Pacific Ocean\n       </a>\n       and the western coast of tropical South America, but which affects climate worldwide.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       Records of past climate conditions are recovered <br/>\n       Anomalies in oscillations sometimes occur when they coincide, as in the\n       <a href="/wiki/Arctic_dipole_anomaly" title="Arctic dipole anomaly">\n        Arctic dipole anomaly\n       </a>\n       (a combination of the Arctic and North Atlantic oscillations) and the longer-term\n       <a href="/wiki/Younger_Dryas" title="Younger Dryas">\n        Younger Dryas\n       </a>\n       , a sudden non-linear cooling event that occurred at the onset of the current\n       <a href="/wiki/Holocene" title="Holocene">\n        Holocene\n       </a>\n       <a href="/wiki/Interglacial" title="Interglacial">\n        interglacial\n       </a>\n       . In the case of volcanoes, large eruptions such as\n       <a href="/wiki/Mount_Tambora" title="Mount Tambora">\n        Mount Tambora\n       </a>\n       in 1816, which led to the\n       <a href="/wiki/Year_Without_a_Summer" title="Year Without a Summer">\n        Year Without a Summer\n       </a>\n       , typically cool the climate, especially when the volcano is located in the\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tropical_climate_zone" title="Tropical climate zone">\n        tropics\n       </a>\n       . Around 70 000 years ago the\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Toba_Volcano" title="Toba Volcano">\n        Toba supervolcano\n       </a>\n       eruption created an especially cold period during the ice age, leading to a possible\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Toba_bottleneck" title="Toba bottleneck">\n        genetic bottleneck\n       </a>\n       in human populations. However,\n       <a href="/wiki/Outgassing" title="Outgassing">\n        outgassing\n       </a>\n       from\n       <a href="/wiki/Large_igneous_province" title="Large igneous province">\n        large igneous provinces\n       </a>\n       such as the\n       <a href="/wiki/Permian" title="Permian">\n        Permian\n       </a>\n       <a href="/wiki/Siberian_Traps" title="Siberian Traps">\n        Siberian Traps\n       </a>\n       can input\n       <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">\n        carbon dioxide\n       </a>\n       into the atmosphere, warming the climate. Triggering of other mechanisms, such as\n       <a href="/wiki/Methane_clathrate" title="Methane clathrate">\n        methane clathrate\n       </a>\n       deposits as during the\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Paleocene-Eocene_Thermal_Maximum" title="Paleocene-Eocene Thermal Maximum">\n        Paleocene-Eocene Thermal Maximum\n       </a>\n       , increased the rate of climatic temperature change and oceanic extinctions.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       Another longer-term near-millennial oscillation involves the\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Daansgard-Oeschger_cycles" title="Daansgard-Oeschger cycles">\n        Daansgard-Oeschger cycles\n       </a>\n       , occurring on roughly 1,500-year cycles during the\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Last_glacial_maximum" title="Last glacial maximum">\n        last glacial maximum\n       </a>\n       . They may be related to the Holocene\n       <a href="/wiki/Bond_event" title="Bond event">\n        Bond events\n       </a>\n       , and may involve factors similar to those responsible for\n       <a href="/wiki/Heinrich_event" title="Heinrich event">\n        Heinrich events\n       </a>\n       .\n      </p>\n      <h2>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Origins_and_causes">\n        Origins and causes\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_oscillation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Origins and causes">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h2>\n      <p>\n       There are close correlations between Earth\'s climate oscillations and astronomical factors (\n       <a href="/wiki/Barycenter" title="Barycenter">\n        barycenter\n       </a>\n       changes,\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solar_variation" title="Solar variation">\n        solar variation\n       </a>\n       ,\n       <a href="/wiki/Cosmic_ray" title="Cosmic ray">\n        cosmic ray\n       </a>\n       flux,\n       <a href="/wiki/Cloud_albedo" title="Cloud albedo">\n        cloud albedo\n       </a>\n       <a href="/wiki/Cloud_feedback" title="Cloud feedback">\n        feedback\n       </a>\n       ,\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Milankovic_cycle" title="Milankovic cycle">\n        Milankovic cycles\n       </a>\n       ), and modes of\n       <a href="/wiki/Thermodynamics" title="Thermodynamics">\n        heat distribution\n       </a>\n       between the ocean-atmosphere climate system. In some cases, current, historical and\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Paleoclimate" title="Paleoclimate">\n        paleoclimatological\n       </a>\n       natural oscillations may be masked by significant\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Volcanic_eruption" title="Volcanic eruption">\n        volcanic eruptions\n       </a>\n       ,\n       <a href="/wiki/Impact_event" title="Impact event">\n        impact events\n       </a>\n       , irregularities in\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Climate_proxy" title="Climate proxy">\n        climate proxy\n       </a>\n       data,\n       <a href="/wiki/Positive_feedback" title="Positive feedback">\n        positive feedback\n       </a>\n       processes or\n       <a href="/wiki/Human_impact_on_the_environment" title="Human impact on the environment">\n        anthropogenic\n       </a>\n       <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas#Greenhouse_gas_emissions" title="Greenhouse gas">\n        emissions\n       </a>\n       of substances such as\n       <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas" title="Greenhouse gas">\n        greenhouse gases\n       </a>\n       .\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Scafetta_2-1">\n        <a href="#cite_note-Scafetta-2">\n         [2]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">\n        <a href="#cite_note-4">\n         [4]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <h2>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Effects">\n        Effects\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_oscillation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Effects">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h2>\n      <p>\n       Extreme phases of short-term climate oscillations such as ENSO can result in characteristic patterns of\n       <a href="/wiki/Flood" title="Flood">\n        floods\n       </a>\n       and\n       <a href="/wiki/Drought" title="Drought">\n        droughts\n       </a>\n       (including\n       <a href="/wiki/Megadrought" title="Megadrought">\n        megadroughts\n       </a>\n       ),\n       <a href="/wiki/Monsoon" title="Monsoon">\n        monsoonal\n       </a>\n       disruption and extreme temperatures in the form of\n       <a href="/wiki/Heat_wave" title="Heat wave">\n        heat waves\n       </a>\n       and\n       <a href="/wiki/Cold_wave" title="Cold wave">\n        cold waves\n       </a>\n       . Shorter-term climate oscillations typically do not directly result in longer-term\n       <a href="/wiki/Climate_change" title="Climate change">\n        climate change\n       </a>\n       in temperatures. However, the effects of underlying climate trends such as recent\n       <a href="/wiki/Global_warming" title="Global warming">\n        global warming\n       </a>\n       and oscillations can be cumulative to\n       <a href="/wiki/Global_temperature" title="Global temperature">\n        global temperature\n       </a>\n       , producing shorter-term fluctuations in the\n       <a href="/wiki/Instrumental_temperature_record" title="Instrumental temperature record">\n        instrumental\n       </a>\n       and\n       <a href="/wiki/Satellite_temperature_measurements" title="Satellite temperature measurements">\n        satellite\n       </a>\n       <a href="/wiki/Temperature_record" title="Temperature record">\n        temperature records\n       </a>\n       .\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       Collapses of past civilizations such as the\n       <a href="/wiki/Maya_civilization" title="Maya civilization">\n        Maya\n       </a>\n       may be related to cycles of precipitation, especially drought, that in this example also correlates to the\n       <a href="/wiki/Western_Hemisphere_Warm_Pool" title="Western Hemisphere Warm Pool">\n        Western Hemisphere Warm Pool\n       </a>\n       .\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       One example of possible correlations between factors affecting the climate and global events, popular with the media, is a 2003 study on the correlation between\n       <a href="/wiki/Wheat" title="Wheat">\n        wheat\n       </a>\n       prices and sunspot numbers.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">\n        <a href="#cite_note-5">\n         [5]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <h2>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Analysis_and_uncertainties">\n        Analysis and uncertainties\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_oscillation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Analysis and uncertainties">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h2>\n      <p>\n       <a href="/wiki/Radiative_forcing" title="Radiative forcing">\n        Radiative forcings\n       </a>\n       and other factors in a climate oscillation must obey the laws of\n       <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_thermodynamics" title="Atmospheric thermodynamics">\n        atmospheric thermodynamics\n       </a>\n       . However, because Earth\'s climate is inherently a\n       <a href="/wiki/Complex_system" title="Complex system">\n        complex system\n       </a>\n       , simple\n       <a href="/wiki/Fourier_analysis" title="Fourier analysis">\n        Fourier analysis\n       </a>\n       or\n       <a href="/wiki/Climate_model" title="Climate model">\n        climate modelling\n       </a>\n       often does not create a perfect replication of the observed or inferred conditions. No climate cycle is found to be perfectly periodic, although the\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Milankovich_cycle" title="Milankovich cycle">\n        Milankovich cycles\n       </a>\n       (based on multiple superimposed orbital cycles and\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Axial_precession_(astronomy)" title="Axial precession (astronomy)">\n        Earth\'s precession\n       </a>\n       ) are quite close to being periodic (perhaps\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Almost_periodic" title="Almost periodic">\n        almost periodic\n       </a>\n       ?).\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       One difficulty in detecting climate cycles is that the Earth\'s climate has been changing in non-cyclic ways over most paleoclimatological timescales. For instance, we are now in a period of global warming that appears anthropogenic. In a larger timeframe, the Earth is\n       <a href="/wiki/Holocene_glacial_retreat" title="Holocene glacial retreat">\n        emerging\n       </a>\n       from the latest ice age, cooling from the\n       <a href="/wiki/Holocene_climatic_optimum" title="Holocene climatic optimum">\n        Holocene climatic optimum\n       </a>\n       and warming from the so-called "\n       <a href="/wiki/Little_Ice_Age" title="Little Ice Age">\n        Little Ice Age\n       </a>\n       ", which means that climate has been constantly changing over the last 15,000 years or so. During warm periods, temperature fluctuations are often of a lesser amplitude. The\n       <a href="/wiki/Pleistocene" title="Pleistocene">\n        Pleistocene\n       </a>\n       period, dominated by repeated\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Glaciation" title="Glaciation">\n        glaciations\n       </a>\n       , developed out of more stable conditions in the\n       <a href="/wiki/Miocene" title="Miocene">\n        Miocene\n       </a>\n       and\n       <a href="/wiki/Pliocene" title="Pliocene">\n        Pliocene\n       </a>\n       <a href="/wiki/Pliocene_climate" title="Pliocene climate">\n        climate\n       </a>\n       . Holocene climate has been relatively stable. All of these changes complicate the task of looking for cyclical behavior in the climate.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       <a href="/wiki/Positive_feedback" title="Positive feedback">\n        Positive feedback\n       </a>\n       ,\n       <a href="/wiki/Negative_feedback" title="Negative feedback">\n        negative feedback\n       </a>\n       , and\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ecological_inertia" title="Ecological inertia">\n        ecological inertia\n       </a>\n       from the land-ocean-atmosphere system often attenuate or reverse smaller effects, whether from orbital forcings, solar variations or changes in concentrations of greenhouse gases. Most climatologists recognize the existence of various\n       <a href="/wiki/Tipping_point_(climatology)" title="Tipping point (climatology)">\n        tipping points\n       </a>\n       that push small forcings beyond a certain threshold that makes the change irreversible while the forcings are still in place. Certain feedbacks involvong processes such as clouds are also uncertain; for\n       <a href="/wiki/Contrail" title="Contrail">\n        contrails\n       </a>\n       , natural\n       <a href="/wiki/Cirrus_cloud" title="Cirrus cloud">\n        cirrus\n       </a>\n       clouds, oceanic\n       <a href="/wiki/Dimethyl_sulfide" title="Dimethyl sulfide">\n        dimethyl sulfide\n       </a>\n       and a land-based equivalent, competing theories exist concerning effects on climatic temperatures, for example contrasting the\n       <a href="/wiki/Iris_hypothesis" title="Iris hypothesis">\n        Iris hypothesis\n       </a>\n       and\n       <a href="/wiki/CLAW_hypothesis" title="CLAW hypothesis">\n        CLAW hypothesis\n       </a>\n       .\n      </p>\n The thick black curve is an average.\n        </div>\n       </div>\n      </div>\n      <p>\n       Various\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Climate_forcing" title="Climate forcing">\n        climate forcings\n       </a>\n       are typically in flux throughout\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Geologic_time" title="Geologic time">\n        geologic time\n       </a>\n       , and some processes of the Earth\'s temperature may be\n       <a href="/wiki/Homeostasis" title="Homeostasis">\n        self-regulating\n       </a>\n       . For example, during the\n       <a href="/wiki/Snowball_Earth" title="Snowball Earth">\n        Snowball Earth\n       </a>\n       period, large glacial ice sheets spanned to Earth\'s equator, covering nearly its entire surface, and very high\n       <a href="/wiki/Albedo" title="Albedo">\n        albedo\n       </a>\n       created extremely low temperatures, while the accumulation of snow and ice likely removed carbon dioxide through\n       <a href="/wiki/Deposition_(chemistry)" title="Deposition (chemistry)">\n        atmospheric deposition\n       </a>\n       . However, the absence of\n       <a href="/wiki/Plant_cover" title="Plant cover">\n        plant cover\n       </a>\n       to absorb atmospheric CO\n       <sub>\n        2\n       </sub>\n       emitted by volcanoes meant that the greenhouse gas could accumulate in the atmosphere. There was also an absence of exposed silicate rocks, which use CO\n       <sub>\n        2\n       </sub>\n       when they undergo weathering. This created a warming that later melted the ice and brought Earth\'s temperature back to equilibrium. During the following eons of the\n       <a href="/wiki/Paleozoic" title="Paleozoic">\n        Paleozoic\n       </a>\n       , cosmic ray flux and occasional nearby\n       <a href="/wiki/Supernova" title="Supernova">\n        supernova\n       </a>\n       explosions (one hypothesis for the cause of the\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ordovician%E2%80%93Silurian_extinction_event" title="Ordovician\xe2\x80\x93Silurian extinction event">\n        Ordovician\xe2\x80\x93Silurian extinction event\n       </a>\n       ) and\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gamma_ray_burst" title="Gamma ray burst">\n        gamma ray bursts\n       </a>\n       may have induced ice ages or other sudden climate changes.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       Throughout the\n       <a href="/wiki/Cenozoic" title="Cenozoic">\n        Cenozoic\n       </a>\n       , multiple climate forcings led to warming and cooling of the atmosphere, which led to the early formation of the\n       <a href="/wiki/Antarctic_ice_sheet" title="Antarctic ice sheet">\n        Antarctic ice sheet\n       </a>\n       , subsequent melting, and its later reglaciation. The temperature changes occurred somewhat suddenly, at carbon dioxide concentrations of about 600\xe2\x80\x93760 ppm and temperatures approximately 4\xc2\xa0\xc2\xb0C warmer than today. During the Pleistocene, cycles of glaciations and interglacials occurred on cycles of roughly 100,000\xc2\xa0years, but may stay longer within an interglacial when\n       <a href="/wiki/Orbital_eccentricity" title="Orbital eccentricity">\n        orbital eccentricity\n       </a>\n       approaches zero, as during the current interglacial. Previous interglacials such as the\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eemian_interglacial" title="Eemian interglacial">\n        Eemian\n       </a>\n       phase created temperatures higher than today, higher sea levels, and some partial melting of the\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/West_Antarctic_ice_sheet" title="West Antarctic ice sheet">\n        West Antarctic ice sheet\n       </a>\n       . The warmest part of the current interglacial occurred during the early Holocene Optimum, when temperatures were a few degrees Celsius warmer than today, and a strong\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/African_Monsoon" title="African Monsoon">\n        African Monsoon\n       </a>\n       created grassland conditions in the\n       <a href="/wiki/Sahara" title="Sahara">\n        Sahara\n       </a>\n       during the\n       <a href="/wiki/Neolithic_Subpluvial" title="Neolithic Subpluvial">\n        Neolithic Subpluvial\n       </a>\n       . Since that time, several\n       <a href="/wiki/Stadial" title="Stadial">\n        cooling events\n       </a>\n       have occurred, including:\n      </p>\n      <ul>\n       <li>\n        the\n        <a href="/wiki/Piora_Oscillation" title="Piora Oscillation">\n         Piora Oscillation\n        </a>\n       </li>\n       <li>\n        the\n        <a href="/wiki/Middle_Bronze_Age_Cold_Epoch" Epoch">\n         Iron Age Cold Epoch\n        </a>\n       </li>\n       <li>\n        cooling during the\n        <a href="/wiki/Dark_Ages_(historiography)" title="Dark Ages (historiography)">\n         Dark Ages\n        </a>\n       </li>\n       <li>\n        the\n        <a href="/wiki/Sp%C3%B6rer_Minimum" title="Sp\xc3\xb6rer Minimum">\n         Sp\xc3\xb6rer Minimum\n        </a>\n       </li>\n       <li>\n        the "\n        <a href="/wiki/Little_Ice_Age" title="Little Ice Age">\n         Little Ice Age\n        </a>\n        "\n       </li>\n       <li>\n        the\n        <a title="Laki">\n         Laki\n        </a>\n        in Iceland\n       </li>\n       <li>\n        the phase of cooling c. 1940-1970, which led to\n        <a href="/wiki/Global_cooling" title="Global cooling">\n         global cooling\n        </a>\n        hypotheses\n       </li>\n      </ul>\n      <p>\n       In contrast, several warm periods have also taken place, and they include but are not limited to:\n      </p>\n      <ul>\n       <li>\n        the\n        <a href="/wiki/Older_Peron" title="Older Peron">\n         Older Peron\n        </a>\n        during the late Holocene optimum\n       </li>\n       <li>\n        a warm period during the apex of the\n        <a href="/wiki/Minoan_civilization" title="Minoan civilization">\n         Minoan civilization\n        </a>\n       </li>\n       <li>\n        the\n        <a href="/wiki/Roman_Warm_Period" title="Roman Warm Period">\n         Roman Warm Period\n        </a>\n       </li>\n       <li>\n        the\n        <a href="/wiki/Retreat_of_glaciers_since_1850" title="Retreat of glaciers since 1850">\n         retreat of glaciers since 1850\n        </a>\n       </li>\n       <li>\n        the "\n        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Modern_Warming" title="Modern Warming">\n         Modern Warming\n        </a>\n        " during the 20th century\n       </li>\n      </ul>\n      <p>\n       Certain effects have occurred during these cycles. For example, during the Medieval Warm Period, the\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/American_Midwest" title="American Midwest">\n        American Midwest\n       </a>\n       was in drought, including the\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sand_Hills_(Nebraska)" title="Sand Hills (Nebraska)">\n        Sand Hills of Nebraska\n       </a>\n       which were active\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sand_dune" title="Sand dune">\n        sand dunes\n       </a>\n       . The\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Black_death" title="Black death">\n        black death\n       </a>\n       plague of\n       <i>\n        <a href="/wiki/Yersinia_pestis" title="Yersinia pestis">\n         Yersinia pestis\n        </a>\n       </i>\n       also occurred during Medieval temperature fluctuations, and may be related to changing climates.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       Given that records of solar activity are accurate, solar activity may have contributed to part of the modern warming that peaked in the 1930s, in addition to the 60-year temperature cycles that result in roughly 0.5\xc2\xa0\xc2\xb0C of warming during the increasing temperature phase. However, solar cycles fail to account for warming observed since the 1980s to the present day\n       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">\n        [\n        <i>\n         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">\n          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2016)">\n           citation needed\n          </span>\n         </a>\n        </i>\n        ]\n       </sup>\n       . Events such as the opening of the\n       <a href="/wiki/Northwest_Passage" title="Northwest Passage">\n        Northwest Passage\n       </a>\n       and recent record low ice minima of the modern\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arctic_shrinkage" title="Arctic shrinkage">\n        Arctic shrinkage\n       </a>\n       have not taken place for at least several centuries, as early explorers were all unable to make an Arctic crossing, even in summer.
