<a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone" title="Tropical cyclone">\n        tropical cyclone\n       </a>\n       to strike the\n       <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">\n        United States\n       </a>\n       on record. The most intense storm of the\n       <a href="/wiki/1969_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="1969 Atlantic hurricane season">\n        1969 Atlantic hurricane season\n       </a>\n       , Camille formed as a tropical depression on August 14 south of\n       <a href="/wiki/Cuba" title="Cuba">\n        Cuba\n       </a>\n       from a long-tracked\n       <a href="/wiki/Tropical_wave" title="Tropical wave">\n        tropical wave\n       </a>\n       . Located in a favorable environment for strengthening, the storm quickly intensified into a Category\xc2\xa02 hurricane before striking the western part of the nation on August 15. Emerging into the\n       <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_Mexico" title="Gulf of Mexico">\n        Gulf of Mexico\n       </a>\n       , Camille underwent another period of\n       <a href="/wiki/Rapid_intensification" title="Rapid intensification">\n        rapid intensification\n       </a>\n       and became a\n       <a href="/wiki/List_of_Category_5_Atlantic_hurricanes" title="List of Category 5 Atlantic hurricanes">\n        Category\xc2\xa05 hurricane\n       </a>\n       the next day as it moved northward towards the\n       <a href="/wiki/Louisiana" title="Louisiana">\n        Louisiana\n       </a>\n       \xe2\x80\x93\n       <a href="/wiki/Mississippi" title="Mississippi">\n        Mississippi\n       </a>\n       region. Despite weakening slightly on August 17, the hurricane quickly re-intensified back to a Category 5 before it made landfall in\n       <a href="/wiki/Waveland,_Mississippi" title="Waveland, Mississippi">\n        Waveland, Mississippi\n       </a>\n       early on August 18 with a pressure of 900\xc2\xa0mbar (26.58\xc2\xa0inHg). This was the second-lowest pressure recorded for a U.S. landfall; only the\n       <a href="/wiki/1935_Labor_Day_hurricane" title="1935 Labor Day hurricane">\n        1935 Labor Day hurricane\n       </a>\n       had a lower pressure at landfall. As Camille pushed inland, it quickly weakened and was a tropical depression by the time it was over the\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ohio_Valley" title="Ohio Valley">\n        Ohio Valley\n       </a>\n       . Once it emerged offshore, it was able to restrengthen to a strong tropical storm, before it became extratropical, being absorbed by a\n       <a href="/wiki/Cold_front" title="Cold front">\n        cold front\n       </a>\n       over the North Atlantic on August 22.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       Camille caused tremendous damage in its wake, and also produced a peak official\n       <a href="/wiki/Storm_surge" title="Storm surge">\n        storm surge\n       </a>\n       of 24 feet (7.3\xc2\xa0m). The hurricane flattened nearly everything along the coast of the\n       <a href="/wiki/U.S._state" title="U.S. state">\n        U.S. state\n       </a>\n       of\n       <a href="/wiki/Mississippi" title="Mississippi">\n        Mississippi\n       </a>\n       , and caused additional flooding and deaths inland while crossing the\n       <a href="/wiki/Appalachian_Mountains" title="Appalachian Mountains">\n        Appalachian Mountains\n       </a>\n       of\n       <a href="/wiki/Virginia" title="Virginia">\n        Virginia\n       </a>\n       . In total, Camille killed 259\xc2\xa0people and caused $1.42\xc2\xa0billion (1969\n       <a href="/wiki/United_States_dollar" title="United States dollar">\n        USD\n       </a>\n       , equivalent to $9.27\xc2\xa0billion 2016\xc2\xa0USD\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-inflation-US_2-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-inflation-US-2">\n         [2]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       ) in damages. To date, a complete understanding of the reasons for the system\'s power, extremely rapid intensification over open water and strength at landfall has not been achieved.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n links\n          </span>\n         </a>\n        </li>\n       </ul>\n      </div>\n      <p>\n      </p>\n      <h2>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Meteorological_history">\n        Meteorological history\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurricane_Camille&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Meteorological history">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h2>\n      <div class="thumb tleft">\n       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">\n        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Camille_1969_track.png">\n         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1505" data-file-width="2000" height="166" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fc/Camille_1969_track.png/220px-Camille_1969_track.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fc/Camille_1969_track.png/330px-Camille_1969_track.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fc/Camille_1969_track.png/440px-Camille_1969_track.png 2x" width="220"/>\n        </a>\n        <div class="thumbcaption">\n         <div class="magnify">\n          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Camille_1969_track.png" title="Enlarge">\n          </a>\n         </div>\n         Map plotting the track and intensity of the storm, according to the\n         <a href="/wiki/Saffir%E2%80%93Simpson_scale" title="Saffir\xe2\x80\x93Simpson scale">\n          Saffir\xe2\x80\x93Simpson scale\n         </a>\n        </div>\n       </div>\n      </div>\n      <p>\n       The origins of Hurricane Camille were from a\n       <a href="/wiki/Tropical_wave" title="Tropical wave">\n        tropical wave\n       </a>\n       off the western coast of\n       <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">\n        Africa\n       </a>\n       on August\xc2\xa05, 1969. It tracked quickly westward along the\n       <a href="/wiki/15th_parallel_north" title="15th parallel north">\n        15th parallel north\n       </a>\n       ,\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-thebigmap_3-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-thebigmap-3">\n         [3]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       a tropical disturbance became clearly identifiable on satellite imagery on August\xc2\xa09. By that time, the thunderstorm activity concentrated into a circular area of\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Convection_(meteorology)" title="Convection (meteorology)">\n        convection\n       </a>\n       . The next day, it moved through the\n       <a href="/wiki/Lesser_Antilles" title="Lesser Antilles">\n        Lesser Antilles\n       </a>\n       , although there was no evidence of a closed\n       <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_circulation" title="Atmospheric circulation">\n        circulation\n       </a>\n       . On August\xc2\xa013, the wave passed near or over the southern coast of\n       <a href="/wiki/Jamaica" title="Jamaica">\n        Jamaica\n       </a>\n       as its convection spread northeastward through the\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bahamas" title="Bahamas">\n        Bahamas\n       </a>\n       . Subsequently, it began a slower motion to the northwest. It is believed that a tropical depression formed shortly thereafter, early on August\xc2\xa014, and it became a tropical storm a few hours later. On the morning of August\xc2\xa014, the\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hurricane_Hunters" title="Hurricane Hunters">\n        Hurricane Hunters\n       </a>\n       flew to investigate for a closed circulation near the Bahamas as well as near the\n       <a href="/wiki/Cayman_Islands" title="Cayman Islands">\n        Cayman Islands\n       </a>\n       .\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_4-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-mwr-4">\n         [4]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       The crew observed a developing center in the western Caribbean, and winds had reached tropical storm status. By then, the storm had strengthened into a strong tropical storm with winds of 60\xc2\xa0mph (95\xc2\xa0km/h), about 50 miles (80\xc2\xa0km) west-northwest of\n       <a href="/wiki/Grand_Cayman" title="Grand Cayman">\n        Grand Cayman\n       </a>\n       .\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_4-1">\n        <a href="#cite_note-mwr-4">\n         [4]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tcr_5-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-tcr-5">\n         [5]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       Upon first being classified as a tropical storm, Camille was located in an area favorable for further strengthening, although initially it slowly intensified. It was located within an area of very light\n       <a href="/wiki/Wind_shear" title="Wind shear">\n        wind shear\n       </a>\n       and an overall warm environment. Additionally, the storm developed strong\n       <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone#Mechanics" title="Tropical cyclone">\n        low-level inflow\n       </a>\n       from the deep southern Caribbean, which continuously brought moisture into the storm.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_4-2">\n        <a href="#cite_note-mwr-4">\n         [4]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       Throughout its duration, it was a small tropical cyclone, although with a radius of gale-force winds spreading 100 miles (160\xc2\xa0km) to the north, the storm\'s thunderstorm area quickly spread over Cuba. As the storm approached the western coast of Cuba, it began\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rapid_deepening" title="Rapid deepening">\n        rapid deepening\n       </a>\n       , reaching hurricane status and less than 12\xc2\xa0hours later attained winds of 110\xc2\xa0mph (175\xc2\xa0km/h). Prior to\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Landfall_(meteorology)" title="Landfall (meteorology)">\n        landfall\n       </a>\n       , its\n       <a href="/wiki/Eye_(cyclone)" title="Eye (cyclone)">\n        eye\n       </a>\n       was tracked by\n       <a href="/wiki/Radar" title="Radar">\n        radar\n       </a>\n       from\n       <a href="/wiki/Havana" title="Havana">\n        Havana\n       </a>\n       ; it is estimated the hurricane moved ashore between\n       <a href="/wiki/Cape_San_Antonio,_Cuba" title="Cape San Antonio, Cuba">\n        Cape San Antonio\n       </a>\n       and\n       <a href="/wiki/Guane" title="Guane">\n        Guane\n       </a>\n       late on August\xc2\xa015 as a strong Category 2 hurricane. Camille was a small hurricane as it crossed western Cuba, and its winds decreased slightly to 105\xc2\xa0mph (165\xc2\xa0km/h) over land before it emerged into the Gulf of Mexico.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tcr_5-1">\n        <a href="#cite_note-tcr-5">\n         [5]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <div class="thumb tright">\n       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">\n        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:CamilleNEWradarAug17196922z.png">\n         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="412" data-file-width="320" height="283" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ab/CamilleNEWradarAug17196922z.png/220px-CamilleNEWradarAug17196922z.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/a/ab/CamilleNEWradarAug17196922z.png 1.5x" width="220"/>\n        </a>\n        <div class="thumbcaption">\n         <div class="magnify">\n          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:CamilleNEWradarAug17196922z.png" title="Enlarge">\n          </a>\n         </div>\n         This New Orleans radar image of Hurricane Camille was taken less than 50\xc2\xa0miles from its center on August\xc2\xa017 1969 at 10 p.m. CST\n        </div>\n       </div>\n      </div>\n      <p>\n       Initially, Hurricane Camille was forecast to turn northeastward toward the\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Florida_panhandle" title="Florida panhandle">\n        Florida panhandle\n       </a>\n       . Instead, it continued northwestward and rapidly intensified once again almost immediately after leaving Cuba. Its eye contracted to a diameter of less than 8 miles (13\xc2\xa0km), and strong\n       <a href="/wiki/Outflow_(meteorology)" title="Outflow (meteorology)">\n        rainbands\n       </a>\n       developed around the entire hurricane. Due to the small eye, Hurricane Hunters at first had difficulties in obtaining the strength; however a flight late on August\xc2\xa016 found a strong\n       <a href="/wiki/List_of_Category_5_Atlantic_hurricanes" title="List of Category 5 Atlantic hurricanes">\n        Category 5 hurricane\n       </a>\n       on the\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saffir-Simpson_Hurricane_Wind_Scale" title="Saffir-Simpson Hurricane Wind Scale">\n        Saffir-Simpson Hurricane Wind Scale\n       </a>\n       and recorded a very low pressure of 908\n       <a href="/wiki/Bar_(unit)" title="Bar (unit)">\n        mbar\n       </a>\n       (\n       <a href="/wiki/Pascal_(unit)" title="Pascal (unit)">\n        hPa\n       </a>\n       ; 26.82\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Inches_of_mercury" title="Inches of mercury">\n        inHg\n       </a>\n       ), with winds estimated at 175\xc2\xa0mph (280\xc2\xa0km/h).\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_4-3">\n        <a href="#cite_note-mwr-4">\n         [4]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       At the time, it was not expected to intensify further.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tcr_5-2">\n        <a href="#cite_note-tcr-5">\n         [5]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       However, a subsequent Hurricane Hunters flight early on 17 August recorded a pressure of 905\n       <a href="/wiki/Bar_(unit)" title="Bar (unit)">\n        mbar\n       </a>\n       (\n       <a href="/wiki/Pascal_(unit)" title="Pascal (unit)">\n        hPa\n       </a>\n       ; 26.73\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Inches_of_mercury" title="Inches of mercury">\n        inHg\n       </a>\n       ), at the time the\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_most_intense_tropical_cyclones" title="List of most intense tropical cyclones">\n        lowest pressure\n       </a>\n       recorded by reconnaissance aircraft. That made Camille the most intense hurricane since the\n       <a href="/wiki/1935_Labor_Day_hurricane" title="1935 Labor Day hurricane">\n        1935 Labor Day hurricane\n       </a>\n       ; currently it is the sixth most intense Atlantic hurricane, as ranked by lowest pressure. At the same time, maximum wind speeds in the hurricane peaked at 175\xc2\xa0mph (280\xc2\xa0km/h) by 00\n       <a href="/wiki/Coordinated_Universal_Time" title="Coordinated Universal Time">\n        UTC\n       </a>\n       on August\xc2\xa017.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-metadata_6-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-metadata-6">\n         [6]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT_7-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-7">\n         [7]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       As it continued toward the\n       <a href="/wiki/Gulf_Coast_of_the_United_States" title="Gulf Coast of the United States">\n        Gulf Coast of the United States\n       </a>\n       , Camille maintained its small eye, and forecasters continued to anticipate a turn toward Florida.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tcr_5-3">\n        <a href="#cite_note-tcr-5">\n         [5]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       Late on August\xc2\xa017, Camille briefly weakened to a Category 4 storm due to an\n       <a href="/wiki/Eyewall_replacement_cycle" title="Eyewall replacement cycle">\n        eyewall replacement cycle\n       </a>\n       ; a reconnaissance flight was forced to end its mission early due to a damaged engine.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_4-4">\n        <a href="#cite_note-mwr-4">\n         [4]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       Before they left the storm, the crew recorded a pressure of 919\n       <a href="/wiki/Bar_(unit)" title="Bar (unit)">\n        mbar\n       </a>\n       (\n       <a href="/wiki/Pascal_(unit)" title="Pascal (unit)">\n        hPa\n       </a>\n       ; 27.14\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Inches_of_mercury" title="Inches of mercury">\n        inHg\n       </a>\n       ) and estimated surface winds at 155\xc2\xa0mph (250\xc2\xa0km/h), while Camille was located about 100 miles (160\xc2\xa0km) southeast of the\n       <a href="/wiki/Mississippi_River_Delta" title="Mississippi River Delta">\n        Mississippi River Delta\n       </a>\n       .\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tcr_5-4">\n        <a href="#cite_note-tcr-5">\n         [5]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       There were no subsequent Hurricane Hunter flights, but surface observations recorded later suggested that Camille quickly re-strengthened and regained Category 5 intensity. After passing very near southeastern Louisiana, Hurricane Camille made landfall early on August\xc2\xa018 in\n       <a href="/wiki/Waveland,_Mississippi" title="Waveland, Mississippi">\n        Waveland, Mississippi\n       </a>\n       .\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-metadata_6-1">\n        <a href="#cite_note-metadata-6">\n         [6]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       Maximum wind speeds near the coastline were estimated to have been about 175\xc2\xa0mph (280\xc2\xa0km/h) with a pressure of 900\n       <a href="/wiki/Bar_(unit)" title="Bar (unit)">\n        mbar\n       </a>\n       (\n       <a href="/wiki/Pascal_(unit)" title="Pascal (unit)">\n        hPa\n       </a>\n       ; 26.58\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Inches_of_mercury" title="Inches of mercury">\n        inHg\n       </a>\n       ).\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_4-5">\n        <a href="#cite_note-mwr-4">\n         [4]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       The hurricane weakened as it progressed inland, and within 14 hours of moving ashore, Camille weakened to tropical storm status. About 12 hours later, it weakened to tropical depression status, by which time it began a turn to the north and northeast. On August\xc2\xa020, Tropical Depression Camille turned eastward through Kentucky, dropping heavy rainfall in West Virginia and Virginia. Later that day it emerged into the Atlantic Ocean east of\n       <a href="/wiki/Norfolk,_Virginia" title="Norfolk, Virginia">\n        Norfolk\n       </a>\n       , and by that afternoon as it was emerging offshore it regained tropical storm status. Camille accelerated east-northeastward, attaining peak winds of 70\xc2\xa0mph (110\xc2\xa0km/h) as it interacted with larger\n       <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Debbie_(1969)" title="Hurricane Debbie (1969)">\n        Hurricane Debbie\n       </a>\n       to its southeast (although the poor sampling of the region means that it is possible that the storm may have regained hurricane intensity in a location where measurements of wind speed were not taken).\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_4-6">\n        <a href="#cite_note-mwr-4">\n         [4]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       Subsequently, Camille began to interact with a\n       <a href="/wiki/Cold_front" title="Cold front">\n        cold front\n       </a>\n       , causing it to gradually change into an\n       <a href="/wiki/Extratropical_cyclone" title="Extratropical cyclone">\n        extratropical cyclone\n       </a>\n       as it entrained cooler air. On August\xc2\xa022, Camille was absorbed by the cold front to the south of\n       <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Canada" title="Atlantic Canada">\n        Atlantic Canada\n       </a>\n       .\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_4-7">\n        <a href="#cite_note-mwr-4">\n         [4]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tcr_5-5">\n        <a href="#cite_note-tcr-5">\n         [5]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <h2>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Preparations">\n        Preparations\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurricane_Camille&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Preparations">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h2>\n      <div class="thumb tright">\n       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">\n        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:CamilleTrackForecasts.png">\n         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="622" data-file-width="610" height="224" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/52/CamilleTrackForecasts.png/220px-CamilleTrackForecasts.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/52/CamilleTrackForecasts.png/330px-CamilleTrackForecasts.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/52/CamilleTrackForecasts.png/440px-CamilleTrackForecasts.png 2x" width="220"/>\n        </a>\n        <div class="thumbcaption">\n         <div class="magnify">\n          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:CamilleTrackForecasts.png" title="Enlarge">\n          </a>\n         </div>\n         National Hurricane Center Track Forecasts for Camille from western Cuba onward. The system\'s actual track is marked by the line farthest to the west.\n        </div>\n       </div>\n      </div>\n      <p>\n       Shortly after Camille formed, the National Hurricane Center advised residents on the\n       <a href="/wiki/Isla_de_la_Juventud" title="Isla de la Juventud">\n        Isle of Pines\n       </a>\n       and in western Cuba to prepare for gale-force winds, heavy rains, and rising tides. The agency also recommended small boats to remain in harbor.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tcr_5-6">\n        <a href="#cite_note-tcr-5">\n         [5]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       The threat of the storm prompted officials to evacuate thousands along the western coast of Cuba and on the Isle of Pines; on the island, 10,000\xc2\xa0cattle and 6,000\xc2\xa0turkeys were moved to safer areas.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ap817_8-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-ap817-8">\n         [8]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       As Camille impacted Cuba, small craft were advised not to venture out too far from the coasts of Florida.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">\n        <a href="#cite_note-9">\n         [9]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       On 15 August, the National Hurricane Center issued a hurricane watch for a 110 miles (180\xc2\xa0km) stretch of land in Florida, between\n       <a href="/wiki/Apalachicola,_Florida" title="Apalachicola, Florida">\n        Apalachicola\n       </a>\n       and\n       <a href="/wiki/Fort_Walton_Beach,_Florida" title="Fort Walton Beach, Florida">\n        Fort Walton Beach\n       </a>\n       . The next day, as these hurricane watches were upgraded to a hurricane warning, thousands of people boarded up their homes and evacuated inland. During the afternoon of 16 August, the weather bureau ordered that a hurricane watch should be placed in force for the coastline from\n       <a href="/wiki/Biloxi,_Mississippi" title="Biloxi, Mississippi">\n        Biloxi\n       </a>\n       to\n       <a href="/wiki/St._Marks,_Florida" title="St. Marks, Florida">\n        St Marks\n       </a>\n       , Florida. Later that day, the hurricane watches were upgraded to a hurricane warning for the northwestern Florida coast, from Fort Walton to St Marks.\n       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">\n        [\n        <i>\n         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">\n          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2015)">\n           citation needed\n          </span>\n         </a>\n        </i>\n        ]\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       By Saturday morning, a hurricane watch was issued for the coast from Biloxi eastward. Civil defense organizations in coastal counties went on alert.\n       <a href="/wiki/Keesler_Air_Force_Base" title="Keesler Air Force Base">\n        Keesler Air Force Base\n       </a>\n       and the Naval Construction Battalion Center prepared for the storm. By 5 p.m. on Sunday, a hurricane warning was issued for the coast. This activated the\n       <a href="/wiki/National_Guard_of_the_United_States" title="National Guard of the United States">\n        National Guard\n       </a>\n       units. Many refused to believe the reports concerning Camille\'s intensity that afternoon. Many people living at elevations of 20 feet (6.1\xc2\xa0m) above\n       <a href="/wiki/Sea_level" title="Sea level">\n        sea level\n       </a>\n       refused to believe they were going to be submerged. The mayor of\n       <a href="/wiki/Gulfport,_Mississippi" title="Gulfport, Mississippi">\n        Gulfport\n       </a>\n       ordered the release of prisoners from the city jail as winds increased at 9 p.m. on Sunday evening, but none would leave.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sullivan_10-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-Sullivan-10">\n         [10]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n </td>\n        <td align="center">\n         21\n        </td>\n        <td align="center">\n         33\n        </td>\n       </tr>\n      </table>\n      <p>\n       Making landfall in\n       <a href="/wiki/Waveland,_Mississippi" title="Waveland, Mississippi">\n        Waveland, Mississippi\n       </a>\n       , as a Category\xc2\xa05 hurricane,\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-metadata_6-2">\n        <a href="#cite_note-metadata-6">\n         [6]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       Camille caused damage and destruction across much of the\n       <a href="/wiki/Gulf_Coast_of_the_United_States" title="Gulf Coast of the United States">\n        Gulf Coast of the United States\n       </a>\n       . Because it moved quickly through the region, Hurricane Camille dropped only moderate precipitation in most areas. Areas in and around Pass Christian, its point of landfall, reported from 7 to 10 inches (180 to 250\xc2\xa0mm).\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">\n        <a href="#cite_note-12">\n         [12]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       The area of total destruction in\n       <a href="/wiki/Harrison_County,_Mississippi" title="Harrison County, Mississippi">\n        Harrison County, Mississippi\n       </a>\n       was 68 square miles (180\xc2\xa0km\n       <sup>\n        2\n       </sup>\n       ).\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-harrison_13-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-harrison-13">\n         [13]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       The total estimated cost of damage was $1.42\xc2\xa0billion (1969\n       <a href="/wiki/United_States_dollar" title="United States dollar">\n        USD\n       </a>\n       , $9.27\xc2\xa0billion 2017\xc2\xa0USD).\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-inflation_14-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-inflation-14">\n         [14]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       This made Camille the second-most expensive hurricane in the United States, up to that point (behind\n       <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Betsy" title="Hurricane Betsy">\n        Hurricane Betsy\n       </a>\n       ).\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Deadliest_15-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-Deadliest-15">\n         [15]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       The storm directly killed 143\xc2\xa0people along\n       <a href="/wiki/Alabama" title="Alabama">\n        Alabama\n       </a>\n       ,\n       <a href="/wiki/Mississippi" title="Mississippi">\n        Mississippi\n       </a>\n       , and\n       <a href="/wiki/Louisiana" title="Louisiana">\n        Louisiana\n       </a>\n       . An additional 153\xc2\xa0people perished as a result of catastrophic flooding in\n       <a href="/wiki/Nelson_County,_Virginia" title="Nelson County, Virginia">\n        Nelson County, Virginia\n       </a>\n       and other areas nearby. In all, 8,931\xc2\xa0people were injured, 5,662 homes were destroyed, and 13,915 homes experienced major damage, with many of the fatalities being coastal residents who had refused to evacuate.\n      </p>\n      <h3>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Caribbean_and_Offshore_Gulf_of_Mexico">\n        Caribbean and Offshore Gulf of Mexico\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurricane_Camille&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Caribbean and Offshore Gulf of Mexico">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h3>\n      <p>\n       As a developing tropical storm, Camille brought rain showers to\n       <a href="/wiki/Grand_Cayman" title="Grand Cayman">\n        Grand Cayman\n       </a>\n       , although there were no reports of damage.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tcr_5-7">\n        <a href="#cite_note-tcr-5">\n         [5]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       Stations in Cuba on the outer fringes of the storm reported winds of 50\xc2\xa0mph (80\xc2\xa0km/h). East of where it moved ashore, the city of Guane recorded winds of 92\xc2\xa0mph (148\xc2\xa0km/h), although no wind reports were taken in the landfall location. The hurricane produced up to 10 inches (250\xc2\xa0mm) near Guane, as well as on the\n       <a href="/wiki/Isla_de_la_Juventud" title="Isla de la Juventud">\n        Isle of Pines\n       </a>\n       .\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tcr_5-8">\n        <a href="#cite_note-tcr-5">\n         [5]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       On the Isle of Pines, the storm inflicted damage to about 100\xc2\xa0houses.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ap817_8-1">\n        <a href="#cite_note-ap817-8">\n         [8]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       Throughout\n       <a href="/wiki/Pinar_del_R%C3%ADo_Province" title="Pinar del R\xc3\xado Province">\n        Pinar del R\xc3\xado Province\n       </a>\n       , Camille caused heavy damage,\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ap816_16-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-ap816-16">\n         [16]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       primarily from river flooding;\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ap818_17-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-ap818-17">\n         [17]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       about 20,000\xc2\xa0people were left homeless in the province. Strong winds downed trees and power lines, which caused power outages eastward through the capital city of\n       <a href="/wiki/Havana" title="Havana">\n        Havana\n       </a>\n       . Initially, the government reported no casualties from the storm.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ap816_16-1">\n        <a href="#cite_note-ap816-16">\n         [16]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       Subsequent research indicated the hurricane killed five people in the country during its passage, and damage was estimated at $5\xc2\xa0million (1969\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_States_Dollar" title="United States Dollar">\n        USD\n       </a>\n       ).\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pdfnoaa_18-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-pdfnoaa-18">\n         [18]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       In the days after the storm struck Cuba, the government deployed medical teams to affected regions to provide\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Typhoid" title="Typhoid">\n        typhoid\n       </a>\n       vaccine shots. Officials noted the potential for the spread of disease, due to flooding from Camille as well as previously wet conditions.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ap817_8-2">\n        <a href="#cite_note-ap817-8">\n         [8]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       In the open\n       <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_Mexico" title="Gulf of Mexico">\n        Gulf of Mexico\n       </a>\n       , the hurricane produced\n       <a href="/wiki/Wave_height" title="Wave height">\n        wave heights\n       </a>\n       of at least 70 feet (21\xc2\xa0m), as measured by\n       <a href="/wiki/Shell_Oil_Company" title="Shell Oil Company">\n        Shell Oil Company\n       </a>\n       . Along the ocean floor, the storm created mudslides which lowered the ocean floor; its combination with strong waves and winds destroyed three oil platforms, including one that at the time was the deepest oil well. Property damages to the offshore oil industry were initially estimated at $100\xc2\xa0million (1969\xc2\xa0USD).\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19">\n        <a href="#cite_note-19">\n  Lower Ninth Ward\n       </a>\n       , which suffered the most severe flooding during Betsy.\n      </p>\n      <h3>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Mississippi">\n        Mississippi\n Fires consumed coastal communities, with the exceptions of\n       <a href="/wiki/Bay_St._Louis,_Mississippi" title="Bay St. Louis, Mississippi">\n        Bay St. Louis\n       </a>\n       and\n       <a href="/wiki/Waveland,_Mississippi" title="Waveland, Mississippi">\n        Waveland\n       </a>\n       . Who the survivor is, how many party guests there were, and just how far the sole survivor was swept by the storm varies with the retelling. An\n       <a href="/wiki/American_Broadcasting_Company" title="American Broadcasting Company">\n        ABC\n       </a>\n       made-for-TV movie called\n       <i>\n        Hurricane\n       </i>\n       , starring\n       <a href="/wiki/Frank_Sutton" title="Frank Sutton">\n        Frank Sutton\n       </a>\n       of\n       <i>\n        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gomer_Pyle_USMC" title="Gomer Pyle USMC">\n         Gomer Pyle USMC\n        </a>\n       </i>\n       ,\n       <a href="/wiki/Larry_Hagman" title="Larry Hagman">\n        Larry Hagman\n       </a>\n       ,\n       <a href="/wiki/Patrick_Duffy" title="Patrick Duffy">\n        Patrick Duffy\n       </a>\n       ,\n       <a href="/wiki/Will_Geer" title="Will Geer">\n        Will Geer\n       </a>\n       ,\n       <a href="/wiki/Martin_Milner" title="Martin Milner">\n        Martin Milner\n       </a>\n       , and\n       <a href="/wiki/Michael_Learned" title="Michael Learned">\n        Michael Learned\n       </a>\n       , was loosely based on the supposed hurricane party. However, the site and names were changed to fictitious ones for the movie, as was the name of the hurricane. The television show\n       <i>\n        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Quantum_Leap_(TV_series)" title="Quantum Leap (TV series)">\n         Quantum Leap\n        </a>\n       </i>\n       carried an episode regarding the parties of Hurricane Camille and the actual hurricane itself. Twenty-three people are known to have stayed in the Richelieu Apartments during the hurricane, eight of whom died. The tale of the lone survivor and the party appears to have originated with survivor Mary Ann Gerlach.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sullivan_10-2">\n        <a href="#cite_note-Sullivan-10">\n         [10]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       Survivor Ben Duckworth has expressed irritation at the story.\n       <i>\n        There was no hurricane party.\n       </i>\n       Duckworth reiterated in 2001.\n       <i>\n        We were exhausted from boarding up windows and helping the police move cars. We were too tired to party. I can\xe2\x80\x99t tell you why that story persists, or why people didn\'t put two and two together. I guess the hurricane party makes a good story.\n       </i>\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24">\n        <a href="#cite_note-24">\n         [24]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       The site of the Richelieu Apartments, the corner of Henderson Avenue and US 90 in Pass Christian, later became a shopping center. Coincidentally, it was destroyed by\n       <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Katrina" title="Hurricane Katrina">\n        Hurricane Katrina\n       </a>\n       .\n      </p>\n      <h3>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Alabama_and_Florida">\n        Alabama and Florida\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurricane_Camille&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Alabama and Florida">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h3>\n      <p>\n       Alabama also experienced damage along U.S. Highway 90: 26,000 homes and over 1,000 businesses were wiped out completely across the state of Alabama. Camille\'s large circulation also resulted in a 3-to-5-foot (0.91 to 1.52\xc2\xa0m) storm surge in\n       <a href="/wiki/Apalachicola,_Florida" title="Apalachicola, Florida">\n        Apalachicola, Florida\n       </a>\n       . The highest rainfall report received within Alabama was 6.52 inches (166\xc2\xa0mm) two miles northeast of\n       <a href="/wiki/Fairhope,_Alabama" title="Fairhope, Alabama">\n        Fairhope\n       </a>\n       .\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TCgulfrain_21-2">\n        <a href="#cite_note-TCgulfrain-21">\n         [21]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       Camille caused about $8 million (1969 dollars) of damage in Alabama.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_4-10">\n        <a href="#cite_note-mwr-4">\n         [4]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       Places farther east across the western\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Florida_panhandle" title="Florida panhandle">\n        Florida panhandle\n       </a>\n       saw lesser rains, as 4.16 inches (106\xc2\xa0mm) was measured at\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pensacola_Naval_Air_Station" title="Pensacola Naval Air Station">\n        Pensacola Naval Air Station\n       </a>\n       .\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Florida_Rain_25-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-Florida_Rain-25">\n         [25]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <h3>\n       <span     </div>\n      </div>\n      <p>\n       Camille caused moderate rainfall in\n       <a href="/wiki/Tennessee" title="Tennessee">\n        Tennessee\n       </a>\n       and\n       <a West\xc2\xa0Virginia\n       </a>\n       , there was flash flooding which destroyed 36 houses and 12 trailers, a total of three quarters of a million dollars in damage.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CamilleTCR_27-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-CamilleTCR-27">\n         [27]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <h3>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Virginia">\n        Virginia\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurricane_Camille&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Virginia">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h3>\n      <p>\n       Because the hurricane was expected to quickly dissipate over land, few were prepared for the flash flooding.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mrw_28-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-mrw-28">\n         [28]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       Arriving in\n       <a href="/wiki/Virginia" title="Virginia">\n        Virginia\n       </a>\n       on the evening of August\xc2\xa019, Camille was no longer a hurricane, but it carried high amounts of moisture and contained sufficient strength and low pressure to pull in additional moisture.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       A widespread area of western and central Virginia received over 8 inches (200\xc2\xa0mm) of rain from Camille\'s remains, leading to significant flooding across the state. A total of 153 people lost their lives from\n       <a href="/wiki/Blunt_trauma" title="Blunt trauma">\n        blunt trauma\n       </a>\n       sustained during mountain slides, related to the flash flooding, not drowning.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CamilleErosion1_29-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-CamilleErosion1-29">\n         [29]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       More than 123 of these deaths, including 21 members of one family, the Huffmans, were in Nelson County. Debris\n       <a href="/wiki/Avalanche" title="Avalanche">\n        avalanches\n       </a>\n       occurred on hillsides with a slope greater than 35\xc2\xa0percent.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CamilleErosion1_29-1">\n        <a href="#cite_note-CamilleErosion1-29">\n         [29]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       In Nelson County, the number of deaths amounted to over one percent of the county\'s population.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Divinewind_30-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-Divinewind-30">\n         [30]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       The worst of the damage was reported in\n       <a href="/wiki/Massies_Mill,_Virginia" title="Massies Mill, Virginia">\n        Massies Mill\n       </a>\n       ,\n       <a href="/wiki/Woods_Mill,_Virginia" title="Woods Mill, Virginia">\n        Woods Mill\n       </a>\n       ,\n       <a href="/wiki/Roseland,_Virginia" title="Roseland, Virginia">\n        Roseland\n       </a>\n       ,\n       <a href="/wiki/Bryant,_Virginia" title="Bryant, Virginia">\n        Bryant\n       </a>\n       ,\n       <a href="/wiki/Tyro,_Virginia" title="Tyro, Virginia">\n        Tyro\n       </a>\n       ,\n       <a href="/wiki/Montebello,_Virginia" title="Montebello, Virginia">\n        Montebello\n       </a>\n       ,\n       <a href="/wiki/Lovingston,_Virginia" title="Lovingston, Virginia">\n        Lovingston\n       </a>\n       ,\n       <a href="/wiki/Norwood,_Nelson_County,_Virginia" title="Norwood, Nelson County, Virginia">\n        Norwood\n       </a>\n       ,\n       <a href="/wiki/Rockfish,_Virginia" title="Rockfish, Virginia">\n        Rockfish\n       </a>\n       , and along the Davis and Muddy creeks.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CamilleErosion1_29-2">\n        <a href="#cite_note-CamilleErosion1-29">\n         [29]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       The James and Tye rivers crested well above flood stage in many areas, including a record high of 41.3 feet (12.6\xc2\xa0m) at\n       <a href="/wiki/Columbia,_Virginia" title="Columbia, Virginia">\n        Columbia, Virginia\n       </a>\n       . Hurricane Camille caused more than $140 million of damage (1969 dollars) in Virginia.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_4-11">\n        <a href="#cite_note-mwr-4">\n         [4]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tcr_5-9">\n        <a href="#cite_note-tcr-5">\n         [5]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       Camille was considered one of the worst\n       <a href="/wiki/Natural_disaster" title="Natural disaster">\n        natural disasters\n       </a>\n       in central Virginia\'s recorded history.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CamilleErosion1_29-3">\n        <a href="#cite_note-CamilleErosion1-29">\n         [29]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <div class="thumb tleft">\n       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;">\n        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Rt._626_at_the_Rockfish_River_(7797529596).jpg">\n         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="4350" data-file-width="5550" height="157" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d6/Rt._626_at_the_Rockfish_River_%287797529596%29.jpg/200px-Rt._626_at_the_Rockfish_River_%287797529596%29.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d6/Rt._626_at_the_Rockfish_River_%287797529596%29.jpg/300px-Rt._626_at_the_Rockfish_River_%287797529596%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d6/Rt._626_at_the_Rockfish_River_%287797529596%29.jpg/400px-Rt._626_at_the_Rockfish_River_%287797529596%29.jpg 2x" width="200"/>\n        </a>\n        <div class="thumbcaption">\n         <div class="magnify">\n          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Rt._626_at_the_Rockfish_River_(7797529596).jpg" title="Enlarge">\n          </a>\n         </div>\n         Flooding damage at\n         <a href="/wiki/Howardsville,_Albemarle_County,_Virginia" title="Howardsville, Albemarle County, Virginia">\n          Howardsville, Virginia\n         </a>\n         ; the bridge formerly carried Virginia Route 626 over the\n         <a href="/wiki/Rockfish_River" title="Rockfish River">\n          Rockfish River\n         </a>\n         .\n        </div>\n       </div>\n      </div>\n      <p>\n       The storm dropped torrential rainfall of 12 to 20 inches (300 to 510\xc2\xa0mm), with a maximum of 27 inches (690\xc2\xa0mm).\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DR_26-1">\n        <a href="#cite_note-DR-26">\n         [26]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       Most of the rainfall occurred in Virginia during a 3-5\xc2\xa0hour period on 19\xe2\x80\x9320 August. Five (5) plus inches of rain fell near the North Fork of the Tye River in only half an hour with the grounds already saturated from previous rains.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mrw_28-1">\n        <a href="#cite_note-mrw-28">\n         [28]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       Many rivers flooded across the state, with the worst being the\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/James_River_(Virginia)" title="James River (Virginia)">\n        James River\n       </a>\n       in\n       <a href="/wiki/Richmond,_Virginia" title="Richmond, Virginia">\n        Richmond\n       </a>\n       with a peak crest of 28.6 feet (8.7\xc2\xa0m). Many rivers in Virginia and West Virginia set records for peak flood stages, causing numerous mudslides along mountainsides. In the mountain slopes between\n       <a href="/wiki/Charlottesville,_Virginia" title="Charlottesville, Virginia">\n        Charlottesville\n       </a>\n       and\n       <a href="/wiki/Lynchburg,_Virginia" title="Lynchburg, Virginia">\n        Lynchburg\n       </a>\n       , more than 26 inches (660\xc2\xa0mm) of rain fell in 12 hours, but the worst was in\n       <a href="/wiki/Nelson_County,_Virginia" title="Nelson County, Virginia">\n        Nelson County\n       </a>\n       where 27 inches (690\xc2\xa0mm) fell. There, rainfall was so heavy that reports were received of birds drowning in trees, cows floating down the Hatt Creek and of survivors having to cup hands around their mouth and nose in order to breathe through the deluge. Though the official rainfall was recorded as 27 inches, unofficial estimates are much greater. Some estimate that more than 40 inches of rain fell at Davis Creek. Most gauges were washed away; however, it was reported that an empty 55 gallon drum that was not even in the center of the heaviest rainfall had 31 inches of water in it after Camille passed.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31">\n        <a href="#cite_note-31">\n         [31]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       "So much rain fell in such a short time in Nelson County that, according to the National Weather Service at the time, it was \'the probable maximum rainfall which meteorologists compute to be theoretically possible.\' "\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32">\n        <a href="#cite_note-32">\n         [32]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       The ensuing flash floods and mudslides killed 153\xc2\xa0people. In Nelson County alone, 133 bridges washed out, while in some places entire communities were under water.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CamilleTCR_27-1">\n        <a href="#cite_note-CamilleTCR-27">\n         [27]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       The major flooding that occurred downstream cut off all communication between Richmond and the\n       <a href="/wiki/Shenandoah_Valley" title="Shenandoah Valley">\n        Shenandoah Valley\n       </a>\n       .\n       <a href="/wiki/Waynesboro,_Virginia" title="Waynesboro, Virginia">\n        Waynesboro\n       </a>\n       on the\n       <a href="/wiki/South_River_(South_Fork_Shenandoah_River)" title="South River (South Fork Shenandoah River)">\n        South River\n       </a>\n       saw eight feet of water downtown, and\n       <a href="/wiki/Buena_Vista,_Virginia" title="Buena Vista, Virginia">\n        Buena Vista\n       </a>\n       had more than five feet.\n      </p>\n id="cite_ref-srh_37-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-srh-37">\n         [37]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       but this pressure was later corrected in 1969 by researchers to 919\xc2\xa0mb (27.14\xc2\xa0inHg).\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-metadata_6-4">\n        <a href="#cite_note-metadata-6">\n         [6]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       The wind speed of Camille can only be approximated, as no meteorological equipment survived the extreme conditions at landfall, but Camille is estimated to have had sustained winds of 190\xc2\xa0mph (305\xc2\xa0km/h) at landfall, with gusts exceeding 230\xc2\xa0mph (370\xc2\xa0km/h),\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-metadata_6-5">\n        <a href="#cite_note-metadata-6">\n         [6]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-srh_37-1">\n        <a href="#cite_note-srh-37">\n         [37]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       although a reanalysis in April 2014 concluded that Camille had maximum winds of 175\xc2\xa0mph (280\xc2\xa0km/h) rather than the 190 beforehand. Before\n       <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Katrina" title="Hurricane Katrina">\n        Hurricane Katrina\n       </a>\n       in 2005, Camille likely had the highest\n       <a href="/wiki/Storm_surge" title="Storm surge">\n        storm surge\n       </a>\n       measured in the United States, at over 24 feet (7.3\xc2\xa0m).\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-army_38-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-army-38">\n         [38]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       The 24-foot (7.3\xc2\xa0m) storm surge quoted by the Army Corps of Engineers was based on high-water marks inside surviving buildings, of which there were but three. Prior to the collapse of the Richelieu Apartments, Ben Duckworth shone a flashlight down a stairwell and found the water within one step of the third-story floor; this establishes a surge height of 28 feet (8.5\xc2\xa0m) at that spot at that time. About 15\xc2\xa0minutes later, the building collapsed and the evidence vanished with it.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       In addition, Camille forced the\n       <a href="/wiki/Mississippi_River" title="Mississippi River">\n        Mississippi River\n       </a>\n       to flow backwards for a river-distance of 125\xc2\xa0miles (from its mouth to a point north of\n       <a href="/wiki/New_Orleans" title="New Orleans">\n        New Orleans\n       </a>\n       ). The river further backed up for an additional 120 miles (190\xc2\xa0km), to a point north of\n       <a href="/wiki/Baton_Rouge,_Louisiana" title="Baton Rouge, Louisiana">\n        Baton Rouge\n       </a>\n       .\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cat5book_39-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-cat5book-39">\n         [39]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       In 1969 the naming conventions for hurricanes were not strictly controlled as they are today. There were only three requirements: the name had to be female (male names were not used at that time), the names had to remain in alphabetical order, and the name could not have been retired.\n       </div>\n      </div>\n      <p>\n       The response after the storm involved many federal, state, and local agencies and volunteer organizations. The main organization for coordinating the federal response to the disaster was the Office of Emergency Preparedness, which provided $76\xc2\xa0million (1969\xc2\xa0USD, $403\xc2\xa0million 2005\xc2\xa0USD)\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-inflation_14-2">\n        <a href="#cite_note-inflation-14">\n         [14]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       to administer and coordinate disaster relief programs. Food and shelter were available the day after the storm. On 19 August, parts of Mississippi and Louisiana were declared major disaster areas and became eligible for federal disaster relief funds.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-thirty_40-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-thirty-40">\n         [40]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       President\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Richard_M._Nixon" title="Richard M. Nixon">\n        Richard M. Nixon\n       </a>\n       ordered 1450 regular troops and 800 United States Army Engineers into the area to bring tons of food, vehicles, and aircraft.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sullivan_10-3">\n        <a href="#cite_note-Sullivan-10">\n         [10]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       Large organizations contributing to the relief effort included the Federal Power Commission, which helped fully return power to affected areas by 25 November 1969. The Coast Guard (then under the Department of Transportation), Air Force, Army, Army Corps of Engineers, Navy Seabees, and Marine Corps all helped with evacuations, search and rescue, clearing debris, and distribution of food. The Department of Defense contributed $34\xc2\xa0million (1969\xc2\xa0USD, $180\xc2\xa0million 2005\xc2\xa0USD)\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-inflation_14-3">\n        <a href="#cite_note-inflation-14">\n         [14]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       and 16,500\xc2\xa0military troops overall to the recovery. The Department of Health provided 4\xc2\xa0million dollars towards medicine, vaccines and other health related needs.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-thirty_40-1">\n        <a href="#cite_note-thirty-40">\n         [40]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       On Monday, the Air National Guard and those at Keesler Air Force Base airlifted patients to\n       <a href="/wiki/Jackson,_Mississippi" title="Jackson, Mississippi">\n        Jackson\n       </a>\n       and other more inland locations. Volunteers searched for those injured and dead, as well as helping refugees. When many of the evacuees returned by Tuesday, Governor\n       <a href="/wiki/John_Bell_Williams" title="John Bell Williams">\n        John Bell Williams\n       </a>\n       declared\n       <a href="/wiki/Martial_law" title="Martial law">\n        martial law\n       </a>\n       , blocking highways into the area and leading to a 6 p.m. to 6 a.m. curfew. The governor also opened\n       <a href="/wiki/Camp_Shelby" title="Camp Shelby">\n        Camp Shelby\n       </a>\n       , dormitories within the\n       <a href="/wiki/University_of_Southern_Mississippi" title="University of Southern Mississippi">\n        University of Southern Mississippi\n       </a>\n       , and the Robert E. Lee Hotel to serve as shelters for those who lost homes. Sections of Bay St. Louis and Pass Christian were evacuated. Survivors were found for days after the storm, with 35 trapped by high water north of Bay St. Louis rescued on Thursday. Army engineers disposed of 25 tons of dead animals, mostly cows, during the week following Camille. One week after the storm, Keesler-based air planes sprayed\n       <a href="/wiki/Malathion" title="Malathion">\n        malathion\n       </a>\n       at low altitudes to kill the thriving insect population. Martial law would be lifted on 27 August. The federal and state military presence would continue for several weeks. During the evening of 8 September, President Nixon visited the Biloxi-Gulfport Regional Airport and gave a speech to elevate the spirits of local residents struggling with the storm\'s aftermath. During the rebuilding process, stricter building codes were enforced by local governments. In 1973, hurricane hunters and their associated reconnaissance aircraft relocated to Keesler Air Force Base when their previous headquarters at\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ramey,_Puerto_Rico" title="Ramey, Puerto Rico">\n        Ramey, Puerto Rico\n       </a>\n       , closed.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sullivan_10-4">\n        <a $159\xc2\xa0million 2005\xc2\xa0USD)\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-inflation_14-4">\n        <a href="#cite_note-inflation-14">\n         [14]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       towards planned and coordinated redevelopment of affected areas. NOAA Weather Radio was expanded to coastal locations during the 1970s in the wake of Camille based upon recommendations made by the Department of Commerce in September 1969.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41">\n        <a href="#cite_note-41">\n         [41]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       The devastation of Camille inspired the implementation of the\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saffir-Simpson_Hurricane_Scale" title="Saffir-Simpson Hurricane Scale">\n        Saffir-Simpson Hurricane Scale\n       </a>\n       . After the storm, many Gulf Coast residents commented that hurricane warnings were not clear enough in conveying the expected intensity of the coming storm. The Saffir-Simpson scale offered a much more concise statement of storm intensity than barometric pressure and wind-speed measurements, and veterans of previous hurricanes could analogize the power of the approaching storm to those they had experienced.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42">\n        <a href="#cite_note-42">\n         [42]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       In a 1999 report on Hurricane Camille sponsored by the NOAA Coastal Services Center, the authors concluded: "With Camille, the preparations for the event and the response were based on processes put in place long before the storm made landfall. Coordination between government agencies as well as with state and local officials was enhanced because of preexisting plans."\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-thirty_40-2">\n        <a href="#cite_note-thirty-40">\n         [40]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       One small compensation was that recovery from flood damage in\n       <a href="/wiki/Nelson_County,_Virginia" title="Nelson County, Virginia">\n        Nelson County, Virginia\n       </a>\n       led to the discovery of the\n       <a href="/wiki/Ginger_Gold" title="Ginger Gold">\n        Ginger Gold\n       </a>\n       apple in the orchards of Clyde Harvey.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ginger_Gold_43-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-Ginger_Gold-43">\n         [43]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <h3>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Retirement">\n        Retirement\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurricane_Camille&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Retirement">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h3>\n      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">\n       See also:\n       <a href="/wiki/List_of_retired_Atlantic_hurricane_names" title="List of retired Atlantic hurricane names">\n        List of retired Atlantic hurricane names\n       </a>\n      </div>\n      <p>\n       Due to the major destruction and death in much of the Southern United States, the name\n       <i>\n        Camille\n       </i>\n       was retired after the 1969 season,\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44">\n        <a href="#cite_note-44">\n         [44]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       and will never again be used for an Atlantic or Gulf hurricane or tropical storm. The name Cindy was scheduled to replace the name in 1973,\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45">\n        <a href="#cite_note-45">\n         [45]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       although a new ten-year list of names was created for the 1972-1980 Atlantic hurricane seasons.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Naples_46-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-Naples-46">\n href="/wiki/Hurricane_Katrina" title="Hurricane Katrina">\n        Hurricane Katrina\n       </a>\n       took different paths, they both reached the same section of the coast of Mississippi with similar destructive effects. Camille intensified more rapidly than Katrina, and unlike Katrina, Camille re-intensified a second time and maintained status as a Category 5 hurricane until landfall. Both hurricanes shared the common aspect of undergoing periods of rapid intensification.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47">\n        <a href="#cite_note-47">\n         [47]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       The size of Camille\'s\n       <a href="/wiki/Radius_of_maximum_wind" title="Radius of maximum wind">\n        radius of maximum wind\n       </a>\n       was less than one-third that of Katrina, more similar to the intense but small\n       <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Andrew" title="Hurricane Andrew">\n        Hurricane Andrew\n       </a>\n       . Also unlike Katrina, Camille caused little damage in\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_Orleans,_Louisiana" title="New Orleans, Louisiana">\n        New Orleans, Louisiana\n       </a>\n       , though Camille itself just barely missed the city.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48">\n        <a href="#cite_note-48">\n         [48]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       The area of hurricane-force winds within Camille was just over two-thirds the size of Hurricane Katrina. Both storms were moving at a similar forward motion at the time of landfall. Although Camille\'s wind speed at landfall was higher, Katrina\'s storm surge exceeded Camille\'s storm surge at all known locations due to its greater size.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49">\n        <a href="#cite_note-49">\n         [49]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       Both hurricanes\' names were retired.
