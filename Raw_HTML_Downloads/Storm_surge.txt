The next deadliest in this century was caused by the\n       <a href="/wiki/Typhoon_Haiyan" title="Typhoon Haiyan">\n        Typhoon Haiyan\n       </a>\n       (Yolanda), which killed more than 6,000 Texas">\n        Galveston, Texas\n       </a>\n       , drove a devastating surge ashore; between 6,000 and 12,000\xc2\xa0lives were lost, making it the deadliest\n       <a accounts was produced by the 1899\n       <a href="/wiki/Cyclone_Mahina" title="Cyclone Mahina">\n        Cyclone Mahina\n       </a>\n       , estimated at almost 44\xc2\xa0ft (13\xc2\xa0metres) at\n       <a href="/wiki/Bathurst_Bay" title="Bathurst Bay">\n        Bathurst Bay\n       </a>\n       ,\n       <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">\n        Australia\n       </a>\n       , but research published in 2000 concluded that the majority of this likely was wave run-up because of the steep coastal topography.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nott_and_Hayne_7-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-Nott_and_Hayne-7">\n         [7]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       In the United States, one of the greatest recorded storm surges was generated by\n       <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Katrina" title="Hurricane Katrina">\n        Hurricane Katrina\n       </a>\n       in 2005, which produced a maximum storm surge of more than 25\xc2\xa0ft (8\xc2\xa0metres) in southern\n       <a href="/wiki/Mississippi" title="Mississippi">\n        Mississippi\n       </a>\n       , with a storm surge height of 27.8\xc2\xa0ft (8.5\xc2\xa0m) in\n       <a </a>\n       </sup>\n       Another record storm surge occurred in this same area from\n       <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Camille" title="Hurricane Camille">\n        Hurricane Camille\n       </a>\n       in 1969, with a storm tide of 24.6\xc2\xa0ft (7.5\xc2\xa0m), also at Pass Christian.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Simpson.2C_1969_10-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-Simpson.2C_1969-10">\n         [10]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       A storm surge of 14\xc2\xa0ft (4.2\xc2\xa0m) occurred in\n       <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">\n        New York City\n       </a>\n       during\n       <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Sandy" title="Hurricane Sandy">\n        Hurricane Sandy\n       </a>\n       in October 2012.\n      </p>\n      <h2>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Mechanics">\n        Mechanics\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Storm_surge&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Mechanics">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h2>\n      <p>\n       At least five processes can be involved in altering\n       <a href="/wiki/Tide" title="Tide">\n        tide\n       </a>\n       levels during storms:\n      </p>\n      <ul>\n       <li>\n        The atmospheric pressure effect\n       </li>\n       <li>\n        The direct wind effect\n       </li>\n       <li>\n        The effect of the Earth\'s rotation\n       </li>\n       <li>\n        The effect of waves near the shore\n       </li>\n       <li>\n        The rainfall effect.\n        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Har63_11-0">\n         <a href="#cite_note-Har63-11">\n          [11]\n         </a>\n        </sup>\n       </li>\n      </ul>\n      <p>\n       The pressure effects of a tropical cyclone will cause the water level in the open ocean to rise in regions of low\n       <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_pressure" title="Atmospheric pressure">\n        atmospheric pressure\n       </a>\n       and fall in regions of high atmospheric pressure. The rising water level will counteract the low atmospheric pressure such that the total pressure at some plane beneath the water surface remains constant. This effect is estimated at a 10\xc2\xa0mm (0.39\xc2\xa0in) increase in sea level for every\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Millibar" title="Millibar">\n        millibar\n       </a>\n       (hPa) drop in atmospheric pressure.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Har63_11-1">\n        <a href="#cite_note-Har63-11">\n         [11]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       Strong surface winds cause surface currents at a 45\xc2\xb0 angle to the wind direction, by an effect known as the\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ekman_Spiral" title="Ekman Spiral">\n        Ekman Spiral\n       </a>\n       . Wind stresses cause a phenomenon referred to as "wind set-up", which is the tendency for water levels to increase at the downwind shore and to decrease at the upwind shore. Intuitively, this is caused by the storm blowing the water toward one side of the basin in the direction of its winds. Because the Ekman Spiral effects spread vertically through the water, the effect is inversely proportional to depth. The pressure effect and the wind set-up on an open coast will be driven into bays in the same way as the astronomical tide.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Har63_11-2">\n        <a href="#cite_note-Har63-11">\n         [11]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       The Earth\'s rotation causes the\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coriolis_effect" title="Coriolis effect">\n        Coriolis effect\n       </a>\n       , which bends currents to the right in the Northern Hemisphere and to the left in the Southern Hemisphere. When this bend brings the currents into more perpendicular contact with the shore, it can amplify the surge, and when it bends the current away from the shore it has the effect of lessening the surge.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Har63_11-3">\n        <a href="#cite_note-Har63-11">\n         [11]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       The effect of waves, while directly powered by the wind, is distinct from a storm\'s wind-powered currents. Powerful wind whips up large, strong waves in the direction of its movement.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Har63_11-4">\n        <a href="#cite_note-Har63-11">\n         [11]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       Although these surface waves are responsible for very little water transport in open water, they may be responsible for significant transport near the shore. When waves are breaking on a line more or less parallel to the beach, they carry considerable water shoreward. As they break, the water particles moving toward the shore have considerable momentum and may run up a sloping beach to an elevation above the mean water line, which may exceed twice the wave height before breaking.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Granthem_1953_12-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-Granthem_1953-12">\n         [12]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       The rainfall effect is experienced predominantly in\n       <a href="/wiki/Estuary" title="Estuary">\n        estuaries\n       </a>\n       . Hurricanes may dump as much as 12\xc2\xa0in (300\xc2\xa0mm) of rainfall in 24\xc2\xa0hours over large areas and higher rainfall densities in localized areas. As a result,\n       <a href="/wiki/Drainage_basin" title="Drainage basin">\n        watersheds\n       </a>\n       can quickly surge water into the rivers that drain them. This can increase the water level near the head of tidal estuaries as storm-driven waters surging in from the ocean meet rainfall flowing from the estuary.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Har63_11-5">\n        <a href="#cite_note-Har63-11">\n         [11]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <h3>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Other_processes">\n        Other processes\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Storm_surge&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Other processes">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h3>\n      <p>\n       In addition to the above processes, surge and wave heights on shore are also affected by the flow of water over the underlying topography, i.e. the configuration and bathymetry of the ocean bottom and affected coastal area. A narrow shelf, for example, or one that has a steep drop from the shoreline and subsequently produces deep water in proximity to the shoreline, tends to produce a lower surge but a higher and more powerful wave. This situation is well exemplified by the southeast coast of\n       <a href="/wiki/Florida" title="Florida">\n        Florida\n       </a>\n       . The edge of the Floridian Plateau, where the water depths reach 91 metres (299\xc2\xa0ft), lies just 3,000\xc2\xa0m (9,800\xc2\xa0ft) offshore of\n       <a href="/wiki/Palm_Beach,_Florida" title="Palm Beach, Florida">\n        Palm Beach\n       </a>\n       ; just 7,000\xc2\xa0m (23,000\xc2\xa0ft) offshore, the depth increases to over 180\xc2\xa0m (590\xc2\xa0ft).\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lane_1980_13-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-Lane_1980-13">\n         [13]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       The 180\xc2\xa0m (590\xc2\xa0ft) depth contour followed southward from\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Palm_Beach_County" title="Palm Beach County">\n        Palm Beach County\n       </a>\n       lies more than 30,000\xc2\xa0m (98,000\xc2\xa0ft) to the east of the\n       <a href="/wiki/Florida_Keys" title="Florida Keys">\n        Florida Keys\n       </a>\n       .\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       Conversely, coastlines along North America such as those along the\n       <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_Mexico" title="Gulf of Mexico">\n        Gulf of Mexico\n       </a>\n       coast from Texas to Florida, and\n       <a href="/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">\n        Asia\n       </a>\n       such as the Bay of Bengal, have long, gently sloping shelves and shallow water depths. On the Gulf side of Florida, the edge of the Floridian Plateau lies more than 160 kilometres (99\xc2\xa0mi) offshore of Marco Island in\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Collier_County" title="Collier County">\n        Collier County\n       </a>\n       .\n       <a href="/wiki/Florida_Bay" title="Florida Bay">\n        Florida Bay\n       </a>\n       , lying between the Florida Keys and the mainland, is also very shallow; depths typically vary between 0.3\xc2\xa0m (0.98\xc2\xa0ft) and 2\xc2\xa0m (6.6\xc2\xa0ft).\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lane_1981_14-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-Lane_1981-14">\n         [14]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       These areas are subject to higher storm surges with smaller waves. This difference is because in deeper water, a surge can be dispersed down and away from the hurricane. However, upon entering a shallow, gently sloping shelf, the surge cannot be disperse, but is driven ashore by the wind stresses of the hurricane. Topography of the land surface is another important element in storm surge extent. Areas where the land lies less than a few meters above sea level are at particular risk from storm surge inundation.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Har63_11-6">\n        <a href="#cite_note-Har63-11">\n         [11]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       For a given topography and bathymetry the surge height is not solely affected by peak wind speed; the size of the storm also affects the peak surge. With any storm, the area of piled up water can flow out of the storm perimeter, and this escape mechanism is reduced in proportion to the surge force (for the same peak wind speed) when the storm covers more area (storm perimeter length per area is inversely proportional to a circular storm\'s diameter).\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">\n        <a href="#cite_note-15">\n         [15]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <div class="thumb tright">\n       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:232px;">\n        <a class="image"      <div class="magnify">\n          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Hurricane_Ike_Gilchrist_damage.jpg" title="Enlarge">\n          </a>\n         </div>\n         <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Ike"  </a>\n         in 2008.\n        </div>\n       </div>\n      </div>\n      <h3>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Extratropical_storms">\n        Extratropical storms\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Storm_surge&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Extratropical storms">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h3>\n      <p>\n       Similar to tropical cyclones, extra-tropical storms cause an offshore rise of water. However, unlike most tropical cyclone storm surges, extra-tropical storms can cause higher water levels across a large area for longer periods of time, depending on the system.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       In North America, extra-tropical storm surges may occur on the Pacific and Alaska coasts, and north of 31\xc2\xb0N on the Atlantic Coast. Extra-tropical storm surges may be possible for the\n       <a href="/wiki/Gulf_Coast_of_the_United_States" title="Gulf Coast of the United States">\n        Gulf coast\n       </a>\n       mostly during the wintertime, when extra-tropical cyclones affect the coast, such as in the\n       <a href="/wiki/1993_Storm_of_the_Century" title="1993 Storm of the Century">\n        1993 Storm of the Century\n       </a>\n       .\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       November 9\xe2\x80\x9313, 2009, marked a significant extratropical storm surge event on the United States east coast when the remnants of\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hurricane_Ida_(2009)" title="Hurricane Ida (2009)">\n        Hurricane Ida\n       </a>\n       developed into a\n       <a href="/wiki/Nor%27easter" title="Nor\'easter">\n        Nor\'easter\n       </a>\n       off the southeast U.S. coast. During the event, winds from the east were present along the northern periphery of the low pressure center for a number of days, forcing water into locations such as\n       <a href="/wiki/Chesapeake_Bay" title="Chesapeake Bay">\n        Chesapeake Bay\n       </a>\n       . Water levels rose significantly and remained as high as 8 feet (2.4\xc2\xa0m) above normal in numerous locations throughout the Chesapeake for a number of days as water was continually built-up inside the estuary from the onshore winds and freshwater rains flowing into the bay. In many locations, water levels were shy of records by only 0.1 feet (3\xc2\xa0cm).\n       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">\n        [\n        <i>\n         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">\n          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2014)">\n           citation needed\n          </span>\n         </a>\n        </i>\n        ]\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <h2>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Measuring_surge">\n        Measuring surge\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Storm_surge&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Measuring surge">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h2>\n      <p>\n       Surge can be measured directly at coastal tidal stations as the difference between the forecast tide and the observed rise of water.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-VIMS_16-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-VIMS-16">\n         [16]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       Another method of measuring surge is by the deployment of pressure transducers along the coastline just ahead of an approaching tropical cyclone. This was first tested for\n       <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Rita" title="Hurricane Rita">\n        Hurricane Rita\n       </a>\n       in 2005.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USGS_17-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-USGS-17">\n         [17]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       These types of sensors can be placed in locations that will be submerged and can accurately measure the height of water above them.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HOBO_18-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-HOBO-18">\n         [18]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       After surge from a cyclone has receded, teams of surveyors map high-water marks (HWM) on land, in a rigorous and detailed process that includes photographs and written descriptions of the marks. HWMs denote the location and elevation of flood waters from a storm event. When HWMs are analyzed, if the various components of the water height can be broken out so that the portion attributable to surge can be identified, then that mark can be classified as storm surge. Otherwise, it is classified as storm tide. HWMs on land are referenced to a vertical datum (a reference coordinate system). During evaluation, HWMs are divided into four categories based on the confidence in the mark; only HWMs evaluated as "excellent" are used by\n       <a href="/wiki/National_Hurricane_Center" title="National Hurricane Center">\n        National Hurricane Center\n       </a>\n       in post-storm analysis of the surge.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Katrina-surge_19-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-Katrina-surge-19">\n         [19]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       Two different measures are used for storm tide and storm surge measurements. Storm tide is measured using a\n       <a href="/wiki/Geodesy" title="Geodesy">\n        geodetic\n       </a>\n       vertical datum (\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/National_Geodetic_Vertical_Datum_of_1929" title="National Geodetic Vertical Datum of 1929">\n        NGVD 29\n       </a>\n       or\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NAVD_88" title="NAVD 88">\n        NAVD 88\n       </a>\n       ). Since storm surge is defined as the rise of water beyond what would be expected by the normal movement caused by tides, storm surge is measured using tidal predictions, with the assumption that the tide prediction is well-known and only slowly varying in the region subject to the surge. Since tides are a localized phenomenon, storm surge can only be measured in relationship to a nearby tidal station. Tidal bench mark information at a station provides a translation from the geodetic vertical datum to\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mean_sea_level" title="Mean sea level">\n        mean sea level (MSL)\n       </a>\n       at that location, then subtracting the tidal prediction yields a surge height above the normal water height.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-VIMS_16-1">\n        <a href="#cite_note-VIMS-16">\n         [16]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Katrina-surge_19-1">\n        <a href="#cite_note-Katrina-surge-19">\n         [19]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <h2>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="SLOSH">\n        SLOSH\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Storm_surge&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: SLOSH">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h2>\n      <div class="thumb tright">\n       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">\n        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Sloshrun.gif">\n         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="312" data-file-width="328" height="209" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e8/Sloshrun.gif/220px-Sloshrun.gif" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/e/e8/Sloshrun.gif 1.5x" width="220"/>\n        </a>\n        <div class="thumbcaption">\n         <div class="magnify">\n          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Sloshrun.gif" title="Enlarge">\n          </a>\n         </div>\n         Example of a SLOSH run\n        </div>\n       </div>\n      </div>\n      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">\n       Main article:\n       <a href="/wiki/Sea,_Lake,_and_Overland_Surge_from_Hurricanes" title="Sea, Lake, and Overland Surge from Hurricanes">\n        Sea, Lake, and Overland Surge from Hurricanes\n       </a>\n      </div>\n      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">\n       See also:\n       <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone_forecasting" title="Tropical cyclone forecasting">\n        Tropical cyclone forecasting\n       </a>\n      </div>\n      <p>\n       The National Hurricane Center forecasts storm surge using the SLOSH model, which is an abbreviation for Sea, Lake and Overland Surges from Hurricanes. The model is accurate to within 20\xc2\xa0percent.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NHC_SLOSH_model_20-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-NHC_SLOSH_model-20">\n         [20]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       SLOSH inputs include the central pressure of a tropical cyclone, storm size, the cyclone\'s forward motion, its track, and maximum sustained winds. Local topography, bay and river orientation, depth of the sea bottom, astronomical tides, as well as other physical features, are taken into account in a predefined grid referred to as a SLOSH basin. Overlapping SLOSH basins are defined for the southern and eastern coastline of the continental U.S.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SLOSH_map_21-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-SLOSH_map-21">\n         [21]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       Some storm simulations use more than one SLOSH basin; for instance, Hurricane Katrina SLOSH model runs used both the\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lake_Ponchartrain" title="Lake Ponchartrain">\n        Lake Ponchartrain\n       </a>\n       /\n       <a href="/wiki/New_Orleans" title="New Orleans">\n        New Orleans\n       </a>\n       basin, and the\n       <a href="/wiki/Mississippi_Sound" title="Mississippi Sound">\n        Mississippi Sound\n       </a>\n       basin, for the northern Gulf of Mexico landfall. The final output from the model run will display the maximum envelope of water, or MEOW, that occurred at each location.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       To allow for track or forecast uncertainties, usually several model runs with varying input parameters are generated to create a map of MOMs, or Maximum of Maximums.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PC_Weather_Products_22-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-PC_Weather_Products-22">\n         [22]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       For hurricane evacuation studies, a family of storms with representative tracks for the region, and varying intensity, eye diameter, and speed, are modeled to produce worst-case water heights for any tropical cyclone occurrence. The results of these studies are typically generated from several thousand SLOSH runs. These studies have been completed by the\n       <a href="/wiki/United_States_Army_Corps_of_Engineers" title="United States Army Corps of Engineers">\n        United States Army Corps of Engineers\n       </a>\n       , under contract to the\n       <a href="/wiki/Federal_Emergency_Management_Agency" title="Federal Emergency Management Agency">\n        Federal Emergency Management Agency\n       </a>\n       , for several states and are available on their Hurricane Evacuation Studies (HES) website.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HESHOME_23-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-HESHOME-23">\n         [23]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       They include coastal county maps, shaded to identify the minimum category of hurricane that will result in flooding, in each area of the county.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HES_Jackson_24-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-HES_Jackson-24">\n         [24]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <h2>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Mitigation">\n        Mitigation\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Storm_surge&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Mitigation">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h2>\n      <p>\n       Although meteorological surveys alert about hurricanes or severe storms, in the areas where the risk of coastal flooding is particularly high, there are specific storm surge warnings. These have been implemented, for instance, in the\n       <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">\n        Netherlands\n       </a>\n       ,\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rijkswaterstaat_25-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-Rijkswaterstaat-25">\n         [25]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">\n        Spain\n       </a>\n       ,\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Forcast_system_26-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-Forcast_system-26">\n         [26]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gobierno_27-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-Gobierno-27">\n         [27]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       the United States,\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stevens_Institute_28-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-Stevens_Institute-28">\n         [28]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Franklin_29-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-Franklin-29">\n         [29]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       and the\n       <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">\n        United Kingdom\n       </a>\n       .\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Environment_Agency_30-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-Environment_Agency-30">\n         [30]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       A prophylactic method introduced after the\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/North_Sea_Flood_of_1953" title="North Sea Flood of 1953">\n        North Sea Flood of 1953\n       </a>\n       is the construction of dams and storm-surge barriers (\n       <a href="/wiki/Flood_barrier" title="Flood barrier">\n        flood barriers\n       </a>\n       ). They are open and allow free passage, but close when the land is under threat of a storm surge. Major storm surge barriers are the\n       <a href="/wiki/Oosterscheldekering" title="Oosterscheldekering">\n        Oosterscheldekering\n       </a>\n       and\n       <a href="/wiki/Maeslantkering" title="Maeslantkering">\n        Maeslantkering\n       </a>\n       in the Netherlands, which are part of the\n       <a href="/wiki/Delta_Works" title="Delta Works">\n        Delta Works\n       </a>\n       project; the\n       <a href="/wiki/Thames_Barrier" title="Thames Barrier">\n        Thames Barrier\n       </a>\n       protecting\n       <a href="/wiki/London" title="London">\n        London\n       </a>\n       ; and the\n       <a href="/wiki/Saint_Petersburg_Dam" title="Saint Petersburg Dam">\n        Saint Petersburg Dam\n       </a>\n       in\n       <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">\n        Russia\n       </a>\n       .\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       Another modern development (in use in the Netherlands) is the creation of housing communities at the edges of wetlands with floating structures, restrained in position by vertical pylons.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-San_Francisco_Chronicle_31-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-San_Francisco_Chronicle-31">\n         [31]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       Such wetlands can then be used to accommodate runoff and surges without causing damage to the structures while also protecting conventional structures at somewhat higher low-lying elevations, provided that dikes prevent major surge intrusion.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       For mainland areas, storm surge is more of a threat when the storm strikes land from seaward, rather than approaching from landward.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fl100527_32-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-fl100527-32">\n         [32]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <h2>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Reverse_storm_surge">\n        Reverse storm surge\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Storm_surge&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Reverse storm surge">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h2>\n      <p>\n       Water can also be sucked away from shore prior to a storm surge. This was the case on the western Florida coast in 2017, just before\n       <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Irma" title="Hurricane Irma">\n        Hurricane Irma\n       </a>\n       made landfall, uncovering land usually underwater.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33">\n        <a href="#cite_note-33">\n         [33]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       This phenomenon is known as a\n       <b>\n        reverse storm surge\n       </b>\n       ,\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34">\n        <a href="#cite_note-34">\n         [34]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       or a\n       <b>\n        negative storm surge\n       </b>\n       .\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35">\n        <a href="#cite_note-35">\n         [35]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n
