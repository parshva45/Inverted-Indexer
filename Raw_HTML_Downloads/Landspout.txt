href="/wiki/File:GID_Landspout.jpg">\n         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="308" data-file-width="272" height="249" </a>\n        <div class="thumbcaption">\n         <div class="magnify">\n          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:GID_Landspout.jpg" title="\xe6\x94\xbe\xe5\xa4\xa7">\n          </a>\n         </div>\n         A landspout near\n         <a href="/wiki/North_Platte,_Nebraska" title="North Platte, Nebraska">\n          North Platte, Nebraska\n         </a>\n         on May 22, 2004. Note the characteristic smooth, tubular shape, similar to that of a\n         <a href="/wiki/Waterspout" title="Waterspout">\n          waterspout\n         </a>\n         .\n        </div>\n       </div>\n      </div>\n      <p>\n       A\n       <b>\n        landspout\n       </b>\n       is a term coined by meteorologist\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Howard_B._Bluestein" title="Howard B. Bluestein">\n        Howard B. Bluestein\n       </a>\n       in 1985 for a kind of\n       <a href="/wiki/Tornado" title="Tornado">\n        tornado\n       </a>\n       not associated with a\n       <a href="/wiki/Mesocyclone" title="Mesocyclone">\n        mesocyclone\n       </a>\n       .\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-landspout_1-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-landspout-1">\n         [1]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       The\n       <i>\n        Glossary of Meteorology\n       </i>\n       defines a landspout as\n      </p>\n      <dl>\n       <dd>\n        "Colloquial expression describing tornadoes occurring with a parent\n        <a href="/wiki/Cloud" title="Cloud">\n         cloud\n        </a>\n        in its growth stage and with its vorticity originating in the boundary layer.\n       </dd>\n       <dd>\n        The parent cloud does not contain a preexisting mid-level mesocyclone. The landspout was so named because it looks like "a weak\n        <a href="/wiki/Florida_Keys" title="Florida Keys">\n         Florida Keys\n        </a>\n        <a href="/wiki/Waterspout" title="Waterspout">\n         waterspout\n        </a>\n        over land."\n        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Glossary_of_Meteorology_2-0">\n         <a href="#cite_note-Glossary_of_Meteorology-2">\n          [2]\n         </a>\n        </sup>\n       </dd>\n      </dl>\n      <p>\n       Landspouts form during the growth stage of a\n       <a href="/wiki/Cumulus_congestus_cloud" title="Cumulus congestus cloud">\n        cumulus congestus cloud\n       </a>\n       by stretching\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atmospheric_boundary_layer" title="Atmospheric boundary layer">\n        boundary layer\n       </a>\n       <a href="/wiki/Vorticity" title="Vorticity">\n        vorticity\n       </a>\n       upward and into the\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cumulus_congestus" title="Cumulus congestus">\n        cumulus congestus\n       </a>\n       \'s updraft. They generally are smaller and weaker than\n       <a href="/wiki/Supercell" title="Supercell">\n        supercell\n       </a>\n       tornadoes and do not form from a\n       <a href="/wiki/Mesocyclone" title="Mesocyclone">\n        mesocyclone\n       </a>\n       or pre-existing rotation in the cloud. Because of this, landspouts are rarely detected by\n       <a href="/wiki/Weather_radar" title="Weather radar">\n        Doppler weather radar\n       </a>\n       .\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">\n        <a href="#cite_note-3">\n         [3]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       Landspouts share a strong resemblance and development process to that of\n       <a href="/wiki/Waterspout" title="Waterspout">\n        waterspouts\n       </a>\n       , usually taking the form of a translucent and highly\n       <a href="/wiki/Laminar_flow" title="Laminar flow">\n        laminar\n       </a>\n       helical tube. Landspouts are considered tornadoes since a rotating column of air is in contact with both the surface and a cumuliform cloud. Not all landspouts are visible, and many are first sighted as debris swirling at the surface before eventually filling in with condensation and dust.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       A few landspouts can persist in excess of 15 minutes and have produced\n       <a href="/wiki/Fujita_scale" title="Fujita scale">\n        F3\n       </a>\n       damage;\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">\n        <a href="#cite_note-4">\n         [4]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       however, they rarely produce damage greater than EF-2.\n       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">\n        [\n        <i>\n         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">\n          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2017)">\n           citation needed\n          </span>\n         </a>\n        </i>\n        ]\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <h2>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">\n
