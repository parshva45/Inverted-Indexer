class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=180th_meridian&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Software representation problems">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h2>\n      <p>\n       Many geographic software libraries or data formats project the world to a rectangle; very often this rectangle is split exactly at the 180th meridian. This often makes it non-trivial to do simple tasks (like representing an area, or a line) over the 180th meridian. Some examples:\n      </p>\n      <ul>\n       <li>\n        The\n        <a href="/wiki/GeoJSON" title="GeoJSON">\n         GeoJSON\n        </a>\n        specification strongly suggests splitting geometries so that neither of their parts cross the antimeridian.\n        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">\n         <a href="#cite_note-2">\n          [2]\n         </a>\n        </sup>\n       </li>\n       <li>\n        In\n        <a href="/wiki/OpenStreetMap" title="OpenStreetMap">\n         OpenStreetMap\n        </a>\n        , areas (like the boundary of Russia) are split at the 180th meridian.\n       </li>\n      </ul>\n      <h2>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">\n        See also\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span
