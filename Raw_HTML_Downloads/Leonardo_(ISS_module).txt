        </div>\n         Interior of Leonardo\n        </div>\n       </div>\n      </div>\n      <p>\n       The\n       <b>\n        Leonardo Permanent Multipurpose Module\n       </b>\n       (\n       <b>\n        PMM\n       </b>\n       ) is a module of the\n       <a href="/wiki/International_Space_Station" title="International Space Station">\n        International Space Station\n       </a>\n       . It was flown into space aboard the\n       <a href="/wiki/Space_Shuttle" title="Space Shuttle">\n        Space Shuttle\n       </a>\n       on\n       <a href="/wiki/STS-133" title="STS-133">\n        STS-133\n       </a>\n       on 24 February 2011 and installed on 1 March. Leonardo is primarily used for storage of spares, supplies and waste on the ISS, which was until then stored in many different places within the space station. The Leonardo PMM was a\n       <a href="/wiki/Multi-Purpose_Logistics_Module" title="Multi-Purpose Logistics Module">\n        Multi-Purpose Logistics Module\n       </a>\n       (MPLM) before 2011, but was modified into its current configuration. It was formerly one of three MPLM used for bringing cargo to and from the ISS with the\n       <a href="/wiki/Space_Shuttle" title="Space Shuttle">\n        Space Shuttle\n       </a>\n       . The module was named for Italian polymath\n       <a href="/wiki/Leonardo_da_Vinci" title="Leonardo da Vinci">\n        Leonardo da Vinci\n       </a>\n       .\n      </p>\n      <p>\n      </p>\n </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a      ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h2>\n      <p>\n       A European proposal suggested equipping the\n       <i>\n        Donatello\n       </i>\n       MPLM with enhanced\n       <a href="/wiki/Micrometeoroid" title="Micrometeoroid">\n        micrometeoroid\n       </a>\n       and\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Orbital_debris" title="Orbital debris">\n        orbital debris\n       </a>\n       protection and cooling systems and leaving it attached to the ISS after the Space Shuttle fleet is retired. Costs for such MPLM modifications were estimated at $20M to $40M per unit. The MPLM would then be called a Permanent Multipurpose Module (PMM), and it would house spare parts and supplies, allowing longer times between resupply missions. The proposal was rejected by\n       <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">\n        NASA\n       </a>\n       because it would require changes to existing plans and would entail additional costs.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PLM_1-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-PLM-1">\n         [1]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       Internal discussions continued\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PLM2_2-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-PLM2-2">\n         [2]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       and managers considered the possibility of\n       <a href="/wiki/STS-133" title="STS-133">\n        STS-133\n       </a>\n       , which at that time was planned to be the last Shuttle flight, leaving its MPLM permanently attached.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PLM3_3-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-PLM3-3">\n         [3]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       The\n       <a href="/wiki/United_Launch_Alliance" title="United Launch Alliance">\n        United Launch Alliance\n       </a>\n       also published a proposal for a system that could allow additional ISS modules to be launched on an\n       <a href="/wiki/Evolved_Expendable_Launch_Vehicle" title="Evolved Expendable Launch Vehicle">\n        EELV\n       </a>\n       even after the retirement of the Shuttle.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PLM4_4-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-PLM4-4">\n         [4]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       On August 5, 2009 it was announced that STS-133 would indeed leave one MPLM permanently attached to the station.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PLM5_5-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-PLM5-5">\n         [5]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       In October 2009, it was confirmed that\n       <i>\n        Leonardo\n       </i>\n       would be the MPLM converted to a PMM.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">\n        <a href="#cite_note-6">\n         [6]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       It was launched on February 24, 2011. The PMM was transferred to the station using the\n       <a href="/wiki/Canadarm" title="Canadarm">\n        Shuttle robotic arm\n       </a>\n       and mated to the\n       <a href="/wiki/Nadir" title="Nadir">\n        nadir\n       </a>\n       (Earth) facing port of\n       <a href="/wiki/Unity_(ISS_module)" title="Unity (ISS module)">\n        <i>\n         Unity\n        </i>\n       </a>\n       .\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       On May 27, 2015, at 13:08 UTC, the PMM was relocated from the nadir port of the\n       <i>\n        Unity\n       </i>\n       module to the forward-facing port of the\n       <a href="/wiki/Tranquility_(ISS_module)" title="Tranquility (ISS module)">\n        <i>\n         Tranquility\n        </i>\n       </a>\n       (Node 3) module. This was done in order to allow\n       <i>\n        Unity\n       </i>\n       \'s nadir port to serve as a berthing port for resupply craft, which in turn frees up\n       <a title="Commercial Crew Development">\n        Commercial Crew Development\n       </a>\n       spacecraft.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">\n        <a href="#cite_note-7">\n <i>\n        Leonardo\n       </i>\n       MPLM was moved to the\n       <a href="/wiki/Space_Station_Processing_Facility" title="Space Station Processing Facility">\n        Space Station Processing Facility\n       </a>\n       (SSPF) at the\n       <a href="/wiki/Kennedy_Space_Center" title="Kennedy Space Center">\n        Kennedy Space Center\n       </a>\n       in Florida to undergo modifications. To convert the\n       <i>\n        Leonardo\n       </i>\n       MPLM into the PMM, NASA made the following modifications: removal of +Y grapple fixture (Flight Releasable Grapple Fixture, or FRGF), removal of ROFU (Remotely Operated Fluid Umbilical) components, replacement of\n       <a href="/wiki/Common_Berthing_Mechanism" title="Common Berthing Mechanism">\n        CBM\n       </a>\n       seal, installation of new forward end cone\n       <a href="/wiki/Whipple_shield" title="Whipple shield">\n        MMOD shields\n       </a>\n       , feed through seal replacement, and installation of visiting vehicle retro-reflectors.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       In addition, the\n       <a href="/wiki/Multi-layer_insulation" title="Multi-layer insulation">\n        Multi-layer insulation\n       </a>\n       (MLI) blankets from the never-flown\n       <i>\n        Donatello\n       </i>\n       MPLM were cannibalized for use on\n       <i>\n        Leonardo\n       </i>\n       . The blankets were removed and returned to Italy where they were reinforced with Nextel/\n       <a href="/wiki/Kevlar" title="Kevlar">\n        Kevlar\n       </a>\n       to provide better protection against micro-meteorites.     </i>\n        MPLM\n       </b>\n       , also known as\n       <b>\n        MPLM-1\n       </b>\n       , was one of three\n       <a href="/wiki/Multi-Purpose_Logistics_Module" .\n       <i>\n        Leonardo\n       </i>\n       was used for eight of the twelve MPLM flights to the space station, with\n       <i>\n        <a href="/wiki/Raffaello_MPLM" title="Raffaello MPLM">\n         Raffaello\n        </a>\n       </i>\n       being used for the other four. It was the first MPLM to be launched, making its first flight in March 2001, aboard\n       <span class="nowrap">\n        <a href="/wiki/Space_Shuttle" title="Space Shuttle">\n         Space Shuttle\n        </a>\n       </span>\n       <a href="/wiki/Space_Shuttle_Discovery" title="Space Shuttle Discovery">\n        <i>\n         Discovery\n        </i>\n       </a>\n       on\n       <a href="/wiki/STS-102" title="STS-102">\n        STS-102\n       </a>\n       . Following its last flight as an MPLM, on\n       <a href="/wiki/STS-131" title="STS-131">\n        STS-131\n       </a>\n       in April 2010, it underwent conversion to become the\n       <b>\n        Permanent Multipurpose Module\n       </b>\n       , and was launched aboard Discovery on\n       <a href="/wiki/STS-133" title="STS-133">\n        STS-133\n       </a>\n       to become a permanent component of the station.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       Like the other Multi-Purpose Logistics Modules, it was constructed by the\n       <a href="/wiki/Italian_Space_Agency" title="Italian Space Agency">\n        Italian Space Agency\n       </a>\n       , who chose to name it after\n       <a href="/wiki/Leonardo_da_Vinci" title="Leonardo da Vinci">\n        Leonardo da Vinci\n       </a>\n
