</a>\n         </div>\n         Aerial view of Outer banks (looking north), with ocean on the right and sound on the left\n        </div>\n       </div>\n      </div>\n      <p>\n       The\n       <b>\n        Outer Banks\n       </b>\n       (OBX) is a 200-mile-long (320\xc2\xa0km) string of\n       <a href="/wiki/Barrier_island" title="Barrier island">\n        barrier islands\n       </a>\n       and\n       <a href="/wiki/Spit_(landform)" title="Spit (landform)">\n        spits\n       </a>\n       off the coast of\n       <a href="/wiki/North_Carolina" title="North Carolina">\n        North Carolina\n       </a>\n       and southeastern\n       <a href="/wiki/Virginia" title="Virginia">\n        Virginia\n       </a>\n       , on the east coast of the\n       <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">\n        United States\n       </a>\n       . They cover most of the North Carolina coastline, separating the\n       <a href="/wiki/Currituck_Sound" title="Currituck Sound">\n        Currituck Sound\n       </a>\n       ,\n       <a href="/wiki/Albemarle_Sound" title="Albemarle Sound">\n        Albemarle Sound\n       </a>\n       , and\n       <a href="/wiki/Pamlico_Sound" title="Pamlico Sound">\n        Pamlico Sound\n       </a>\n       from the\n       <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Ocean" title="Atlantic Ocean">\n        Atlantic Ocean\n       </a>\n       . The Outer Banks are a major tourist destination and are known around the world for their subtropical climate and wide expanse of open\n       <a href="/wiki/Beach" title="Beach">\n        beachfront\n       </a>\n       . The\n       <a href="/wiki/Cape_Hatteras_National_Seashore" title="Cape Hatteras National Seashore">\n        Cape Hatteras National Seashore\n       </a>\n       has four campgrounds open to visitors.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">\n        <a href="#cite_note-1">\n         [1]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       The treacherous seas off the Outer Banks and the large number of\n       <a href="/wiki/Shipwreck" title="Shipwreck">\n        shipwrecks\n       </a>\n       that have occurred there have given these seas the nickname\n       <a href="/wiki/Graveyard_of_the_Atlantic" title="Graveyard of the Atlantic">\n        Graveyard of the Atlantic\n       </a>\n       . The Graveyard of the Atlantic Museum is located in\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hatteras_Village" title="Hatteras Village">\n        Hatteras Village\n       </a>\n       near a\n       <a href="/wiki/United_States_Coast_Guard" title="United States Coast Guard">\n        United States Coast Guard\n       </a>\n       facility and the Hatteras ferry.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       The English\n       <a href="/wiki/Roanoke_Colony" title="Roanoke Colony">\n        Roanoke Colony\n       </a>\n       \xe2\x80\x94where\n       <a href="/wiki/Virginia_Dare" title="Virginia Dare">\n        Virginia Dare\n       </a>\n       was born\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">\n        <a href="#cite_note-2">\n         [2]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       \xe2\x80\x94vanished from\n       <a href="/wiki/Roanoke_Island" title="Roanoke Island">\n        Roanoke Island\n       </a>\n       in 1587.\n       <i>\n        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lost_Colony_(play)" title="Lost Colony (play)">\n         The Lost Colony\n        </a>\n        ,\n       </i>\n       written and performed to commemorate the original colonists, is the second longest running outdoor drama in the United States and its theater acts as a cultural focal point for much of the Outer Banks.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       The\n       <a href="/wiki/Wright_brothers" title="Wright brothers">\n        Wright brothers\n       </a>\n       \' first flight in a controlled, powered, heavier-than-air vehicle took place on the Outer Banks on December 7, 1903, at\n       <a href="/wiki/Kitty_Hawk,_North_Carolina" title="Kitty Hawk, North Carolina">\n        Kitty Hawk\n       </a>\n       .\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WDL_3-0">\n        <a First Flight Airport\n       </a>\n       is a small, general-aviation airfield located there.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n      </p>\n      <div class="toc" id="toc">\n       <div class="toctitle">\n <p>\n      </p>\n      <h2>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Geography">\n        Geography\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Outer_Banks&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Geography">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h2>\n      <p>\n       The Outer Banks is a string of peninsulas and barrier islands separating the Atlantic Ocean from mainland\n       <a href="/wiki/North_Carolina" title="North Carolina">\n        North Carolina\n       </a>\n       . From north to south, the largest of these include:\n       <a href="/wiki/Bodie_Island" title="Bodie Island">\n        Bodie Island\n       </a>\n       (which used to be an island but is now a peninsula due to tropical storms and hurricanes),\n       <a href="/wiki/Hatteras_Island" title="Hatteras Island">\n        Hatteras Island\n       </a>\n       ,\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ocracoke_Island" title="Ocracoke Island">\n        Ocracoke Island\n       </a>\n       ,\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Portsmouth_Island,_North_Carolina" title="Portsmouth Island, North Carolina">\n        Portsmouth Island\n       </a>\n       , and the\n       <a href="/wiki/Core_Banks,_North_Carolina" title="Core Banks, North Carolina">\n        Core Banks\n       </a>\n       .\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">\n        <a href="#cite_note-4">\n         [4]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       Over time, the exact number of islands and inlets changes as new inlets are opened up, often during a breach created during violent storms, and older inlets close, usually due to gradually sifting sands during the dynamic processes of\n       <a href="/wiki/Beach_evolution" title="Beach evolution">\n        beach evolution\n       </a>\n       .\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       The Outer Banks stretch southward from\n       <a href="/wiki/Sandbridge,_Virginia_Beach,_Virginia" title="Sandbridge, Virginia Beach, Virginia">\n        Sandbridge\n       </a>\n       in\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Virginia_Beach" title="Virginia Beach">\n        Virginia Beach\n       </a>\n       down the North Carolina coastline. Sources differ regarding the southern terminus of the Outer Banks. Generations of North Carolina schoolchildren have learned that the term includes the state\'s three prominent capes:\n       <a href="/wiki/Cape_Hatteras" title="Cape Hatteras">\n        Cape Hatteras\n       </a>\n       ,\n       <a href="/wiki/Cape_Lookout_(North_Carolina)" title="Cape Lookout (North Carolina)">\n        Cape Lookout\n       </a>\n       , and\n       <a href="/wiki/Cape_Fear_(headland)" title="Cape Fear (headland)">\n        Cape Fear.\n       </a>\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">\n        <a href="#cite_note-5">\n         [5]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">\n        <a href="#cite_note-6">\n         [6]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       Other sources limit the definition to two capes (\n       <a href="/wiki/Cape_Hatteras" title="Cape Hatteras">\n        Cape Hatteras\n       </a>\n       and\n       <a href="/wiki/Cape_Lookout_(North_Carolina)" title="Cape Lookout (North Carolina)">\n        Cape Lookout\n       </a>\n       ) and coastal areas in four counties (\n       <a href="/wiki/Currituck_County,_North_Carolina" title="Currituck County, North Carolina">\n        Currituck County\n       </a>\n       ,\n       <a href="/wiki/Dare_County,_North_Carolina" title="Dare County, North Carolina">\n        Dare County\n       </a>\n       ,\n       <a href="/wiki/Hyde_County,_North_Carolina" title="Hyde County, North Carolina">\n        Hyde County,\n       </a>\n       and\n       <a href="/wiki/Carteret_County,_North_Carolina" title="Carteret County, North Carolina">\n        Carteret County\n       </a>\n       ).\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ncpedia.org_7-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-ncpedia.org-7">\n         [7]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       Some authors include Carteret\'s\n       <a href="/wiki/Shackleford_Banks" title="Shackleford Banks">\n        Shackelford Banks\n       </a>\n       and\n       <a href="/wiki/Bogue_Banks" title="Bogue Banks">\n        Bogue Banks\n       </a>\n       in their descriptions,\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ncpedia.org_7-1">\n        <a href="#cite_note-ncpedia.org-7">\n         [7]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       while others exclude\n       <a href="/wiki/Bogue_Banks" title="Bogue Banks">\n        Bogue Banks\n       </a>\n       .\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">\n        <a href="#cite_note-8">\n         [8]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       Still other references restrict the definition to the northern three counties of Currituck, Dare, and Hyde.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">\n        <a href="#cite_note-9">\n         [9]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       The abbreviations\n       <b>\n        OBX\n       </b>\n       (Outer Banks) and\n       <b>\n        SOBX\n       </b>\n       (Southern Outer Banks) are modern terms used to promote tourism and to market a variety of stickers, t-shirts, and other items to vacationers. OBX, which originated first, is generally used in the northern Outer Banks. SOBX is used primarily in\n       <a href="/wiki/Carteret_County,_North_Carolina" title="Carteret County, North Carolina">\n        Carteret County\n       </a>\n       , which is also known as the\n       <a href="/wiki/Crystal_Coast" title="Crystal Coast">\n        Crystal Coast\n       </a>\n       .\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       The northern part of the Outer Banks, from\n       <a href="/wiki/Oregon_Inlet" title="Oregon Inlet">\n        Oregon Inlet\n       </a>\n       northward, is actually a part of the\n       <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">\n        North American\n       </a>\n       mainland, since the northern inlets of Bodie Island and Currituck Banks no longer exist.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">\n        <a href="#cite_note-10">\n         [10]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       It is separated by the\n       <a href="/wiki/Currituck_Sound" title="Currituck Sound">\n        Currituck Sound\n       </a>\n       and the\n       <a href="/wiki/Intracoastal_Waterway" title="Intracoastal Waterway">\n        Intracoastal Waterway\n       </a>\n       , which passes through the\n       <a href="/wiki/Great_Dismal_Swamp" title="Great Dismal Swamp">\n        Great Dismal Swamp\n       </a>\n       occupying much of the mainland west of the Outer Banks. Road access to the northern Outer Banks is cut off between\n       <a href="/wiki/Sandbridge,_Virginia_Beach,_Virginia" title="Sandbridge, Virginia Beach, Virginia">\n        Sandbridge\n       </a>\n       and\n       <a href="/wiki/Corolla,_North_Carolina" title="Corolla, North Carolina">\n        Corolla, North Carolina\n       </a>\n       , with communities such as\n       <a href="/wiki/Carova_Beach,_North_Carolina" title="Carova Beach, North Carolina">\n        Carova Beach\n       </a>\n       accessible only by\n       <a href="/wiki/Four-wheel_drive" title="Four-wheel drive">\n        four-wheel drive\n       </a>\n       vehicles.\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/North_Carolina_State_Highway_12" title="North Carolina State Highway 12">\n        North Carolina State Highway 12\n       </a>\n       links most of the popular Outer Banks communities in this section of the coast. The easternmost point is Cape Point at\n       <a href="/wiki/Cape_Hatteras" title="Cape Hatteras">\n        Cape Hatteras\n       </a>\n       on Hatteras Island, site of the\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cape_Hatteras_Lighthouse" title="Cape Hatteras Lighthouse">\n        Cape Hatteras Lighthouse\n       </a>\n       .\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       The Outer Banks are not anchored to offshore\n       <a href="/wiki/Coral_reef" title="Coral reef">\n        coral reefs\n       </a>\n       like some other barrier islands and as a consequence they often suffer significant beach\n       <a href="/wiki/Erosion" title="Erosion">\n        erosion\n       </a>\n       during major storms. In fact, their location jutting out into the Atlantic makes them the most\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hurricane" title="Hurricane">\n        hurricane\n       </a>\n       -prone area north of\n       <a href="/wiki/Florida" title="Florida">\n        Florida\n       </a>\n       , for both landfalling storms and brushing storms offshore. Hatteras Island was cut in half on September 18, 2003, when\n       <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Isabel" title="Hurricane Isabel">\n        Hurricane Isabel\n       </a>\n       washed a 2,000 foot (600 m) wide and 15 foot (5 m) deep channel called\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Isabel_Inlet" title="Isabel Inlet">\n        Isabel Inlet\n       </a>\n       through the community of Hatteras Village on the southern end of the island.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">\n        <a href="#cite_note-11">\n         [11]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       The tear was subsequently repaired and restored by sand dredging by the\n       <a href="/wiki/United_States_Army_Corps_of_Engineers" title="United States Army Corps of Engineers">\n        U.S. Army Corps of Engineers\n       </a>\n       . It was cut off once again in 2011 by\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hurricane_Irene_(2011)" title="Hurricane Irene (2011)">\n        Hurricane Irene\n       </a>\n       . Access to the island was largely limited to boat access only from August to late October until another temporary bridge could be built.\n      </p>\n      <h2>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Vegetation">\n        Vegetation\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Outer_Banks&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Vegetation">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h2>\n      <p>\n       The vegetation of the Outer Banks varies. In the northeast part of the Outer Banks, from\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Virginia_Beach" title="Virginia Beach">\n        Virginia Beach\n       </a>\n       southward past the\n       <a href="/wiki/North_Carolina" title="North Carolina">\n        North Carolina\n       </a>\n       border to\n       <a href="/wiki/Oregon_Inlet" title="Oregon Inlet">\n        Oregon Inlet\n       </a>\n       , the main types of vegetation are sea grasses, beach grasses and other beach plants including\n       <i>\n        <a href="/wiki/Opuntia_humifusa" title="Opuntia humifusa">\n         Opuntia humifusa\n        </a>\n       </i>\n       on the Atlantic side and\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wax_myrtle" title="Wax myrtle">\n        wax myrtles\n       </a>\n       ,\n       <a href="/wiki/Gordonia_lasianthus" title="Gordonia lasianthus">\n        bays\n       </a>\n       , and grasses on the Sound side with areas of pine and\n       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_moss" title="Spanish moss">\n        Spanish moss\n       </a>\n       -covered\n       <a href="/wiki/Live_oak" title="Live oak">\n        live oaks\n       </a>\n       .\n       <i>\n        <a href="/wiki/Yucca_aloifolia" title="Yucca aloifolia">\n         Yucca aloifolia\n        </a>\n       </i>\n       and\n       <i>\n        <a href="/wiki/Yucca_gloriosa" title="Yucca gloriosa">\n         Yucca gloriosa\n        </a>\n       </i>\n       can be found growing wild here in the northern parts of its range on the beach.\n       <a href="/wiki/Sabal_minor" title="Sabal minor">\n        Sabal Minor\n       </a>\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Palm_(plant)" title="Palm (plant)">\n        palms\n       </a>\n       were once indigenous to the entire Outer Banks, and they are still successfully planted and grown. Its current most northerly known native stand is on\n       <a href="/wiki/Monkey_Island,_North_Carolina" title="Monkey Island, North Carolina">\n        Monkey Island\n       </a>\n       in Currituck County.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">\n        <a href="#cite_note-12">\n         [12]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">\n        <a href="#cite_note-13">\n         [13]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       From\n       <a href="/wiki/Cape_Hatteras_National_Seashore" title="Cape Hatteras National Seashore">\n        Cape Hatteras National Seashore\n       </a>\n       southward, the vegetation does include that of the northeastern Outer Banks such as\n       <i>\n        <a href="/wiki/Sabal_minor" title="Sabal minor">\n         Sabal minor\n        </a>\n       </i>\n       ,\n       <i>\n        Yucca aloifolia\n       </i>\n       and\n       <i>\n        Yucca gloriosa\n       </i>\n       ; however, the main vegetation consists of Cabbage palmetto (\n       <i>\n        <a href="/wiki/Sabal_palmetto" title="Sabal palmetto">\n         Sabal palmetto\n        </a>\n       </i>\n       ), which can be found in the north, although they are native in the southern part of the Outer Banks, specifically prevelent from Cape Hatteras and all points southward.\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pindo_palm" title="Pindo palm">\n        Pindo palms\n       </a>\n       and\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Windmill_palm" title="Windmill palm">\n        windmill palms\n       </a>\n       are also planted widely throughout the Outer Banks; although, they are not indigenous to the area.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       A wide variety of native plants can be found at the Elizabethan Gardens in\n       <a href="/wiki/Manteo,_North_Carolina" title="Manteo, North Carolina">\n        Manteo\n       </a>\n       on\n       <a href="/wiki/Roanoke_Island" title="Roanoke Island">\n        Roanoke Island\n       </a>\n       .\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">\n        <a href="#cite_note-14">\n         [14]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       The gardens are open year round, 7 days a week.\n      </p>\n      <h2>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Weather">\n        Weather\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Outer_Banks&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Weather">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h2>\n      <p>\n       The Outer Banks have unusual weather patterns because of their unique geographical location. As the islands are jutted out from the eastern seaboard into the Atlantic Gulf Stream, the Outer Banks has a predisposition to be affected by hurricanes, Nor\'easters (usually in the form of rain, and rarely snow or mixed precipitation), and other ocean-driven storms.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       The winters are typically milder than in inland areas, averaging lows in the upper 30s and highs in the lower 50s, and is more frequently overcast than in the summer. However, the exposure of the Outer Banks makes them prone to higher winds, often causing wind chills to make the apparent temperature as cold as the inland areas. The summer months average lows from the mid-70s to highs in the upper 80s, depending on the time of the summer. The spring and fall are typically milder seasons. The fall and winter are usually warmer than areas inland, while the spring and the summer are often slightly cooler because of the moderating effects of being surrounded by water.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       Although snow is possible, averaging from 3 inches in the north to less than 1/2 inch per year in the south, there are many times when years pass between snowfalls.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">\n        <a href="#cite_note-15">\n         [15]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       The majority of nor\'easters are "born" off the coasts of the Outer Banks.\n      </p>\n      <h2>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Culture">\n        Culture\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n    </div>\n      </div>\n      <p>\n       The Outer Banks were first settled by English European colonial settlers, many of whom still have descendants living on the islands to this day. Before bridges were built in the 1930s, the only form of transport between or off the islands was by boat, which allowed for the islands to stay isolated from much of the rest of the mainland. This helped to preserve the maritime culture and the\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/High_tider" title="High tider">\n        distinctive Outer Banks accent\n       </a>\n       or\n       <i>\n        <a href="/wiki/Brogue" title="Brogue">\n         brogue\n        </a>\n        ,\n       </i>\n       which sounds more like an English accent than it does an American accent. Many "bankers" have often been mistaken for being from England or Ireland when traveling to areas outside of the Outer Banks. The brogue is more distinctive the further south one travels on the Outer Banks, with it being the thickest on Ocracoke Island and\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Harkers_Island" title="Harkers Island">\n        Harkers Island\n       </a>\n       .\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       Some residents of the Outer Banks, known as wreckers, made part of their living by scavenging\n       <a href="/wiki/Shipwreck" title="Shipwreck">\n        wrecked ships\n       </a>\n       \xe2\x80\x94or by luring ships to their destruction. Horses with\n       <a href="/wiki/Lantern" title="Lantern">\n        lanterns\n       </a>\n       tied to their necks would be walked along the beach; the lanterns\' up and down motion would appear to ships to represent clear water and a ship ahead; the unsuspecting captain would then drive his ship ashore following this false light.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">\n        <a href="#cite_note-16">\n         [16]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       The islands are home to herds of feral horses, sometimes called "\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Banker_Horse" title="Banker Horse">\n        banker ponies\n       </a>\n       ," which according to local legend are descended from Spanish\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mustang_(horse)" title="Mustang (horse)">\n        Mustangs\n       </a>\n       washed ashore centuries ago in shipwrecks. Populations are found on Ocracoke Island, Shackleford Banks, Currituck Banks, and in the\n       <a href="/wiki/Rachel_Carson" title="Rachel Carson">\n        Rachel Carson\n       </a>\n       Estuarine Sanctuary.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       <a href="/wiki/Ocracoke,_North_Carolina" title="Ocracoke, North Carolina">\n        Ocracoke\n       </a>\n       was the last refuge of\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pirate" title="Pirate">\n        pirate\n       </a>\n       Edward Teach, better known as\n       <a href="/wiki/Blackbeard" title="Blackbeard">\n        Blackbeard\n       </a>\n       . It is also where the infamous pirate was killed November 22, 1718 in a fierce battle with troops from Virginia.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-D._Moore._1997_pp._31_17-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-D._Moore._1997_pp._31-17">\n         [17]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       The Outer Banks is home to Yaupon Holly (\n       <i>\n        <a href="/wiki/Ilex_vomitoria" title="Ilex vomitoria">\n         Ilex vomitoria\n        </a>\n       </i>\n       ), the roasted leaves of which were brewed into a high caffeine beverage called\n       <a href="/wiki/Black_drink" title="Black drink">\n        black drink\n       </a>\n       by the\n       <a href="/wiki/Native_Americans_in_the_United_States" title="Native Americans in the United States">\n        Native Americans\n       </a>\n       . The Outer Banks may be one of the few places where it is still consumed.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18">\n        <a href="#cite_note-18">\n         [18]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <h2>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Fishing">\n        Fishing\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n </sup>\n       In modern times, tourists will flock to the area just for the abundance of fishing opportunities.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Love_19-1">\n        <a first settlers arrived, to choose from in the Outer Banks.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kiger_20-4">\n        <a href="#cite_note-Kiger-20">\n         [20]\n        </a>\n </sup>\n       </dd>\n       <dt>\n        Brackish fishing\n       </dt>\n       <dd>\n        This is a separate type of fishing than complete saltwater because of the abundance of new species that are able to live in brackish water.\n        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Thomas_22-0">\n         <a href="#cite_note-Thomas-22">\n          [22]\n         </a>\n        </sup>\n        The Outer Banks has multiple Sounds that receive water from the\n        <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Ocean" title="Atlantic Ocean">\n         Atlantic Ocean\n        </a>\n        and many North Carolina fresh-water rivers, creating an\n        <a href="/wiki/Estuary" title="Estuary">\n         estuary\n        </a>\n        for many types of fish including,\n        <a href="/wiki/Striped_bass" , where giant species such as\n        <a href="/wiki/Billfish" title="Billfish">\n         billfish\n        </a>\n        , tuna and\n        <a href="/wiki/Dolphin" title="Dolphin">\n         dolphin\n        </a>\n        lurk.\n        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stearns_23-0">\n         <a href="#cite_note-Stearns-23">\n          [23]\n         </a>\n        </sup>\n href="#cite_note-Outer_Banks_Charter_Fishing-24">\n          [24]\n         </a>\n        </sup>\n       </dd>\n       <dt>\n        Pier fishing\n       </dt>\n       <dd>\n        The Outer Banks are one of the few coastal areas left with more than one\n        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fishing_pier" title="Fishing pier">\n         fishing pier\n        </a>\n        along the coast, so anglers attract to the area for an opportunity to pier fish.\n        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Seldon_25-0">\n         <a href="#cite_note-Seldon-25">\n          [25]\n         </a>\n        </sup>\n        Pier Fishing is an easy way for anglers to get a little off-shore mixed with some inshore fishing.\n        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Seldon_25-1">\n         <a href="#cite_note-Seldon-25">\n          [25]\n         </a>\n        </sup>\n        It is where fishermen can catch any type of fish they please, as long as it is the season for it.\n        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Seldon_25-2">\n         <a href="#cite_note-Seldon-25">\n          [25]\n         </a>\n        </sup>\n       </dd>\n      </dl>\n      <p>\n       The Outer Banks are a popular destination for bottom fish like\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spot_croaker" title="Spot croaker">\n        spot\n       </a>\n       and\n       <a href="/wiki/Mullet_(fish)" title="Mullet (fish)">\n        mullet\n       </a>\n       , these two types of fish are caught throughout the year.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Finch_26-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-Finch-26">\n         [26]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       The peak for fishing bottom and top water feeders is early to the middle of May.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Finch_26-1">\n        <a href="#cite_note-Finch-26">\n         [26]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       If the angler is solely searching for top water fish, then late May and June would be the ideal time to fish, especially if they are searching for\n       <a href="/wiki/Mackerel" title="Mackerel">\n        Mackerel\n       </a>\n       .\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Finch_26-2">\n        <a href="#cite_note-Finch-26">\n         [26]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       In the offseason, or winter months, the true fishermen are out searching for\n       <a href="/wiki/Trout" title="Trout">\n        Trout\n       </a>\n       ,\n       <a href="/wiki/Black_sea_bass" title="Black sea bass">\n        Sea Bass\n       </a>\n       ,\n       <a   ,\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oysters" title="Oysters">\n        Oysters\n       </a>\n       ,\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/King_Mackerel" title="King Mackerel">\n        King Mackerel\n       </a>\n       ,\n       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Bay_Scallops&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Bay Scallops (page does not exist)">\n        Bay Scallops\n       </a>\n       , and\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Striped_Bass" title="Striped Bass">\n        Striped Bass\n       </a>\n       .\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stearns_23-1">\n        <a href="#cite_note-Stearns-23">\n         [23]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       In the spring, the popular catches are\n       <a href="/wiki/Grouper" title="Grouper">\n        Grouper\n       </a>\n       ,\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sea_Trout" title="Sea Trout">\n        Sea Trout\n       </a>\n       , Sea Bass, Bluefish, Bluefin and\n       <a href="/wiki/Yellowfin_tuna" title="Yellowfin tuna">\n        Yellowfin tuna\n       </a>\n       , Oysters, Snapper, Striped Bass, Red Drum,\n       <a href="/wiki/Sciaenidae" title="Sciaenidae">\n        Croaker\n       </a>\n       , Sea Mullet, King Mackerel, and\n       <a href="/wiki/Wahoo" title="Wahoo">\n        Wahoo\n       </a>\n       <sup class="reference" to move into the coastal waters near the Outer Banks, because of the warming waters and the increased visibility in the water\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Finch_26-3">\n        <a href="#cite_note-Finch-26">\n         [26]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       The top water fish can see baitfish easily due to the decrease in murky water\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Finch_26-4">\n        <a href="#cite_note-Finch-26">\n         [26]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       Plus they will start to migrate back North as the coastal waters to the south become too hot, the waters farther North will warm to their desired temperatures.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Finch_26-5">\n        <a href="#cite_note-Finch-26">\n         [26]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       Yet, in the prime tourist season of the summer, fishermen and many tourists have plenty of game options such as\n       <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_blue_marlin"   ,\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spanish_Mackerel" title="Spanish Mackerel">\n        Spanish Mackerel\n       </a>\n       ,\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Crabs"       </a>\n       , and Sea Mullet.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Love_19-4">\n        <a href="#cite_note-Love-19">\n         [19]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       By the fall, [19]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       The prime season for fall fishing is late September to October 23.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Finch_26-6">\n        <a href="#cite_note-Finch-26">\n         [26]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       Though the water is more turbulent and murky due to the strong northeast\n       <a href="/wiki/Prevailing_winds" id="cite_ref-Love_19-6">\n        <a href="#cite_note-Love-19">\n         [19]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       This makes it a prime time for smaller bottom fish who love to feed in murky, colder
