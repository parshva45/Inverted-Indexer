    , also called\n       <b>\n        extraterrestrial dust\n       </b>\n       or\n       <b>\n        space dust\n       </b>\n       , is\n       <a href="/wiki/Dust" title="Dust">\n        dust\n       </a>\n       which exists in\n       <a href="/wiki/Outer_space" title="Outer space">\n        outer space\n       </a>\n       , as well as all over planet\n       <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">\n        Earth\n       </a>\n       .\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYT-20170310_1-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-NYT-20170310-1">\n         [1]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GEO-20161201_2-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-GEO-20161201-2">\n         [2]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       Most cosmic dust particles are between a few\n       <a href="/wiki/Molecule" title="Molecule">\n        molecules\n       </a>\n       to 0.1\n       <a href="/wiki/Micrometre" title="Micrometre">\n        \xc2\xb5m\n       </a>\n       in size. A smaller fraction of all dust in space consists of larger refractory minerals that condensed as matter left the stars. It is called "stardust" and is included in a separate section below. The dust density falling to Earth is approximately 10\n       <sup>\n        \xe2\x88\x926\n       </sup>\n       /m\n       <sup>\n        3\n       </sup>\n       with each grain having a mass between 10\n       <sup>\n        \xe2\x88\x9216\n       </sup>\n       kg (0.1 pg) and 10\n       <sup>\n        \xe2\x88\x924\n       </sup>\n       kg (100\xc2\xa0mg).\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Zook_2001_3-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-Zook_2001-3">\n         [3]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MatloffJohnson2005_4-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-MatloffJohnson2005-4">\n         [4]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       Cosmic dust can be further distinguished by its astronomical location:\n       <a href="/wiki/Intergalactic_dust" title="Intergalactic dust">\n        intergalactic dust\n       </a>\n       , interstellar dust, interplanetary dust (such as in the\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Zodiacal_cloud" title="Zodiacal cloud">\n        zodiacal cloud\n       </a>\n       ) and\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Circumplanetary_dust" title="Circumplanetary dust">\n        circumplanetary dust\n       </a>\n       (such as in a\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Planetary_ring" title="Planetary ring">\n        planetary ring\n       </a>\n       ). In the\n       <a href="/wiki/Solar_System" title="Solar System">\n        Solar System\n       </a>\n       , interplanetary dust causes the\n       <a href="/wiki/Zodiacal_light" title="Zodiacal light">\n        zodiacal light\n       </a>\n       . Sources of Solar System dust include\n       <a href="/wiki/Comet_dust" title="Comet dust">\n        comet dust\n       </a>\n       ,\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Asteroidal_dust" title="Asteroidal dust">\n        asteroidal dust\n       </a>\n       , dust from the\n       <a href="/wiki/Kuiper_belt" title="Kuiper belt">\n        Kuiper belt\n       </a>\n       , and interstellar dust passing through the Solar System. The terminology has no specific application for describing materials found on the planet\n       <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">\n        Earth\n       </a>\n       except for dust that has demonstrably fallen to Earth. By one estimate, as much as 40,000 tons of cosmic dust reaches the Earth\'s surface every year.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Zook_2001_3-1">\n        <a href="#cite_note-Zook_2001-3">\n         [3]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       In October 2011, scientists reported that cosmic dust contains complex\n       <a href="/wiki/Organic_compound" title="Organic compound">\n        organic\n       </a>\n       matter (amorphous organic solids with a mixed\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aromatic" title="Aromatic">\n        aromatic\n       </a>\n       \xe2\x80\x93\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aliphatic" title="Aliphatic">\n        aliphatic\n       </a>\n       structure) that could be created naturally, and rapidly, by\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stars" title="Stars">\n        stars\n       </a>\n       .\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Space-20111026_5-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-Space-20111026-5">\n         [5]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ScienceDaily-20111026_6-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-ScienceDaily-20111026-6">\n         [6]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nature-20111026_7-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-Nature-20111026-7">\n         [7]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       In August 2014, </a>\n       since returning to Earth in 2006.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NASA-20140814_8-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-NASA-20140814-8">\n         [8]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AP-20140814_9-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-AP-20140814-9">\n         [9]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       <sup class="reference" </a>\n         </sup>\n        </div>\n       </div>\n      </div>\n      <p>\n       The study of dust is a largely researched topic that brings together different scientific fields:\n       <a href="/wiki/Physics" title="Physics">\n        physics\n       </a>\n       (\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solid_state_physics" title="Solid state physics">\n        solid-state\n       </a>\n       ,\n       <a href="/wiki/Electromagnetism" title="Electromagnetism">\n        electromagnetic theory\n       </a>\n       , surface physics,\n       <a href="/wiki/Statistical_physics" title="Statistical physics">\n        statistical physics\n       </a>\n       ,\n       <a href="/wiki/Thermal_physics" title="Thermal physics">\n        thermal physics\n       </a>\n       ),\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fractal_mathematics" title="Fractal mathematics">\n        fractal mathematics\n       </a>\n       ,\n       <a href="/wiki/Chemistry" title="Chemistry">\n        chemistry\n       </a>\n       (\n       <a href="/wiki/Chemical_reaction" title="Chemical reaction">\n        chemical reactions\n       </a>\n       on grain surfaces),\n       <a href="/wiki/Meteoritics" title="Meteoritics">\n        meteoritics\n       </a>\n       , as well as every branch of\n       <a href="/wiki/Astronomy" title="Astronomy">\n        astronomy\n       </a>\n       and\n       <a href="/wiki/Astrophysics" title="Astrophysics">\n        astrophysics\n       </a>\n       .\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gr.C3.BCn_15-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-Gr.C3.BCn-15">\n         [15]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       These disparate research areas can be linked by the following theme: the cosmic dust particles evolve cyclically; chemically, physically and dynamically. The evolution of dust traces out paths in which the Universe recycles material, in processes analogous to the daily recycling steps with which many people are familiar: production, storage, processing, collection, consumption, and discarding. Observations and measurements of cosmic dust in different regions provide an important insight into the Universe\'s recycling processes; in the clouds of the diffuse\n       <a href="/wiki/Interstellar_medium" title="Interstellar medium">\n        interstellar medium\n       </a>\n       , in\n       <a href="/wiki/Molecular_cloud" title="Molecular cloud">\n        molecular clouds\n       </a>\n       , in the\n       <a href="/wiki/Circumstellar_dust" title="Circumstellar dust">\n        circumstellar dust\n       </a>\n       of\n       <a href="/wiki/Young_stellar_object" title="Young stellar object">\n        young stellar objects\n       </a>\n       , and in\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Planetary_systems" title="Planetary systems">\n        planetary systems\n       </a>\n       such as the\n       <a href="/wiki/Solar_System" title="Solar System">\n        Solar System\n       </a>\n       , where astronomers consider dust as in its most recycled state. The astronomers accumulate observational \xe2\x80\x98snapshots\xe2\x80\x99 of dust at different stages of its life and, over time, form a more complete movie of the Universe\'s complicated recycling steps.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       Parameters such as the particle\'s initial motion, material properties, intervening\n       <a href="/wiki/Plasma_(physics)" title="Plasma (physics)">\n        plasma\n       </a>\n       and\n       <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_field" title="Magnetic field">\n        magnetic field\n       </a>\n       determined the dust particle\'s arrival at the dust detector. Slightly changing any of these parameters can give significantly different dust dynamical behavior. Therefore, one can learn about where that object came from, and what is (in) the intervening medium.\n      </p>\n      <h2>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Detection_methods">\n        Detection methods\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a      </span>\n       </span>\n      </h2>\n      <div class="thumb tright">\n       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;">\n        <a class="image"    <div class="magnify">\n          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Andromeda_galaxy_Ssc2005-20a1.jpg" title="Enlarge">\n          </a>\n         </div>\n         Cosmic dust of the\n         <a methods that utilize the\n       <a href="/wiki/Electromagnetic_radiation" title="Electromagnetic radiation">\n        radiative\n       </a>\n       properties of the cosmic dust particles.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       Cosmic dust can also be detected directly (\'in-situ\') using a variety of collection methods and from a variety of collection locations. Estimates of the daily influx of extraterrestrial material entering the Earth\'s atmosphere range between 5 and 300 tonnes.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">\n        <a href="#cite_note-16">\n         [16]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17">\n        <a href="#cite_note-17">\n         [17]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">\n        NASA\n       </a>\n       collects samples of star dust particles in the Earth\'s atmosphere using plate collectors under the wings of stratospheric-flying\n       <a href="/wiki/Airplane" title="Airplane">\n        airplanes\n       </a>\n       . Dust samples are also collected from surface deposits on the large Earth ice-masses (Antarctica and Greenland/the Arctic) and in deep-sea sediments.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       <a href="/wiki/Donald_E._Brownlee" title="Donald E. Brownlee">\n        Don Brownlee\n       </a>\n       at the\n       <a href="/wiki/University_of_Washington" title="University of Washington">\n        University of Washington\n       </a>\n       in Seattle first reliably identified the extraterrestrial nature of collected dust particles in the latter 1970s. Another source is the\n       <a href="/wiki/Meteorite" title="Meteorite">\n        meteorites\n       </a>\n       , which contain\n       <a href="#Stardust">\n        stardust\n       </a>\n       extracted from them. Stardust grains are solid refractory pieces of individual presolar stars. They are recognized by their extreme isotopic compositions, which can only be isotopic compositions within evolved stars, prior to any mixing with the interstellar medium. These grains condensed from the stellar matter as it cooled while leaving the star.\n      </p>\n      <div class="thumb tleft">\n       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">\n        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Horsehead-Hubble.jpg">\n         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2065" data-file-width="2839" height="160" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/46/Horsehead-Hubble.jpg/220px-Horsehead-Hubble.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/46/Horsehead-Hubble.jpg/330px-Horsehead-Hubble.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/46/Horsehead-Hubble.jpg/440px-Horsehead-Hubble.jpg 2x" width="220"/>\n        </a>\n        <div class="thumbcaption">\n         <div class="magnify">\n          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Horsehead-Hubble.jpg" title="Enlarge">\n          </a>\n         </div>\n         Cosmic dust of the\n         <a href="/wiki/Horsehead_Nebula" title="Horsehead Nebula">\n          Horsehead Nebula\n         </a>\n         as revealed by the\n         <a href="/wiki/Hubble_Space_Telescope" title="Hubble Space Telescope">\n          Hubble Space Telescope\n         </a>\n         .\n        </div>\n       </div>\n      </div>\n      <p>\n       In interplanetary space, dust detectors on planetary spacecraft have been built and flown, some are presently flying, and more are presently being built to fly. The large orbital velocities of dust particles in interplanetary space (typically 10\xe2\x80\x9340\xc2\xa0km/s) make intact particle capture problematic. Instead, in-situ dust detectors are generally devised to measure parameters associated with the high-velocity impact of dust particles on the instrument, and then derive physical properties of the particles (usually mass and velocity) through laboratory calibration (i.e. impacting accelerated particles with known properties onto a laboratory replica of the dust detector). Over the years dust detectors have measured, among others, the impact light flash, acoustic signal and impact ionisation. Recently the dust instrument on\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stardust_Mission" title="Stardust Mission">\n        Stardust\n       </a>\n       captured particles intact in low-density\n       <a href="/wiki/Aerogel" title="Aerogel">\n        aerogel\n       </a>\n       .\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       Dust detectors in the past flew on the\n       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=HEOS-2&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="HEOS-2 (page does not exist)">\n        HEOS-2\n       </a>\n       ,\n       <i>\n        <a href="/wiki/Helios_(spacecraft)" title="Helios (spacecraft)">\n         Helios\n        </a>\n       </i>\n       ,\n       <i>\n        <a href="/wiki/Pioneer_10" title="Pioneer 10">\n         Pioneer 10\n        </a>\n       </i>\n       ,\n       <i>\n        <a href="/wiki/Pioneer_11" title="Pioneer 11">\n         Pioneer 11\n        </a>\n       </i>\n       ,\n       <i>\n        <a href="/wiki/Giotto_(spacecraft)" title="Giotto (spacecraft)">\n         Giotto\n        </a>\n       </i>\n       , and\n       <i>\n        <a href="/wiki/Galileo_(spacecraft)" title="Galileo (spacecraft)">\n         Galileo\n        </a>\n       </i>\n       space missions, on the Earth-orbiting\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/LDEF" title="LDEF">\n        LDEF\n       </a>\n       ,\n       <a href="/wiki/European_Retrievable_Carrier" title="European Retrievable Carrier">\n        EURECA\n       </a>\n       , and Gorid satellites, and some scientists have utilized the\n       <i>\n        <a href="/wiki/Voyager_1" title="Voyager 1">\n         Voyager 1\n        </a>\n       </i>\n       and\n       <i>\n        <a href="/wiki/Voyager_2" title="Voyager 2">\n         2\n        </a>\n       </i>\n       spacecraft as giant\n       <a href="/wiki/Langmuir_probe" title="Langmuir probe">\n        Langmuir probes\n       </a>\n       to directly sample the cosmic dust. Presently dust detectors are flying on the\n       <i>\n        <a href="/wiki/Ulysses_(spacecraft)" title="Ulysses (spacecraft)">\n         Ulysses\n        </a>\n       </i>\n       ,\n       <i>\n        <a href="/wiki/Cassini%E2%80%93Huygens" title="Cassini\xe2\x80\x93Huygens">\n         Cassini\n        </a>\n       </i>\n       ,\n       <a href="/wiki/PROBA" title="PROBA">\n        Proba\n       </a>\n       ,\n       <i>\n        <a href="/wiki/Rosetta_(spacecraft)" title="Rosetta (spacecraft)">\n         Rosetta\n        </a>\n       </i>\n       ,\n       <i>\n        <a href="/wiki/Stardust_(spacecraft)" title="Stardust (spacecraft)">\n         Stardust\n        </a>\n       </i>\n       , and the\n       <i>\n        <a href="/wiki/New_Horizons" title="New Horizons">\n         New Horizons\n        </a>\n       </i>\n       spacecraft. The collected dust at Earth or collected further in space and returned by sample-return space missions is then analyzed by dust scientists in their respective laboratories all over the world. One large storage facility for cosmic dust exists at the NASA Houston JSC.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       Infrared light can penetrate the cosmic dust clouds, allowing us to peer into regions of star formation and the centers of galaxies.\n       <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">\n        NASA\n       </a>\n       \'s\n       <a href="/wiki/Spitzer_Space_Telescope" title="Spitzer Space Telescope">\n        Spitzer Space Telescope\n       </a>\n       is the largest infrared telescope ever launched into space. The Spitzer Space Telescope (formerly SIRTF, the Space Infrared Telescope Facility) was launched into space by a Delta rocket from Cape Canaveral, Florida on 25 August 2003. During its mission, Spitzer will obtain images and spectra by detecting the infrared energy, or heat, radiated by objects in space between wavelengths of 3 and 180 micrometres. Most of this infrared radiation is blocked by the Earth\'s atmosphere and cannot be observed from the ground. The findings from the Spitzer already revitalized the studies of cosmic dust. A recent report from a Spitzer team shows some evidence that cosmic dust is formed near a supermassive black hole.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18">\n        <a href="#cite_note-18">\n         [18]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       Another detection mechanism is polarimetry. Dust grains are not spherical and tend to align to interstellar magnetic fields, preferentially polarising starlight that passes through dust clouds. In nearby interstellar space, where cosmic reddening is not sensitive enough to be detected, high precision optical polarimetry has been used to glean the structure of dust within the\n       <a href="/wiki/Local_Bubble" title="Local Bubble">\n        Local Bubble\n       </a>\n       .\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19">\n        <a href="#cite_note-19">\n         [19]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <h2>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Radiative_properties">\n        Radiative properties\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Cosmic_dust&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Radiative properties">\n style="width:222px;">\n        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:A_glowing_jet_from_a_young_star.tif">\n         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1058" data-file-width="1033" height="225" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8b/A_glowing_jet_from_a_young_star.tif/lossy-page1-220px-A_glowing_jet_from_a_young_star.tif.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8b/A_glowing_jet_from_a_young_star.tif/lossy-page1-330px-A_glowing_jet_from_a_young_star.tif.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8b/A_glowing_jet_from_a_young_star.tif/lossy-page1-440px-A_glowing_jet_from_a_young_star.tif.jpg 2x" width="220"/>\n        </a>\n        <div class="thumbcaption">\n         <div class="magnify">\n          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:A_glowing_jet_from_a_young_star.tif" title="Enlarge">\n          </a>\n         </div>\n         <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=HH_151&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="HH 151 (page does not exist)">\n          HH 151\n         </a>\n         is a bright jet of glowing material trailed by an intricate, orange-hued plume of gas and dust.\n         <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20">\n          <a href="#cite_note-20">\n           [20]\n          </a>\n         </sup>\n        </div>\n       </div>\n      </div>\n      <p>\n       A dust particle interacts with\n       <a href="/wiki/Electromagnetic_radiation" title="Electromagnetic radiation">\n        electromagnetic radiation\n       </a>\n       in a way that depends on its\n       <a href="/wiki/Cross_section_(geometry)" title="Cross section (geometry)">\n        cross section\n       </a>\n       , the\n       <a href="/wiki/Wavelength" title="Wavelength">\n        wavelength\n       </a>\n       of the electromagnetic radiation, and on the nature of the grain: its\n       <a href="/wiki/Refractive_index" title="Refractive index">\n        refractive index\n       </a>\n       , size, etc. The radiation process for an individual grain is called its\n       <i>\n        <a href="/wiki/Emissivity" title="Emissivity">\n         emissivity\n        </a>\n       </i>\n       , dependent on the grain\'s\n       <i>\n        efficiency factor\n       </i>\n       . Furthermore, we have to specify whether the emissivity process is\n       <a href="/wiki/Extinction_(astronomy)" title="Extinction (astronomy)">\n        extinction\n       </a>\n       ,\n       <a href="/wiki/Scattering" title="Scattering">\n        scattering\n       </a>\n       ,\n       <a href="/wiki/Absorption_(electromagnetic_radiation)" title="Absorption (electromagnetic radiation)">\n        absorption\n       </a>\n       , or\n       <a href="/wiki/Polarization_(waves)" title="Polarization (waves)">\n        polarisation\n       </a>\n       . In the radiation emission curves, several important signatures identify the composition of the emitting or absorbing dust particles.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       Dust particles can scatter light nonuniformly. Forward-scattered light means that light is redirected slightly by\n       <a href="/wiki/Diffraction" title="Diffraction">\n        diffraction\n       </a>\n       off its path from the star/sunlight, and back-scattered light is reflected light.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       The scattering and extinction ("dimming") of the radiation gives useful information about the dust grain sizes. For example, if the object(s) in one\'s data is many times brighter in forward-scattered visible light than in back-scattered visible light, then we know that a significant fraction of the particles are about a micrometer in diameter.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       The scattering of light from dust grains in long exposure visible photographs is quite noticeable in\n       <a href="/wiki/Reflection_nebula" title="Reflection nebula">\n        reflection nebulae\n       </a>\n       , and gives clues about the individual particle\'s light-scattering properties. In X-ray wavelengths, many scientists are investigating the scattering of X-rays by interstellar dust, and some have suggested that\n       <a href="/wiki/Astrophysical_X-ray_source" title="Astrophysical X-ray source">\n        astronomical X-ray sources\n       </a>\n       would possess diffuse haloes, due to the dust.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Smith_21-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-Smith-21">\n         [21]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <h2>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Stardust">\n        Stardust\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Cosmic_dust&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Stardust">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h2>\n      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">\n       Main article:\n       <a href="/wiki/Presolar_grains" title="Presolar grains">\n        Presolar grains\n       </a>\n      </div>\n      <p>\n       Stardust grains (also called\n       <a href="/wiki/Presolar_grains" title="Presolar grains">\n        presolar grains\n       </a>\n       by meteoriticists\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22">\n        <a href="#cite_note-22">\n         [22]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       ) are contained within meteorites, from which they are extracted in terrestrial laboratories. Stardust was a component of the dust in the interstellar medium before its incorporation into meteorites. The meteorites have stored those stardust grains ever since the meteorites first assembled within the\n       <a href="/wiki/Formation_and_evolution_of_the_Solar_System#Formation" title="Formation and evolution of the Solar System">\n        planetary accretion disk\n       </a>\n       more than four billion years ago. So-called\n       <a href="/wiki/Carbonaceous_chondrite" title="Carbonaceous chondrite">\n        carbonaceous chondrites\n       </a>\n       are especially fertile reservoirs of stardust. Each stardust grain existed before the Earth was formed.\n       <i>\n        Stardust\n       </i>\n       is a scientific term referring to refractory dust grains that condensed from cooling ejected gases from individual presolar stars and incorporated into the cloud from which the Solar System condensed.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23">\n        <a href="#cite_note-23">\n         [23]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       Many different types of stardust have been identified by laboratory measurements of the highly unusual isotopic composition of the chemical elements that comprise each stardust grain. These refractory mineral grains may earlier have been coated with volatile compounds, but those are lost in the dissolving of meteorite matter in acids, leaving only insoluble refractory minerals. Finding the grain cores without dissolving most of the meteorite has been possible, but difficult and labor-intensive (see\n       <a href="/wiki/Presolar_grains" title="Presolar grains">\n        presolar grains\n       </a>\n       ).\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       Many new aspects of\n       <a href="/wiki/Nucleosynthesis" title="Nucleosynthesis">\n        nucleosynthesis\n       </a>\n       have been discovered from the isotopic ratios within the stardust grains.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24">\n        <a href="#cite_note-24">\n         [24]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       An important property of stardust is the hard, refractory, high-temperature nature of the grains. Prominent are\n       <a href="/wiki/Silicon_carbide" title="Silicon carbide">\n        silicon carbide\n       </a>\n       ,\n       <a href="/wiki/Graphite" title="Graphite">\n        graphite\n       </a>\n       ,\n       <a href="/wiki/Aluminium_oxide" title="Aluminium oxide">\n        aluminium oxide\n       </a>\n       , aluminium\n       <a href="/wiki/Spinel" title="Spinel">\n        spinel\n       </a>\n       , and other such solids that would condense at high temperature from a cooling gas, such as in stellar winds or in the decompression of the inside of a\n       <a href="/wiki/Supernova" title="Supernova">\n        supernova\n       </a>\n       . They differ greatly from the solids formed at low temperature within the interstellar medium.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       Also important are their extreme isotopic compositions, which are expected to exist nowhere in the interstellar medium. This also suggests that the stardust condensed from the gases of individual stars before the\n       <a href="/wiki/Isotope" title="Isotope">\n        isotopes\n       </a>\n       could be diluted by mixing with the interstellar medium. These allow the source stars to be identified. For example, the heavy elements within the silicon carbide (SiC) grains are almost pure\n       <a href="/wiki/S-process" title="S-process">\n        S-process\n       </a>\n       isotopes, fitting their condensation within\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Asymptotic_Giant_Branch" title="Asymptotic Giant Branch">\n        AGB\n       </a>\n       star red giant winds inasmuch as the AGB stars are the main source of S-process nucleosynthesis and have atmospheres observed by astronomers to be highly enriched in dredged-up s process elements.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       Another dramatic example is given by the so-called supernova condensates, usually shortened by acronym to SUNOCON (from SUperNOva CONdensate\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25">\n        <a href="#cite_note-25">\n         [25]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       ) to distinguish them from other stardust condensed within stellar atmospheres. SUNOCONs contain in their calcium an excessively large abundance\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26">\n        <a href="#cite_note-26">\n         [26]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       of\n       <sup>\n        44\n       </sup>\n       Ca, demonstrating that they condensed containing abundant radioactive\n       <sup>\n        44\n       </sup>\n       Ti, which has a 65-year\n       <a href="/wiki/Half-life" title="Half-life">\n        half-life\n       </a>\n       . The outflowing\n       <sup>\n        44\n       </sup>\n       Ti nuclei were thus still "alive" (radioactive) when the SUNOCON condensed near one year within the expanding supernova interior, but would have become an\n       <a href="/wiki/Extinct_radionuclide" title="Extinct radionuclide">\n        extinct radionuclide\n       </a>\n       (specifically\n       <sup>\n        44\n       </sup>\n       Ca) after the time required for mixing with the interstellar gas. Its discovery proved the prediction\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27">\n        <a href="#cite_note-27">\n         [27]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       from 1975 that it might be possible to identify SUNOCONs in this way. The SiC SUNOCONs (from supernovae) are only about 1% as numerous as are SiC stardust from AGB stars.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       Stardust itself (SUNOCONs and AGB grains that come from specific stars) is but a modest fraction of the condensed cosmic dust, forming less than 0.1% of the mass of total interstellar solids. The high interest in stardust derives from new information that it has brought to the sciences of\n       <a href="/wiki/Stellar_evolution" title="Stellar evolution">\n        stellar evolution\n       </a>\n       and\n       <a href="/wiki/Nucleosynthesis" title="Nucleosynthesis">\n        nucleosynthesis\n       </a>\n       .\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       Laboratories have studied solids that existed before the Earth existed.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28">\n        <a href="#cite_note-28">\n         [28]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       This was once thought impossible, especially in the 1970s when cosmochemists were confident that the Solar System began as a hot gas\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29">\n        <a href="#cite_note-29">\n         [29]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       virtually devoid of any remaining solids, which would have been vaporized by high temperature. The existence of stardust proved this historic picture incorrect.\n      </p>\n      <h2>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Some_bulk_properties">\n        Some bulk properties\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Cosmic_dust&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Some bulk properties">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h2>\n      <div class="thumb tleft">\n       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;">\n        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Smooth_chondriteIDP.jpg">\n         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="502" data-file-width="757" height="166" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/2/2f/Smooth_chondriteIDP.jpg/250px-Smooth_chondriteIDP.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/2/2f/Smooth_chondriteIDP.jpg/375px-Smooth_chondriteIDP.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/2/2f/Smooth_chondriteIDP.jpg/500px-Smooth_chondriteIDP.jpg 2x" width="250"/>\n        </a>\n        <div class="thumbcaption">\n         <div class="magnify">\n          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Smooth_chondriteIDP.jpg" title="Enlarge">\n          </a>\n         </div>\n         Smooth chondrite interplanetary dust particle.\n        </div>\n       </div>\n      </div>\n      <p>\n       Cosmic dust is made of dust grains and aggregates of dust grains. These particles are irregularly shaped, with\n       <a href="/wiki/Porosity" title="Porosity">\n        porosity\n       </a>\n       ranging from\n       <i>\n        fluffy\n       </i>\n       to\n       <i>\n        compact\n       </i>\n       . The composition, size, and other properties depends on where the dust is found, and conversely, a compositional analysis of a dust particle can reveal much about the dust particle\'s origin. General diffuse\n       <a href="/wiki/Interstellar_medium" title="Interstellar medium">\n        interstellar medium\n       </a>\n       dust, dust grains in\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Interstellar_clouds" title="Interstellar clouds">\n        dense clouds\n       </a>\n       ,\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Planetary_rings" title="Planetary rings">\n        planetary rings\n       </a>\n       dust, and\n       <a href="/wiki/Circumstellar_dust" title="Circumstellar dust">\n        circumstellar dust\n       </a>\n       , are each different in their characteristics. For example, grains in dense clouds have acquired a mantle of ice and on average are larger than dust particles in the diffuse interstellar medium.\n       <i>\n        Interplanetary dust particles\n       </i>\n       (IDPs) are generally larger still.\n      </p>\n      <div class="thumb tright">\n       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:352px;">\n        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:IDPmajorelements.png">\n         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="563" data-file-width="814" height="242" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/8/8c/IDPmajorelements.png/350px-IDPmajorelements.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/8/8c/IDPmajorelements.png/525px-IDPmajorelements.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/8/8c/IDPmajorelements.png/700px-IDPmajorelements.png 2x" width="350"/>\n        </a>\n        <div class="thumbcaption">\n         <div class="magnify">\n          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:IDPmajorelements.png" title="Enlarge">\n          </a>\n         </div>\n         Major elements of 200 stratospheric interplanetary dust particles.\n        </div>\n       </div>\n      </div>\n      <p>\n       Most of the influx of extraterrestrial matter that falls onto the Earth is dominated by meteoroids with diameters in the range 50 to 500 micrometers, of average density 2.0 g/cm\xc2\xb3 (with porosity about 40%). The densities of most IDPs\n       <a href="/wiki/Interplanetary_dust_cloud#Collecting_interplanetary_dust_on_Earth" title="Interplanetary dust cloud">\n        captured\n       </a>\n       in the Earth\'s\n       <a href="/wiki/Stratosphere" title="Stratosphere">\n        stratosphere\n       </a>\n       range between 1 and 3 g/cm\xc2\xb3, with an average density at about 2.0 g/cm\xc2\xb3.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30">\n        <a href="#cite_note-30">\n         [30]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       Other specific dust properties:\n      </p>\n      <ul>\n       <li>\n        In\n        <i>\n         circumstellar dust\n        </i>\n        , astronomers have found molecular signatures of\n        <a href="/wiki/Carbon_monoxide" href="/wiki/Silicate" title="Silicate">\n         amorphous silicate\n        </a>\n        ,\n        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Polycyclic_aromatic_hydrocarbons" title="Polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbons">\n         polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbons\n        </a>\n        ,\n        <a href="/wiki/Ice" title="Ice">\n         water ice\n        </a>\n        , and\n        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Polyformaldehyde" title="Polyformaldehyde">\n         polyformaldehyde\n        </a>\n        , among others (in the diffuse\n        <a href="/wiki/Interstellar_medium" title="Interstellar medium">\n         interstellar medium\n        </a>\n        , there is evidence for silicate and carbon grains).\n       </li>\n       <li>\n        <i>\n         <a href="/wiki/Comet_dust" title="Comet dust">\n          Cometary dust\n         </a>\n        </i>\n        is generally different (with overlap) from\n        <i>\n         <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Asteroid_dust" title="Asteroid dust">\n          asteroidal dust\n         </a>\n        </i>\n        . Asteroidal dust resembles\n        <a href="/wiki/Carbonaceous_chondrite" title="Carbonaceous chondrite">\n         carbonaceous chondritic meteorites\n        </a>\n        . Cometary dust resembles\n        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Interstellar_dust" title="Interstellar dust">\n         interstellar grains\n        </a>\n        which can include silicates, polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbons, and water ice.\n       </li>\n      </ul>\n      <h2>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Dust_grain_formation">\n        Dust grain formation\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Cosmic_dust&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Dust grain formation">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h2>\n      <p>\n       The large grains in interstellar space are probably complex, with refractory cores that condensed within stellar outflows topped by layers acquired subsequently during incursions into cold dense interstellar clouds. That cyclic process of growth and destruction outside of the clouds has been modeled\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31">\n        <a href="#cite_note-31">\n         [31]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32">\n        <a href="#cite_note-32">\n         [32]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       to demonstrate that the cores live much longer than the average lifetime of dust mass. Those cores mostly start with silicate particles condensing in the atmospheres of cool oxygen rich red-giant stars and carbon grains condensing in the atmospheres of cool\n       <a href="/wiki/Carbon_star" title="Carbon star">\n        carbon stars\n       </a>\n       . The red-giant stars have evolved off the\n       <a href="/wiki/Main_sequence" title="Main sequence">\n        main sequence\n       </a>\n       and have entered the\n       <a href="/wiki/Giant_star" title="Giant star">\n        giant\n       </a>\n       phase of their evolution and are the major source of refractory dust grain cores in galaxies. Those refractory cores are also called Stardust (section above), which is a scientific term for the small fraction of cosmic dust that condensed thermally within stellar gases as they were ejected from the stars. Several percent of refractory grain cores have condensed within expanding interiors of supernovae, a type of cosmic decompression chamber. And meteoriticists that study this refractory stardust extracted from meteorites often call it\n       <a href="/wiki/Presolar_grains" title="Presolar grains">\n        presolar grains\n       </a>\n       , although the refractory stardust that they study is actually only a small fraction of all presolar dust. Stardust condenses within the stars via considerably different condensation chemistry than that of the bulk of cosmic dust, which accretes cold onto preexisting dust in dark molecular clouds of the galaxy. Those molecular clouds are very cold, typically less than 50K, so that ices of many kinds may accrete onto grains, perhaps to be destroyed later. Finally, when the Solar System formed, interstellar dust grains were further modified by chemical reactions within the planetary accretion disk. So the history of the complex grains in the early Solar System is complicated and only partially understood.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       Astronomers know that the dust is formed in the envelopes of late-evolved stars from specific observational signatures. In infrared light, emission at 9.7 micrometres is a signature of silicate dust in cool evolved oxygen-rich giant stars. Emission at 11.5 micrometres indicates the presence of silicon carbide dust in cool evolved carbon-rich giant stars. These help provide evidence that the small silicate particles in space came from the ejected outer envelopes of these stars.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33">\n        <a href="#cite_note-33">\n         [33]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34">\n        <a href="#cite_note-34">\n         [34]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       Conditions in interstellar space are generally not suitable for the formation of silicate cores. This would take excessive time to accomplish, even if it might be possible. The arguments are that: given an observed typical grain diameter\n       <i>\n        a\n       </i>\n       , the time for a grain to attain\n       <i>\n        a\n       </i>\n       , and given the temperature of interstellar gas, it would take considerably longer than the age of the Universe for interstellar grains to form.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35">\n        <a href="#cite_note-35">\n         [35]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       On the other hand, grains are seen to have recently formed in the vicinity of nearby stars, in\n       <a href="/wiki/Nova" title="Nova">\n        nova\n       </a>\n       and\n       <a href="/wiki/Supernova" title="Supernova">\n        supernova\n       </a>\n       ejecta, and in\n       <a href="/wiki/R_Coronae_Borealis_variable" title="R Coronae Borealis variable">\n        R Coronae Borealis variable\n       </a>\n       stars which seem to eject discrete clouds containing both gas and dust. So mass loss from stars is unquestionably where the refractory cores of grains formed.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       Most dust in the\n       <a href="/wiki/Solar_System" title="Solar System">\n        Solar System\n       </a>\n       is highly processed dust, recycled from the material out of which the Solar System formed and subsequently collected in the planetesimals, and leftover solid material such as\n       <a href="/wiki/Comet" title="Comet">\n        comets\n       </a>\n       and\n       <a href="/wiki/Asteroid" title="Asteroid">\n        asteroids\n       </a>\n       , and reformed in each of those bodies\' collisional lifetimes. During the Solar System\'s formation history, the most abundant element was (and still is) H\n       <sub>\n        2\n       </sub>\n       . The metallic elements: magnesium, silicon, and iron, which are the principal ingredients of rocky planets, condensed into solids at the highest temperatures of the planetary disk. Some molecules such as CO, N\n       <sub>\n        2\n       </sub>\n       , NH\n       <sub>\n        3\n       </sub>\n       , and free oxygen, existed in a gas phase. Some molecules, for example, graphite (C) and SiC would condense into solid grains in the planetary disk; but carbon and SiC grains found in meteorites are presolar based on their isotopic compositions, rather than from the planetary disk formation. Some molecules also formed complex organic compounds and some molecules formed frozen ice mantles, of which either could coat the "refractory" (Mg, Si, Fe) grain cores. Stardust once more provides an exception to the general trend, as it appears to be totally unprocessed since its thermal condensation within stars as refractory crystalline minerals. The condensation of graphite occurs within supernova interiors as they expand and cool, and do so even in gas containing more oxygen than carbon,\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36">\n        <a href="#cite_note-36">\n         [36]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       a surprising carbon chemistry made possible by the intense radioactive environment of supernovae. This special example of dust formation has merited specific review.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37">\n        <a href="#cite_note-37">\n         [37]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       Planetary disk formation of precursor molecules was determined, in large part, by the temperature of the solar nebula. Since the temperature of the solar nebula decreased with heliocentric distance, scientists can infer a dust grain\'s origin(s) with knowledge of the grain\'s materials. Some materials could only have been formed at high temperatures, while other grain materials could only have been formed at much lower temperatures. The materials in a single interplanetary dust particle often show that the grain elements formed in different locations and at different times in the solar nebula. Most of the matter present in the original solar nebula has since disappeared; drawn into the Sun, expelled into interstellar space, or reprocessed, for example, as part of the planets, asteroids or comets.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       Due to their highly processed nature, IDPs (interplanetary dust particles) are fine-grained mixtures of thousands to millions of mineral grains and\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Amorphous" title="Amorphous">\n        amorphous\n       </a>\n       components. We can picture an IDP as a "matrix" of material with embedded elements which were formed at different times and places in the\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solar_nebula" title="Solar nebula">\n        solar nebula\n       </a>\n       and before the solar nebula\'s formation. title="Calcium-Aluminum-Inclusions">\n        CAIs\n       </a>\n       .\n      </p>\n      <h2>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="From_the_solar_nebula_to_Earth">\n        From the solar nebula href="#cite_note-NASA-20140221-42">\n         [42]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       In March 2015, NASA scientists reported that, for the first time, complex\n       <a href="/wiki/DNA" title="DNA">\n        DNA\n       </a>\n       and\n       <a href="/wiki/RNA" title="RNA">\n        RNA\n       </a>\n       <a href="/wiki/Organic_compound" title="Organic compound">\n        organic compounds\n       </a>\n       of\n       <a href="/wiki/Life" title="Life">\n        life\n       </a>\n       , including\n       <a href="/wiki/Uracil" title="Uracil">\n        uracil\n       </a>\n       ,\n       <a href="/wiki/Cytosine" title="Cytosine">\n        cytosine\n       </a>\n       and\n       <a href="/wiki/Thymine" title="Thymine">\n        thymine\n       </a>\n       , have been formed in the laboratory under\n       <a href="/wiki/Outer_space" title="Outer space">\n        outer space\n       </a>\n       conditions, using starting chemicals, such as\n       <a href="/wiki/Pyrimidine" title="Pyrimidine">\n        pyrimidine\n       </a>\n       , found in\n       <a href="/wiki/Meteorite" title="Meteorite">\n        meteorites\n       </a>\n       . Pyrimidine, like\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Polycyclic_aromatic_hydrocarbons" title="Polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbons">\n        polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbons\n       </a>\n       (PAHs), the most carbon-rich chemical found in the\n       <a href="/wiki/Universe" title="Universe">\n        Universe\n       </a>\n       , may have been formed in\n       <a href="/wiki/Red_giant" title="Red giant">\n        red giants\n       </a>\n       or in interstellar dust and gas clouds, according to the scientists.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NASA-20150303_46-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-NASA-20150303-46">\n         [46]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <h2>\n       <span id=\'Some_"dusty"_clouds_in_the_universe\'>\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Some_.22dusty.22_clouds_in_the_universe">\n        Some "dusty" clouds in the universe\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Cosmic_dust&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title=\'Edit section: Some "dusty" clouds in the universe\'>\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h2>\n      <p>\n       The Solar System has its own\n       <a href="/wiki/Interplanetary_dust_cloud" title="Interplanetary dust cloud">\n        interplanetary dust cloud\n       </a>\n       , as do extrasolar systems.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       There are different types of nebulae with different physical causes and processes. One might see these classifications:\n      </p>\n      <ul>\n       <li>\n        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Diffuse_nebula" title="Diffuse nebula">\n         diffuse nebula\n        </a>\n       </li>\n       <li>\n        infrared (IR)\n        <a href="/wiki/Reflection_nebula" title="Reflection nebula">\n         reflection nebula\n        </a>\n       </li>\n       <li>\n        <a href="/wiki/Supernova_remnant" title="Supernova remnant">\n         supernova remnant\n        </a>\n       </li>\n       <li>\n        <a href="/wiki/Molecular_cloud" title="Molecular cloud">\n         molecular cloud\n        </a>\n       </li>\n       <li>\n        <a href="/wiki/H_II_region" title="H II region">\n         HII regions\n        </a>\n       </li>\n       <li>\n        <a href="/wiki/Photodissociation_region" title="Photodissociation region">\n         photodissociation regions\n        </a>\n       </li>\n       <li>\n        <a href="/wiki/Dark_nebula" title="Dark nebula">\n         Dark Nebula\n        </a>\n       </li>\n      </ul>\n      <p>\n       Distinctions between those types of nebula are that different radiation processes are at work. For example, H II regions, like the\n       <a href="/wiki/Orion_Nebula" title="Orion Nebula">\n        Orion Nebula\n       </a>\n       , where a lot of star-formation is taking place, are characterized as thermal emission nebulae. Supernova remnants, on the other hand, like the\n       <a href="/wiki/Crab_Nebula" title="Crab Nebula">\n        Crab Nebula\n       </a>\n       , are characterized as nonthermal emission (\n       <a href="/wiki/Synchrotron_radiation" title="Synchrotron radiation">\n        synchrotron radiation\n       </a>\n       ).\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       Some of the better known dusty regions in the Universe are the diffuse nebulae in the Messier catalog, for example:\n       <a href="/wiki/Crab_Nebula" title="Crab Nebula">\n        M1\n       </a>\n       ,\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Messier_8" title="Messier 8">\n        M8\n       </a>\n       ,\n       <a href="/wiki/Eagle_Nebula" title="Eagle Nebula">\n        M16\n       </a>\n       ,\n       <a href="/wiki/Omega_Nebula" title="Omega Nebula">\n        M17\n       </a>\n       ,\n       <a href="/wiki/Trifid_Nebula" title="Trifid Nebula">\n        M20\n       </a>\n       ,\n       <a href="/wiki/Orion_Nebula" title="Orion Nebula">\n        M42\n       </a>\n       ,\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/M43_(nebula)" title="M43 (nebula)">\n        M43\n       </a>\n       .\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47">\n        <a href="#cite_note-47">\n         [47]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       Some larger dust catalogs are:\n      </p>\n      <ul>\n       <li>\n        Sharpless (1959) A Catalogue of HII Regions\n       </li>\n       <li>\n        Lynds (1965) Catalogue of Bright Nebulae\n       </li>\n       <li>\n        Lunds (1962) Catalogue of Dark Nebulae\n       </li>\n       <li>\n        van den Bergh (1966) Catalogue of Reflection Nebulae\n       </li>\n       <li>\n        Green (1988) Rev.      </li>\n      </ul>\n      <h2>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Interstellar_dust_sample_return">\n        Interstellar dust sample return\n       </span>\n       <span
