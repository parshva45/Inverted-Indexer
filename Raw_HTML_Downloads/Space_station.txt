</div>\n       </div>\n      </div>\n      <p>\n       A\n       <b>\n        space station\n       </b>\n       , also known as an\n       <b>\n        orbital station\n       </b>\n       or an\n       <b>\n        orbital space station\n       </b>\n       , is a\n       <a href="/wiki/Spacecraft" title="Spacecraft">\n        spacecraft\n       </a>\n       capable of supporting crewmembers, which is designed to remain in\n       <a href="/wiki/Outer_space" title="Outer space">\n        space\n       </a>\n       (most commonly as an\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Artificial_satellite" title="Artificial satellite">\n        artificial satellite\n       </a>\n       in\n       <a href="/wiki/Low_Earth_orbit" title="Low Earth orbit">\n        low Earth orbit\n       </a>\n       ) for an extended period of time and for other spacecraft to dock. A space station is distinguished from other spacecraft used for\n       <a href="/wiki/Human_spaceflight" title="Human spaceflight">\n        human spaceflight\n       </a>\n       by lack of major\n       <a href="/wiki/Spacecraft_propulsion" title="Spacecraft propulsion">\n        propulsion\n       </a>\n       or\n       <a href="/wiki/Landing" title="Landing">\n        landing\n       </a>\n       systems. Instead, other vehicles transport people and cargo to and from the station. As of September 2016\n       <sup class="plainlinks noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;">\n        <a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Space_station&amp;action=edit">\n         [update]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       three space stations are in orbit: the\n       <a href="/wiki/International_Space_Station" title="International Space Station">\n        International Space Station\n       </a>\n       , which is permanently manned, China\'s\n       <a href="/wiki/Tiangong-1" title="Tiangong-1">\n        Tiangong-1\n       </a>\n       (defunct) and\n       <a href="/wiki/Tiangong-2" title="Tiangong-2">\n        Tiangong-2\n       </a>\n       (launched 15 September 2016, not permanently manned).\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">\n        <a href="#cite_note-1">\n         [1]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NASAspaceflight_2-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-NASAspaceflight-2">\n         [2]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       Previous stations include the\n       <a href="/wiki/Almaz" title="Almaz">\n        Almaz\n       </a>\n       and\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Salyut_Program" title="Salyut Program">\n        Salyut series\n       </a>\n       ,\n       <a href="/wiki/Skylab" title="Skylab">\n        Skylab\n       </a>\n       , and most recently\n       <i>\n        <a href="/wiki/Mir" title="Mir">\n         Mir\n        </a>\n       </i>\n       .\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       Today\'s space stations are research platforms, used to study the effects of long-term space flight on the human body as well as to provide platforms for greater number and length of scientific studies than available on other space vehicles. Each crew member stays aboard the station for weeks or months, but rarely more than a year. Since the ill-fated flight of\n       <a href="/wiki/Soyuz_11" title="Soyuz 11">\n        Soyuz 11\n       </a>\n       to\n       <a href="/wiki/Salyut_1" title="Salyut 1">\n        Salyut 1\n       </a>\n       , all manned spaceflight duration records have been set aboard space stations. The\n       <a href="/wiki/List_of_spaceflight_records#Ten_longest_human_space_flights" title="List of spaceflight records">\n        duration record for a single spaceflight\n       </a>\n       is 437.7 days, set by\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Valeriy_Polyakov" title="Valeriy Polyakov">\n        Valeriy Polyakov\n       </a>\n       aboard\n       <i>\n        Mir\n       </i>\n       from 1994 to 1995. As of 2016\n       <sup class="plainlinks noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;">\n        <a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Space_station&amp;action=edit">\n         [update]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       , four cosmonauts have completed single missions of over a year, all aboard\n       <i>\n        Mir\n       </i>\n       .\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       Space stations have also been used for both military and civilian purposes. The last military-use space station was\n       <a href="/wiki/Salyut_5" title="Salyut 5">\n        Salyut 5\n       </a>\n       , which was used by the\n       <a href="/wiki/Almaz" title="Almaz">\n Pursuing Oberth\'s idea of a space-based weapon, the so-called "\n       <a href="/wiki/Sun_gun" title="Sun gun">\n        sun gun\n       </a>\n       " was a concept of a space station orbiting Earth at a height of 8,200 kilometres (5,100\xc2\xa0mi), with a weapon that was to utilize the sun\'s energy.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Time_5-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-Time-5">\n         [5]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       In 1951, in\n       <i>\n        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Collier%27s_Weekly" title="Collier\'s Weekly">\n         Collier\'s Weekly\n        </a>\n       </i>\n       ,\n       <a href="/wiki/Wernher_von_Braun" title="Wernher von Braun">\n        Wernher von Braun\n       </a>\n       published his design for a\n       <a href="/wiki/Rotating_wheel_space_station" title="Rotating wheel space station">\n        rotating wheel space station\n       </a>\n       , which referenced Poto\xc4\x8dnik\'s idea \xe2\x80\x93 however these concepts would never leave the concept stage during the 20th century.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-boys_4-2">\n        <a href="#cite_note-boys-4">\n         [4]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       During the same time as von Braun pursued Poto\xc4\x8dnik\'s ideas, the Soviet design bureaus \xe2\x80\x93 chiefly\n       <a href="/wiki/Vladimir_Chelomey" title="Vladimir Chelomey">\n        Vladimir Chelomey\n       </a>\n       \'s\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/OKB-52" title="OKB-52">\n        OKB-52\n       </a>\n       \xe2\x80\x93 were pursuing Tsiolkovsky\'s ideas for space stations. The work by OKB-52 would lead to the Almaz programme and (together with\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/OKB-1" title="OKB-1">\n        OKB-1\n       </a>\n       ) to the first space station:\n       <a href="/wiki/Salyut_1" title="Salyut 1">\n        Salyut 1\n       </a>\n       . The developed hardware laid the ground for the\n       <a href="/wiki/Salyut_programme" title="Salyut programme">\n        Salyut and Mir space stations\n       </a>\n       , and is even today a station was\n       <a href="/wiki/Salyut_1" title="Salyut 1">\n        Salyut 1\n       </a>\n       , which was launched by the\n       <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">\n        Soviet Union\n       </a>\n       on April 19, 1971. Like all the early space stations, it was "monolithic", intended to be constructed and launched in one piece, and then manned by a crew later. As such, monolithic stations generally contained all their supplies and experimental equipment when launched, and were considered "expended", and then abandoned, when these were used up.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       The earlier Soviet stations were all designated "Salyut", but among these there were two distinct types: civilian and military. The military stations,\n       <a href="/wiki/Salyut_2" title="Salyut 2">\n        Salyut 2\n       </a>\n       ,\n       <a href="/wiki/Salyut_3" title="Salyut 3">\n        Salyut 3\n       </a>\n       , and\n       <a href="/wiki/Salyut_5" title="Salyut 5">\n        Salyut 5\n       </a>\n       , were also known as\n       <a href="/wiki/Almaz" title="Almaz">\n        Almaz\n       </a>\n       stations.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       The civilian stations\n       <a href="/wiki/Salyut_6" title="Salyut 6">\n        Salyut 6\n       </a>\n       and\n       <a href="/wiki/Salyut_7" title="Salyut 7">\n        Salyut 7\n       </a>\n       were built with two docking ports, which allowed a second crew to visit, bringing a new spacecraft with them; the\n       <a href="/wiki/Soyuz_7K-T" title="Soyuz 7K-T">\n        Soyuz ferry\n       </a>\n       could spend 90 days in space, after which point it needed to be replaced by a fresh Soyuz spacecraft.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MHH_6-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-MHH-6">\n         [6]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       This allowed for a crew to man the station continually.\n       <a href="/wiki/Skylab" title="Skylab">\n        Skylab\n       </a>\n       was also equipped with two docking ports, like second-generation stations, but the extra port was never utilized. The presence of a second port on the new stations allowed\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Progress_spacecraft" title="Progress spacecraft">\n        Progress\n       </a>\n       supply vehicles to be docked to the station, meaning that fresh supplies could be brought to aid long-duration missions. This concept was expanded on Salyut 7, which "hard docked" with a\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/TKS_spacecraft" title="TKS spacecraft">\n        TKS tug\n       </a>\n       shortly before it was abandoned; this served as a proof-of-concept for the use of modular space stations.        <div class="magnify">\n          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Mir_during_STS-74_(cropped).jpg" title="Enlarge">\n          </a>\n         </div>\n         Earth and the\n         <i>\n          Mir\n         </i>\n         station\n        </div>\n       </div>\n      </div>\n      <p>\n       Unlike previous stations, the Soviet space station\n       <i>\n        <a href="/wiki/Mir" launched, and additional modules, generally with a specific role, were later added to that. This method allows for greater flexibility in operation, as well as removing the need for a single immensely powerful\n       <a href="/wiki/Launch_vehicle" title="Launch vehicle">\n        launch vehicle\n       </a>\n       . Modular stations are also designed from the outset to have their supplies provided by logistical support, which allows for a longer lifetime at the cost of requiring regular support launches.\n      </p>\n      <h3>\n       <span id="ISS_(1998\xe2\x80\x93present)">\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="ISS_.281998.E2.80.93present.29">\n        ISS (1998\xe2\x80\x93present)\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Space_station&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: ISS (1998\xe2\x80\x93present)">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h3>\n      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">\n       Main article:\n       <a href="/wiki/International_Space_Station" title="International Space Station">\n        International Space Station\n       </a>\n      </div>\n      <p>\n       The core module of the International Space Station was launched in 1998.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       The ISS is divided into two main sections, the\n       <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">\n        Russian\n       </a>\n       orbital segment (ROS), and the\n       <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">\n        United States\n       </a>\n       operational segment (USOS).\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       USOS modules were brought to the station by the\n       <a href="/wiki/Space_Shuttle" title="Space Shuttle">\n        Space Shuttle\n       </a>\n       and manually attached to the ISS by crews during\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Extra-vehicular_activity" title="Extra-vehicular activity">\n        EVAs\n       </a>\n       . Connections are made manually for electrical, data, propulsion and cooling fluids. This results in a single piece which is not designed for disassembly.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">\n        <a href="#cite_note-7">\n         [7]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <div class="thumb tright">\n       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">\n        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:STS-116_spacewalk_1.jpg">\n         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2000" data-file-width="3032" height="145" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/89/STS-116_spacewalk_1.jpg/220px-STS-116_spacewalk_1.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/89/STS-116_spacewalk_1.jpg/330px-STS-116_spacewalk_1.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/89/STS-116_spacewalk_1.jpg/440px-STS-116_spacewalk_1.jpg 2x" width="220"/>\n        </a>\n        <div class="thumbcaption">\n         <div class="magnify">\n          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:STS-116_spacewalk_1.jpg" title="Enlarge">\n          </a>\n         </div>\n         ISS under construction\n        </div>\n       </div>\n      </div>\n      <p>\n       The Russian orbital segment\'s modules are able to launch, fly and dock themselves without human intervention using\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Proton_(rocket)" title="Proton (rocket)">\n        Proton rockets\n       </a>\n       .\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">\n        <a href="#cite_note-8">\n         [8]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       Connections are automatically made for power, data and propulsion fluids and gases. The Russian approach allows assembly of space stations orbiting other worlds in preparation for manned missions. The\n       <a href="/wiki/Nauka_(ISS_module)" title="Nauka (ISS module)">\n        Nauka module\n       </a>\n       of the ISS will be used in the 12th Russian/Soviet space station,\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/OPSEK" title="OPSEK">\n        OPSEK\n       </a>\n       , whose main goal is supporting manned deep space exploration.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       Russian Modular or \'next generation\' space stations differ from \'Monolithic\' single piece stations by allowing reconfiguration of the station to suit changing needs. According to a 2009 report,\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/RKK_Energia" title="RKK Energia">\n        RKK Energia\n       </a>\n       is considering methods to remove from the station some modules of the Russian Orbital Segment when the end of mission is reached for the\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ISS" title="ISS">\n        ISS\n       </a>\n       and use them as a basis for a new station, known as the\n       <a href="/wiki/Orbital_Piloted_Assembly_and_Experiment_Complex" title="Orbital Piloted Assembly and Experiment Complex">\n        Orbital Piloted Assembly and Experiment Complex\n       </a>\n       . None of these modules would have reached the end of their useful lives in 2016 or 2020. The unmanned\n       <a href="/wiki/Shenzhou_8" title="Shenzhou 8">\n        Shenzhou 8\n       </a>\n       then successfully performed an automatic rendezvous and docking in November 2011. The manned\n       <a href="/wiki/Shenzhou_9" title="Shenzhou 9">\n        Shenzhou 9\n       </a>\n       then docked with Tiangong-1 in June 2012, the manned\n       <a href="/wiki/Shenzhou_10" </div>\n      </div>\n      <p>\n       These stations have various issues that limit their long-term habitability, such as very low recycling rates, relatively high radiation levels and a lack of weight. Some of these problems cause discomfort and long-term health effects. In the case of solar flares, all current habitants are protected by the Earth\'s magnetic field, and are below the\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Van_Allen_belts" title="Van Allen belts">\n        Van Allen belts\n       </a>\n       .\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       Future\n       <a href="/wiki/Space_habitat" title="Space habitat">\n        space habitats\n       </a>\n       may attempt to address these issues, and could be intended for long-term occupation. Some designs might even accommodate large numbers of people, essentially "cities in space" where people would make their homes. No such design has yet been constructed, since even for a small station, the current (2016) launch costs are not economically or politically viable.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       Possible ways to deal with these costs would be to build a large number of rockets (economies of scale), employ reusable rockets,\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ISRU" title="ISRU">\n        In Situ Resource Utilisation\n       </a>\n       , or\n       <a href="/wiki/Non-rocket_spacelaunch" title="Non-rocket spacelaunch">\n        non-rocket spacelaunch\n       </a>\n       methods such as\n       <a href="/wiki/Space_elevator" title="Space elevator">\n        space elevators\n       </a>\n       . enough mass in space to allow high\n       <a href="/wiki/Space_colonization#Radiation_protection" title="Space colonization">\n        radiation shielding\n       </a>\n       , the most ambitious historical\n       <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">\n        NASA\n       </a>\n       study, a conceptual 10000-person spacestation, envisioned a future\n       <a href="/wiki/Mass_driver"
