This has come about due to the collisions which occurred in the Dover Straits in 2002 when vessels struck the new wreck of the\n        <a href="/wiki/MV_Tricolor" title="MV Tricolor">\n         MV Tricolor\n        </a>\n        .\n        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">\n         <a href="#cite_note-4">\n          [4]\n         </a>\n        </sup>\n       </li>\n       <li>\n        <a href="/wiki/Navigational_aid#Lateral_marker" title="Navigational aid">\n         lateral marker\n        </a>\n        buoy\n       </li>\n       <li>\n        Mooring buoys \xe2\x80\x93 used to keep one end of a\n        <a href="/wiki/Mooring_(watercraft)" title="Mooring (watercraft)">\n         mooring\n        </a>\n        cable or chain on the water\'s surface so that\n        <a href="/wiki/Ship" title="Ship">\n         ships\n        </a>\n        or boats can tie on to it\n       </li>\n       <li>\n        Tripping buoys \xe2\x80\x93 used to keep one end of a \'tripping line\' on the water\'s surface so that a stuck\n        <a href="/wiki/Anchor" title="Anchor">\n         anchor\n        </a>\n        can more easily be freed\n       </li>\n       <li>\n        <a href="/wiki/Weather_buoy" title="Weather buoy">\n         Weather buoys\n        </a>\n        \xe2\x80\x93 equipped to measure weather parameters such as air temperature, barometric pressure, wind speed and direction and to report these data via satellite radio links such as the purpose-built\n        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Argos_System" title="Argos System">\n         Argos System\n        </a>\n        or commercial\n        <a href="/wiki/Satellite_phone" title="Satellite phone">\n         satellite phone\n        </a>\n        networks to meteorological centres for use in forecasting and climate study. May be anchored (moored buoys) or allowed to drift (drifting buoys) in the open ocean currents. Position is calculated by the satellite. Weather buoys are sometimes referred to as\n        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ocean_Data_Acquisition_System" title="Ocean Data Acquisition System">\n         ODAS\n        </a>\n        buoys or\n        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ocean_Data_Acquisition_System" title="Ocean Data Acquisition System">\n         Ocean Data Acquisition Systems\n        </a>\n        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">\n         <a href="#cite_note-5">\n          [5]\n         </a>\n        </sup>\n        and may be marked on charts as "Superbuoys."\n        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_2-1">\n         <a href="#cite_note-:0-2">\n          [2]\n         </a>\n        </sup>\n       </li>\n       <li>\n        <a href="/wiki/Deep-ocean_Assessment_and_Reporting_of_Tsunamis" title="Deep-ocean Assessment and Reporting of Tsunamis">\n         Tsunami buoys\n        </a>\n        \xe2\x80\x93 anchored buoys that can detect sudden changes in undersea water pressure are used as part of\n        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tsunami_warning_systems" title="Tsunami warning systems">\n         tsunami warning systems\n        </a>\n        in the\n        <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Tsunami_Warning_Center" title="Pacific Tsunami Warning Center">\n         Pacific Tsunami Warning Center\n        </a>\n        and Indian Oceans.\n       </li>\n       <li>\n        <a href="/wiki/Spar_buoy" title="Spar buoy">\n         Spar buoy\n        </a>\n        \xe2\x80\x93 a tall, thin buoy that floats upright in the water, e.g.\n        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/R/P_FLIP" title="R/P FLIP">\n         R/P FLIP\n        </a>\n        .\n       </li>\n       <li>\n        Profiling buoy \xe2\x80\x93 specialized models which adjust buoyancy so that they will sink at a controlled rate to 2,000 metres below the surface while measuring sea temperatures and salinity. After a time, typically 10 days, the buoy returns to the surface, transmits its data via satellite, and then sinks again.\n        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kery1989_6-0">\n         <a href="#cite_note-Kery1989-6">\n          [6]\n         </a>\n        </sup>\n        See\n        <a href="/wiki/Argo_(oceanography)" title="Argo (oceanography)">\n         Argo (oceanography)\n        </a>\n        .\n       </li>\n       <li>\n        Ice marking buoys \xe2\x80\x93 used for marking ice holes in frozen lakes and rivers, so that snowmobiles do not drive over the holes.\n       </li>\n       <li>\n        Marker buoys \xe2\x80\x93 used in\n        <a href="/wiki/Naval_warfare" title="Naval warfare">\n         naval warfare\n        </a>\n        , particularly\n        <a href="/wiki/Anti-submarine_warfare" title="Anti-submarine warfare">\n         anti-submarine warfare\n        </a>\n        , is a light-emitting or smoke-emitting, or both, marker using some kind of\n        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pyrotechnic" title="Pyrotechnic">\n         pyrotechnic\n        </a>\n        to provide the\n        <a href="/wiki/Flare" title="Flare">\n         flare\n        </a>\n        and\n        <a href="/wiki/Smoke" title="Smoke">\n         smoke\n        </a>\n        . It is commonly a 3-inch (76\xc2\xa0mm) diameter device about 20\xc2\xa0inches (500\xc2\xa0mm) long that is set off by contact with seawater and floats on the surface. Some markers extinguish after a set period and others are made to sink.\n       </li>\n       <li>\n        Lobster trap buoys \xe2\x80\x93 brightly colored buoys used for the marking of lobster trap locations so the person\n        <a href="/wiki/Lobster_fishing" title="Lobster fishing">\n         lobster fishing\n        </a>\n        can find their lobster traps. Each lobster fisherman has his or her own color markings or registration numbers so they know which ones are theirs. They are only allowed to haul their own traps and must display their buoy color or license number on their boat so law enforcement officials know what they should be hauling. The buoys are brightly colored with highly visible numbers so they can be seen under conditions when there is poor visibility like\n        <a href="/wiki/Rain" title="Rain">\n         rain\n        </a>\n        ,\n        <a href="/wiki/Fog" title="Fog">\n         fog\n        </a>\n        ,\n        <a href="/wiki/Sea_smoke" title="Sea smoke">\n         sea smoke\n        </a>\n        , etc.\n        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">\n         <a href="#cite_note-7">\n          [7]\n         </a>\n        </sup>\n        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">\n         <a href="#cite_note-8">\n          [8]\n         </a>\n        </sup>\n       </li>\n       <li>\n        <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Wave_buoy&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Wave buoy (page does not exist)">\n         Wave buoy\n        </a>\n        \xe2\x80\x93 used to measure the movement of the water surface as a wave train. The wave train is analysed to determine statistics like the\n        <a href="/wiki/Significant_wave_height" title="Significant wave height">\n         significant wave height\n        </a>\n        and period, and wave direction.\n       </li>\n       <li>\n        Target buoy \xe2\x80\x93 used to simulate target (like small boat) in live fire exercise by naval and coastal forces, usually targeted by weapons (medium size) like HMG\'s, rapid fire cannons (20 or so mm), autocannons (bigger ones up to 40 and 57mm) and also anti-tank rockets.\n       </li>\n       <li>\n        Wreck buoy \xe2\x80\x93 a buoy to mark a wrecked ship to warn other ships to keep away because of unseen hazards.\n       </li>\n       <li>\n        <a href="/wiki/Self-locating_datum_marker_buoy" title="Self-locating datum marker buoy">\n         Self-locating datum marker buoy\n        </a>\n        (SLDMB) \xe2\x80\x93 A 70% scale Coastal Ocean Dynamics Experiment (CODE)/Davis-style oceanographic surface drifter with drogue vanes between 30 and 100\xc2\xa0cm deep.\n        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-METOCEAN_9-0">\n         <a href="#cite_note-METOCEAN-9">\n          [9]\n         </a>\n        </sup>\n        This particular surface drifter is designed specifically for deployment from a\n        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._Coast_Guard" title="U.S. Coast Guard">\n         U.S. Coast Guard\n        </a>\n        vessel or airframe for search and rescue. Since the SLDMB has a very small surface area above the ocean surface and a high underwater surface area, there is very little\n        <a href="/wiki/Leeway" title="Leeway">\n         leeway\n        </a>\n        in response to the direct forcing of winds and waves.\n        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bang_10-0">\n         <a href="#cite_note-Bang-10">\n          [10]\n         </a>\n        </sup>\n       </li>\n       <li>\n        The space buoy is a common element in science fiction that refers to a stationary object in\n        <a href="/wiki/Outer_space" title="Outer space">\n         outer space\n        </a>\n        that provides navigation data or warnings about that particular area.\n       </li>\n       <li>\n        Buoy racing is the most prevalent form of\n        <a href="/wiki/Yacht_racing" title="Yacht racing">\n         yacht racing\n        </a>\n       </li>\n      </ul>\n      <h2>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Other_uses">\n        Other uses\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Buoy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Other uses">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h2>\n      <ul>\n       <li>\n        The word "buoyed" can also be used figuratively.
