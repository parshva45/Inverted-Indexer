 <div class="magnify">\n          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Endeavour_silhouette_STS-130.jpg" title="Enlarge">\n          </a>\n         </div>\n         <a href="/wiki/Space_Shuttle" It contains approximately 75% of the\n       <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere" title="Atmosphere">\n        atmosphere\n       </a>\n       \'s\n       <a href="/wiki/Mass" title="Mass">\n        mass\n       </a>\n       and 99% of the total mass of\n       <a href="/wiki/Water_vapor" title="Water vapor">\n        water vapor\n       </a>\n       and\n       <a href="/wiki/Aerosol" title="Aerosol">\n        aerosols\n       </a>\n       .\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">\n        <a href="#cite_note-2">\n         [2]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       The average depths of the troposphere are 20\xc2\xa0km (12\xc2\xa0mi) in the\n       <a href="/wiki/Tropics" title="Tropics">\n        tropics\n       </a>\n       , 17\xc2\xa0km (11\xc2\xa0mi) in the mid latitudes, and 7\xc2\xa0km (4.3\xc2\xa0mi) in the\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Polar_regions" title="Polar regions">\n        polar regions\n       </a>\n       in winter. The lowest part of the troposphere, where\n       <a href="/wiki/Friction" title="Friction">\n        friction\n       </a>\n       with the Earth\'s surface influences air flow, is the\n       <a href="/wiki/Planetary_boundary_layer" title="Planetary boundary layer">\n        planetary boundary layer\n       </a>\n       . This layer is typically a few hundred meters to 2\xc2\xa0km (1.2\xc2\xa0mi) deep depending on the\n       <a href="/wiki/Landform" title="Landform">\n        landform\n       </a>\n       and time of day. Atop the troposphere is the\n       <a href="/wiki/Tropopause" title="Tropopause">\n        tropopause\n       </a>\n       , which is the border between the troposphere and\n       <a href="/wiki/Stratosphere" title="Stratosphere">\n        stratosphere\n       </a>\n       . The tropopause is an\n       <a href="/wiki/Inversion_(meteorology)" title="Inversion (meteorology)">\n        inversion layer\n       </a>\n       , where the air temperature ceases to decrease with height and remains constant through its thickness.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DLA_3-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-DLA-3">\n         [3]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       The word\n       <i>\n        troposphere\n       </i>\n       derives from the\n       <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">\n        Greek\n       </a>\n       :\n       <span lang="el" xml:lang="el">\n        <i>\n         tropos\n        </i>\n       </span>\n       for "turn, turn toward, trope" and "-sphere" (as in, the Earth), reflecting the fact that rotational\n       <a href="/wiki/Turbulence" title="Turbulence">\n        turbulent\n       </a>\n       mixing plays an important role in the troposphere\'s structure and behaviour. Most of the phenomena associated with day-to-day weather occur in the troposphere.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DLA_3-1">\n        <a href="#cite_note-DLA-3">\n         [3]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <div class="thumb tright">\n       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:82px;">\n        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:EarthAtmosphereBig.jpg">\n         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2500" data-file-width="161" height="1242" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/9a/EarthAtmosphereBig.jpg/80px-EarthAtmosphereBig.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/9a/EarthAtmosphereBig.jpg/120px-EarthAtmosphereBig.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/9a/EarthAtmosphereBig.jpg/160px-EarthAtmosphereBig.jpg 2x" width="80"/>\n        </a>\n        <div class="thumbcaption">\n         <div class="magnify">\n          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:EarthAtmosphereBig.jpg" title="Enlarge">\n          </a>\n         </div>\n         Diagram showing the five primary layers of the Earth\'s atmosphere:\n         <a href="/wiki/Exosphere" title="Exosphere">\n          exosphere\n         </a>\n         ,\n         <a href="/wiki/Thermosphere" title="Thermosphere">\n          thermosphere\n         </a>\n         ,\n         <a href="/wiki/Mesosphere" title="Mesosphere">\n          mesosphere\n         </a>\n         ,\n         <a href="/wiki/Stratosphere" title="Stratosphere">\n          stratosphere\n         </a>\n         , and troposphere. Air also contains a variable amount of\n       <a href="/wiki/Water_vapor" title="Water vapor">\n        water vapor\n       </a>\n       . Except for the water vapor content, the composition of the troposphere is essentially uniform. The source of water vapor is at the Earth\'s surface through the process of\n       <a href="/wiki/Evaporation" title="Evaporation">\n        evaporation\n       </a>\n       . The temperature of the troposphere decreases with\n       <a href="/wiki/Altitude" title="Altitude">\n        altitude\n       </a>\n       . And,\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saturation_vapor_pressure" title="Saturation vapor pressure">\n        saturation vapor pressure\n       </a>\n       decreases strongly as temperature drops. The ELR is nothing more than the difference in temperature between the surface and the tropopause divided by the height. The ELR assumes that the air is perfectly still, i.e. that there is no mixing of the layers of air from vertical convection, nor winds that would create turbulence and hence mixing of the layers of air. The reason for this temperature difference is that the ground absorbs most of the sun\'s energy, which then heats the lower levels of the atmosphere with which it is in contact. The dry adiabatic lapse rate accounts for the effect of the expansion of dry air as it rises in the atmosphere and wet adiabatic lapse rates includes the effect of the condensation of water vapor on the lapse rate.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       When a parcel of air rises, it expands, because the pressure is lower at higher altitudes. As the air parcel expands, it pushes the surrounding air outward, transferring energy in the form of\n       <a href="/wiki/Work_(thermodynamics)" title="Work (thermodynamics)">\n        work\n       </a>\n       from that parcel to the atmosphere. As energy transfer to a parcel of air by way of heat is very slow, it is assumed to not exchange energy by way of\n       <a href="/wiki/Heat" title="Heat">\n        heat\n       </a>\n       with the environment. Such a process is called an\n       <a href="/wiki/Adiabatic_process" title="Adiabatic process">\n        adiabatic\n       </a>\n       process (no energy transfer by way of heat). Since the rising parcel of air is losing energy as it does work on the surrounding atmosphere and no energy is transferred into it as heat from the atmosphere to make up for the loss, the parcel of air is losing energy, which manifests itself as a decrease in the temperature of the air parcel.    </a>\n       , then cooling of the air can cause the water to condense, and the behavior is no longer that of an ideal gas. If the air is at the\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saturation_vapor_pressure" title="Saturation vapor pressure">\n        saturated vapor pressure\n       </a>\n       , then the rate at which temperature drops with height is called the\n       <a href="/wiki/Lapse_rate#Saturated_adiabatic_lapse_rate" title="Lapse rate">\n        saturated adiabatic lapse rate\n       </a>\n       . More generally, the actual rate at which the temperature drops with altitude is called the\n       <a href="/wiki/Lapse_rate#Environmental_lapse_rate" title="Lapse rate">\n        environmental lapse rate\n       </a>\n       . In the troposphere, the average environmental lapse rate is a drop of about 6.5\xc2\xa0\xc2\xb0C for every 1\xc2\xa0km (1,000 meters) in increased height.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DLA_3-3">\n        <a href="#cite_note-DLA-3">\n         [3]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       The environmental lapse rate (the actual rate at which temperature drops with height,\n       <span class="mwe-math-element">\n        <span class="mwe-math-mathml-inline mwe-math-mathml-a11y" style="display: none;">\n         <math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">\n          <semantics>\n           <mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">\n            <mstyle displaystyle="true" scriptlevel="0">\n             <mi>\n              d\n             </mi>\n             <mi>\n              T\n             </mi>\n             <mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">\n              <mo>\n               /\n              </mo>\n             </mrow>\n             <mi>\n              d\n             </mi>\n             <mi>\n              z\n             </mi>\n            </mstyle>\n           </mrow>\n           <annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\n            {\\displaystyle dT/dz}\n           </annotation>\n          </semantics>\n         </math>\n        </span>\n        <img alt="dT/dz" aria-hidden="true" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline" src="https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/66bc8e6b877b7cf93d634e47fbe4a2c02cea45e4" style="vertical-align: -0.838ex; width:6.371ex; height:2.843ex;"/>\n       </span>\n       ) is not usually equal to the adiabatic lapse rate (or correspondingly,\n       <span class="mwe-math-element">\n        <span class="mwe-math-mathml-inline mwe-math-mathml-a11y" style="display: none;">\n         <math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">\n          <semantics>\n           <mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">\n            <mstyle displaystyle="true" scriptlevel="0">\n             <mi>\n              d\n             </mi>\n             <mi>\n              S\n             </mi>\n             <mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">\n              <mo>\n               /\n              </mo>\n             </mrow>\n             <mi>\n              d\n             </mi>\n             <mi>\n              z\n             </mi>\n             <mo>\n              \xe2\x89\xa0\n              <!-- \xe2\x89\xa0 -->\n             </mo>\n             <mn>\n              0\n             </mn>\n            </mstyle>\n           </mrow>\n           <annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\n            {\\displaystyle dS/dz\\neq 0}\n           </annotation>\n          </semantics>\n         </math>\n        </span>\n        <img alt="dS/dz\\neq 0" aria-hidden="true" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline" src="https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5ae0d2a4d84dedcffc9b9aba41a5528bbc2c5fdd" style="vertical-align: -0.838ex; width:10.516ex; height:2.843ex;"/>\n       </span>\n       ). If the upper air is warmer than predicted by the adiabatic lapse rate (\n       <span class="mwe-math-element">\n        <span class="mwe-math-mathml-inline mwe-math-mathml-a11y" style="display: none;">\n         <math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">\n          <semantics>\n           <mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">\n            <mstyle displaystyle="true" scriptlevel="0">\n             <mi>\n              d\n             </mi>\n             <mi>\n              S\n             </mi>\n             <mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">\n              <mo>\n               /\n              </mo>\n             </mrow>\n             <mi>\n              d\n             </mi>\n             <mi>\n              z\n             </mi>\n             <mo>\n              &gt;\n             </mo>\n             <mn>\n              0\n             </mn>\n            </mstyle>\n           </mrow>\n           <annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\n            {\\displaystyle dS/dz&gt;0}\n           </annotation>\n          </semantics>\n         </math>\n        </span>\n        <img alt="dS/dz&gt;0" aria-hidden="true" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline" src="https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5015c1f973a632f4534085c97777371bb2849239" style="vertical-align: -0.838ex; width:10.516ex; height:2.843ex;"/>\n       </span>\n       ), then when a parcel of air rises and expands, it will arrive at the new height at a lower temperature than its surroundings. In this case, the air parcel is denser than its surroundings, so it sinks back to its original height, and the air is stable against being lifted. If, on the contrary, the upper air is cooler than predicted by the adiabatic lapse rate, then when the air parcel rises to its new height it will have a higher temperature and a lower density than its surroundings, and will continue to accelerate upward.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DLA_3-4">\n        <a href="#cite_note-DLA-3">\n         [3]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LL_4-1">\n        <a href="#cite_note-LL-4">\n         [4]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       The troposphere is heated from below by\n       <a href="/wiki/Latent_heat" title="Latent heat">\n        latent heat\n       </a>\n       ,\n       <a href="/wiki/Outgoing_longwave_radiation" title="Outgoing longwave radiation">\n        longwave radiation\n       </a>\n       , and\n       <a href="/wiki/Sensible_heat" title="Sensible heat">\n        sensible heat\n       </a>\n       . Surplus heating and vertical expansion of the troposphere occurs in the tropics. At middle latitudes, tropospheric temperatures decrease from an average of 15\xc2\xa0\xc2\xb0C at sea level to about \xe2\x88\x9255\xc2\xa0\xc2\xb0C at the\n       <a href="/wiki/Tropopause" title="Tropopause">\n        tropopause\n       </a>\n       . At the\n       <a href="/wiki/Geographical_pole" title="Geographical pole">\n        poles\n       </a>\n       , tropospheric temperature only decreases from an average of 0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xb0C at sea level to about \xe2\x88\x9245\xc2\xa0\xc2\xb0C at the tropopause. At the\n       <a href="/wiki/Equator" title="Equator">\n        equator\n       </a>\n       , tropospheric temperatures decrease from an average of 20\xc2\xa0\xc2\xb0C at sea level to about \xe2\x88\x9270 to \xe2\x88\x9275\xc2\xa0\xc2\xb0C at the tropopause. The troposphere is thinner at the poles and thicker at the equator. The average thickness of the tropical tropopause is roughly 7 kilometers greater than the average tropopause thickness at the poles.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ZonalFlowDef_7-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-ZonalFlowDef-7">\n         [7]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <h3>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Tropopause">\n        Tropopause\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Troposphere&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Tropopause">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h3>\n      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">\n       Main article:\n       <a href="/wiki/Tropopause" title="Tropopause">\n        Tropopause\n       </a>\n      </div>\n      <p>\n       The tropopause is the boundary region between the troposphere and the\n       <a href="/wiki/Stratosphere" title="Stratosphere">\n        stratosphere\n       </a>\n       .\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       Measuring the temperature change with height through the troposphere and the stratosphere identifies the location of the tropopause. In the troposphere, temperature decreases with altitude. In the stratosphere, however, the temperature remains constant for a while and then increases with altitude. The region of the atmosphere where the lapse rate changes from positive (in the troposphere) to negative (in the stratosphere), is defined as the tropopause.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DLA_3-5">\n        <a href="#cite_note-DLA-3">\n         [3]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       Thus, the tropopause is an\n       <a href="/wiki/Inversion_(meteorology)" title="Inversion (meteorology)">\n        inversion layer\n       </a>\n       , and there is little mixing between the two layers of the atmosphere.\n      </p>\n      <h2>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Atmospheric_flow">\n        Atmospheric flow\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Troposphere&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Atmospheric flow">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h2>\n      <p>\n       The flow of the atmosphere generally moves in a west to east direction. This, however, can often become interrupted, creating a more north to south or south to north flow. These scenarios are often described in meteorology as zonal or meridional. These terms, however, tend to be used in reference to localised areas of atmosphere (at a\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Synoptic_scale" title="Synoptic scale">\n        synoptic scale\n       </a>\n       ). A fuller explanation of the flow of atmosphere around the Earth as a whole can be found in the three-cell model.\n      </p>\n      <h3>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Zonal_flow">\n        Zonal flow\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Troposphere&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Zonal flow">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h3>\n      <div class="thumb tright">\n       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;">\n        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Zonalflow.gif">\n         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="265" data-file-width="413" height="160" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0c/Zonalflow.gif/250px-Zonalflow.gif" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0c/Zonalflow.gif/375px-Zonalflow.gif 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/0/0c/Zonalflow.gif 2x" width="250"/>\n        </a>\n        <div class="thumbcaption">\n         <div class="magnify">\n          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Zonalflow.gif" title="Enlarge">\n          </a>\n         </div>\n         A\n         <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Zonal_flow_regime" title="Zonal flow regime">\n          zonal flow regime\n         </a>\n         . Note the dominant west-to-east flow as shown in the 500 hPa height pattern.\n        </div>\n       </div>\n      </div>\n      <p>\n       A\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Zonal_flow_regime" title="Zonal flow regime">\n        zonal flow regime\n       </a>\n       is the\n       <a href="/wiki/Meteorology" title="Meteorology">\n        meteorological\n       </a>\n       term meaning that the general flow pattern is west to east along the Earth\'s latitude lines, with weak shortwaves embedded in the flow.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ZonalFlowDef2_8-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-ZonalFlowDef2-8">\n         [8]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       The use of the word "zone" refers to the flow being along the Earth\'s latitudinal "zones". This pattern can buckle and thus become a meridional flow.\n      </p>\n      <h3>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Meridional_flow">\n        Meridional flow\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Troposphere&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Meridional flow">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h3>\n      <div class="thumb tright">\n       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;">\n        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Meridionalflowpattern.jpg">\n         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="475" data-file-width="500" height="238" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4c/Meridionalflowpattern.jpg/250px-Meridionalflowpattern.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4c/Meridionalflowpattern.jpg/375px-Meridionalflowpattern.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/4/4c/Meridionalflowpattern.jpg 2x" width="250"/>\n        </a>\n        <div class="thumbcaption">\n         <div class="magnify">\n          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Meridionalflowpattern.jpg" title="Enlarge">\n          </a>\n         </div>\n         Meridional Flow pattern of October 23, 2003. Note the amplified troughs and ridges in this 500 hPa height pattern.\n        </div>\n       </div>\n      </div>\n      <p>\n       When the zonal flow buckles, the atmosphere can flow in a more longitudinal (or meridional) direction, and thus the term "\n       <a href="/wiki/Meridional_flow" title="Meridional flow">\n        meridional flow\n       </a>\n       " arises. Meridional flow patterns feature strong, amplified troughs of low pressure and ridges of high pressure, with more north-south flow in the general pattern than west-to-east flow.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MeridonialFlow_9-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-MeridonialFlow-9">\n         [9]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <h3>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Three-cell_model">\n        Three-cell model\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Troposphere&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Three-cell model">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h3>\n      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">\n       Main article:\n       <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_circulation" title="Atmospheric circulation">\n        Atmospheric circulation\n       </a>\n      </div>\n      <div class="thumb tright">\n       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;">\n        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:AtmosphCirc2.png">\n         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="507" data-file-width="576" height="176" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cd/AtmosphCirc2.png/200px-AtmosphCirc2.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cd/AtmosphCirc2.png/300px-AtmosphCirc2.png         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:AtmosphCirc2.png" title="Enlarge">\n          </a>\n         </div>\n         Atmospheric circulation shown with three large cells.\n        </div>\n       </div>\n      </div>\n      <p>\n       The three cells model of the atmosphere attempts to describe the actual flow of the Earth\'s atmosphere as a whole. It divides the Earth into the tropical (\n       <a href="/wiki/Hadley_cell" title="Hadley cell">\n        Hadley cell\n       </a>\n       ), mid latitude (\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ferrel_cell" title="Ferrel cell">\n        Ferrel cell\n       </a>\n       ), and polar (\n       <a href="/wiki/Polar_vortex" title="Polar vortex">\n        polar cell\n       </a>\n       ) regions, to describe energy flow and global atmospheric circulation (mass flow). Its fundamental principle is that of balance \xe2\x80\x93 the energy that the Earth absorbs from the sun each year is equal to that which it loses to space by radiation. This overall Earth energy balance, however, does not apply in each latitude due to the varying strength of the sun in each "cell" as a result of the tilt of the Earth\'s axis in relation to its orbit. The result is a circulation of the atmosphere that transports warm air poleward from the tropics and cold air equatorward from the poles. The effect of the three cells is the tendency to even out the heat and moisture in the Earth\'s atmosphere around the planet\n       <b>\n        .\n        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ThreecellEncarta_10-0">\n         <a href="#cite_note-ThreecellEncarta-10">\n          [10]\n         </a>\n        </sup>\n       </b>\n      </p>\n      <h2>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Synoptic_scale_observations_and_concepts">\n        Synoptic scale observations and concepts\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Troposphere&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Synoptic scale observations and concepts">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h2>\n      <h3>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Forcing">\n        Forcing\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Troposphere&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Forcing">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h3>\n      <p>\n       Forcing is a term used by meteorologists to describe the situation where a change or an event in one part of the atmosphere causes a strengthening change in another part of the atmosphere. It is usually used to describe connections between upper, middle or lower levels (such as upper-level divergence causing lower level convergence in cyclone formation), but also be to describe such connections over lateral distance rather than height alone. In some respects,\n       <a href="/wiki/Teleconnection" title="Teleconnection">\n        teleconnections\n       </a>\n       could be considered a type of forcing.\n      </p>\n      <h3>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Divergence_and_convergence">\n        Divergence and convergence\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Troposphere&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Divergence and convergence">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h3>\n      <p>\n       An area of convergence is one in which the total mass of air is increasing with time, resulting in an increase in pressure at locations below the convergence level (recall that atmospheric pressure is just the total weight of air above a given point). Divergence is the opposite of convergence \xe2\x80\x93 an area where the total mass of air is decreasing with time, resulting in falling pressure in regions below the area of divergence. Where divergence is occurring in the upper atmosphere, there will be air coming in to try to balance the net loss of mass (this is called the principle of mass conservation), and there is a resulting upward motion (positive vertical velocity). Another way to state this is to say that regions of upper air divergence are conducive to lower level convergence, cyclone formation, and positive vertical velocity. Therefore, identifying regions of upper air divergence is an important step in forecasting the formation of a surface low pressure area.\n      </p>\n      <h2>\n       <span class="mw-headline"
