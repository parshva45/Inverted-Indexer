The deep blue of the rivers is spread across the countryside in the flood image.\n        </div>\n       </div>\n      </div>\n      <p>\n       A\n       <b>\n        flood\n       </b>\n       is an overflow of water that submerges land that is usually dry.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">\n        <a href="#cite_note-1">\n         [1]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       The\n       <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">\n        European Union\n       </a>\n       (EU)\n       <a href="/wiki/Floods_Directive" title="Floods Directive">\n        Floods Directive\n       </a>\n       defines a flood as a covering by\n       <a href="/wiki/Water" title="Water">\n        water\n       </a>\n       of land not normally covered by water.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">\n        <a href="#cite_note-2">\n         [2]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       In the sense of "flowing water", the word may also be applied to the inflow of the\n       <a href="/wiki/Tide" title="Tide">\n        tide\n       </a>\n       .\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       Flooding may occur as an overflow of water from water bodies, such as a\n       <a href="/wiki/River" title="River">\n        river\n       </a>\n       ,\n       <a href="/wiki/Lake" title="Lake">\n        lake\n       </a>\n       , or ocean, in which the water overtops or breaks\n       <a href="/wiki/Levee" title="Levee">\n        levees\n       </a>\n       , resulting in some of that water escaping its usual boundaries,\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">\n        <a href="#cite_note-3">\n         [3]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       or it may occur due to an accumulation of rainwater on saturated ground in an areal flood. While the size of a lake or other body of water will vary with seasonal changes in\n       <a href="/wiki/Precipitation" title="Precipitation">\n        precipitation\n       </a>\n       and snow melt, these changes in size are unlikely to be considered significant unless they flood\n       <a href="/wiki/Property" title="Property">\n        property\n       </a>\n       or\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Drown" title="Drown">\n        drown\n       </a>\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Domestic_animals" title="Domestic animals">\n        domestic animals\n       </a>\n       .\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       Floods can also occur in rivers when the flow rate exceeds the capacity of the\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/River_channel" title="River channel">\n        river channel\n       </a>\n       , particularly at bends or\n       <a href="/wiki/Meander" title="Meander">\n        meanders\n       </a>\n       in the\n       <a href="/wiki/Waterway" title="Waterway">\n        waterway\n       </a>\n       . Floods often cause damage to homes and businesses if they are in the natural flood plains of rivers. While riverine flood damage can be eliminated by moving away from rivers and other bodies of water, people have traditionally lived and worked by rivers because the land is usually flat and\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fertility_(soil)" title="Fertility (soil)">\n        fertile\n       </a>\n       and because rivers provide easy travel and access to commerce and industry.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       Some floods develop slowly, while others such as\n       <a href="/wiki/Flash_flood" title="Flash flood">\n        flash floods\n       </a>\n       , can develop in just a few minutes and without visible signs of rain. Additionally, floods can be local, impacting a neighborhood or community, or very large, affecting entire river basins.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n      </p>\n      <div class="toc" id="toc">\n id="Etymology">\n        Etymology\n       </span>\n      </h2>\n      <p>\n       The word "flood" comes from the\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Old_English_language" title="Old English language">\n        Old English\n       </a>\n       <i>\n        flod\n       </i>\n       , a word common to\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Germanic_language" title="Germanic language">\n        Germanic languages\n       </a>\n       (compare\n       <a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">\n        German\n       </a>\n       <i>\n        Flut\n       </i>\n       ,\n       <a href="/wiki/Dutch_language" title="Dutch language">\n        Dutch\n       </a>\n       <i>\n        vloed\n       </i>\n       from the same root as is seen in\n       <i>\n        flow, float\n       </i>\n       ; also compare with\n       <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">\n        Latin\n       </a>\n       <i>\n        fluctus\n       </i>\n       ,\n       <i>\n        flumen\n       </i>\n       ).\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Deluge_myth" title="Deluge myth">\n        Deluge myths\n       </a>\n       are\n       <a href="/wiki/Mythology" title="Mythology">\n        mythical\n       </a>\n       stories of a great flood sent by a\n       <a href="/wiki/Deity" title="Deity">\n        deity\n       </a>\n       or deities to destroy civilization as an act of\n       <a href="/wiki/Divine_retribution" title="Divine retribution">\n        divine retribution\n       </a>\n       , and they are featured in the mythology of many cultures.\n      </p>\n      <h2>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Principal_types">\n        Principal types\n       </span>\n      </h2>\n      <h3>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Areal">\n        Areal\n       </span>\n      </h3>\n      <p>\n       Floods can happen on flat or low-lying areas when water is supplied by rainfall or snowmelt more rapidly than it can either\n       <a href="/wiki/Infiltration_(hydrology)" title="Infiltration (hydrology)">\n        infiltrate\n       </a>\n       or\n       <a href="/wiki/Surface_runoff" title="Surface runoff">\n        run off\n       </a>\n       . The excess accumulates in place, sometimes to hazardous depths. Surface\n       <a href="/wiki/Soil" title="Soil">\n        soil\n       </a>\n       can become saturated, which effectively stops infiltration, where the\n       <a href="/wiki/Water_table" title="Water table">\n        water table\n       </a>\n       is shallow, such as a\n       <a href="/wiki/Floodplain" title="Floodplain">\n        floodplain\n       </a>\n       , or from intense rain from one or a\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Storm_train" title="Storm train">\n        series of storms\n       </a>\n       . Infiltration also is slow to negligible through frozen ground, rock,\n       <a href="/wiki/Concrete" title="Concrete">\n        concrete\n       </a>\n       , paving, or roofs. Areal flooding begins in flat areas like floodplains and in local depressions not connected to a stream channel, because the velocity of\n       <a href="/wiki/Surface_runoff" title="Surface runoff">\n        overland flow\n       </a>\n       depends on the surface slope.\n       <a href="/wiki/Endorheic_basin" title="Endorheic basin">\n        Endorheic basins\n       </a>\n       may experience areal flooding during periods when precipitation exceeds evaporation.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">\n        <a href="#cite_note-4">\n         [4]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <h3>\n       <span id="Riverine_(Channel)">\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Riverine_.28Channel.29">\n        Riverine (Channel)\n       </span>\n      </h3>\n      <p>\n       Floods occur in all types of\n       <a href="/wiki/River" title="River">\n        river\n       </a>\n       and\n       <a href="/wiki/Stream" title="Stream">\n        stream\n       </a>\n       channels, from the smallest\n       <a href="/wiki/Stream#Intermittent_and_ephemeral_streams" title="Stream">\n        ephemeral streams\n       </a>\n       in humid zones to\n       <a href="/wiki/Arroyo_(creek)" title="Arroyo (creek)">\n        normally-dry channels\n       </a>\n       in arid climates to the\n       <a href="/wiki/Amazon_River" title="Amazon River">\n        world\'s largest\n       </a>\n       rivers. When overland flow occurs on tilled fields, it can result in a\n       <a href="/wiki/Muddy_flood" title="Muddy flood">\n        muddy flood\n       </a>\n       where\n       <a href="/wiki/Sediment" title="Sediment">\n        sediments\n       </a>\n       are\n       <a href="/wiki/Erosion" title="Erosion">\n        picked up by run off\n       </a>\n       and carried as suspended matter or\n       <a href="/wiki/Bed_load" title="Bed load">\n        bed load\n       </a>\n       . Localized flooding may be caused or exacerbated by drainage obstructions such as\n       <a href="/wiki/Landslide" title="Landslide">\n        landslides\n       </a>\n       ,\n       <a href="/wiki/Ice_dam" title="Ice dam">\n        ice\n       </a>\n       ,\n       <a href="/wiki/Debris" title="Debris">\n        debris\n       </a>\n       , or\n       <a href="/wiki/Beaver" title="Beaver">\n        beaver\n       </a>\n       dams.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       Slow-rising floods most commonly occur in large rivers with large\n       <a href="/wiki/Drainage_basin" title="Drainage basin">\n        catchment areas\n       </a>\n       . The increase in flow may be the result of sustained rainfall, rapid snow melt,\n       <a href="/wiki/Monsoon" title="Monsoon">\n        monsoons\n       </a>\n       , or\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclones" title="Tropical cyclones">\n        tropical cyclones\n       </a>\n       . However, large rivers may have rapid flooding events in areas with dry climate, since they may have large basins but small river channels and rainfall can be very intense in smaller areas of those basins.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       Rapid flooding events, including\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Flash_floods" title="Flash floods">\n        flash floods\n       </a>\n       , more often occur on smaller rivers, rivers with steep valleys, rivers that flow for much of their length over impermeable terrain, or normally-dry channels. The cause may be localized\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Convective_precipitation" title="Convective precipitation">\n        convective precipitation\n       </a>\n       (intense\n       <a href="/wiki/Thunderstorm" title="Thunderstorm">\n        thunderstorms\n       </a>\n       ) or sudden release from an upstream impoundment created behind a\n       <a href="/wiki/Dam" title="Dam">\n        dam\n       </a>\n       , landslide, or\n       <a href="/wiki/Glacier" title="Glacier">\n        glacier\n       </a>\n       . In one instance, a flash flood killed eight people enjoying the water on a Sunday afternoon at a popular waterfall in a narrow canyon. Without any observed rainfall, the flow rate increased from about 50 to 1,500 cubic feet per second (1.4 to 42\xc2\xa0m\n       <sup>\n        3\n       </sup>\n       /s) in just one minute.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hjalmarson_5-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-Hjalmarson-5">\n         [5]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       Two larger floods occurred at the same site within a week, but no one was at the waterfall on those days. The deadly flood resulted from a thunderstorm over part of the drainage basin, where steep, bare rock slopes are common and the thin soil was already saturated.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       Flash floods are the most common flood type in normally-dry channels in arid zones, known as\n       <a href="/wiki/Arroyo_(creek)" title="Arroyo (creek)">\n        arroyos\n       </a>\n       in the southwest United States and many other names elsewhere. In that setting, the first flood water to arrive is depleted as it wets the sandy stream bed. The leading edge of the flood thus advances more slowly than later and higher flows. As a result, the rising limb of the\n       <a href="/wiki/Hydrograph" title="Hydrograph">\n        hydrograph\n       </a>\n       becomes ever quicker as the flood moves downstream, until the flow rate is so great that the depletion by wetting soil becomes insignificant.\n      </p>\n      <h3>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Estuarine_and_coastal">\n        Estuarine and coastal\n       </span>\n      </h3>\n      <p>\n       Flooding in\n       <a href="/wiki/Estuary" title="Estuary">\n        estuaries\n       </a>\n       is commonly caused by a combination of sea tidal surges caused by\n       <a href="/wiki/Wind" title="Wind">\n        winds\n       </a>\n       and low\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Barometric_pressure" title="Barometric pressure">\n        barometric pressure\n       </a>\n       , and they may be exacerbated by high upstream river flow.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       Coastal areas may be flooded by storm events at sea, resulting in waves over-topping defenses or in severe cases by\n       <a href="/wiki/Tsunami" title="Tsunami">\n        tsunami\n       </a>\n       or tropical cyclones. A\n       <a href="/wiki/Storm_surge" title="Storm surge">\n        storm surge\n       </a>\n       , from either a\n       <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone" title="Tropical cyclone">\n        tropical cyclone\n       </a>\n       or an\n       <a href="/wiki/Extratropical_cyclone" title="Extratropical cyclone">\n        extratropical cyclone\n       </a>\n       , falls within this category. Research from the NHC (National Hurricane Center) explains: "Storm surge is an abnormal rise of water generated by a storm, over and above the predicted astronomical tides.\n       <a href="/wiki/Storm_surge" title="Storm surge">\n        Storm surge\n       </a>\n       should not be confused with storm tide, which is defined as the water level rise due to the combination of storm surge and the astronomical tide. This rise in water level can cause extreme flooding in coastal areas particularly when storm surge coincides with normal high tide, resulting in storm tides reaching up to 20 feet or more in some cases."\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">\n        <a href="#cite_note-6">\n         [6]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <h3>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Urban_flooding">\n        Urban flooding\n       </span>\n      </h3>\n      <p>\n       Urban flooding is the inundation of land or property in a\n       <a href="/wiki/Built_environment" title="Built environment">\n        built environment\n       </a>\n       , particularly in more densely populated areas, caused by rainfall overwhelming the capacity of drainage systems, such as\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Storm_sewers" title="Storm sewers">\n        storm sewers\n       </a>\n       . Although sometimes triggered by events such as flash flooding or\n       <a href="/wiki/Snowmelt" title="Snowmelt">\n        snowmelt\n       </a>\n       , urban flooding is a condition, characterized by its repetitive and systemic impacts on communities, that can happen regardless of whether or not affected communities are located within designated floodplains or near any body of water.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cnt_7-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-cnt-7">\n         [7]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       Aside from potential overflow of rivers and lakes, snowmelt,\n       <a href="/wiki/Stormwater" title="Stormwater">\n        stormwater\n       </a>\n       or water released from damaged\n       <a href="/wiki/Water_supply_network#Water_distribution_network" title="Water supply network">\n        water mains\n       </a>\n       may accumulate on property and in public rights-of-way, seep through building walls and floors, or backup into buildings through sewer pipes, toilets and sinks.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       In urban areas, flood effects can be exacerbated by existing paved streets and roads, which increase the speed of flowing water.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       The flood flow in urbanized areas constitutes a hazard to both the population and infrastructure. Some recent catastrophes include the inundations of\n       <a href="/wiki/N%C3%AEmes" title="N\xc3\xaemes">\n        N\xc3\xaemes\n       </a>\n       (France) in 1998 and\n       <a href="/wiki/Vaison-la-Romaine" title="Vaison-la-Romaine">\n        Vaison-la-Romaine\n       </a>\n       (France) in 1992, the flooding of\n       <a href="/wiki/New_Orleans" title="New Orleans">\n        New Orleans\n       </a>\n       (USA) in 2005, and the flooding in\n       <a href="/wiki/Rockhampton" title="Rockhampton">\n        Rockhampton\n       </a>\n       ,\n       <a href="/wiki/Bundaberg" title="Bundaberg">\n        Bundaberg\n       </a>\n       ,\n       <a href="/wiki/Brisbane" title="Brisbane">\n        Brisbane\n       </a>\n       during the 2010\xe2\x80\x932011 summer in\n       <a href="/wiki/Queensland" title="Queensland">\n        Queensland\n       </a>\n       (Australia). Flood flows in urban environments have been studied relatively recently despite many centuries of flood events.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chanson_2011c_8-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-Chanson_2011c-8">\n         [8]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       Some recent research has considered the criteria for safe evacuation of individuals in flooded areas.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chanson_2014_9-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-Chanson_2014-9">\n         [9]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <h3>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Catastrophic">\n        Catastrophic\n       </span>\n      </h3>\n      <p>\n       Catastrophic riverine flooding is usually associated with major infrastructure failures such as the collapse of a dam, but they may also be caused by drainage channel modification from a\n       <a href="/wiki/Landslide" title="Landslide">\n        landslide\n       </a>\n       ,\n       <a href="/wiki/Earthquake" title="Earthquake">\n        earthquake\n       </a>\n       or\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Volcanic_eruption" title="Volcanic eruption">\n        volcanic eruption\n       </a>\n       . Examples include\n       <a href="/wiki/Outburst_flood" title="Outburst flood">\n        outburst floods\n       </a>\n       and\n       <a href="/wiki/Lahar" title="Lahar">\n        lahars\n       </a>\n       .\n       <a href="/wiki/Tsunami" title="Tsunami">\n        Tsunamis\n       </a>\n       can cause catastrophic coastal flooding, most commonly resulting from undersea earthquakes.\n      </p>\n      <h2>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Causes">\n        Causes\n       </span>\n      </h2>\n      <div class="thumb tright">\n       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">\n title="Visakhapatnam">\n          Visakhapatnam\n         </a>\n        </div>\n       </div>\n      </div>\n      <h3>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Upslope_factors">\n        Upslope factors\n       </span>\n      </h3>\n      <p>\n       The amount, location, and timing of water reaching a drainage channel from natural precipitation and controlled or uncontrolled reservoir releases determines the flow at downstream locations. Some precipitation evaporates, some slowly percolates through soil, some may be temporarily sequestered as snow or ice, and some may produce rapid runoff from surfaces including rock, pavement, roofs, and saturated or frozen ground. The fraction of incident precipitation promptly reaching a drainage channel has been observed from nil for light rain on dry, level ground to as high as 170 percent for warm rain on accumulated snow.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">\n        <a href="#cite_note-10">\n         [10]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       Most precipitation records are based on a measured depth of water received within a fixed time interval.\n       <i>\n        Frequency\n       </i>\n       of a precipitation threshold of interest may be determined from the number of measurements exceeding that threshold value within the total time period for which observations are available. Individual data points are converted to\n       <i>\n        intensity\n       </i>\n       by dividing each measured depth by the period of time between observations. This intensity will be less than the actual peak intensity if the\n       <i>\n        duration\n       </i>\n       of the rainfall event was less than the fixed time interval for which measurements are reported. Convective precipitation events (thunderstorms) tend to produce shorter duration storm events than orographic precipitation. Duration, intensity, and frequency of rainfall events are important to flood prediction. Short duration precipitation is more significant to flooding within small drainage basins.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">\n        <a href="#cite_note-11">\n         [11]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       The most important upslope factor in determining flood magnitude is the land area of the watershed upstream of the area of interest. Rainfall intensity is the second most important factor for watersheds of less than approximately 30 square miles or 80 square kilometres. The main channel slope is the second most important factor for larger watersheds. Channel slope and rainfall intensity become the third most important factors for small and large watersheds, respectively.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Simon_12-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-Simon-12">\n         [12]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Time_of_Concentration" title="Time of Concentration">\n        Time of Concentration\n       </a>\n       is the time required for runoff from the most distant point of the upstream drainage area to reach the point of the drainage channel controlling flooding of the area of interest. The time of concentration defines the critical duration of peak rainfall for the area of interest.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Urquhart_13-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-Urquhart-13">\n         [13]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       The critical duration of intense rainfall might be only a few minutes for roof and parking lot drainage structures, while cumulative rainfall over several days would be critical for river basins.\n      </p>\n      <h3>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Downslope_factors">\n        Downslope factors\n       </span>\n      </h3>\n      <p>\n       Water flowing downhill ultimately encounters downstream conditions slowing movement. The final limitation is often the\n       <a href="/wiki/Ocean" title="Ocean">\n        ocean\n       </a>\n       or a natural or artificial\n       <a href="/wiki/Lake" title="Lake">\n        lake\n       </a>\n       . Elevation changes such as tidal fluctuations are significant determinants of coastal and estuarine flooding. Less predictable events like tsunamis and storm surges may also cause elevation changes in large bodies of water. Elevation of flowing water is controlled by the geometry of the flow channel.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Simon_12-1">\n        <a href="#cite_note-Simon-12">\n         [12]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       Flow channel restrictions like bridges and canyons tend to control water elevation above the restriction. The actual control point for any given reach of the drainage may change with changing water elevation, so a closer point may control for lower water levels until a more distant point controls at higher water levels.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       Effective flood channel geometry may be changed by growth of vegetation, accumulation of ice or debris, or construction of bridges, buildings, or levees within the flood channel.\n      </p>\n      <h3>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Coincidence">\n        Coincidence\n       </span>\n      </h3>\n      <p>\n       Extreme flood events often result from coincidence such as unusually intense, warm rainfall melting heavy snow pack, producing channel obstructions from floating ice, and releasing small impoundments like\n       <a href="/wiki/Beaver" title="Beaver">\n        beaver\n       </a>\n       dams.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Abbett_14-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-Abbett-14">\n         [14]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       Coincident events may cause extensive flooding to be more frequent than anticipated from\n       <a href="/wiki/100-year_flood" title="100-year flood">\n        simplistic statistical prediction models\n       </a>\n       considering only precipitation runoff flowing within unobstructed drainage channels.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BR_15-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-BR-15">\n         [15]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       Debris modification of channel geometry is common when heavy flows move uprooted woody vegetation and flood-damaged structures and vehicles, including boats and\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Railway" title="Railway">\n        railway\n       </a>\n       equipment. Recent field measurements during the\n       <a href="/wiki/2010%E2%80%9311_Queensland_floods" title="2010\xe2\x80\x9311 Queensland floods">\n        2010\xe2\x80\x9311 Queensland floods\n       </a>\n       showed that any criterion solely based upon the flow velocity, water depth or specific momentum cannot account for the hazards caused by velocity and water depth fluctuations.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chanson_2011c_8-1">\n        <a href="#cite_note-Chanson_2011c-8">\n         [8]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       These considerations ignore further the risks associated with large debris entrained by the flow motion.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chanson_2014_9-1">\n        <a href="#cite_note-Chanson_2014-9">\n         [9]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       Some researchers have mentioned the storage effect in urban areas with transportation corridors created by\n       <a href="/wiki/Cut_and_fill" title="Cut and fill">\n        cut and fill\n       </a>\n       . Culverted fills may be converted to impoundments if the\n       <a href="/wiki/Culvert" title="Culvert">\n        culverts\n       </a>\n       become blocked by debris, and flow may be diverted along streets. Several studies have looked into the flow patterns and redistribution in streets during storm events and the implication on flood modelling.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">\n        <a href="#cite_note-16">\n         [16]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <h2>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Effects">\n        Effects\n       </span>\n      </h2>\n      <h3>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Primary_effects">\n        Primary effects\n       </span>\n      </h3>\n      <p>\n       The primary effects of flooding include\n       <a href="/wiki/Death" title="Death">\n        loss of life\n       </a>\n       , damage to buildings and other structures, including bridges,\n       <a href="/wiki/Sewerage" title="Sewerage">\n        sewerage\n       </a>\n       systems, roadways, and canals.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       Floods also frequently damage\n       <a href="/wiki/Power_transmission" title="Power transmission">\n        power transmission\n       </a>\n       and sometimes\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Power_generation" title="Power generation">\n        power generation\n       </a>\n       , which then has\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Knock-on_effect" title="Knock-on effect">\n        knock-on effects\n       </a>\n       caused by the loss of power. This includes loss of drinking\n       <a href="/wiki/Water_treatment" title="Water treatment">\n        water treatment\n       </a>\n       and water supply, which may result in loss of drinking water or severe water contamination. It may also cause the loss of sewage disposal facilities. Lack of clean water combined with\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Human_sewage" title="Human sewage">\n        human sewage\n       </a>\n       in the flood waters raises the risk of\n       <a href="/wiki/Waterborne_diseases" title="Waterborne diseases">\n        waterborne diseases\n       </a>\n       , which can include\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Typhoid" title="Typhoid">\n        typhoid\n       </a>\n       ,\n       <a href="/wiki/Giardia" title="Giardia">\n        giardia\n       </a>\n       ,\n       <a href="/wiki/Cryptosporidium" title="Cryptosporidium">\n        cryptosporidium\n       </a>\n       ,\n       <a href="/wiki/Cholera" title="Cholera">\n        cholera\n       </a>\n       and many other diseases depending upon the location of the flood.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       Damage to roads and transport infrastructure may make it difficult to mobilize aid to those affected or to provide emergency health treatment.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       Flood waters typically inundate farm land, making the land unworkable and preventing crops from being planted or harvested, which can lead to shortages of food both for humans and farm animals. Entire harvests for a country can be lost in extreme flood circumstances. Some tree species may not survive prolonged flooding of their root systems.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17">\n        <a href="#cite_note-17">\n         [17]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <h3>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Secondary_and_long-term_effects">\n        Secondary and long-term effects\n       </span>\n      </h3>\n      <p>\n       Economic hardship due to a temporary decline in tourism, rebuilding costs, or food shortages leading to price increases is a common after-effect of severe flooding. The impact on those affected may cause psychological damage to those affected, in particular where deaths, serious injuries and loss of property occur.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       Urban flooding can lead to chronically wet houses, which are linked to an increase in\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Respiratory" title="Respiratory">\n        respiratory\n       </a>\n       problems and other illnesses.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18">\n        <a href="#cite_note-18">\n         [18]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       Urban flooding also has significant economic implications for affected neighborhoods. In the\n       <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">\n        United States\n       </a>\n       , industry experts estimate that wet basements can lower property values by 10\xe2\x80\x9325 percent and are cited among the top reasons for not purchasing a home.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19">\n        <a href="#cite_note-19">\n         [19]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       According to the U.S.\n       <a href="/wiki/Federal_Emergency_Management_Agency" title="Federal Emergency Management Agency">\n        Federal Emergency Management Agency\n       </a>\n       (FEMA), almost 40 percent of small businesses never reopen their doors following a flooding disaster.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20">\n        <a href="#cite_note-20">\n         [20]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       In the United States,\n       <a href="/wiki/National_Flood_Insurance_Program" title="National Flood Insurance Program">\n        insurance\n       </a>\n       is available against flood damage to both homes and businesses.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NFIP_21-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-NFIP-21">\n         [21]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <h3>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Benefits">\n        Benefits\n       </span>\n      </h3>\n      <p>\n       Floods (in particular more frequent or smaller floods) can also bring many benefits, such as recharging\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ground_water" title="Ground water">\n        ground water\n       </a>\n       , making soil more\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fertility_(soil)" title="Fertility (soil)">\n        fertile\n       </a>\n       and increasing\n       <a href="/wiki/Nutrient" title="Nutrient">\n        nutrients\n       </a>\n       in some soils. Flood waters provide much needed water resources in\n       <a href="/wiki/Arid" title="Arid">\n        arid\n       </a>\n       and\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Semi-arid" title="Semi-arid">\n        semi-arid\n       </a>\n       regions where precipitation can be very unevenly distributed throughout the year and kills pests in the farming land. Freshwater floods particularly play an important role in maintaining\n       <a href="/wiki/Ecosystem" title="Ecosystem">\n        ecosystems\n       </a>\n       in river corridors and are a key factor in maintaining floodplain\n       <a href="/wiki/Biodiversity" title="Biodiversity">\n        biodiversity\n       </a>\n       .\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22">\n        <a href="#cite_note-22">\n         [22]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       Flooding can spread nutrients to lakes and rivers, which can lead to increased\n       <a href="/wiki/Biomass" title="Biomass">\n        biomass\n       </a>\n       and improved\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fisheries" title="Fisheries">\n        fisheries\n       </a>\n       for a few years.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       For some fish species, an inundated floodplain may form a highly suitable location for\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spawning" title="Spawning">\n        spawning\n       </a>\n       with few predators and enhanced levels of nutrients or food.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23">\n        <a href="#cite_note-23">\n         [23]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       Fish, such as the\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Weather_fish" title="Weather fish">\n        weather fish\n       </a>\n       , make use of floods in order to reach new habitats. Bird populations may also profit from the boost in food production caused by flooding.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24">\n        <a href="#cite_note-24">\n         [24]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       Periodic flooding was essential to the well-being of ancient communities along the\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tigris-Euphrates" title="Tigris-Euphrates">\n        Tigris-Euphrates\n       </a>\n       Rivers, the\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nile_River" title="Nile River">\n        Nile River\n       </a>\n       , the\n       <a href="/wiki/Indus_River" title="Indus River">\n        Indus River\n       </a>\n       , the\n       <a href="/wiki/Ganges" title="Ganges">\n        Ganges\n       </a>\n       and the\n       <a href="/wiki/Yellow_River" title="Yellow River">\n        Yellow River\n       </a>\n       among others. The viability of\n       <a href="/wiki/Hydropower" title="Hydropower">\n        hydropower\n       </a>\n       , a renewable source of energy, is also higher in flood prone regions.\n      </p>\n      <h2>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Flood_safety_planning">\n        Flood safety planning\n       </span>\n      </h2>\n      <p>\n       At the most basic level, the best defense against floods is to seek higher ground for high-value uses while balancing the foreseeable risks with the benefits of occupying flood hazard zones.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Eychaner_25-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-Eychaner-25">\n         [25]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       <sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">\n        :22\xe2\x80\x9323\n       </sup>\n       Critical community-safety facilities, such as hospitals, emergency-operations centers, and police, fire, and\n       <a href="/wiki/Rescue" title="Rescue">\n        rescue\n       </a>\n       services, should be built in areas least at risk of flooding. Structures, such as bridges, that must unavoidably be in flood hazard areas should be designed to withstand flooding. Areas most at risk for flooding could be put to valuable uses that could be abandoned temporarily as people retreat to safer areas when a flood is imminent.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       Planning for flood safety involves many aspects of analysis and engineering, including:\n      </p>\n      <ul>\n       <li>\n        observation of previous and present flood heights and inundated areas,\n       </li>\n       <li>\n        statistical,\n        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydrologic" title="Hydrologic">\n         hydrologic\n        </a>\n        , and hydraulic model analyses,\n       </li>\n       <li>\n        mapping inundated areas and flood heights for future flood scenarios,\n       </li>\n       <li>\n        long-term\n        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Land_use_planning" title="Land use planning">\n         land use planning\n        </a>\n        and regulation,\n       </li>\n       <li>\n        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Engineering_design" title="Engineering design">\n         engineering design\n        </a>\n        and construction of structures to control or withstand flooding,\n       </li>\n       <li>\n        intermediate-term monitoring,\n        <a href="/wiki/Flood_forecasting" title="Flood forecasting">\n         forecasting\n        </a>\n        , and emergency-response planning, and\n       </li>\n       <li>\n        short-term monitoring,\n        <a href="/wiki/Flood_warning" title="Flood warning">\n         warning\n        </a>\n        , and response operations.\n       </li>\n      </ul>\n      <p>\n       Each topic presents distinct yet related questions with varying scope and scale in time, space, and the people involved. Attempts to understand and manage the mechanisms at work in floodplains have been made for at least six millennia.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26">\n        <a href="#cite_note-26">\n         [26]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">\n        [\n        <i>\n         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources">\n          <span title="This citation requires a reference to the specific page or range of pages in which the material appears. (July 2015)">\n           page\xc2\xa0needed\n          </span>\n         </a>\n        </i>\n        ]\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       In the United States, the Association of State Floodplain Managers works to promote education, policies, and activities that mitigate current and future losses, costs, and human suffering caused by flooding and to protect the natural and beneficial functions of floodplains \xe2\x80\x93 all without causing adverse impacts.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27">\n        <a href="#cite_note-27">\n         [27]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       A portfolio of\n       <a href="/wiki/Best_practice" title="Best practice">\n        best practice\n       </a>\n       examples for\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Disaster_mitigation" title="Disaster mitigation">\n        disaster mitigation\n       </a>\n       in the United States is available from the Federal Emergency Management Agency.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28">\n        <a href="#cite_note-28">\n         [28]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <h3>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Control">\n        Control\n       </span>\n      </h3>\n      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">\n       Main article:\n       <a href="/wiki/Flood_control" title="Flood control">\n        Flood control\n       </a>\n      </div>\n      <p>\n       In many countries around the world, waterways prone to floods are often carefully managed. Defenses such as\n       <a href="/wiki/Detention_basin" title="Detention basin">\n        detention basins\n       </a>\n       ,\n       <a href="/wiki/Levee" title="Levee">\n        levees\n       </a>\n       ,\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29">\n        <a href="#cite_note-29">\n         [29]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       <a href="/wiki/Bunding" title="Bunding">\n        bunds\n       </a>\n       ,\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Reservoir_(water)" title="Reservoir (water)">\n        reservoirs\n       </a>\n       , and\n       <a href="/wiki/Weir" title="Weir">\n        weirs\n       </a>\n       are used to prevent waterways from overflowing their banks. When these defenses fail, emergency measures such as\n       <a href="/wiki/Sandbag" title="Sandbag">\n        sandbags\n       </a>\n       or portable inflatable tubes are often used to try to stem flooding. Coastal flooding has been addressed in portions of Europe and the Americas with\n       <a href="/wiki/Coastal_management" title="Coastal management">\n        coastal defenses\n       </a>\n       , such as\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sea_wall" title="Sea wall">\n        sea walls\n       </a>\n       ,\n       <a href="/wiki/Beach_nourishment" title="Beach nourishment">\n        beach nourishment\n       </a>\n       , and\n       <a href="/wiki/Barrier_island" title="Barrier island">\n        barrier islands\n       </a>\n       .\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       In the\n       <a href="/wiki/Riparian_zone" title="Riparian zone">\n        riparian zone\n       </a>\n       near rivers and streams,\n       <a href="/wiki/Erosion_control" title="Erosion control">\n        erosion control\n       </a>\n       measures can be taken to try to slow down or reverse the natural forces that cause many waterways to meander over long periods of time. Flood controls, such as dams, can be built and maintained over time to try to reduce the occurrence and severity of floods as well. In the United States, the\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._Army_Corps_of_Engineers" title="U.S. Army Corps of Engineers">\n        U.S. Army Corps of Engineers\n       </a>\n       maintains a network of such flood control dams.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       In areas prone to urban flooding, one solution is the repair and expansion of man-made sewer systems and stormwater infrastructure. Another strategy is to reduce impervious surfaces in streets, parking lots and buildings through natural drainage channels,\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Porous_paving" title="Porous paving">\n        porous paving\n       </a>\n       , and\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wetlands" title="Wetlands">\n        wetlands\n       </a>\n       (collectively known as\n       <a href="/wiki/Green_infrastructure" title="Green infrastructure">\n        green infrastructure\n       </a>\n       or\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sustainable_urban_drainage_systems" title="Sustainable urban drainage systems">\n        sustainable urban drainage systems\n       </a>\n       (SUDS)). Areas identified as flood-prone can be converted into parks and playgrounds that can tolerate occasional flooding. Ordinances can be adopted to require developers to retain stormwater on site and require buildings to be elevated, protected by\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Floodwall" title="Floodwall">\n        floodwalls\n       </a>\n       and\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Levees" title="Levees">\n        levees\n       </a>\n       , or designed to withstand temporary inundation. Property owners can also invest in solutions themselves, such as re-landscaping their property to take the flow of water away from their building and installing\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rain_barrels" title="Rain barrels">\n        rain barrels\n       </a>\n       ,\n       <a href="/wiki/Sump_pump" title="Sump pump">\n        sump pumps\n       </a>\n       , and\n       <a href="/wiki/Check_valve" title="Check valve">\n        check valves\n       </a>\n       .\n      </p>\n      <h2>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Analysis_of_flood_information">\n        Analysis of flood information\n       </span>\n      </h2>\n      <p>\n       A series of annual maximum flow rates in a stream reach can be analyzed\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Extreme_value_analysis" title="Extreme value analysis">\n        statistically\n       </a>\n       to estimate the\n       <a href="/wiki/100-year_flood" title="100-year flood">\n        100-year flood\n       </a>\n       and floods of other\n       <a href="/wiki/Return_period" title="Return period">\n        recurrence intervals\n       </a>\n       there. Similar estimates from many sites in a hydrologically similar region can be related to measurable characteristics of each drainage basin to allow\n       <a href="/wiki/Regression_analysis" title="Regression analysis">\n        indirect estimation\n       </a>\n       of flood recurrence intervals for stream reaches without sufficient data for direct analysis.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       Physical process models of channel reaches are generally well understood and will calculate the depth and area of inundation for given channel conditions and a specified flow rate, such as for use in floodplain mapping and\n       <a href="/wiki/Flood_insurance" title="Flood insurance">\n        flood insurance\n       </a>\n       . Conversely, given the observed inundation area of a recent flood and the channel conditions, a model can calculate the flow rate. Applied to various potential channel configurations and flow rates, a reach model can contribute to selecting an optimum design for a modified channel. Various reach models are available as of 2015, either\n       <a href="/wiki/One-dimensional_space" title="One-dimensional space">\n        1D\n       </a>\n       models (flood levels measured in the\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stream_channel" title="Stream channel">\n        channel\n       </a>\n       ) or\n       <a href="/wiki/Two-dimensional_space" title="Two-dimensional space">\n        2D\n       </a>\n       models (variable flood depths measured across the extent of a floodplain).\n       <a href="/wiki/HEC-RAS" title="HEC-RAS">\n        HEC-RAS\n       </a>\n       ,\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30">\n        <a href="#cite_note-30">\n         [30]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       the Hydraulic Engineering Center model, is among the most popular\n       <a href="/wiki/Software" title="Software">\n        software\n       </a>\n       , if only because it is available free of charge. Other models such as TUFLOW\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31">\n        <a href="#cite_note-31">\n         [31]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       combine 1D and 2D components to derive flood depths across both river channels and the entire floodplain.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       <a href="/wiki/Computer_simulation" title="Computer simulation">\n        Physical process models\n       </a>\n       of complete drainage basins are even more complex. Although many processes are well understood at a point or for a small area, others are poorly understood at all scales, and process interactions under normal or extreme climatic conditions may be unknown. Basin models typically combine land-surface process components (to estimate how much rainfall or snowmelt reaches a channel) with a series of reach models. For example, a basin model can calculate the runoff\n       <a href="/wiki/Hydrograph" title="Hydrograph">\n        hydrograph\n       </a>\n       that might result from a 100-year storm, although the recurrence interval of a storm is rarely equal to that of the associated flood. Basin models are commonly used in flood forecasting and warning, as well as in analysis of the effects of land use change and\n       <a href="/wiki/Climate_change" title="Climate change">\n        climate change\n       </a>\n       .\n      </p>\n      <h3>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Flood_forecasting">\n        Flood forecasting\n       </span>\n      </h3>\n      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">\n       Main articles:\n       <a href="/wiki/Flood_forecasting" title="Flood forecasting">\n        Flood forecasting\n       </a>\n       and\n       <a href="/wiki/Flood_warning" title="Flood warning">\n        flood warning\n       </a>\n      </div>\n      <p>\n       Anticipating floods before they occur allows for precautions to be taken and people to be warned\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32">\n        <a href="#cite_note-32">\n         [32]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       so that they can be prepared in advance for flooding conditions. For example, farmers can remove animals from low-lying areas and utility services can put in place emergency provisions to re-route services if needed. Emergency services can also make provisions to have enough resources available ahead of time to respond to emergencies as they occur. People can evacuate areas to be flooded.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       In order to make the most accurate flood forecasts for\n       <a href="/wiki/Waterway" title="Waterway">\n        waterways\n       </a>\n       , it is best to have a long time-series of historical data that relates\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stream_flow" title="Stream flow">\n        stream flows\n       </a>\n       to measured past rainfall events.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33">\n        <a href="#cite_note-33">\n         [33]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       Coupling this historical information with\n       <a href="/wiki/Real-time_data" title="Real-time data">\n        real-time knowledge\n       </a>\n       about volumetric capacity in catchment areas, such as spare capacity in reservoirs, ground-water levels, and the degree of\n       <a href="/wiki/Phreatic_zone" title="Phreatic zone">\n        saturation\n       </a>\n       of area\n       <a href="/wiki/Aquifer" title="Aquifer">\n        aquifers\n       </a>\n       is also needed in order to make the most acrate flood forecasts.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       <a href="/wiki/Weather_radar" title="Weather radar">\n        Radar\n       </a>\n       estimates of rainfall and general\n       <a href="/wiki/Weather_forecasting" title="Weather forecasting">\n        weather forecasting\n       </a>\n       techniques are also important components of good flood forecasting. In areas where good quality data is available, the intensity and height of a flood can be predicted with fairly good accuracy and plenty of lead time. The output of a flood forecast is typically a maximum expected water level and the likely time of its arrival at key locations along a waterway,\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Advanced_Hydrologic_Prediction_System_34-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-Advanced_Hydrologic_Prediction_System-34">\n         [34]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       and it also may allow for the computation of the likely statistical return period of a flood. In many developed countries, urban areas at risk of flooding are protected against a 100-year flood \xe2\x80\x93 that is a flood that has a probability of around 63% of occurring in any 100-year period of time.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       According to the U.S.\n       <a href="/wiki/National_Weather_Service" title="National Weather Service">\n        National Weather Service\n       </a>\n       (NWS) Northeast River Forecast Center (RFC) in\n       <a href="/wiki/Taunton,_Massachusetts" title="Taunton, Massachusetts">\n        Taunton, Massachusetts\n       </a>\n       , a rule of thumb for flood forecasting in urban areas is that it takes at least 1 inch (25\xc2\xa0mm) of rainfall in around an hour\'s time in order to start significant\n       <a href="/wiki/Ponding" title="Ponding">\n        ponding\n       </a>\n       of water on\n       <a href="/wiki/Impervious_surface" title="Impervious surface">\n        impermeable surfaces\n       </a>\n       . Many NWS RFCs routinely issue Flash Flood Guidance and Headwater Guidance, which indicate the general amount of rainfall that would need to fall in a short period of time in order to cause flash flooding or flooding on larger\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Water_basin" title="Water basin">\n        water basins\n       </a>\n       .\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Flash_Flood_Guidance_35-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-Flash_Flood_Guidance-35">\n         [35]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       In the United States, an integrated approach to real-time hydrologic computer modelling utilizes observed data from the\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._Geological_Survey" title="U.S. Geological Survey">\n        U.S. Geological Survey\n       </a>\n       (USGS),\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USGS_WaterWatch_36-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-USGS_WaterWatch-36">\n         [36]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       various\n       <a href="/wiki/Weather_spotting" title="Weather spotting">\n        cooperative observing networks\n       </a>\n       ,\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Community_Collaborative_Rain.2C_Hail_and_Snow_Network_37-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-Community_Collaborative_Rain.2C_Hail_and_Snow_Network-37">\n         [37]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       various\n       <a href="/wiki/Automated_airport_weather_station" title="Automated airport weather station">\n        automated weather sensors\n       </a>\n       , the\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NOAA" title="NOAA">\n        NOAA\n       </a>\n       National Operational Hydrologic Remote Sensing Center (NOHRSC),\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-National_Operational_Hydrologic_Remote_Sensing_Center_38-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-National_Operational_Hydrologic_Remote_Sensing_Center-38">\n         [38]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       various\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydroelectric" title="Hydroelectric">\n        hydroelectric\n       </a>\n       companies, etc. combined with\n       <a href="/wiki/Quantitative_precipitation_forecast" title="Quantitative precipitation forecast">\n        quantitative precipitation forecasts\n       </a>\n       (QPF) of expected rainfall and/or snow melt to generate daily or as-needed hydrologic forecasts.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Advanced_Hydrologic_Prediction_System_34-1">\n        <a href="#cite_note-Advanced_Hydrologic_Prediction_System-34">\n         [34]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       The NWS also cooperates with\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Environment_Canada" title="Environment Canada">\n        Environment Canada\n       </a>\n       on hydrologic forecasts that affect both the USA and Canada, like in the area of the\n       <a href="/wiki/Saint_Lawrence_Seaway" title="Saint Lawrence Seaway">\n        Saint Lawrence Seaway\n       </a>\n       .\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       The Global Flood Monitoring System, "GFMS," a computer tool which maps flood conditions worldwide, is available\n       <a class="external text" href="http://flood.umd.edu/" rel="nofollow">\n        online\n       </a>\n       . Users anywhere in the world can use GFMS to determine when floods may occur in their area. GFMS uses precipitation data from\n       <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">\n        NASA\n       </a>\n       \'s Earth observing satellites and the\n       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Global_Precipitation_Measurement_satellite&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Global Precipitation Measurement satellite (page does not exist)">\n        Global Precipitation Measurement satellite\n       </a>\n       , "GPM." Rainfall data from GPM is combined with a land surface model that incorporates vegetation cover, soil type, and terrain to determine how much water is soaking into the ground, and how much water is flowing into\n       <a href="/wiki/Streamflow" title="Streamflow">\n        streamflow\n       </a>\n       .\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       Users can view statistics for rainfall, streamflow, water depth, and flooding every 3 hours, at each 12\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kilometer" title="Kilometer">\n        kilometer\n       </a>\n       gridpoint on a global map. Forecasts for these parameters are 5 days into the future. Users can zoom in to see inundation maps (areas estimated to be covered with water) in 1 kilometer resolution.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39">\n        <a href="#cite_note-39">\n         [39]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40">\n        <a href="#cite_note-40">\n         [40]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <h2>\n
