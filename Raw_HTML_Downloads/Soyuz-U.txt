Soyuz-U was part of the\n       <a href="/wiki/R-7_(rocket_family)" title="R-7 (rocket family)">\n        R-7 family\n       </a>\n       of rockets based on the\n       <a href="/wiki/R-7_Semyorka" title="R-7 Semyorka">\n        R-7 Semyorka\n       </a>\n       missile. Members of this rocket family were designed by the TsSKB design bureau and constructed at the Progress Factory in\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Samara,_Russia" title="Samara, Russia">\n        Samara, Russia\n       </a>\n       (now a united company,\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/TsSKB-Progress" title="TsSKB-Progress">\n        TsSKB-Progress\n       </a>\n       ). The first Soyuz-U flight took place on 18 May 1973, carrying as its payload\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cosmos_(satellite)" title="Cosmos (satellite)">\n        Kosmos 559\n       </a>\n       , a\n       <a href="/wiki/Zenit_(satellite)" title="Zenit (satellite)">\n        Zenit\n       </a>\n       military surveillance satellite.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwade_6-1">\n        <a href="#cite_note-mwade-6">\n         [6]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       The final flight of a Soyuz-U rocket took place on February 22, 2017, carrying\n       <a href="/wiki/Progress_MS-05" title="Progress MS-05">\n        Progress MS-05\n       </a>\n       to the\n       <a href="/wiki/International_Space_Station" title="International Space Station">\n        International Space Station\n       </a>\n       .\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       Soyuz-U was in use continuously for almost 44 years, the longest lifetime of an orbital rocket worldwide. Production of R-7 derived launch vehicles peaked in the late 1970s-early 1980s at 55\xe2\x80\x9360 a year. Soyuz-U held the world record of highest launch rate in a year\n       <a href="/wiki/1979_in_spaceflight#Launches" title="1979 in spaceflight">\n        in 1979 with 47 flights\n       </a>\n       . Over its operational lifetime, the Soyuz-U variant flew a total of 786 missions, another world record. Soyuz-U has also been one of the most reliable launchers, with a success rate of\n       <span class="sortkey" style="display:none">\n        7001973000000000000\xe2\x99\xa0\n       </span>\n       97.3%.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n      </p>\n      <div class="toc" id="toc">\n       <div class="toctitle">\n        <h2>\n         Contents\n        </h2>\n       </div>\n       <ul>\n        <li <p>\n      </p>\n      <h2>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Development">\n        Development\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Soyuz-U&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Development">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h2>\n      <p>\n       The earlier\n       <a href="/wiki/Soyuz_(rocket)" title="Soyuz (rocket)">\n        Soyuz 11A511\n       </a>\n       was the first attempt at creating a standardized R-7 core in place of the numerous variations that had been used up to 1966. Starting that year, the 11A511 Blok I and strap-on boosters were added to the\n       <a href="/wiki/Voskhod_(rocket)" title="Voskhod (rocket)">\n        Voskhod\n       </a>\n       (11A57),\n       <a href="/wiki/Vostok-2_(rocket)" title="Vostok-2 (rocket)">\n        Vostok-2\n       </a>\n       (8A92), and\n       <a href="/wiki/Molniya-M" title="Molniya-M">\n        Molniya-M\n       </a>\n       (8K78M) vehicles as well as minor R-7 variants flown once or twice for specialized payloads.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       The uprated 11A511U core was introduced to the R-7 family in 1973, yielding the carrier rocket variant named Soyuz-U, although adoption across the board was not complete until 1977 when the existing stock of 11A511-derived boosters was used up.\n      </p>\n      <h2>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Versions">\n        Versions\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Soyuz-U&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Versions">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h2>\n      <p>\n       Two versions of Soyuz-U were fitted with an additional upper stage:\n      </p>\n      <ul>\n       <li>\n        <b>\n         Soyuz-U/Ikar\n        </b>\n        with the\n        <a href="/wiki/Ikar_(rocket_stage)" title="Ikar (rocket stage)">\n         Ikar\n        </a>\n        third stage, produced by the Progress State Research and Production Rocket Space Center,\n        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/TsSKB-Progress" title="TsSKB-Progress">\n         TsSKB-Progress\n        </a>\n        . Ikar is used to deliver various payloads with masses of 750\xc2\xa0kg to 3920\xc2\xa0kg to heights 250\xc2\xa0km to 1400\xc2\xa0km. The performance of the Ikar upper stage is lower than that of the Fregat upper stage, but it is more precise in maneuvering and it can operate autonomously longer. This version was launched 6 times in 1999, carrying four\n        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/GlobalStar" title="GlobalStar">\n         GlobalStar\n        </a>\n        satellites on each mission.\n        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gunter-ikar_3-1">\n         <a href="#cite_note-gunter-ikar-3">\n          [3]\n         </a>\n        </sup>\n       </li>\n       <li>\n        <b>\n         Soyuz-U/Fregat\n        </b>\n        with the\n        <a href="/wiki/Fregat" title="Fregat">\n         Fregat\n        </a>\n        third stage, developed and produced by\n        <a href="/wiki/Lavochkin" title="Lavochkin">\n         Lavochkin Association\n        </a>\n        in\n        <a href="/wiki/Khimki" title="Khimki">\n         Khimki\n        </a>\n        . This version only flew 4 times in 2000;\n        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gunter-fregat_4-1">\n         <a href="#cite_note-gunter-fregat-4">\n          [4]\n         </a>\n        </sup>\n        the Fregat upper stage was subsequently flown regularly atop\n        <a href="/wiki/Soyuz-FG" title="Soyuz-FG">\n         Soyuz-FG\n        </a>\n        and\n        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Soyuz-2_(rocket)" title="Soyuz-2 (rocket)">\n         Soyuz-2\n        </a>\n        boosters.\n       </li>\n      </ul>\n      <p>\n       An older variant of Soyuz-U, the\n       <a href="/wiki/Soyuz-U2" title="Soyuz-U2">\n        Soyuz-U2\n       </a>\n       launcher, first flown in 1982, had the same hardware as the basic Soyuz-U. Instead of standard\n       <a href="/wiki/RP-1" title="RP-1">\n        RP-1\n       </a>\n       , it used a high energy, synthetic version,\n       <a href="/wiki/Syntin" title="Syntin">\n        Syntin\n       </a>\n       , as the first stage fuel. This variant, mainly used to transport crew and cargo to the\n       <a href="/wiki/Mir" title="Mir">\n        Mir\n       </a>\n       space station, last flew in 1995, after production of Syntin ended due to cost reasons.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       Soyuz-U was the basic platform for the development of the\n       <a href="/wiki/Soyuz-FG" title="Soyuz-FG">\n        Soyuz-FG\n       </a>\n       variant, which used an all-new first stage and took over crew transport to the\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ISS" title="ISS">\n        ISS\n       </a>\n       in 2002. Since 2013, both Soyuz-U and Soyuz-FG are gradually being replaced by the modernized\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Soyuz-2_(rocket)" title="Soyuz-2 (rocket)">\n        Soyuz-2\n       </a>\n       launch vehicle.\n      </p>\n      <h2>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Human_spaceflight">\n        Human spaceflight\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Soyuz-U&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Human spaceflight">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h2>\n      <p>\n       The first use of a Soyuz-U to launch a\n       <a href="/wiki/Human_spaceflight" title="Human spaceflight">\n        crewed\n       </a>\n       mission took place 2 December 1974, when the\n       <a href="/wiki/Soyuz_16" title="Soyuz 16">\n        Soyuz 16\n       </a>\n       crew was launched in preparation for the\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Apollo-Soyuz_Test_Project" title="Apollo-Soyuz Test Project">\n        Apollo-Soyuz Test Project\n       </a>\n       (ASTP).\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Soyuz_19" title="Soyuz 19">\n        Soyuz 19\n       </a>\n       , which as part of the ASTP docked with the last\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Apollo_spacecraft" title="Apollo spacecraft">\n        Apollo spacecraft\n       </a>\n       ever flown, was also launched by a Soyuz-U rocket.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwade_6-2">\n        <a href="#cite_note-mwade-6">\n         [6]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       On 6 July 1976 a Soyuz-U launched\n       <a href="/wiki/Soyuz_21" title="Soyuz 21">\n        Soyuz 21\n       </a>\n       , which took a crew of two to the\n       <a href="/wiki/Salyut_5" title="Salyut 5">\n        Salyut 5\n       </a>\n       space station. Many subsequent space station crews were launched on Soyuz-U launchers. The final crewed mission to utilize the Soyuz-U was\n       <a href="/wiki/Soyuz_TM-34" title="Soyuz TM-34">\n        Soyuz TM-34\n       </a>\n       , a\n       <a href="/wiki/Soyuz_(spacecraft)" title="Soyuz (spacecraft)">\n        Soyuz\n       </a>\n       ferry flight to the\n       <a href="/wiki/International_Space_Station" title="International Space Station">\n        International Space Station\n       </a>\n       .\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       A spectacular accident occurred on 26 September 1983, when the launcher for the\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Soyuz_T-10a" title="Soyuz T-10a">\n        Soyuz T-10a\n       </a>\n       mission was destroyed by fire on the launch pad. The crew was saved by activation of the\n       <a href="/wiki/Launch_escape_system" title="Launch escape system">\n        launch escape system\n       </a>\n       a few seconds before the explosion.\n      </p>\n      <h2>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Recent_missions">\n        Recent missions\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Soyuz-U&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Recent missions">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h2>\n      <p>\n       Since the early 2000s, Soyuz-U vehicles have been used by the\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Russian_Federal_Space_Agency" title="Russian Federal Space Agency">\n        Russian Federal Space Agency\n       </a>\n       primarily to launch\n       <a href="/wiki/Progress_(spacecraft)" title="Progress (spacecraft)">\n        Progress-M\n       </a>\n       robotic cargo spacecraft on resupply missions to the\n       <a href="/wiki/International_Space_Station" title="International Space Station">\n        International Space Station\n       </a>\n       (ISS).\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       Although the Soyuz-U is generally very reliable, occasional failures have happened, such as the October 2002 launch of a Foton satellite which crashed near the pad at\n       <a href="/wiki/Plesetsk" title="Plesetsk">\n        Plesetsk\n       </a>\n       after the\n       <a href="/wiki/Blok_D" title="Blok D">\n        Blok D\n       </a>\n       strap-on booster suffered an engine malfunction. One person on the ground was killed.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       A Soyuz-U mission failed to launch\n       <a href="/wiki/Progress_M-12M" title="Progress M-12M">\n        Progress M-12M\n       </a>\n       to the ISS on 24 August 2011, when the upper stage experienced a problem and broke up over Siberia. It was the first time a Progress spacecraft had failed to reach orbit. Another cargo ship,\n       <a href="/wiki/Progress_MS-04" title="Progress MS-04">\n        Progress MS-04\n       </a>\n       , was lost on December 1, 2016 shortly after launch, likely due to a problem with the third stage of the Soyuz-U.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       In April 2015 Soyuz-U was declared obsolete; its production has been stopped, and the rocket was scheduled for retirement after launching the remaining vehicles with Progress cargo ships.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-obsolete_10-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-obsolete-10">\n         [10]\n        </a>\n href="/wiki/Baikonur_Cosmodrome" title="Baikonur Cosmodrome">\n        Baikonur Cosmodrome\n       </a>\n       on February 22, 2017, 05:58:33 2017-11-07\xc2\xa022:56\n       <small>\n        <a
