</span>\n       </span>\n      </h2>\n      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">\n       Main article:\n       <a href="/wiki/Corrosion" title="Corrosion">\n        corrosion\n       </a>\n      </div>\n      <p>\n       Due to its\n       <a href="/wiki/Electronegativity" title="Electronegativity">\n        electronegativity\n       </a>\n       , oxygen forms stable\n       <a href="/wiki/Chemical_bond" title="Chemical bond">\n        chemical bonds\n       </a>\n       with almost all elements to give the corresponding oxides.\n       <a href="/wiki/Noble_metal" title="Gold(III) oxide">\n        gold(III) oxide\n       </a>\n       must be generated by indirect routes.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       Two independent pathways for corrosion of elements are hydrolysis and oxidation by oxygen. The combination of water and oxygen is even more corrosive. Virtually all elements burn in an atmosphere of oxygen, or an oxygen rich environment. In the presence of water and oxygen (or simply air), some elements\xe2\x80\x94\n       <a href="/wiki/Sodium" title="Sodium">\n        sodium\n       </a>\n       \xe2\x80\x94react rapidly, even dangerously, to give the hydroxides. In part for this reason, alkali and alkaline earth metals are not found in nature in their metallic, i.e., native, form. Caesium is so reactive with oxygen that it is used as a\n       <a href="/wiki/Getter" title="Getter">\n        getter\n       </a>\n       in\n       <a href="/wiki/Vacuum_tube" title="Vacuum tube">\n        vacuum tubes\n       </a>\n       , and solutions of potassium and sodium, so-called\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NaK" title="NaK">\n        NaK\n       </a>\n       are used to deoxygenate and dehydrate some organic solvents. The surface of most metals consists of oxides and hydroxides in the presence of air. A well-known example is\n       <a href="/wiki/Aluminium_foil" title="Aluminium foil">\n        aluminium foil\n       </a>\n       , which is coated with a thin film of\n       <a href="/wiki/Aluminium_oxide" title="Aluminium oxide">\n        aluminium oxide\n       </a>\n       that\n       <a href="/wiki/Passivation_(chemistry)" title="Passivation (chemistry)">\n        passivates\n       </a>\n       the metal, slowing further\n       <a href="/wiki/Corrosion" title="Corrosion">\n        corrosion\n       </a>\n       . The aluminium oxide layer can be built to greater thickness by the process of\n       <a href="/wiki/Electrolysis" title="Electrolysis">\n        electrolytic\n       </a>\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anodising" title="Anodising">\n        anodising\n       </a>\n       . Though solid magnesium and aluminium react slowly with oxygen at\n       <a href="/wiki/Standard_conditions_for_temperature_and_pressure" title="Standard conditions for temperature and pressure">\n        STP\n       </a>\n       \xe2\x80\x94they, like most metals, burn in air, generating very high temperatures. Finely grained powders of most metals can be dangerously explosive in air. At standard conditions, oxides may range from solids to gases.\n      </p>\n      <h3>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Oxides_of_metals">\n        Oxides of metals\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Oxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Oxides of metals">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h3>\n      <p>\n       Oxides of most metals adopt polymeric structures.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">\n        <a href="#cite_note-3">\n         [3]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       The oxide typically links three metals (e.g., rutile structure) or six metals (\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carborundum" title="Carborundum">\n        carborundum\n       </a>\n       or\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rock_salt" title="Rock salt">\n        rock salt\n       </a>\n       structures). Because the M-O bonds are typically strong and these compounds are crosslinked polymers, the solids tend to be insoluble in solvents, though they are attacked by acids and bases. The formulas are often deceptively simple. Many are\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nonstoichiometric_compound" title="Nonstoichiometric compound">\n        nonstoichiometric compounds\n       </a>\n       .\n       <sup molecules. Examples of molecular oxides are\n       <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">\n        carbon dioxide\n       </a>\n       and\n       <a href="/wiki/Carbon_monoxide" title="Carbon monoxide">\n        carbon monoxide\n       </a>\n       . All simple oxides of nitrogen are molecular, e.g., NO, N\n       <sub>\n        2\n       </sub>\n       O, NO\n       <sub>\n        2\n       </sub>\n       and N\n       <sub>\n        2\n       </sub>\n       O\n       <sub>\n        4\n       </sub>\n       .\n       <a href="/wiki/Phosphorus_pentoxide" title="Phosphorus pentoxide">\n        Phosphorus pentoxide\n       </a>\n       is a more complex molecular oxide with a deceptive name, the real formula being P\n       <sub>\n        4\n       </sub>\n       O\n       <sub>\n        10\n       </sub>\n       . Some polymeric oxides\n       <a href="/wiki/Cracking_(chemistry)" title="Cracking (chemistry)">\n        depolymerize\n       </a>\n       when heated to give molecules, examples being\n       <a href="/wiki/Selenium_dioxide" title="Selenium dioxide">\n        selenium dioxide\n       </a>\n       and\n       <a href="/wiki/Sulfur_trioxide" title="Sulfur trioxide">\n        sulfur trioxide\n       </a>\n       . Tetroxides are rare. The more common examples:\n       <a href="/wiki/Ruthenium_tetroxide" title="Ruthenium tetroxide">\n        ruthenium tetroxide\n       </a>\n       ,\n       <a href="/wiki/Osmium_tetroxide" title="Osmium tetroxide">\n        osmium tetroxide\n       </a>\n       , and\n       <a href="/wiki/Xenon_tetroxide" title="Xenon tetroxide">\n        xenon tetroxide\n       </a>\n       .\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       Many oxyanions are known, such as\n       <a href="/wiki/Polyphosphate" title="Polyphosphate">\n        polyphosphates\n       </a>\n       and\n       <a href="/wiki/Polyoxometalate" title="Polyoxometalate">\n        polyoxometalates\n       </a>\n       . Oxycations are rarer, some examples being\n       <a href="/wiki/Nitrosonium" title="Nitrosonium">\n        nitrosonium\n       </a>\n       (NO\n       <sup>\n        +\n       </sup>\n       ),\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vanadyl" title="Vanadyl">\n        vanadyl\n       </a>\n       (VO\n       <sup>\n        2+\n       </sup>\n       ), and\n       <a href="/wiki/Uranyl" title="Uranyl">\n        uranyl\n       </a>\n       (\n       <span class="chemf nowrap">\n        UO\n        <span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left">\n         2+\n         <br/>\n         2\n        </span>\n       </span>\n       ). Of course many compounds are known with both oxides and other groups. In\n       <a href="/wiki/Organic_chemistry" title="Organic chemistry">\n        organic chemistry\n       </a>\n       , these include\n       <a href="/wiki/Ketone" title="Ketone">\n        ketones\n       </a>\n       and many related\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carbonyl" title="Carbonyl">\n        carbonyl\n       </a>\n       compounds. For the transition metals, many\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oxo_ligand" title="Oxo ligand">\n        oxo complexes\n       </a>\n       are known as well as\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oxyhalide" title="Oxyhalide">\n        oxyhalides\n       </a>\n       .\n      </p>\n      <h2>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Reactivity">\n        Reactivity\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Oxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Reactivity">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h2>\n      <p>\n       Oxides can be reacted by\n       <a href="/wiki/Acid" title="Acid">\n        acids\n       </a>\n       or\n       <a href="/wiki/Base_(chemistry)" title="Base (chemistry)">\n        bases\n       </a>\n       . Those reacted only by acids are basic oxides; those reacted only by bases are acidic oxides. Oxides that react with both acids and bases are\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Amphoteric" title="Amphoteric">\n        amphoteric\n       </a>\n       . Metals tend to form basic oxides, non-metals tend to form acidic oxides, and amphoteric oxides are formed by elements near the boundary between metals and non-metals (\n       <a href="/wiki/Metalloid" title="Metalloid">\n        metalloids\n       </a>\n       ).\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       This reactivity is the basis of many practical processes such, as the extraction of some metals from their ores in the process called\n       <a href="/wiki/Hydrometallurgy" title="Hydrometallurgy">\n        hydrometallurgy\n       </a>\n       .\n      </p>\n      <h3>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Reduction">\n        Reduction\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Oxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Reduction">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h3>\n      <p>\n       Converting the metal oxide back to the pure metal is called reduction. There are many ways of reduction.\n      </p>\n      <h4>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Reduction_by_carbon">\n        Reduction by carbon\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Oxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Reduction by carbon">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h4>\n      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">\n       See also:\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carbothermic_reduction" title="Carbothermic reduction">\n        carbothermic reduction\n       </a>\n      </div>\n      <p>\n       Metals are "won" from their oxides by chemical reduction. A common and cheap reducing agent is carbon in the form of\n       <a href="/wiki/Coke_(fuel)" title="Coke (fuel)">\n        coke\n       </a>\n       . The most prominent example is that of\n       <a href="/wiki/Iron#Industrial_production" title="Iron">\n        iron ore smelting\n       </a>\n       . Many reactions are involved, but the simplified equation is usually shown as:\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Greenwood_2-2">\n        <a href="#cite_note-Greenwood-2">\n         [2]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <dl>\n       <dd>\n        2 Fe\n        <sub>\n         2\n        </sub>\n        O\n        <sub>\n         3\n        </sub>\n        + 3 C \xe2\x86\x92 4 Fe + 3 CO\n        <sub>\n         2\n        </sub>\n       </dd>\n      </dl>\n      <p>\n       Metal oxides can be reduced by organic compounds. This redox process is the basis for many important transformations in chemistry, such as the detoxification of drugs by the\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/P450" title="P450">\n        P450\n       </a>\n       enzymes and the production of\n       <a href="/wiki/Ethylene_oxide" title="Ethylene oxide">\n        ethylene oxide\n       </a>\n       , which is converted to antifreeze. In such systems the metal centre transfers an oxide ligand to the organic compound followed by regeneration of the metal oxide, often by oxygen in air.\n      </p>\n      <h4>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Reduction_by_heating">\n        Reduction by heating\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Oxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Reduction by heating">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h4>\n      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">\n       See also:\n       <a href="/wiki/Thermal_decomposition" title="Thermal decomposition">\n        thermal decomposition\n       </a>\n      </div>\n      <p>\n       Metals that are lower in the\n       <a href="/wiki/Reactivity_series" title="Reactivity series">\n        reactivity series\n       </a>\n       can be reduced by heating alone. For example,\n       <a href="/wiki/Silver_oxide" title="Silver oxide">\n        silver oxide\n       </a>\n       decomposes at 200\xc2\xa0\xc2\xb0C:\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">\n        <a href="#cite_note-4">\n         [4]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <dl>\n       <dd>\n        2 Ag\n        <sub>\n         2\n        </sub>\n        O \xe2\x86\x92 4 Ag + O\n        <sub>\n         2\n        </sub>\n       </dd>\n      </dl>\n      <h4>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Reduction_by_displacement">\n        Reduction by displacement\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Oxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Reduction by displacement">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h4>\n      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">\n       See also:\n       <a href="/wiki/Single_displacement_reaction" title="Single displacement reaction">\n        single displacement reaction\n       </a>\n      </div>\n      <p>\n       Metals that are more\n       <a href="/wiki/Reactivity_series" title="Reactivity series">\n        reactive\n       </a>\n       displace the oxide of the metals that are less reactive. For example,\n       <a href="/wiki/Zinc" title="Zinc">\n        zinc\n       </a>\n       is more reactive than\n       <a href="/wiki/Copper" title="Copper">\n        copper\n       </a>\n       , so it displaces\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Copper_(II)_oxide" title="Copper (II) oxide">\n        copper (II) oxide\n       </a>\n       to form\n       <a href="/wiki/Zinc_oxide" class="mw-headline" id="Reduction_by_hydrogen">\n        Reduction by hydrogen\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h4>\n      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">\n       See also:\n       <a displace metal oxides to form\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydrogen_oxide" title="Hydrogen oxide">\n        hydrogen oxide\n       </a>\n       , also known as water:\n      </p>\n      <dl>\n       <dd>\n        H\n        <sub>\n         2\n        </sub>\n        + CuO \xe2\x86\x92 Cu + H\n        <sub>\n         2\n        </sub>\n        O\n       </dd>\n      </dl>\n      <h4>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Reduction_by_electrolysis">\n        Reduction by electrolysis\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Oxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Reduction by electrolysis">\n some metal oxides must be electrolyzed to be reduced. This includes\n       <a href="/wiki/Sodium_oxide" title="Sodium oxide">\n        sodium oxide\n       </a>\n       ,\n       <a href="/wiki/Potassium_oxide" title="Potassium oxide">\n        potassium oxide\n       </a>\n       ,\n       <a href="/wiki/Calcium_oxide" title="Calcium oxide">\n        calcium oxide\n       </a>\n       ,\n       <a href="/wiki/Magnesium_oxide" title="Magnesium oxide">\n        magnesium oxide\n       </a>\n       , and\n       <a href="/wiki/Aluminium_oxide" title="Aluminium oxide">\n        aluminium oxide\n       </a>\n       . The oxides must be molten before immersing graphite electrodes in it:\n      </p>\n      <dl>\n       <dd>\n        2Al\n        <sub>\n         2\n        </sub>\n        O\n        <sub>\n         3\n        </sub>\n        \xe2\x86\x92 4Al + 3O\n        <sub>\n         2\n        </sub>\n       </dd>\n      </dl>\n      <h3>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Hydrolysis">\n        Hydrolysis\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Oxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Hydrolysis">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h3>\n      <p>\n       Oxides of more\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electropositive" title="Electropositive">\n        electropositive\n       </a>\n       elements tend to be basic. They are called\n       <i>\n        basic anhydrides\n       </i>\n       . Exposed to water, they may form basic\n       <a href="/wiki/Hydroxide" title="Hydroxide">\n        hydroxides\n       </a>\n       . For example,\n       <a href="/wiki/Sodium_oxide" title="Sodium oxide">\n        sodium oxide\n       </a>\n       is basic\xe2\x80\x94when hydrated, it forms\n       <a href="/wiki/Sodium_hydroxide" title="Sodium hydroxide">\n        sodium hydroxide\n       </a>\n       . Some oxides can act as both acid and base. They are\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Amphoteric" title="Amphoteric">\n        amphoteric\n       </a>\n       . An example is\n       <a href="/wiki/Aluminium_oxide" title="Aluminium oxide">\n        aluminium oxide\n       </a>\n       . Some oxides do not show behavior as either acid or base.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       The oxide\n       <a href="/wiki/Ion" title="Ion">\n        ion\n       </a>\n       has the formula O\n       <sup>\n        2\xe2\x88\x92\n       </sup>\n       . It is the\n       <a href="/wiki/Conjugate_acid" title="Conjugate acid">\n        conjugate base\n       </a>\n       of the\n       <a href="/wiki/Hydroxide" title="Hydroxide">\n        hydroxide\n       </a>\n       ion, OH\n       <sup>\n        \xe2\x88\x92\n       </sup>\n       , and is encountered in\n       <a href="/wiki/Ionic_compound" title="Ionic compound">\n        ionic\n       </a>\n       solid such as\n       <a href="/wiki/Calcium_oxide" title="Calcium oxide">\n        calcium oxide\n       </a>\n       . O\n       <sup>\n        2\xe2\x88\x92\n       </sup>\n       is unstable in\n       <a href="/wiki/Water" title="Water">\n        aqueous\n       </a>\n       <a href="/wiki/Solution" title="Solution">\n        solution\n       </a>\n       \xe2\x88\x92 its affinity for H\n       <sup>\n        +\n       </sup>\n       is so great (p\n       <i>\n        K\n       </i>\n       <sub>\n        b\n       </sub>\n       ~ \xe2\x88\x9238) that it abstracts a\n       <a href="/wiki/Proton" title="Proton">\n        proton\n       </a>\n       from a solvent H\n       <sub>\n  </b>\n       after the\n       <a href="/wiki/Calcination" title="Calcination">\n        calcination\n       </a>\n       process used to produce oxides.\n       <i>\n        Calx\n       </i>\n       was later replaced by\n       <i>\n        oxyd.\n       </i>\n      </p>\n      <h2>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Nomenclature_and_formulas">\n        Nomenclature and formulas\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Oxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13"  Sometimes, metal-oxygen ratios are used to name oxides. Thus, NbO would be called niobium monoxide and TiO\n       <sub>\n        2\n       </sub>\n       is titanium dioxide. This naming follows the\n       <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">\n        Greek\n       </a>\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Numerical_prefix" title="Numerical prefix">\n        numerical prefixes\n       </a>\n       . In the older literature and continuing in industry, oxides are named by adding the suffix\n       <i>\n        <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/-a#Etymology_4" title="wikt:-a">\n         -a\n        </a>\n       </i>\n       to the element\'s name. Even the chemical formula of O\n       <sub>\n        4\n       </sub>\n       ,\n       <a href="/wiki/Tetraoxygen" title="Tetraoxygen">\n        tetraoxygen\n       </a>\n       , is predictable as a\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Group_16_element" title="Group 16 element">\n        group 16 element\n       </a>\n       . One exception is\n       <a href="/wiki/Copper" title="Copper">\n        copper\n       </a>\n       , for which the highest oxidation state oxide is\n       <a href="/wiki/Copper(II)_oxide" title="Copper(II) oxide">\n        copper(II) oxide\n       </a>\n       and not\n       <a href="/wiki/Copper(I)_oxide" title="Copper(I) oxide">\n        copper(I) oxide\n       </a>\n       . Another exception is\n       <a href="/wiki/Fluoride" title="Fluoride">\n        fluoride\n       </a>\n       , which does not exist as one might expect\xe2\x80\x94as F\n       <sub>\n        2\n       </sub>\n       O\n       <sub>\n        7\n       </sub>\n       \xe2\x80\x94but as\n       <a href="/wiki/Oxygen_difluoride" title="Oxygen difluoride">\n        OF\n        <sub>\n         2\n        </sub>\n       </a>\n       .\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">\n        <a href="#cite_note-5">\n         [5]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       Since fluorine is more\n       <a href="/wiki/Electronegativity" title="Electronegativity">\n        electronegative\n       </a>\n       than oxygen,\n       <a href="/wiki/Oxygen_difluoride" title="Oxygen difluoride">\n        oxygen difluoride\n       </a>\n       (OF\n       <sub>\n        2\n       </sub>\n       ) does not represent an oxide of fluorine, but instead represents a\n       <a href="/wiki/Fluoride" title="Fluoride">\n        fluoride\n       </a>\n       of oxygen.\n      </p>\n      <h2>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Examples_of_oxides">\n        Examples of oxides\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Oxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Examples of oxides">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h2>\n      <p>\n       The following table gives examples of commonly encountered oxides.
