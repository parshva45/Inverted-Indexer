Although the word has been used to describe certain large fires,\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-American_National_Fire_Protection_Association_2005_https:.2F.2Fbooks.google.com.2Fbooks.3Fid.3DIWW8qOXd6sgC.26pg.3DPA68.23v.3Donepage.26q.26f.3Dfalse_68_1-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-American_National_Fire_Protection_Association_2005_https:.2F.2Fbooks.google.com.2Fbooks.3Fid.3DIWW8qOXd6sgC.26pg.3DPA68.23v.3Donepage.26q.26f.3Dfalse_68-1">\n         [1]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       the phenomenon\'s determining characteristic is a fire with its own\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Storm-force" title="Storm-force">\n        storm-force\n       </a>\n       winds from every point of the\n       <a href="/wiki/Compass" title="Compass">\n        compass\n       </a>\n       .\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">\n        <a href="#cite_note-2">\n         [2]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">\n        <a href="#cite_note-3">\n         [3]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       The\n       <a href="/wiki/Black_Saturday_bushfires" title="Black Saturday bushfires">\n        Black Saturday bushfires\n       </a>\n       and the Great\n       <a href="/wiki/Peshtigo_Fire" title="Peshtigo Fire">\n        Peshtigo Fire\n       </a>\n       are possible examples of forest fires with some portion of combustion due to a firestorm, as is the\n       <a href="/wiki/Great_Hinckley_Fire" title="Great Hinckley Fire">\n        Great Hinckley Fire\n       </a>\n       . Firestorms have also occurred in cities, usually as a deliberate effect of targeted\n       <a href="/wiki/Explosive_material" title="Explosive material">\n        explosives\n       </a>\n       such as occurred as a result of the\n       <a href="/wiki/Firebombing" title="Firebombing">\n        aerial firebombings\n       </a>\n       of\n       <a href="/wiki/Bombing_of_Hamburg_in_World_War_II" title="Bombing of Hamburg in World War II">\n        Hamburg\n       </a>\n       ,\n       <a href="/wiki/Bombing_of_Dresden_in_World_War_II" title="Bombing of Dresden in World War II">\n        Dresden\n       </a>\n       , and the\n       <a href="/wiki/Atomic_bombings_of_Hiroshima_and_Nagasaki" title="Atomic bombings of Hiroshima and Nagasaki">\n        atomic bombing of Hiroshima\n       </a>\n       .\n      </p>\n      <p>\n      </p>\n      <div class="toc" id="toc">\n       <div class="toctitle">\n        <h2>\n         Contents\n        </h2>\n       </div>\n       <ul>\n </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Firestorm&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Mechanism">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h2>\n      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">\n       See also:\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thermal_column" title="Thermal column">\n        Thermal column\n       </a>\n      </div>\n      <div class="thumb tright">\n       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">\n        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Firestorm_thermal_column.svg">\n         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="265" data-file-width="339" height="172" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9b/Firestorm_thermal_column.svg/220px-Firestorm_thermal_column.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9b/Firestorm_thermal_column.svg/330px-Firestorm_thermal_column.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9b/Firestorm_thermal_column.svg/440px-Firestorm_thermal_column.svg.png 2x" width="220"/>\n        </a>\n        <div class="thumbcaption">\n         <div class="magnify">\n          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Firestorm_thermal_column.svg" title="Enlarge">\n          </a>\n         </div>\n         Firestorm: fire (1), updraft (2), strong gusty winds (3) (A)\n         <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pyrocumulonimbus_cloud" title="Pyrocumulonimbus cloud">\n          pyrocumulonimbus cloud\n         </a>\n         .\n        </div>\n       </div>\n      </div>\n      <p>\n       A firestorm is created as a result of the\n       <a href="/wiki/Stack_effect" title="Stack effect">\n        stack effect\n       </a>\n       as the heat of the original fire draws in more and more of the surrounding air. This draft can be quickly increased if a low-level\n       <a href="/wiki/Jet_stream" title="Jet stream">\n        jet stream\n       </a>\n       exists over or near the fire. As the updraft mushrooms, strong inwardly-directed gusty winds develop around the fire, supplying it with additional air. This would seem to prevent the firestorm from spreading on the wind, but the tremendous turbulence created may also cause the strong surface inflow winds to change direction erratically. Firestorms resulting from the\n       <a href="/wiki/Bombardment" title="Bombardment">\n        bombardment\n       </a>\n       of\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Urban_areas" title="Urban areas">\n        urban areas\n       </a>\n       in the Second World War were generally confined to the areas initially seeded with incendiary devices, and the firestorm did not appreciably spread outward.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AD673703_1961.2C_pg_8_.26_9_4-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-AD673703_1961.2C_pg_8_.26_9-4">\n         [4]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       A firestorm may also develop into a\n       <a href="/wiki/Mesocyclone" title="Mesocyclone">\n        mesocyclone\n       </a>\n       and induce true tornadoes/\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fire_whirls" title="Fire whirls">\n        fire whirls\n       </a>\n       . This occurred with the 2002 Durango fire,\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWeaverBiko_5-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWeaverBiko-5">\n         [5]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       and probably with the much greater\n       <a href="/wiki/Peshtigo_Fire" title="Peshtigo Fire">\n        Peshtigo Fire\n       </a>\n       .\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Denise_Gess_6-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-Denise_Gess-6">\n         [6]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">\n        <a href="#cite_note-7">\n         [7]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       The greater draft of a firestorm draws in greater quantities of\n       <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">\n        oxygen\n       </a>\n       , which significantly increases combustion, thereby also substantially increasing the production of heat. The intense heat of a firestorm manifests largely as radiated heat (\n       <a href="/wiki/Infrared" title="Infrared">\n        infrared\n       </a>\n       radiation), which may ignite flammable material at a distance ahead of the fire itself.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">\n        <a href="#cite_note-8">\n         [8]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">\n        <a href="#cite_note-9">\n         [9]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">\n        [\n        <i>\n         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability">\n          <span title="The material near this tag failed verification of its source citation(s). (May 2013)">\n           not in citation given\n          </span>\n         </a>\n        </i>\n        ]\n       </sup>\n       This also serves to expand the area and the intensity of the firestorm.\n       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">\n        [\n        <i>\n         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability">\n          <span title="The material near this tag failed verification of its source citation(s). (May 2013)">\n           not in citation given\n          </span>\n         </a>\n        </i>\n        ]\n       </sup>\n       Violent, erratic wind drafts suck movables into the fire and as is observed with all intense conflagrations, radiated heat from the fire can melt\n       <a href="/wiki/Asphalt" title="Asphalt">\n        asphalt\n       </a>\n       , some metals, and glass, and turn street\n       <a href="/wiki/Tarmac" title="Tarmac">\n        tarmac\n       </a>\n       into flammable hot liquid. The very high temperatures ignite anything that might possibly burn, until the firestorm runs low on fuel.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       According to experts,\n       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">\n        [\n        <i>\n         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch">\n          <span title="The material near this tag possibly uses too-vague attribution or weasel words. (August 2015)">\n           who?\n          </span>\n         </a>\n        </i>\n        ]\n       </sup>\n       a firestorm does not appreciably ignite material at a distance ahead of itself; more accurately, the heat desiccates those materials and makes them more vulnerable to ignition by embers or firebrands, increasing the rate of fire spotting. During the formation of a firestorm many fires merge to form a single convective column of hot gases rising from the burning area and strong, fire-induced, radial (inwardly directed) winds are associated with the convective column. Thus the fire front is essentially stationary and the outward spread of fire is prevented by the in-rushing wind.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">\n        <a href="#cite_note-10">\n         [10]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       A firestorm is characterized by strong to gale-force winds blowing toward the fire, everywhere around the fire perimeter, an effect which is caused by the\n       <a href="/wiki/Buoyancy" title="Buoyancy">\n        buoyancy\n       </a>\n       of the rising column of hot gases over the intense mass fire, drawing in cool air from the periphery. These winds from the perimeter blow the\n       <a href="/wiki/Wildfire" title="Wildfire">\n        fire brands\n       </a>\n       into the burning area and tend to cool the unignited fuel outside the fire area so that ignition of material outside the periphery by radiated heat and fire embers is more difficult, thus limiting fire spread.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AD673703_1961.2C_pg_8_.26_9_4-1">\n        <a href="#cite_note-AD673703_1961.2C_pg_8_.26_9-4">\n         [4]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       At Hiroshima, this inrushing to feed the fire is said to have prevented the firestorm perimeter from expanding, and thus the firestorm was confined to the area of the city damaged by the blast.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">\n        <a href="#cite_note-11">\n         [11]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <div class="thumb tright">\n       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">\n        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:POLARCATfigure4.jpg">\n         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="736" data-file-width="577" height="281" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5e/POLARCATfigure4.jpg/220px-POLARCATfigure4.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5e/POLARCATfigure4.jpg/330px-POLARCATfigure4.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5e/POLARCATfigure4.jpg/440px-POLARCATfigure4.jpg 2x" width="220"/>\n        </a>\n        <div class="thumbcaption">\n         <div class="magnify">\n          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:POLARCATfigure4.jpg" title="Enlarge">\n          </a>\n         </div>\n         Picture of a pyro-cumulonimbus taken from a commercial airliner cruising at about 10 km. In 2002 various sensing instruments detected 17 distinct pyrocumulonimbus cloud events in North America alone.\n         <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">\n          <a href="#cite_note-12">\n           [12]\n          </a>\n         </sup>\n        </div>\n       </div>\n      </div>\n      <p>\n       Large\n       <a href="/wiki/Wildfire" title="Wildfire">\n        wildfire\n       </a>\n       <a href="/wiki/Conflagration" title="Conflagration">\n        conflagrations\n       </a>\n       are distinct from firestorms if they have moving fire fronts which are driven by the ambient wind and do not develop their own wind system like true firestorms. (This does not mean that a firestorm\n       <i>\n        must\n       </i>\n       be stationary; as with any other convective storm, the circulation may follow surrounding pressure gradients and winds, if those lead it onto fresh fuel sources.) Furthermore, non-firestorm conflagrations can develop from a single ignition, whereas firestorms have only been observed where large numbers of fires are burning simultaneously over a relatively large area,\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">\n        <a href="#cite_note-13">\n         [13]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       with the important caveat that the density of simultaneously burning fires needs to be above a critical threshold for a firestorm to form (a notable example of large numbers of fires burning simultaneously over a large area without a firestorm developing was the\n       <a href="/wiki/Kuwaiti_oil_fires" title="Kuwaiti oil fires">\n        Kuwaiti oil fires\n       </a>\n       of 1991, where the distance between individual fires was too large).\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       The high temperatures within the firestorm zone ignite most everything that might possibly burn, until a tipping point is reached, that is, upon running low on fuel, which occurs after the firestorm has consumed so much of the available fuel within the firestorm zone that the necessary fuel density required to keep the firestorm\'s wind system active drops below the threshold level, at which time the firestorm breaks up into isolated\n       <a href="/wiki/Conflagration" title="Conflagration">\n        conflagrations\n       </a>\n       .\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       In Australia, the prevalence of\n       <a href="/wiki/Eucalyptus" title="Eucalyptus">\n        eucalyptus\n       </a>\n       trees that have oil in their leaves results in forest fires that are noted for their extremely tall and intense flame front. Hence the bush fires appear more as a firestorm than a simple forest fire. Sometimes, emission of combustible gases from swamps (e.g.,\n       <a href="/wiki/Methane" title="Methane">\n        methane\n       </a>\n       ) has a similar effect. For instance, methane explosions enforced the Peshtigo Fire.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Denise_Gess_6-1">\n        <a href="#cite_note-Denise_Gess-6">\n         [6]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKartmanBrown197148_14-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKartmanBrown197148-14">\n         [14]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <h3>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Weather_and_climate_effects">\n        Weather and climate effects\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Firestorm&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Weather and climate effects">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h3>\n      <p>\n       Firestorms will produce hot buoyant smoke clouds of primarily water vapor that will form\n       <a href="/wiki/Condensation" title="Condensation">\n        condensation\n       </a>\n       clouds as it enters the cooler upper atmosphere, generating what is known as\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pyrocumulus_cloud" title="Pyrocumulus cloud">\n        pyrocumulus clouds\n       </a>\n       ("fire clouds") or, if large enough,\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pyrocumulonimbus" title="Pyrocumulonimbus">\n        pyrocumulonimbus\n       </a>\n       ("fire storm") clouds. For example, the black rain that began to fall at ~20 minutes after the\n       <a href="/wiki/Atomic_bombings_of_Hiroshima_and_Nagasaki" title="Atomic bombings of Hiroshima and Nagasaki">\n        atomic bombing of Hiroshima\n       </a>\n       produced in total 5\xe2\x80\x9310\xc2\xa0cm of black soot-filled rain in a 1\xe2\x80\x933 hour period.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">\n        <a href="#cite_note-15">\n         [15]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       Moreover, if the conditions are right, a large pyrocumulus can grow into a pyrocumulonimbus and produce\n       <a href="/wiki/Lightning" title="Lightning">\n        lightning\n       </a>\n       , which could potentially set off further fires. Apart from city and forest fires, pyrocumulus clouds can also be produced by\n       <a href="/wiki/Volcano" title="Volcano">\n        volcanic eruptions\n       </a>\n       due to the comparable amounts of hot buoyant material formed.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       On a more continental and global extent, away from the direct vicinity of the fire, wildfire firestorms which produce\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pyrocumulonimbus_cloud" title="Pyrocumulonimbus cloud">\n        pyrocumulonimbus cloud\n       </a>\n       events have been found to "surprisingly frequently" generate minor "\n       <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_winter" title="Nuclear winter">\n        nuclear winter\n       </a>\n       " effects.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-agu.org_16-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-agu.org-16">\n         [16]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17">\n        <a href="#cite_note-17">\n         [17]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18">\n        <a href="#cite_note-18">\n         [18]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19">\n        <a href="#cite_note-19">\n         [19]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       These are analogous to minor\n       <a href="/wiki/Volcanic_winter" title="Volcanic winter">\n        volcanic winters\n       </a>\n       , with each mass addition of\n       <a href="/wiki/Sulfate_aerosol" title="Sulfate aerosol">\n        volcanic gases\n       </a>\n       additive in increasing the depth of the "winter" cooling, from near-imperceptible to "\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Year_without_a_summer" title="Year without a summer">\n        year without a summer\n       </a>\n       " levels.\n      </p>\n      <h2>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="City_firestorms">\n        City firestorms\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Firestorm&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: City firestorms">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h2>\n      <p>\n       The same underlying combustion physics can also apply to man-made structures such as cities during war or natural disaster.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       Firestorms are thought to have been part of the mechanism of large urban fires, such as accompanied the\n       <a href="/wiki/1906_San_Francisco_earthquake" title="1906 San Francisco earthquake">\n        1906 San Francisco earthquake\n       </a>\n       and the\n       <a href="/wiki/1923_Great_Kant%C5%8D_earthquake" title="1923 Great Kant\xc5\x8d earthquake">\n        1923 Great Kant\xc5\x8d earthquake\n       </a>\n       . A genuine firestorm was involved in the\n       <a href="/wiki/Oakland_firestorm_of_1991" title="Oakland firestorm of 1991">\n        1991 wildfire disaster in Oakland, California\n       </a>\n       .\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20">\n        <a href="#cite_note-20">\n         [20]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       Firestorms were also created by the\n       <a href="/wiki/Firebombing" title="Firebombing">\n        firebombing\n       </a>\n       raids of World War\xc2\xa0II in cities like\n       <a href="/wiki/Bombing_of_Hamburg_in_World_War_II" title="Bombing of Hamburg in World War II">\n        Hamburg\n       </a>\n       and\n       <a href="/wiki/Bombing_of_Dresden_in_World_War_II" title="Bombing of Dresden in World War II">\n        Dresden\n       </a>\n       .\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Harris-83_21-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-Harris-83-21">\n         [21]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       Of the\n       <a href="/wiki/Atomic_bombings_of_Hiroshima_and_Nagasaki" title="Atomic bombings of Hiroshima and Nagasaki">\n        two nuclear weapons used in combat\n       </a>\n       , only Hiroshima resulted in a firestorm.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-American_National_Fire_Protection_Association_2005_https:.2F.2Fbooks.google.com.2Fbooks.3Fid.3DIWW8qOXd6sgC.26pg.3DPA68.23v.3Donepage.26q.26f.3Dfalse_68_1-1">\n        <a href="#cite_note-American_National_Fire_Protection_Association_2005_https:.2F.2Fbooks.google.com.2Fbooks.3Fid.3DIWW8qOXd6sgC.26pg.3DPA68.23v.3Donepage.26q.26f.3Dfalse_68-1">\n         [1]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       In contrast, experts suggest that due to the nature of modern U.S.\xc2\xa0city design and construction, a firestorm is unlikely after a nuclear detonation.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hps.org_22-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-hps.org-22">\n         [22]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <table Such raids often employ both incendiary devices and high explosives. The high explosive destroys roofs, making it easier for the incendiary devices to penetrate the structures and cause fires. The high explosives also disrupt the ability of\n       <a href="/wiki/Firefighter" title="Firefighter">\n        firefighters\n       </a>\n       to douse the fires.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Harris-83_21-3">\n        <a href="#cite_note-Harris-83-21">\n         [21]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       Although incendiary bombs have been used to destroy buildings since the start of gunpowder warfare, World War\xc2\xa0II saw the first use of\n       <a href="/wiki/Strategic_bombing" title="Strategic bombing">\n        strategic bombing\n       </a>\n       from the air to destroy the ability of the enemy to wage war.\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/London_Blitz" title="London Blitz">\n        London\n       </a>\n       ,\n       <a href="/wiki/Coventry_Blitz" title="Coventry Blitz">\n        Coventry\n       </a>\n       , and many other British cities were firebombed during\n       <a href="/wiki/The_Blitz" title="The Blitz">\n        the Blitz\n       </a>\n       . Most large German cities were extensively firebombed starting in 1942, and almost all large Japanese cities were firebombed during the last six months of World War\xc2\xa0II. As\n       <a href="/wiki/Sir_Arthur_Harris,_1st_Baronet" title="Sir Arthur Harris, 1st Baronet">\n        Sir Arthur Harris\n       </a>\n       , the officer commanding\n       <a href="/wiki/RAF_Bomber_Command" title="RAF Bomber Command">\n        RAF Bomber Command\n       </a>\n       from 1942 through to the end of the war in Europe, pointed out in his post-war analysis, although many attempts were made to create deliberate man-made firestorms during World War\xc2\xa0II, few attempts succeeded:\n      </p>\n      <blockquote class="templatequote">\n       <p>\n        "The Germans again and again missed their chance, ...of setting our cities ablaze by a concentrated attack. Coventry was adequately concentrated in point of space, but all the same there was little concentration in point of time, and nothing like the fire tornadoes of Hamburg or Dresden ever occurred in this country. But they did do us enough damage to teach us the principle of concentration, the principle of starting so many fires at the same time that no fire fighting services, however efficiently and quickly they were reinforced by the fire brigades of other towns could get them under control."\n       </p>\n       <div class="templatequotecite">\n        \xe2\x80\x94\n        <cite>\n         Arthur that due to the nature of modern U.S.\xc2\xa0city design and construction, a firestorm is unlikely to occur even after a nuclear detonation.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hps.org_22-1">\n        <a href="#cite_note-hps.org-22">\n         [22]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       The explanation for this is that\n       <a href="/wiki/Tower_block" id="cite_ref-American_National_Fire_Protection_Association_2005_https:.2F.2Fbooks.google.com.2Fbooks.3Fid.3DIWW8qOXd6sgC.26pg.3DPA68.23v.3Donepage.26q.26f.3Dfalse_68_1-2">\n        <a </a>\n       </sup>\n       nor are firestorms likely in areas where modern buildings have totally collapsed, with the exceptions of Tokyo and Hiroshima due to the nature of their densely packed "flimsy" wooden buildings in World War II.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Firestorms._pg_53_40-1">\n        <a href="#cite_note-Firestorms._pg_53-40">\n         [40]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-osti.gov_44-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-osti.gov-44">\n         [44]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       There is also a sizable difference between the fuel loading of World War II cities that firestormed and that of modern cities, where the quantity of combustibles per square meter in the fire area in the latter is below the necessary requirement for a firestorm to form (40\xc2\xa0kg/m\xc2\xb2).\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45">\n        <a href="#cite_note-45">\n         [45]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46">\n        <a href="#cite_note-46">\n         [46]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       Therefore, firestorms are not to be expected in modern U.S. or Canadian cities following a nuclear detonation, nor are they expected to be likely in modern European cities.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47">\n        <a href="#cite_note-47">\n         [47]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       Similarly, one reason for the lack of success in creating a true firestorm in the\n       <a href="/wiki/Bombing_of_Berlin_in_World_War_II" title="Bombing of Berlin in World War II">\n        bombing of Berlin in World War II\n       </a>\n       was that the building density, or builtupness factor, in Berlin was too low to support easy fire spread from building to building. Another reason was that much of the building construction was newer and better than in most of the old German city centers. Modern building practices in the Berlin of World War II led to more effective firewalls and fire-resistant construction. Mass firestorms never proved to be possible in Berlin. No matter how heavy the raid or what kinds of firebombs were dropped, no true firestorm ever developed.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48">\n        <a href="#cite_note-48">\n         [48]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <h2>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Nuclear_weapons_in_comparison_to_conventional_weapons">\n        Nuclear weapons in comparison to conventional weapons\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Firestorm&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Nuclear weapons in comparison to conventional weapons">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h2>\n      <p>\n       The incendiary effects of a\n       <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_explosion" title="Nuclear explosion">\n        nuclear explosion\n       </a>\n       do not present any especially characteristic features. In principle, the same overall result with respect to destruction of life and property can be achieved by the use of\n       <a href="/wiki/Conventional_weapon" title="Conventional weapon">\n        conventional\n       </a>\n       <a href="/wiki/Incendiary_device" title="Incendiary device">\n        incendiary\n       </a>\n       and\n       <a href="/wiki/Explosive_material#High_explosives" title="Explosive material">\n        high-explosive bombs\n       </a>\n       .\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-The_Effects_of_Nuclear_Weapons_49-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-The_Effects_of_Nuclear_Weapons-49">\n         [49]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       It has been estimated, for example, that the same fire ferocity and damage produced at Hiroshima by one\n       <a href="/wiki/Little_Boy" title="Little Boy">\n        16-kiloton nuclear bomb\n       </a>\n       from a single\n       <a href="/wiki/Boeing_B-29_Superfortress" title="Boeing B-29 Superfortress">\n        B-29\n       </a>\n       could have instead been produced by about 1,200 tons/1.2 kilotons of incendiary bombs from 220 B-29s distributed over the city.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-The_Effects_of_Nuclear_Weapons_49-1">\n        <a href="#cite_note-The_Effects_of_Nuclear_Weapons-49">\n         [49]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-here_50-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-here-50">\n         [50]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51">\n        <a href="#cite_note-51">\n         [51]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       It may seem counterintuitive that the same amount of fire damage caused by a nuclear weapon could have instead been produced by smaller total yield of thousands of incendiary bombs; however, World War II experience supports this assertion. For example, although not a perfect clone of the city of Hiroshima in 1945, in the conventional\n       <a href="/wiki/Bombing_of_Dresden_in_World_War_II" title="Bombing of Dresden in World War II">\n        bombing of Dresden\n       </a>\n       , the combined\n       <a href="/wiki/Royal_Air_Force" title="Royal Air Force">\n        Royal Air Force\n       </a>\n       (RAF) and\n       <a href="/wiki/United_States_Army_Air_Forces" title="United States Army Air Forces">\n        United States Army Air Forces\n       </a>\n       (USAAF) dropped a total of 3441.3 tons (approximately 3.4\n       <a href="/wiki/TNT_equivalent" title="TNT equivalent">\n        kilotons\n       </a>\n       ) of\n       <a href="/wiki/Aircraft_ordnance" title="Aircraft ordnance">\n        ordnance\n       </a>\n       (about half of which was incendiary bombs) on the night of 13\xe2\x80\x9314 February 1945, and this resulted in "more than" 2.5 square miles (6.5\xc2\xa0km\n       <sup>\n        2\n       </sup>\n       ) of the city being destroyed by fire and firestorm effects according to one authoritative source,\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52">\n        <a href="#cite_note-52">\n         [52]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       or approximately 8 square miles (21\xc2\xa0km\n       <sup>\n        2\n       </sup>\n       ) by another.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dtic.mil_24-3">\n        <a href="#cite_note-dtic.mil-24">\n         [24]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       In total about 4.5 kilotons of conventional ordnance was dropped on the city over a number of months during 1945 and this resulted in approximately 15 square miles (39\xc2\xa0km\n       <sup>\n        2\n       </sup>\n       ) of the city being destroyed by blast and fire effects.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53">\n        <a href="#cite_note-53">\n         [53]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       During the\n       <a href="/wiki/Bombing_of_Tokyo" title="Bombing of Tokyo">\n        <i>\n         Operation MeetingHouse\n        </i>\n        firebombing of Tokyo\n       </a>\n       on 9\xe2\x80\x9310 March 1945, 279 of the 334 B-29s dropped 1,665 tons of incendiary and high-explosive bombs on the city, resulting in the destruction of over 10,000 acres of buildings \xe2\x80\x94 16 square miles (41\xc2\xa0km\n       <sup>\n        2\n       </sup>\n       ), a quarter of the city.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Laurence_M._Vance_54-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-Laurence_M._Vance-54">\n         [54]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Joseph_Coleman_55-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-Joseph_Coleman-55">\n         [55]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       In contrast to these raids, when a single 16-kiloton nuclear bomb was dropped on Hiroshima, 4.5 square miles (12\xc2\xa0km\n       <sup>\n        2\n       </sup>\n       ) of the city was destroyed by blast, fire, and firestorm effects.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Firestorms._pg_53_40-2">\n        <a href="#cite_note-Firestorms._pg_53-40">\n         [40]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       Similarly, Major Cortez F. Enloe, a surgeon in the USAAF who worked with the\n       <a href="/wiki/United_States_Strategic_Bombing_Survey" title="United States Strategic Bombing Survey">\n        United States Strategic Bombing Survey\n       </a>\n       (USSBS), said that the\n       <a href="/wiki/Fat_Man" title="Fat Man">\n        22-kiloton nuclear bomb\n       </a>\n       dropped on Nagasaki did not do as much fire damage as the extended\n       <a href="/wiki/Bombing_of_Hamburg_in_World_War_II" title="Bombing of Hamburg in World War II">\n        conventional airstrikes on Hamburg\n       </a>\n       .\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56">\n        <a href="#cite_note-56">\n         [56]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <ul class="gallery mw-gallery-packed">\n       <li class="gallerybox" style="width: 257.33333333333px">\n        <div style="width: 257.33333333333px">\n         <div class="thumb" style="width: 255.33333333333px;">\n          <div style="margin:0px auto;">\n           <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:AtomicEffects-Hiroshima.jpg">\n            <img alt="" data-file-height="512" data-file-width="653" height="200" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2c/AtomicEffects-Hiroshima.jpg/383px-AtomicEffects-Hiroshima.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2c/AtomicEffects-Hiroshima.jpg/574px-AtomicEffects-Hiroshima.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/2/2c/AtomicEffects-Hiroshima.jpg 2x" width="256"/>\n           </a>\n          </div>\n         </div>\n         <div class="gallerytext">\n          <p>\n           Hiroshima after the bombing and firestorm. The firebombing of Tokyo on the night of 9\xe2\x80\x9310 March 1945 was the single deadliest air raid of World War II,\n           <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-deadliest_57-0">\n            <a href="#cite_note-deadliest-57">\n             [57]\n            </a>\n           </sup>\n           with a greater total area of fire damage and loss of life than either nuclear bombing as a single event.\n           <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Laurence_M._Vance_54-1">\n            <a href="#cite_note-Laurence_M._Vance-54">\n             [54]\n            </a>\n           </sup>\n           <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Joseph_Coleman_55-1">\n            <a href="#cite_note-Joseph_Coleman-55">\n             [55]\n            </a>\n           </sup>\n           Due largely to the greater population density and fire conditions. 279 B-29s dropped about 1,700 tons of ordnance on target.\n           <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mar45Chronology_35-1">\n            <a href="#cite_note-Mar45Chronology-35">\n             [35]\n            </a>\n           </sup>\n          </p>\n         </div>\n        </div>\n       </li>\n       <li class="gallerybox" style="width: 288px">\n        <div style="width: 288px">\n         <div class="thumb" style="width: 286px;">\n          <div style="margin:0px auto;">\n           <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Hiroshima_aftermath.jpg">\n            <img alt="" data-file-height="546" data-file-width="780" height="200" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a0/Hiroshima_aftermath.jpg/429px-Hiroshima_aftermath.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a0/Hiroshima_aftermath.jpg/643px-Hiroshima_aftermath.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/a/a0/Hiroshima_aftermath.jpg 2x" width="286"/>\n           </a>\n          </div>\n         </div>\n         <div class="gallerytext">\n          <p>\n           Hiroshima aftermath. Signed by the\n           <a href="/wiki/Enola_Gay" title="Enola Gay">\n            Enola Gay\n           </a>\n           pilot,\n           <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Paul_W._Tibbets" title="Paul W. All that remained standing were concrete buildings in this photograph.\n          </p>\n         </div>\n        </div>\n       </li>\n      </ul>\n      <div style="clear:both;">\n      </div>\n      <p>\n       American historian\n       <a href="/wiki/Gabriel_Kolko" title="Gabriel Kolko">\n        Gabriel Kolko\n       </a>\n       also echoed this sentiment:\n      </p>\n      <blockquote class="templatequote">\n       <p>\n        During November 1944 American B-29\'s began their first\n        <a href="/wiki/Firebombing" title="Firebombing">\n         incendiary bomb raids\n        </a>\n        on Tokyo, and on 9 March 1945, wave upon wave dropped masses of small incendiaries containing an early version of\n        <a href="/wiki/Napalm" title="Napalm">\n         napalm\n        </a>\n        on the city\'s population....Soon small fires spread, connected, grew into a vast firestorm that sucked the oxygen out of the lower atmosphere. The bomb raid was a \'success\' for the Americans; they killed 125,000 Japanese in one attack. The\n        <a href="/wiki/Allies_of_World_War_II" title="Allies of World War II">\n         Allies\n        </a>\n        bombed Hamburg and Dresden in the same manner, and\n        <a href="/wiki/Bombing_of_Nagoya_in_World_War_II" title="Bombing of Nagoya in World War II">\n         Nagoya\n        </a>\n        ,\n        <a href="/wiki/Bombing_of_Osaka" title="Bombing of Osaka">\n         Osaka\n        </a>\n        ,\n        <a href="/wiki/Bombing_of_Kobe_in_World_War_II" title="Bombing of Kobe in World War II">\n         Kobe\n        </a>\n        , and Tokyo again on May 24....in fact the atomic bomb used against Hiroshima was less lethal than massive fire bombing....Only its technique was novel\xe2\x80\x94nothing more....There was another difficulty posed by mass conventional bombing, and that was its very success, a success that made the two modes of human destruction qualitatively identical in fact and in the minds of the\n        <a href="/wiki/United_States_Armed_Forces" title="United States Armed Forces">\n         American military\n        </a>\n        . "I was a little fearful",\n        <a href="/wiki/United_States_Secretary_of_War" title="United States Secretary of War">\n         [Secretary of War]\n        </a>\n        <a href="/wiki/Henry_L._Stimson" title="Henry L. Stimson">\n         Stimson\n        </a>\n        told\n        <a href="/wiki/President_of_the_United_States" title="President of the United States">\n         [President]\n        </a>\n        <a href="/wiki/Harry_S._Truman" title="Harry S. Truman">\n         Truman\n        </a>\n        , "that before we could get ready the Air Force might have Japan so thoroughly bombed out that the new weapon would not have a fair background to show its strength." To this the President "laughed and said he understood."\n        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58">\n         <a href="#cite_note-58">\n          [58]\n         </a>\n        </sup>\n       </p>\n      </blockquote>\n      <p>\n       This break from the linear expectation of more fire damage to occur after greater explosive yield is dropped can be easily explained by two major factors. First, the order of blast and thermal events during a nuclear explosion is not ideal for the creation of fires. In an incendiary bombing raid, incendiary weapons followed after high-explosive blast weapons were dropped, in a manner designed to create the greatest probability of fires from a\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cluster_bomb" title="Cluster bomb">\n        limited quantity of explosive\n       </a>\n       and incendiary weapons. The so-called two-ton "\n       <a href="/wiki/Blockbuster_bomb" title="Blockbuster bomb">\n        cookies\n       </a>\n       ",\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-De-Bruhl-209_28-1">\n        <a href="#cite_note-De-Bruhl-209-28">\n         [28]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       also known as "blockbusters", were dropped first and were intended to rupture water mains, as well as to blow off roofs, doors, and windows, creating an air flow that would feed the fires caused by the incendiaries that would then follow and be dropped, ideally, into holes created by the prior blast weapons, such into attic and roof spaces.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-De-Bruhl-210-11_59-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-De-Bruhl-210-11-59">\n         [59]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Taylor-287-365_60-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-Taylor-287-365-60">\n         [60]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Longmate162-4_61-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-Longmate162-4-61">\n         [61]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       On the other hand, nuclear weapons produce effects that are in the reverse order, with thermal effects and "flash" occurring first, which are then followed by the slower blast wave. It is for this reason that conventional incendiary bombing raids are considered to be a great deal more efficient at causing mass fires than nuclear weapons of comparable yield. It is likely this led the nuclear weapon effects experts\n       <a href="/wiki/Franklin_D%27Olier" title="Franklin D\'Olier">\n        Franklin D\'Olier\n       </a>\n       ,\n       <a href="/wiki/Samuel_Glasstone" title="Samuel Glasstone">\n        Samuel Glasstone\n       </a>\n       and\n       <a href="/wiki/Philip_J._Dolan" title="Philip J. Dolan">\n        Philip J. Dolan\n       </a>\n       to state that the same fire damage suffered at Hiroshima could have instead been produced by about 1 kiloton/1,000 tons of incendiary bombs.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-The_Effects_of_Nuclear_Weapons_49-2">\n        <a href="#cite_note-The_Effects_of_Nuclear_Weapons-49">\n         [49]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-here_50-1">\n        <a href="#cite_note-here-50">\n         [50]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       The second factor explaining the non-intuitive break in the expected results of greater explosive yield producing greater city fire damage is that city fire damage is largely dependent not on the yield of the weapons used, but on the conditions in and around the city itself, with the fuel loading per square meter value of the city being one of the major factors. A few hundred strategically placed incendiary devices would be sufficient to start a firestorm in a city if the conditions for a firestorm, namely high fuel loading, are already inherent to the city (see\n       <a href="/wiki/Bat_bomb" title="Bat bomb">\n        Bat bomb\n       </a>\n       ). The\n       <a href="/wiki/Great_Fire_of_London" title="Great Fire of London">\n        Great Fire of London\n       </a>\n       in 1666, although not forming a firestorm due to the single point of ignition, serves as an example that, given a densely packed and predominately wooden and\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thatch" title="Thatch">\n        thatch\n       </a>\n       building construction in the urban area, a mass fire is conceivable from the mere incendiary power of no more than a domestic fireplace. On the other hand, the largest nuclear weapon conceivable will be incapable of igniting a city into a firestorm if the city\'s properties, namely its fuel density, are not conducive to one developing.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       Despite the disadvantage of nuclear weapons when compared to conventional weapons of lower or comparable yield in terms of effectiveness at starting fires, for the reasons discussed above, nuclear weapons also do not add any fuel to a city, and fires are entirely dependent on what was contained in the city prior to bombing, in direct contrast to the incendiary device effect of conventional raids. One undeniable advantage of nuclear weapons over conventional weapons when it comes to creating fires is that nuclear weapons undoubtedly produce all their thermal and explosive effects in a very short period of time; that is, to use\n       <a href="/wiki/Sir_Arthur_Harris,_1st_Baronet" title="Sir Arthur Harris, 1st Baronet">\n        Arthur Harris\n       </a>\n       \'s terminology, they are the epitome of an air raid guaranteed to be concentrated in "point in time". In contrast, early in World War II, the ability to achieve conventional air raids concentrated in "point of time" depended largely upon the skill of pilots to remain in formation, and their ability to hit the target whilst at times also being under heavy fire from\n       <a href="/wiki/Anti-aircraft_warfare" title="Anti-aircraft warfare">\n        anti-aircraft fire\n       </a>\n       from the air defensives of the cities below. Nuclear weapons largely remove these uncertain variables. Therefore, nuclear weapons reduce the question of whether a city will firestorm or not to a smaller number of variables, to the point of becoming entirely reliant on the intrinsic properties of the city, such as fuel loading, and predictable atmospheric conditions, such as wind speed, in and around the city, and less reliant on the unpredictable possibility of hundreds of bomber crews acting together successfully as a single unit.\n      </p>\n      <h2>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">\n        See also\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span
