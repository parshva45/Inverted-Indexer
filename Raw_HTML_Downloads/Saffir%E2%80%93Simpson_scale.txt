Saffir, a\n       <a href="/wiki/Structural_engineering" title="Structural engineering">\n        structural engineer\n       </a>\n       , who in 1969 went on commission for the\n       <a href="/wiki/United_Nations" title="United Nations">\n        United Nations\n       </a>\n       to study low-cost housing in hurricane-prone areas.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_4-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-:0-4">\n         [4]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       While performing the study, Saffir realized there was no simple scale for describing the likely effects of a hurricane. Mirroring the utility of the\n       <a href="/wiki/Richter_magnitude_scale" title="Richter magnitude scale">\n        Richter magnitude scale\n       </a>\n       in describing earthquakes, he devised a 1\xe2\x80\x935 scale based on\n       <a href="/wiki/Wind_speed" title="Wind speed">\n        wind speed\n       </a>\n       that showed expected damage to structures. Saffir gave the scale to the NHC, and Simpson added the effects of\n       <a href="/wiki/Storm_surge" title="Storm surge">\n        storm surge\n       </a>\n       and flooding.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       In 2009, the NHC made moves to eliminate pressure and storm surge ranges from the categories, transforming it into a pure wind scale, called the Saffir\xe2\x80\x93Simpson Hurricane Wind Scale (Experimental) [SSHWS].\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NHC_SSHS_5-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-NHC_SSHS-5">\n         [5]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       The new scale became operational on May 15, 2010.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">\n        <a href="#cite_note-6">\n         [6]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       The scale excludes flood ranges, storm surge estimations, rainfall, and location, which means a Category\xc2\xa02 hurricane which hits a major city will likely do far more cumulative damage than a Category\xc2\xa05 hurricane that hits a rural area.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LiveScience_7-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-LiveScience-7">\n         [7]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       The agency cited various hurricanes as reasons for removing the "scientifically inaccurate" information, including\n       <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Katrina" title="Hurricane Katrina">\n        Hurricane Katrina\n       </a>\n       (2005) and\n       <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Ike" title="Hurricane Ike">\n        Hurricane Ike\n       </a>\n       (2008), which both had stronger than estimated storm surges, and\n       <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Charley" title="Hurricane Charley">\n        Hurricane Charley\n       </a>\n       (2004), which had weaker than estimated storm surge.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">\n        <a href="#cite_note-8">\n         [8]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       Since removed from the Saffir\xe2\x80\x93Simpson hurricane wind scale, storm surge predicting and modeling is now handled with the use of a computerized numerical model developed by the National Weather Service called "\n       <a href="/wiki/Sea,_Lake,_and_Overland_Surge_from_Hurricanes" title="Sea, Lake, and Overland Surge from Hurricanes">\n        Sea, Lake, and Overland Surge from Hurricanes\n       </a>\n       " (SLOSH).\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       In 2012, the NHC expanded the windspeed range for Category 4 by 1\xc2\xa0mph in both directions, to 130\xe2\x80\x93156\xc2\xa0mph, with corresponding changes in the other units (113\xe2\x80\x93136 kn, 209\xe2\x80\x93251\xc2\xa0km/h), instead of 131\xe2\x80\x93155\xc2\xa0mph (114\xe2\x80\x93135 kn, 210\xe2\x80\x93249\xc2\xa0km/h). The NHC and the\n       <a href="/wiki/Central_Pacific_Hurricane_Center" title="Central Pacific Hurricane Center">\n        Central Pacific Hurricane Center\n       </a>\n       assign tropical cyclone intensities in 5 knot increments, and then convert to mph and km/h with a similar rounding for other reports. So an intensity of 115 knots is rated Category 4, but the conversion to miles per hour (132.3\xc2\xa0mph) would round down to 130\xc2\xa0mph, making it appear to be a Category 3 storm. Likewise, an intensity of 135 knots (~155\xc2\xa0mph, and thus Category 4) is 250.02\xc2\xa0km/h, which according to the definition used before the change would be Category 5. To resolve these issues, the NHC had been obliged to incorrectly report storms with wind speeds of 115 kn as 135\xc2\xa0mph, and 135 kn as 245\xc2\xa0km/h. The change in definition allows storms of 115 kn to be correctly rounded down to 130\xc2\xa0mph, and storms of 135 kn to be correctly reported as 250\xc2\xa0km/h, and still qualify as Category 4. Since the NHC had previously rounded incorrectly to keep storms in Category 4 in each unit of measure, the change does not affect the classification of storms from previous years.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NHC_SSHS_5-1">\n        <a href="#cite_note-NHC_SSHS-5">\n         [5]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       The new scale became operational on May 15, 2012.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">\n        <a href="#cite_note-9">\n         [9]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <h2>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Categories">\n        Categories\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Saffir%E2%80%93Simpson_scale&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Categories">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h2>\n      <p>\n       The scale separates hurricanes into five different categories based on wind. The U.S. National Hurricane Center classifies hurricanes of Category\xc2\xa03 and above as\n       <i>\n        major hurricanes\n       </i>\n       , and the Joint Typhoon Warning Center classifies typhoons of 150\xc2\xa0mph or greater (strong Category 4 and Category 5) as\n       <i>\n        super typhoons\n       </i>\n       (although all tropical cyclones can be very dangerous). Most weather agencies use the definition for sustained winds recommended by the\n       <a href="/wiki/World_Meteorological_Organization" title="World Meteorological Organization">\n        World Meteorological Organization\n       </a>\n       (WMO), which specifies measuring winds at a height of 33\xc2\xa0ft (10.1\xc2\xa0m) for 10\xc2\xa0minutes, and then taking the average. By contrast, the U.S.\n       <a href="/wiki/National_Weather_Service" title="National Weather Service">\n        National Weather Service\n       </a>\n       ,\n       <a href="/wiki/Central_Pacific_Hurricane_Center" title="Central Pacific Hurricane Center">\n        Central Pacific Hurricane Center\n       </a>\n       and the\n       <a href="/wiki/Joint_Typhoon_Warning_Center" title="Joint Typhoon Warning Center">\n        Joint Typhoon Warning Center\n       </a>\n       define sustained winds as average winds over a period of one minute, measured at the same 33\xc2\xa0ft (10.1\xc2\xa0m) height,\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NWSM_Defs_10-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-NWSM_Defs-10">\n         [10]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FEMA_glossary_11-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-FEMA_glossary-11">\n         [11]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       and that is the definition used for this scale. Intensity of example hurricanes is from both the time of landfall and the maximum intensity.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       The scale is roughly logarithmic in wind speed, and the top wind speed for Category "\n       <i>\n        c\n       </i>\n       " (\n       <i>\n        c\n       </i>\n       =1 to 4, as there is no upper limit for category 5) can be expressed as 83x10^(c/15) miles per hour rounded to the nearest multiple of 5 \xe2\x80\x93 except that after the change mentioned above, Category 4 is now widened by 1\xc2\xa0mph in each direction.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       The five categories are, in order of increasing intensity:\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">\n        <a href="#cite_note-12">\n         [12]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <h3>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Category_1">\n        Category 1\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Saffir%E2%80%93Simpson_scale&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Category 1">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h3>\n      <table class="wikitable" style="text-align: center;    <b>\n         Catastrophic damage will occur\n        </b>\n       </i>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       Category\xc2\xa05 is the highest category of the Saffir\xe2\x80\x93Simpson scale. These storms cause complete roof failure on many residences and industrial buildings, and some complete building failures with small utility buildings blown over or away. Collapse of many wide-span roofs and walls, especially those with no interior supports, is common. Very heavy and irreparable damage to many wood frame structures and total destruction to mobile/manufactured homes is prevalent. Only a few types of structures are capable of surviving intact, and only if located at least 3 to 5 miles (5 to 8\xc2\xa0km) inland. They include office, condominium and apartment buildings and hotels that are of solid concrete or steel frame construction, public multi-story concrete parking garages, and residences that are made of either reinforced brick or\n       <a href="/wiki/Concrete" title="Concrete">\n        concrete\n       </a>\n       /\n       <a href="/wiki/Cement" title="Cement">\n        cement\n       </a>\n       block and have\n       <a href="/wiki/Hip_roof" title="Hip roof">\n        hipped roofs\n       </a>\n       with slopes of no less than 35\xc2\xa0degrees from horizontal and no overhangs of any kind, and if the windows are either made of hurricane-resistant safety glass or covered with shutters. Unless all of these requirements are met, the absolute destruction of a structure is certain.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NHC_SSHS_5-6">\n        <a href="#cite_note-NHC_SSHS-5">\n         [5]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       The storm\'s flooding causes major damage to the lower floors of all structures near the shoreline, and many coastal structures can be completely flattened or washed away by the storm surge. Virtually all trees are uprooted or snapped and some may be debarked, isolating most affected communities. Massive\n       <a href="/wiki/Emergency_evacuation" title="Emergency evacuation">\n        evacuation\n       </a>\n       of residential areas may be required if the hurricane threatens populated areas. Total and extremely long-lived power outages and water losses are to be expected, possibly for up to several months.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NHC_SSHS_5-7">\n        <a href="#cite_note-NHC_SSHS-5">\n         [5]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       Historical examples of storms that made landfall at Category 5 status include:\n       <a "Okeechobee"\n       </a>\n       (1928),\n       <a href="/wiki/1932_Bahamas_hurricane" title="1932 Bahamas hurricane">\n        "Bahamas"\n       </a>\n       (1932),\n       <a href="/wiki/1933_Cuba%E2%80%93Brownsville_hurricane" title="1933 Cuba\xe2\x80\x93Brownsville hurricane">\n        "Cuba\xe2\x80\x93Brownsville"\n       </a>\n       (1933),\n       <a     (1988),\n       <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Andrew" title="Hurricane Andrew">\n        Andrew\n       </a>\n       (1992),\n       <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Dean" title="Hurricane Dean">\n Irma">\n        Irma\n       </a>\n       (2017),\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">\n        <a href="#cite_note-13">\n         [13]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       and\n       <a     </a>\n       </sup>\n       No Category 5 hurricane is known to have made landfall at that strength in the eastern Pacific basin.\n      </p>\n      <h2>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Criticism">\n        Criticism\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Saffir%E2%80%93Simpson_scale&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Criticism">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h2>\n      <p>\n       Some scientists, including\n       <a href="/wiki/Kerry_Emanuel" title="Kerry Emanuel">\n        Kerry Emanuel\n       </a>\n       and Lakshmi Kantha, have criticized the scale as being simplistic, indicating that the scale takes into account neither the physical size of a storm nor the amount of\n       <a href="/wiki/Precipitation" title="Precipitation">\n        precipitation\n       </a>\n       it produces.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LiveScience_7-1">\n        <a href="#cite_note-LiveScience-7">\n         [7]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       Additionally, they and others point out that the Saffir\xe2\x80\x93Simpson scale, unlike the Richter scale used to measure\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Earthquakes" title="Earthquakes">\n        earthquakes\n       </a>\n       , is not continuous, and is\n       <a href="/wiki/Quantization_(signal_processing)" title="Quantization (signal processing)">\n        quantized\n       </a>\n       into a small number of categories. Proposed replacement classifications include the Hurricane Intensity Index, which is based on the\n       <a href="/wiki/Dynamic_pressure" title="Dynamic pressure">\n        dynamic pressure\n       </a>\n       caused by a storm\'s winds, and the Hurricane Hazard Index, which is based on surface wind speeds, the\n       <a href="/wiki/Radius_of_maximum_wind" title="Radius of maximum wind">\n        radius of maximum winds\n       </a>\n       of the storm, and its\n       <a href="/wiki/Translation_(geometry)" title="Translation (geometry)">\n        translational\n       </a>\n       velocity.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kantha_HHI_HII_15-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-Kantha_HHI_HII-15">\n         [15]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kantha_BAMS_16-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-Kantha_BAMS-16">\n         [16]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       Both of these scales are continuous, akin to the Richter scale;\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-UCL_alternates_17-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-UCL_alternates-17">\n         [17]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       however, neither of these scales have been used by officials.\n      </p>\n      <h3>\n       <span id=\'"Category_6"\'>\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id=".22Category_6.22">\n        "Category 6"\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Saffir%E2%80%93Simpson_scale&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title=\'Edit section: "Category 6"\'>\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h3>\n      <p>\n       After the series of powerful storm systems of the\n       <a href="/wiki/2005_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="2005 Atlantic hurricane season">\n        2005 Atlantic hurricane season\n       </a>\n       , a few newspaper columnists and scientists brought up the suggestion of introducing Category\xc2\xa06, and they have suggested pegging Category\xc2\xa06 to storms with winds greater than 174 or 180\xc2\xa0mph (78 or 80\xc2\xa0m/s; 151 or 156\xc2\xa0kn; 280 or 290\xc2\xa0km/h).\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LiveScience_7-2">\n        <a href="#cite_note-LiveScience-7">\n         [7]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18">\n        <a href="#cite_note-18">\n         [18]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       Only a few storms of this intensity have been recorded. Of the 33 hurricanes currently considered to have attained Category 5 status in the Atlantic, 17 had wind speeds at 175\xc2\xa0mph (78\xc2\xa0m/s; 152\xc2\xa0kn; 282\xc2\xa0km/h) or greater and only seven had wind speeds at 180\xc2\xa0mph (80\xc2\xa0m/s; 160\xc2\xa0kn; 290\xc2\xa0km/h) or greater (the\n       <a href="/wiki/1935_Labor_Day_hurricane" title="1935 Labor Day hurricane">\n        1935 Labor Day hurricane\n       </a>\n       ,\n       <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Allen" title="Hurricane Allen">\n        Allen\n       </a>\n       ,\n       <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Gilbert" title="Hurricane Gilbert">\n        Gilbert\n       </a>\n       ,\n       <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Mitch" title="Hurricane Mitch">\n        Mitch\n       </a>\n       ,\n       <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Rita" title="Hurricane Rita">\n        Rita\n       </a>\n       ,\n       <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Wilma" title="Hurricane Wilma">\n        Wilma\n       </a>\n       , and\n       <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Irma" title="Hurricane Irma">\n        Irma\n       </a>\n       ). Of the 15 hurricanes currently considered to have attained Category 5 status in the eastern Pacific, only six had wind speeds at 175\xc2\xa0mph (78\xc2\xa0m/s; 152\xc2\xa0kn; 282\xc2\xa0km/h) or greater (\n       <a href="/wiki/1959_Pacific_hurricane_season#Hurricane_Patsy" title="1959 Pacific hurricane season">\n        Patsy\n       </a>\n       ,\n       <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_John_(1994)" title="Hurricane John (1994)">\n        John\n       </a>\n       ,\n       <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Linda_(1997)" title="Hurricane Linda (1997)">\n        Linda\n       </a>\n       ,\n       <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Rick_(2009)" title="Hurricane Rick (2009)">\n        Rick\n       </a>\n       , and\n       <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Patricia" title="Hurricane Patricia">\n        Patricia\n       </a>\n       ), and only three had wind speeds at 180\xc2\xa0mph (80\xc2\xa0m/s; 160\xc2\xa0kn; 290\xc2\xa0km/h) or greater (Linda, Rick, and Patricia). However, most storms which would be eligible for this category were\n       <a href="/wiki/Typhoon" title="Typhoon">\n        typhoons\n       </a>\n       in the western Pacific, most notably\n       <a href="/wiki/Typhoon_Tip" title="Typhoon Tip">\n        Typhoon Tip\n       </a>\n       in 1979 with sustained winds of 190\xc2\xa0mph (310\xc2\xa0km/h) and typhoons\n       <a href="/wiki/Typhoon_Haiyan" title="Typhoon Haiyan">\n        Haiyan\n       </a>\n       and\n       <a href="/wiki/Typhoon_Meranti" title="Typhoon Meranti">\n        Meranti\n       </a>\n       in 2013 and 2016, respectively, with sustained winds of 195\xc2\xa0mph (314\xc2\xa0km/h).\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19">\n        <a href="#cite_note-19">\n         [19]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       According to\n       <a href="/wiki/Robert_Simpson_(meteorologist)" title="Robert Simpson (meteorologist)">\n        Robert Simpson\n       </a>\n       , there are no reasons for a Category\xc2\xa06 on the Saffir\xe2\x80\x93Simpson Scale because it is designed to measure the potential damage of a hurricane to human-made structures. Simpson stated that "...when you get up into winds in excess of 155\xc2\xa0mph (249\xc2\xa0km/h) you have enough damage if that extreme wind sustains itself for as much as six seconds on a building it\'s going to cause rupturing damages that are serious no matter how well it\'s engineered."\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Simpson_interview_3-1">\n        <a href="#cite_note-Simpson_interview-3">\n         [3]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       Despite his statements, the counties of Broward and Miami-Dade in Florida have building codes which require critical infrastructure buildings to be able to withstand Category\xc2\xa05 winds,\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Irma_could_test_strength_of_Florida.E2.80.99s_strict_building_codes_20-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-Irma_could_test_strength_of_Florida.E2.80.99s_strict_building_codes-20">\n         [20]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       which contradicts Mr. Simpson\'s assessment of building strength.\n      </p>\n      <h2>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">\n        See also\n
