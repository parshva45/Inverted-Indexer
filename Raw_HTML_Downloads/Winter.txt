Winter is caused by the axis of the Earth in that hemisphere being oriented away from the\n       <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">\n        Sun\n       </a>\n       . Different cultures define different dates as the start of winter, and some use a definition based on weather. When it is winter in the\n       <a href="/wiki/Northern_Hemisphere" title="Northern Hemisphere">\n        Northern Hemisphere\n       </a>\n       , it is\n       <a href="/wiki/Summer" title="Summer">\n        summer\n       </a>\n       in the\n       <a href="/wiki/Southern_Hemisphere" title="Southern Hemisphere">\n        Southern Hemisphere\n       </a>\n       , and vice versa. In many regions, winter is associated with\n       <a href="/wiki/Snow" title="Snow">\n        snow\n       </a>\n       and\n       <a href="/wiki/Freezing" title="Freezing">\n        freezing\n       </a>\n       temperatures. The moment of\n       <a href="/wiki/Winter_solstice" title="Winter solstice">\n        winter solstice\n       </a>\n       is when the sun\'s elevation with respect to the North or South Pole is at its most negative value (that is, the sun is at its farthest below the horizon as measured from the pole), meaning this day will have the shortest day and the longest night. class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Winter&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h2>\n      <p>\n       The\n       <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">\n        English\n       </a>\n       word "winter" comes from the\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Proto-Indo-European" title="Proto-Indo-European">\n        Proto-Indo-European\n       </a>\n       root "wend," relating to\n       <a href="/wiki/Water" title="Water">\n        water\n       </a>\n       .\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">\n        <a href="#cite_note-1">\n         [1]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <h2>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Cause">\n        Cause\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Winter&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Cause">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h2>\n      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">\n       See also:\n       <a href="/wiki/Effect_of_Sun_angle_on_climate" title="Effect of Sun angle on climate">\n        Effect of Sun angle on climate\n       </a>\n      </div>\n      <p>\n       The tilt of the Earth\'s axis relative to its orbital plane plays a large role in the formation of weather. The Earth is tilted at an angle of 23.44\xc2\xb0 to the plane of its orbit, causing different latitudes to directly face the Sun as the Earth moves through its orbit. This variation brings about seasons. When it is winter in the Northern Hemisphere, the Southern Hemisphere faces the Sun more directly and thus experiences warmer temperatures than the Northern Hemisphere. Conversely, winter in the Southern Hemisphere occurs when the Northern Hemisphere is tilted more toward the Sun. From the perspective of an observer on the Earth, the winter Sun has a lower maximum altitude in the sky than the summer Sun.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       During winter in either hemisphere, the lower altitude of the Sun causes the sunlight to hit that hemisphere at an oblique angle. In regions experiencing winter, the same amount of\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solar_radiation" title="Solar radiation">\n        solar radiation\n       </a>\n       is spread out over a larger area. This effect is compounded by the larger distance that the light must travel through the atmosphere, allowing the atmosphere to dissipate more heat. Compared with these effects, the changes in the distance of the earth from the sun are negligible.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       The manifestation of the meteorological winter (freezing temperatures) in the northerly snow\xe2\x80\x93prone parallels is highly variable depending on elevation, position versus marine winds and the amount of precipitation. A case in point is\n       <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">\n        Canada\n       </a>\n       , a country normally associated with tough winters.\n       <a href="/wiki/Winnipeg" title="Winnipeg">\n        Winnipeg\n       </a>\n       on the\n       <a href="/wiki/Great_Plains" title="Great Plains">\n        Great Plains\n       </a>\n       at a relative distance from large bodies of water has a January high of \xe2\x88\x9211.3\xc2\xa0\xc2\xb0C (11.7\xc2\xa0\xc2\xb0F) and a low of \xe2\x88\x9221.4\xc2\xa0\xc2\xb0C (\xe2\x88\x926.5\xc2\xa0\xc2\xb0F).\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">\n        <a href="#cite_note-2">\n         [2]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       In comparison,\n       <a href="/wiki/Vancouver" title="Vancouver">\n        Vancouver\n       </a>\n       on the coast with a marine influence from moderating Pacific winds has a January low of 1.4\xc2\xa0\xc2\xb0C (34.5\xc2\xa0\xc2\xb0F) with days well above freezing at 6.9\xc2\xa0\xc2\xb0C (44.4\xc2\xa0\xc2\xb0F).\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">\n        <a href="#cite_note-3">\n         [3]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       Both areas are on the\n       <a href="/wiki/49th_parallel_north" title="49th parallel north">\n        49th parallel north\n       </a>\n       and in the same western half of the continent. A similar effect, although with less extreme differentials, is found in Europe where in spite of the northerly latitude of the islands, the British Isles has not a single non-mountain weather station with a below-freezing mean temperature.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">\n        <a href="#cite_note-4">\n         [4]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <h2>\n <b>\n        Meteorological Reckoning\n       </b>\n       is the method of measuring the winter season used by\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Meteorologists" title="Meteorologists">\n        meteorologists\n       </a>\n       based on "sensible weather patterns" for record keeping purposes,\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">\n        <a href="#cite_note-5">\n         [5]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       so the start of meteorological winter varies with latitude.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">\n        <a href="#cite_note-6">\n         [6]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       Winter is often defined by meteorologists to be the three calendar months with the lowest average temperatures. This corresponds to the months of December, January and February in the\n       <a href="/wiki/Northern_Hemisphere" title="Northern Hemisphere">\n        Northern Hemisphere\n       </a>\n       , and June, July and August in the\n       <a href="/wiki/Southern_Hemisphere" title="Southern Hemisphere">\n        Southern Hemisphere\n       </a>\n       . The coldest average temperatures of the season are typically experienced in January or February in the Northern Hemisphere and in June, July or August in the Southern Hemisphere. Nighttime predominates in the winter season, and in some regions winter has the highest rate of\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Precipitation_(meteorology)" title="Precipitation (meteorology)">\n        precipitation\n       </a>\n       as well as prolonged dampness because of permanent snow cover or high precipitation rates coupled with low temperatures, precluding evaporation.\n       <a href="/wiki/Blizzard" title="Blizzard">\n        Blizzards\n       </a>\n       often develop and cause many transportation delays.\n       <a href="/wiki/Diamond_dust" title="Diamond dust">\n        Diamond dust\n       </a>\n       , also known as ice needles or ice crystals, forms at temperatures approaching \xe2\x88\x9240\xc2\xa0\xc2\xb0F (\xe2\x88\x9240\xc2\xa0\xc2\xb0C) due to air with slightly higher moisture from aloft mixing with colder, surface based air.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-glossdia_7-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-glossdia-7">\n         [7]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       They are made of simple ice crystals that are hexagonal in shape.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">\n        <a href="#cite_note-8">\n         [8]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       The\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SMHI" title="SMHI">\n        Swedish meteorological institute\n       </a>\n       (SMHI) define winter as when the daily mean temperatures go below 0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xb0C (32\xc2\xa0\xc2\xb0F) for five consecutive days.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">\n        <a href="#cite_note-9">\n         [9]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       According to the SMHI, winter in Scandinavia is more pronounced when Atlantic low\xe2\x80\x93pressure systems take more southerly and northerly routes, leaving the path open for high\xe2\x80\x93pressure systems to come in and cold temperatures to occur. As a result, the coldest January on record in 1987 was also the sunniest in\n       <a href="/wiki/Stockholm" title="Stockholm">\n        Stockholm\n       </a>\n       .\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">\n        <a href="#cite_note-10">\n         [10]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">\n        <a href="#cite_note-11">\n         [11]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       Accumulations of snow and ice are commonly associated with winter in the Northern Hemisphere, due to the large land masses there. In the Southern Hemisphere, the more maritime climate and the relative lack of land south of 40\xc2\xb0S makes the winters milder; thus, snow and ice are less common in inhabited regions of the Southern Hemisphere. In this region, snow occurs every year in elevated regions such as the Andes, the Great Dividing Range in Australia, and the mountains of New Zealand, and also occurs in the southerly\n       <a href="/wiki/Patagonia" title="Patagonia">\n        Patagonia\n       </a>\n       region of South America. conditions. In one version of this definition, winter begins at the\n       <a href="/wiki/Winter_solstice" title="Winter solstice">\n        winter solstice\n       </a>\n       and ends at the\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vernal_equinox" title="Vernal equinox">\n        vernal equinox\n       </a>\n       .\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">\n        <a href="#cite_note-12">\n         [12]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       These dates are somewhat later than those used to define the beginning and end of the\n       <b>\n        meteorological winter\n       </b>\n       \xe2\x80\x93 usually considered to span the entirety of December, January, and February in the Northern Hemisphere and June, July, and August in the Southern.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">\n        <a href="#cite_note-13">\n         [13]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       Astronomically, the winter solstice, being the day of the year which has fewest hours of daylight, ought to be in the middle of the season,\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">\n        <a href="#cite_note-14">\n         [14]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">\n        <a href="#cite_note-15">\n         [15]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       but\n       <a href="/wiki/Seasonal_lag" title="Seasonal lag">\n        seasonal lag\n       </a>\n       means that the coldest period normally follows the solstice by a few weeks. In the UK, meteorologists consider winter to be the three coldest months of December, January and February.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18">\n        <a href="#cite_note-18">\n         [18]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       In Scandinavia, winter in one tradition begins on 14 October and ends on the last day of February.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19">\n        <a href="#cite_note-19">\n         [19]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       In Russia, currently calendar winter starts on 1 December and lasts through to the end of February, though traditionally it was reckoned from the\n       <a href="/wiki/Christmas" title="Christmas">\n        Christmas\n       </a>\n       (25 December in\n       <a href="/wiki/Julian_calendar" title="Julian calendar">\n        Julian calendar\n       </a>\n       , or 7 January in Gregorian) until the\n       <a href="/wiki/Annunciation" title="Annunciation">\n        Annunciation\n       </a>\n       (25 March in Julian).\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20">\n        <a href="#cite_note-20">\n         [20]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       In many countries in the\n       <a href="/wiki/Southern_Hemisphere" title="Southern Hemisphere">\n        Southern Hemisphere\n       </a>\n       , including Australia,\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21">\n        <a href="#cite_note-21">\n         [21]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22">\n        <a href="#cite_note-22">\n         [22]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       New Zealand and South Africa, winter begins on 1 June and ends on 31 August. In\n       <a href="/wiki/Celtic_nations" title="Celtic nations">\n        Celtic nations\n       </a>\n       such as Ireland (using the\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Irish_calendar" title="Irish calendar">\n        Irish calendar\n       </a>\n       ) and in Scandinavia, the winter solstice is traditionally considered as midwinter, with the winter season beginning 1 November, on\n       <a href="/wiki/Halloween" title="Halloween">\n        All Hallows\n       </a>\n       , or\n       <a href="/wiki/Samhain" title="Samhain">\n        Samhain\n       </a>\n       . Winter ends and spring begins on\n       <a href="/wiki/Imbolc" title="Imbolc">\n        Imbolc\n       </a>\n       , or\n       <a href="/wiki/Candlemas" title="Candlemas">\n        Candlemas\n       </a>\n       , which is 1 or 2 February. This system of seasons is based on the length of days exclusively. (The three-month period of the shortest days and weakest solar radiation occurs during November, December and January in the Northern Hemisphere and May, June and July in the\n       <a href="/wiki/Southern_Hemisphere" title="Southern Hemisphere">\n        Southern Hemisphere\n       </a>\n       .)\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       Also, many mainland European countries tend to recognize\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Martinmas" title="Martinmas">\n        Martinmas\n       </a>\n       or\n       <a href="/wiki/St._Martin%27s_Day" title="St. Martin\'s Day">\n        St. Martin\'s Day\n       </a>\n       (11 November), as the first calendar day of winter.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23">\n        <a href="#cite_note-23">\n         [23]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       The day falls at midpoint between the old Julian equinox and\n       <a href="/wiki/Solstice" title="Solstice">\n        solstice\n       </a>\n       dates. Also,\n       <a href="/wiki/Valentine%27s_Day" title="Valentine\'s Day">\n        Valentine\'s Day\n       </a>\n       (14 February) is recognized by some countries as heralding the first rites of spring, such as flowers blooming.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       In\n       <a href="/wiki/Chinese_astronomy" title="Chinese astronomy">\n        Chinese astronomy\n       </a>\n       and other East Asian\n       <a href="/wiki/Chinese_calendar" title="Chinese calendar">\n        calendars\n       </a>\n       , winter is taken to commence on or around 7 November, with the\n       <i>\n        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jieqi" title="Jieqi">\n         Ji\xc3\xa9q\xc3\xac\n        </a>\n       </i>\n       (known as \xe7\xab\x8b\xe5\x86\xac\n       <i>\n        l\xc3\xac d\xc5\x8dng\n       </i>\n       \xe2\x80\x94literally, "establishment of winter").\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       The three-month period associated with the coldest average temperatures typically begins somewhere in late November or early December in the Northern Hemisphere and lasts through late February or early March. This "thermological winter" is earlier than the solstice delimited definition, but later than the daylight (Celtic) definition. Depending on\n       <a href="/wiki/Seasonal_lag" title="Seasonal lag">\n        seasonal lag\n       </a>\n       , this period will vary between climatic regions.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       Cultural influences such as\n       <a href="/wiki/Christmas_creep" title="Christmas creep">\n        Christmas creep\n       </a>\n       may have led to the winter season being perceived as beginning earlier in recent years, although high latitude countries like Canada are usually well into their real winters before the December solstice.\n      </p>\n      <h2>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Ecological_reckoning_and_activity">\n        Ecological reckoning and activity\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Winter&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Ecological reckoning and activity">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h2>\n      <div class="thumb tright">\n       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:210px;">\n        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Snowshoe_hare.jpg">\n         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="306" data-file-width="208" height="306" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/e/ee/Snowshoe_hare.jpg" width="208"/>\n        </a>\n        <div class="thumbcaption">\n         <div class="magnify">\n          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Snowshoe_hare.jpg" title="Enlarge">\n          </a>\n         </div>\n         The\n         <a href="/wiki/Snowshoe_hare" title="Snowshoe hare">\n          snowshoe hare\n         </a>\n         , and some other animals, change color in winter.\n        </div>\n       </div>\n      </div>\n      <p>\n       Ecological reckoning of winter differs from calendar-based by avoiding the use of fixed dates. It is one of six seasons recognized by most ecologists who customarily use the term\n       <i>\n        hibernal\n       </i>\n       for this period of the year (the other ecological seasons being prevernal, vernal, estival, serotinal, and autumnal).\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24">\n        <a href="#cite_note-24">\n         [24]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       The hibernal season coincides with the main period of biological dormancy each year whose dates vary according to local and regional climates in temperate zones of the Earth. The appearance of flowering plants like the crocus can mark the change from ecological winter to the prevernal season as early as late January in mild temperate climates.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       To survive the harshness of winter, many animals have developed different behavioral and morphological adaptations for\n       <a href="/wiki/Overwintering" title="Overwintering">\n        overwintering\n       </a>\n       :\n      </p>\n      <ul>\n       <li>\n        <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">\n         Migration\n        </a>\n        is a common effect of winter upon animals, notably birds. However, the majority of birds do not migrate\xe2\x80\x94the\n        <a href="/wiki/Cardinal_(bird)" title="Cardinal (bird)">\n         cardinal\n        </a>\n        and\n        <a href="/wiki/European_robin" title="European robin">\n         European robin\n        </a>\n        , for example. Some\n        <a href="/wiki/Butterfly" title="Butterfly">\n         butterflies\n        </a>\n        also migrate seasonally.\n       </li>\n       <li>\n        <a href="/wiki/Hibernation" title="Hibernation">\n         Hibernation\n        </a>\n        is a state of reduced\n        <a href="/wiki/Metabolism" title="Metabolism">\n         metabolic activity\n        </a>\n        during the winter. Some animals "sleep" during winter and only come out when the warm weather returns; e.g.,\n        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gopher_(animal)" title="Gopher (animal)">\n         gophers\n        </a>\n        , frogs, snakes, and bats.\n       </li>\n       <li>\n        Some animals store food for the winter and live on it instead of hibernating completely. This is the case for\n        <a href="/wiki/Squirrel" title="Squirrel">\n         squirrels\n        </a>\n        ,\n        <a href="/wiki/Beaver" title="Beaver">\n         beavers\n        </a>\n        ,\n        <a href="/wiki/Skunk" title="Skunk">\n         skunks\n        </a>\n        ,\n        <a href="/wiki/Badger" title="Badger">\n         badgers\n        </a>\n        , and\n        <a href="/wiki/Raccoon" title="Raccoon">\n         raccoons\n        </a>\n        .\n       </li>\n       <li>\n        Resistance is observed when an animal endures winter but changes in ways such as color and musculature. The color of the fur or plumage changes to white (in order to be confused with snow) and thus retains its\n        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cryptic_(zoology)" title="Cryptic (zoology)">\n         cryptic\n        </a>\n        coloration year-round. Examples are the\n        <a href="/wiki/Rock_ptarmigan" title="Rock ptarmigan">\n         rock ptarmigan\n        </a>\n        ,\n        <a href="/wiki/Arctic_fox" title="Arctic fox">\n         Arctic fox\n        </a>\n        ,\n        <a href="/wiki/Weasel" title="Weasel">\n         weasel\n        </a>\n        ,\n        <a href="/wiki/Hare" title="Hare">\n         white-tailed jackrabbit\n        </a>\n        , and\n        <a href="/wiki/Mountain_hare" title="Mountain hare">\n         mountain hare\n        </a>\n        .\n       </li>\n       <li>\n        Some fur-coated mammals grow a heavier coat during the winter; this improves the heat-retention qualities of the fur. The coat is then shed following the winter season to allow better cooling. The heavier coat in winter made it a favorite season for\n        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Animal_trapping" title="Animal trapping">\n         trappers\n        </a>\n        , who sought more profitable skins.\n       </li>\n       <li>\n        Snow also affects the ways animals behave; many take advantage of the insulating properties of snow by burrowing in it. Mice and\n        <a href="/wiki/Vole" title="Vole">\n         voles\n        </a>\n        typically live under the snow layer.\n       </li>\n      </ul>\n      <p>\n       Some\n       <a href="/wiki/Annual_plant" title="Annual plant">\n        annual plants\n       </a>\n       never survive the winter. Other annual plants require winter cold to complete their life cycle, this is known as\n       <a href="/wiki/Vernalization" title="Vernalization">\n        vernalization\n       </a>\n       . As for\n       <a href="/wiki/Perennial_plant" title="Perennial plant">\n        perennials\n       </a>\n       , many small ones profit from the insulating effects of snow by being buried in it. Larger plants, particularly\n       <a href="/wiki/Deciduous" title="Deciduous">\n        deciduous trees\n       </a>\n       , usually let their upper part go dormant, but their roots are still protected by the snow layer. Few plants bloom in the winter, one exception being the\n       <a href="/wiki/Prunus_mume" title="Prunus mume">\n        flowering plum\n       </a>\n       , which flowers in time for\n       <a href="/wiki/Chinese_New_Year" title="Chinese New Year">\n        Chinese New Year\n       </a>\n       . The process by which plants become acclimated to cold weather is called\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hardening_(botany)" title="Hardening (botany)">\n        hardening\n       </a>\n       .\n      </p>\n      <h2>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Exceptionally_cold_winters">\n        Exceptionally cold winters\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span   </div>\n      </div>\n      <ul>\n       <li>\n        1683\xe2\x80\x931684, "The Great Frost", when the\n        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thames" title="Thames">\n         Thames\n        </a>\n        , hosting the\n        <a href="/wiki/River_Thames_frost_fairs" title="River Thames frost fairs">\n         River Thames frost fairs\n        </a>\n        , was frozen all the way up to the London Bridge and remained frozen for about two months. Ice was about 27\xc2\xa0cm (11\xc2\xa0in) thick in London and about 120\xc2\xa0cm (47\xc2\xa0in) thick in Somerset. The sea froze up to 2 miles (3.2\xc2\xa0km) out around the coast of the southern North Sea, causing severe problems for shipping and preventing use of many harbours.\n       </li>\n       <li>\n        1739\xe2\x80\x931740, one of the most severe winters in the UK on record. The Thames remained frozen-over for about 8 weeks. The\n        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Irish_Famine_(1740%E2%80%931741)" title="Irish Famine (1740\xe2\x80\x931741)">\n         Irish famine\n        </a>\n        of 1740\xe2\x80\x931741 claimed the lives of at least 300,000 people.\n        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25">\n         <a href="#cite_note-25">\n          [25]\n         </a>\n        </sup>\n       </li>\n       <li>\n        1816 was the\n        <a href="/wiki/Year_Without_a_Summer" title="Year Without a Summer">\n         Year Without a Summer\n        </a>\n        in the Northern Hemisphere. The unusual coolness of the winter of 1815\xe2\x80\x931816 and of the following summer was primarily due to the eruption of\n        <a href="/wiki/Mount_Tambora" title="Mount Tambora">\n         Mount Tambora\n        </a>\n        in Indonesia, in April 1815. There were secondary effects from an unknown eruption or eruptions around 1810, and several smaller eruptions around the world between 1812 and 1814. The cumulative effects were worldwide, but were especially strong in the Eastern United States, Atlantic Canada, and Northern Europe. Frost formed in May in New England, killing many newly planted crops, and the summer never recovered. Snow fell in New York and Maine in June, and ice formed in lakes and rivers in July and August. In the UK, snow drifts remained on hills until late July, and the Thames froze in September. Agricultural crops failed and livestock died in much of the Northern Hemisphere, resulting in food shortages and the worst famine of the 19th century.\n       </li>\n       <li>\n        1887\xe2\x80\x931888, there were record cold temperatures in the Upper Midwest, heavy snowfalls worldwide, and amazing storms, including the\n        <a href="/wiki/Schoolhouse_Blizzard" title="Schoolhouse Blizzard">\n         Schoolhouse Blizzard\n        </a>\n        of 1888 (in the Midwest in January), and the\n        <a href="/wiki/Great_Blizzard_of_1888" title="Great Blizzard of 1888">\n         Great Blizzard of 1888\n        </a>\n        (in the Eastern US and Canada in March).\n       </li>\n       <li>\n        In Europe, the winters of early 1947,\n        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26">\n         <a href="#cite_note-26">\n          [26]\n         </a>\n        </sup>\n        February 1956, 1962\xe2\x80\x931963, 1981\xe2\x80\x931982 and\n        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Winter_of_2009%E2%80%932010_in_Europe" title="Winter of 2009\xe2\x80\x932010 in Europe">\n         2009\xe2\x80\x932010\n        </a>\n        were abnormally cold. Micro-evolution in Caucasian, Asiatic and Inuit people show some adaptation to the climate.\n      </p>\n      <h3>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Winter_and_human_health">\n        Winter and human health\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Winter&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Winter and human health">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h3>\n      <p>\n       Humans are sensitive to cold, see\n       <a href="/wiki/Hypothermia" title="Hypothermia">\n        hypothermia\n       </a>\n       .\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Snowblindness" title="Snowblindness">\n        Snowblindness\n       </a>\n       ,\n       <a href="/wiki/Norovirus" title="Norovirus">\n        norovirus\n       </a>\n       ,\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Seasonal_depression" title="Seasonal depression">\n        seasonal depression\n       </a>\n       , slipping on black ice and falling\n       <a href="/wiki/Icicle" title="Icicle">\n        icicles\n       </a>\n       are other health concerns associated with cold and snowy weather. In the Northern Hemisphere, it is not unusual for homeless people to die from hypothermia in the winter.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       One of the most common diseases associated with winter is\n       <a href="/wiki/Influenza" title="Influenza">\n        influenza\n       </a>\n       .
