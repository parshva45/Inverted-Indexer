      </i>\n       </a>\n       launched at 10:39\n       <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Time_Zone" title="Eastern Time Zone">\n        EDT\n       </a>\n       (14:39\n       <a The launch, 907 days (approx. 29 months) after the loss of\n       <i>\n        Columbia\n       </i>\n       , was approved despite unresolved fuel sensor anomalies in the\n       <a href="/wiki/Space_Shuttle_external_tank" title="Space Shuttle external tank">\n        external tank\n       </a>\n       that had prevented the shuttle from launching on 13 July, its originally scheduled date.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       The mission ended on 9 August 2005 when\n       <i>\n        Discovery\n       </i>\n       landed at\n       <a href="/wiki/Edwards_Air_Force_Base" title="Edwards Air Force Base">\n        Edwards Air Force Base\n       </a>\n       in California.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">\n        <a href="#cite_note-2">\n         [2]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       Poor weather over the\n       <a href="/wiki/Kennedy_Space_Center" title="Kennedy Space Center">\n        Kennedy Space Center\n       </a>\n       in Florida hampered the shuttle from using its primary landing site.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       Analysis of the launch footage showed debris separating from the external tank during ascent; it was the issue that had set off the\n       <i>\n        Columbia\n       </i>\n       disaster. </div>\n      <p>\n       STS-114 marked the return to flight of the Space Shuttle after the\n       <i>\n        Columbia\n       </i>\n       disaster and was the second Shuttle flight with a female commander (Eileen Collins, who also commanded the\n       <a href="/wiki/STS-93" title="STS-93">\n        STS-93\n       </a>\n       mission). The STS-114 mission was initially to be flown aboard the orbiter\n       <i>\n        <a href="/wiki/Space_Shuttle_Atlantis" title="Space Shuttle Atlantis">\n         Atlantis\n        </a>\n       </i>\n       , but NASA replaced it with\n       <i>\n        Discovery\n       </i>\n       after improperly installed gear was found in\n       <i>\n        Atlantis\n       </i>\n       \' Rudder Speed Brake system. During OMM for\n       <i>\n        Discovery\n       </i>\n       , an actuator on the RSB system was found to be installed incorrectly. This created a fleet wide suspect condition. The Rudder Speed Brake system was removed and refurbished on all three remaining orbiter vehicles, and since\n       <i>\n        Discovery\n       </i>\n       \'s RSB was corrected first, it became the new Return to Flight vehicle, superseding\n       <i>\n        Atlantis\n       </i>\n       . Seventeen years prior,\n       <i>\n        Discovery\n       </i>\n       had flown NASA\'s previous Return to Flight mission,\n       <a href="/wiki/STS-26" title="STS-26">\n        STS-26\n       </a>\n       .\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       The STS-114 mission delivered supplies to the\n       <a href="/wiki/International_Space_Station" title="International Space Station">\n        International Space Station\n       </a>\n       . However, the major focus of the mission was testing and evaluating new Space Shuttle flight safety techniques, which included new inspection and repair techniques. The crewmembers used the new\n       <a href="/wiki/Orbiter_Boom_Sensor_System" title="Orbiter Boom Sensor System">\n        Orbiter Boom Sensor System\n       </a>\n       (OBSS) \xe2\x80\x93 a set of instruments on a 50-foot (15 m) extension attached to the\n       <a href="/wiki/Canadarm" title="Canadarm">\n        Canadarm\n       </a>\n       . The OBSS instrument package consists of visual imaging equipment and a\n       <a href="/wiki/Laser_Dynamic_Range_Imager" title="Laser Dynamic Range Imager">\n        Laser Dynamic Range Imager\n       </a>\n       (LDRI) to detect problems with the shuttle\'s\n       <a href="/wiki/Space_Shuttle_thermal_protection_system" title="Space Shuttle thermal protection system">\n        Thermal Protection System\n       </a>\n       (TPS). The crew scanned the leading edges of the wings, the nose cap, and the crew compartment for damage, as well as other potential problem areas engineers wished to inspect based on video taken during lift-off.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       STS-114 was classified as Logistics Flight 1. The flight carried the\n       <i>\n        Raffaello\n       </i>\n       <a href="/wiki/Multi-Purpose_Logistics_Module" title="Multi-Purpose Logistics Module">\n        Multi-Purpose Logistics Module\n       </a>\n       , built by the\n       <a href="/wiki/Italian_Space_Agency" title="Italian Space Agency">\n        Italian Space Agency\n       </a>\n       , as well as the\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/External_Stowage_Platform" title="External Stowage Platform">\n        External Stowage Platform\n       </a>\n       -2, which was mounted to the port side of the Quest Airlock. They deployed\n       <a href="/wiki/Materials_International_Space_Station_Experiment#MISSE" title="Materials International Space Station Experiment">\n        MISSE 5\n       </a>\n       to the station\'s exterior, and replaced one of the ISS\'s\n       <a href="/wiki/Control_moment_gyroscope" title="Control moment gyroscope">\n        Control Moment Gyroscopes\n       </a>\n       (CMG). The CMG was carried up on the LMC (Lightweight Multi-Purpose Experiment Support Structure Carrier) at the rear of the payload bay, together with the TPS Repair Box.\n      </p>\n      <div class="thumb tleft">\n       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;">\n        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Sts114_033.jpg">\n         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3032" data-file-width="2000" height="258" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/62/Sts114_033.jpg/170px-Sts114_033.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/62/Sts114_033.jpg/255px-Sts114_033.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/62/Sts114_033.jpg/340px-Sts114_033.jpg 2x" width="170"/>\n        </a>\n        <div class="thumbcaption">\n         <div class="magnify">\n          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Sts114_033.jpg" title="Enlarge">\n          </a>\n         </div>\n         Stephen Robinson on the third spacewalk\n        </div>\n       </div>\n      </div>\n      <p>\n       The crew conducted three\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Extra-vehicular_activity" title="Extra-vehicular activity">\n        spacewalks\n       </a>\n       while at the station. The first demonstrated repair techniques on the Shuttle\'s Thermal Protection System. During the second, the spacewalkers replaced the failed gyroscope. On the third, they installed the External Stowage Platform and repaired the shuttle, the first time repairs had been carried out during a spacewalk on the exterior of a spacecraft in flight. On 1 August, it was announced that protruding gap fillers on the front underside of the shuttle would be inspected and dealt with during the third spacewalk of the mission. The spacewalk was conducted on the morning of 3 August. Robinson easily removed the two fillers with his fingers. Later on the same day, NASA officials said that they were looking closely at a thermal blanket located next to the commander\'s window on the port side of the orbiter. Published reports on 4 August 2005 said that wind tunnel testing had demonstrated that the orbiter was safe to re-enter with the billowed blanket.\n      </p>\n      <div class="thumb tright">\n       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">\n        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:STS_114_day_before_launch.jpg">\n         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2000" data-file-width="3008" height="146" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/97/STS_114_day_before_launch.jpg/220px-STS_114_day_before_launch.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/97/STS_114_day_before_launch.jpg/330px-STS_114_day_before_launch.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/97/STS_114_day_before_launch.jpg/440px-STS_114_day_before_launch.jpg 2x" width="220"/>\n        </a>\n        <div class="thumbcaption">\n         <div class="magnify">\n          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:STS_114_day_before_launch.jpg" title="Enlarge">\n          </a>\n         </div>\n         12 July 2005: STS 114 the night before its original planned launchdate.\n        </div>\n       </div>\n      </div>\n      <p>\n       On 30 July 2005, NASA announced that STS-114 would be extended for one day, so that\n       <i>\n        Discovery\'\n       </i>\n       s crew could help the ISS crew maintain the station while the shuttle fleet was grounded. The extra day was also used to move more items from the shuttle to the ISS, as uncertainty mounted during the mission as to when a shuttle would next visit the station. The orbiter\'s arrival also gave the nearly 200-ton space station a free altitude boost of about 4,000 feet (1,220 m). The station loses about 100 feet (30 m) of altitude a day.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">\n        <a href="#cite_note-3">\n         [3]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       The shuttle hatch was closed the night before it undocked from the ISS. After undocking, the shuttle flew around the station to take photos.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_entry" title="Atmospheric entry">\n        Atmospheric reentry\n       </a>\n       and landing was originally planned for 8 August 2005, at\n       <a href="/wiki/Kennedy_Space_Center" title="Kennedy Space Center">\n        Kennedy Space Center\n       </a>\n       , but unsuitable weather postponed the landing until the next day, then moved it to\n       <a href="/wiki/Edwards_Air_Force_Base" title="Edwards Air Force Base">\n        Edwards Air Force Base\n       </a>\n       in California, where\n       <i>\n        Discovery\n       </i>\n       touched down at 08:11EDT (05:11\xc2\xa0am PDT, 12:11 UTC).\n      </p>\n      <h2>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Launch_sequence_anomalies">\n        Launch sequence anomalies\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=STS-114&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Launch sequence anomalies">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h2>\n      <div class="thumb tleft">\n       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:162px;">\n        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:STS-114-Debris-Animation.gif">\n         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="120" data-file-width="160" height="120" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/d/d7/STS-114-Debris-Animation.gif" width="160"/>\n        </a>\n        <div class="thumbcaption">\n         Video of foam flying off shuttle\'s fuel tank\n        </div>\n       </div>\n      </div>\n      <p>\n       Around 2.5 seconds after lift-off, a large bird\n       <a href="/wiki/Bird_strike" title="Bird strike">\n        struck\n       </a>\n       near the top of the external fuel tank, and appeared in subsequent video frames to slide down the tank. NASA did not expect this to hurt the mission because it did not hit the orbiter, and because the vehicle was traveling relatively slowly at the time.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       A small fragment of thermal tile, estimated to be around 1.5\xc2\xa0inches (38\xc2\xa0mm) in size, was ejected from an edge tile of the front landing gear door at some point before\n       <a href="/wiki/Space_Shuttle_Solid_Rocket_Booster" title="Space Shuttle Solid Rocket Booster">\n        SRB\n       </a>\n       separation. A small white area appeared on the tile as the piece detached, and the loose shard could be seen in a single frame of the video. It is unknown what object (if any) struck the tile to cause the damage. The damaged tile was inspected further when the images from the umbilical camera were downloaded on day three. Engineers requested that this area be inspected by the\n       <a href="/wiki/Orbiter_Boom_Sensor_System" title="Orbiter Boom Sensor System">\n        OBSS\n       </a>\n       , and flight managers scheduled the operation for 29 July 2005. This represented the only known possible damage to\n       <i>\n        Discovery\n       </i>\n       that could have posed a risk during re-entry.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       At 127.1 seconds after liftoff, and 5.3 seconds after SRB separation,\n       <a class="external text" href="http://www.spaceflightnow.com/shuttle/sts114/images/050726debris.jpg" rel="nofollow">\n        a large piece of debris\n       </a>\n       separated from the Protuberance Air Load (PAL) ramp, which is part of the external tank. The debris was thought to have measured 36.3 by 11 by 6.7\xc2\xa0inches (922 by 279 by 170\xc2\xa0mm) \xe2\x80\x93 and to weigh about 0.45 kilograms (0.99\xc2\xa0lb), or half as much as the piece of foam blamed for the loss of\n       <i>\n        Columbia\n       </i>\n       .\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">\n        <a href="#cite_note-4">\n         [4]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       The debris piece did not strike any part of the\n       <i>\n        Discovery\n       </i>\n       orbiter. Images of the external tank taken after separation from the orbiter show multiple areas where foam insulation was missing.\n      </p>\n      <div class="thumb tright">\n       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">\n        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:STS-114_external_tank_foam_missing.jpg">\n         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2000" data-file-width="3032" height="145" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bc/STS-114_external_tank_foam_missing.jpg/220px-STS-114_external_tank_foam_missing.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bc/STS-114_external_tank_foam_missing.jpg/330px-STS-114_external_tank_foam_missing.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bc/STS-114_external_tank_foam_missing.jpg/440px-STS-114_external_tank_foam_missing.jpg 2x" width="220"/>\n        </a>\n        <div class="thumbcaption">\n         <div class="magnify">\n          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:STS-114_external_tank_foam_missing.jpg" title="Enlarge">\n          </a>\n         </div>\n         Handheld still image taken by\n         <a href="/wiki/Space_Shuttle_Discovery" title="Space Shuttle Discovery">\n          <i>\n           Discovery\n          </i>\n          <span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">\n           \'\n          </span>\n          s\n         </a>\n         crew of the\n         <a href="/wiki/Space_Shuttle_external_tank" title="Space Shuttle external tank">\n          external fuel tank\n         </a>\n         as it was jettisoned after launch. In this still image, the area of missing\n         <a href="/wiki/Foam" title="Foam">\n          foam\n         </a>\n         on the tank is indicated by a light spot near the upper edge of the tank just below the\n         <a href="/wiki/Liquid_oxygen" title="Liquid oxygen">\n          liquid oxygen\n         </a>\n         feedline.\n        </div>\n       </div>\n      </div>\n      <p>\n       Around 20 seconds later, a smaller piece of foam separated from the ET and apparently struck the orbiter\'s right wing. Based on the mass of the foam, and the velocity at which it would have struck the wing, NASA estimated it only exerted one-tenth the energy required to cause potential damage. Laser scanning and imaging of the wing by the OBSS did not reveal any damage. On 27 July 2005, NASA announced that it was postponing all Shuttle flights until the foam loss problem could be resolved.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       As with\n       <i>\n        Columbia\n       </i>\n       , NASA at first believed that workers\' improper installation and handling of the external tanks at the\n       <a href="/wiki/Michoud_Assembly_Facility" title="Michoud Assembly Facility">\n        Michoud Assembly Facility\n       </a>\n       in Louisiana caused the foam loss on\n       <i>\n        Discovery\n       </i>\n       .\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">\n        <a href="#cite_note-5">\n         [5]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       NASA Administrator\n       <a href="/wiki/Michael_D._Griffin" title="Michael D. Griffin">\n        Michael Griffin\n       </a>\n       stated that the earliest the next shuttle could launch is 22 September 2005, but that\'s only "if next week, the guys have a\n       <i>\n        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aha!_effect" title="Aha! effect">\n         Aha! effect\n        </a>\n       </i>\n       on the foam and spot why this big chunk came off." Later in August, it became clear that a September launch date would not be possible, and that the earliest date for the next launch would be in March 2006. However, because\n       <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Katrina" title="Hurricane Katrina">\n        Hurricane Katrina\n       </a>\n       hit the\n       <a href="/wiki/Gulf_Coast_of_the_United_States" title="Gulf Coast of the United States">\n        Gulf Coast\n       </a>\n       , the next launch was delayed further. With the destruction suffered by Michoud and\n       <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">\n        NASA\'s\n       </a>\n       <a href="/wiki/John_C._Stennis_Space_Center" title="John C. Stennis Space Center">\n        Stennis Space Center\n       </a>\n       in\n       <a href="/wiki/Mississippi" title="Mississippi">\n        Mississippi\n       </a>\n       due to Hurricane Katrina and the subsequent flooding, the launch of the next shuttle mission (\n       <a href="/wiki/STS-121" title="STS-121">\n        STS-121\n       </a>\n       ) was further delayed until 4 July 2006.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       In December 2005\n       <a href="/wiki/Radiography" title="Radiography">\n        x-ray photographs\n       </a>\n       of another tank showed that\n       <a href="/wiki/Thermal_expansion" title="Thermal expansion">\n        thermal expansion\n       </a>\n       and contraction during filling, not human error, caused the cracks that resulted in foam loss. NASA official\n       <a href="/wiki/Wayne_Hale" title="Wayne Hale">\n        Wayne Hale\n       </a>\n       formally apologized to the Michoud workers who had been blamed for the loss of\n       <i>\n        Columbia\n       </i>\n       for almost three years.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hale20120418_6-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-hale20120418-6">\n         [6]\n The Shuttle\'s heat shield is reflected in his visor.\n        </div>\n       </div>\n      </div>\n      <div class="thumb tright">\n       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">\n        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:STS-114_damaged_blanket.jpg">\n         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="338" data-file-width="512" height="145" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4d/STS-114_damaged_blanket.jpg/220px-STS-114_damaged_blanket.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4d/STS-114_damaged_blanket.jpg/330px-STS-114_damaged_blanket.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4d/STS-114_damaged_blanket.jpg/440px-STS-114_damaged_blanket.jpg 2x" width="220"/>\n        </a>\n        <div class="thumbcaption">\n         <div class="magnify">\n          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:STS-114_damaged_blanket.jpg" title="Enlarge">\n          </a>\n         </div>\n         Damaged thermal blanket\n        </div>\n       </div>\n      </div>\n      <p>\n       On the third\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Extra-vehicular_activity" title="Extra-vehicular activity">\n        EVA\n       </a>\n       of the mission, two areas on the underside of the shuttle where photographic surveying identified protruding gap fillers were dealt with. According to NASA, the gap fillers, which each serve different purposes, are not required for reentry. One filler prevents "chattering" of tiles during ascent, which would occur due to the sonic booms from the noses of the solid rocket boosters and the external fuel tank. The other, in a different location where there is a wider gap between tiles, simply functions to reduce the gap size between tiles, which in turn reduces heat transfer to the shuttle. Even without this filler NASA did not expect the increased heat to cause a problem during reentry (it is present to avoid a level of heating which would only be problematic if experienced many times over a vehicle\'s design life). Since the gap fillers are not necessary for re-entry, it was acceptable to simply pull them out. An overview of the situation, including procedures for dealing with the protrusions, were sent electronically to the crew and printed aboard the shuttle. The crew were also able to watch uploaded videos of NASA personnel on the ground demonstrating the repair techniques. Both the videos and 12-page procedure document\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">\n        <a href="#cite_note-7">\n         [7]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       were also made publicly available via NASA\'s website.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       During the third EVA, both the fillers were successfully removed with less than a pound of force without the need to use any tools.\n       <a href="/wiki/Stephen_Robinson" title="Stephen Robinson">\n        Stephen K. Robinson\n       </a>\n       gave a running commentary of his work: "I\'m grasping it and I\'m pulling it and it\'s coming out very easily" ... "It looks like this big patient is cured".\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       If it was not possible to pull the fillers out, then the protruding sections could have been simply cut off. The gap fillers were made of a cloth impregnated with\n       <a href="/wiki/Ceramic" title="Ceramic">\n        ceramic\n       </a>\n       \xe2\x80\x93 they were stiff and could be easily cut with a tool similar to a hacksaw blade. Protruding gap fillers were a problem because they disrupted the normally\n       <a href="/wiki/Laminar_flow" title="Laminar flow">\n        laminar air flow\n       </a>\n       under the orbiter during reentry, causing\n       <a href="/wiki/Turbulence" title="Turbulence">\n        turbulence\n       </a>\n       at lower speeds. A turbulent air flow would result in a mixing of hot and cold air, which could have a major effect on the shuttle temperature.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       The decision to make the repair balanced the risks of the EVA with the risks of leaving the protruding gap fillers as they were. It is thought that gap filler protrusions of a similar magnitude were present on previous missions, but were not observed in-orbit. Consideration was also given to the risks of elements of the procedure which would involve the ISS arm being used to carry Stephen K. Robinson below the shuttle, possibly the use of a sharp tool which had the potential to damage the EVA suit or shuttle tiles. The possibility of making things worse by attempting a repair was given serious consideration. Cameras on the shuttle arm and on Robinson\'s helmet were used to monitor the activities under the shuttle.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       Protruding gap fillers had been identified as an issue on previous flights, notably\n       <a href="/wiki/STS-28" title="STS-28">\n        STS-28\n       </a>\n       . A post-flight analysis\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">\n        <a href="#cite_note-8">\n         [8]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       identified that a gap filler was the likely cause of the high temperatures observed during this re-entry. Protruding gap fillers were also seen on\n       <a href="/wiki/STS-73" title="STS-73">\n        STS-73\n       </a>\n       .\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       A further in-flight repair was considered to remove or clip a damaged thermal blanket located beneath the commander\'s window on the port side of the orbiter. Wind tunnel testing by NASA determined that the thermal blanket was safe for re-entry, and plans for a fourth spacewalk were cancelled.\n      </p>\n      <h2>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Mission_timeline">\n        Mission timeline\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=STS-114&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Mission timeline">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h2>\n      <p>\n       This timeline is a summary.
