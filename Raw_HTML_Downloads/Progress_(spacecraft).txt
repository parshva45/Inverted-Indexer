<a href="/wiki/Russian_language" title="Russian language">\n        Russian\n       </a>\n       :\n       <span lang="ru" xml:lang="ru">\n        \xd0\x9f\xd1\x80\xd0\xbe\xd0\xb3\xd1\x80\xd0\xb5\xd1\x81\xd1\x81\n       </span>\n       ) is a Russian\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Unmanned_resupply_spacecraft" title="Unmanned resupply spacecraft">\n        expendable cargo spacecraft\n       </a>\n       . Its purpose is to deliver supplies needed to sustain human presence in orbit. While it does not carry a crew it can be boarded by astronauts when docked with a\n       <a href="/wiki/Space_station" title="Space station">\n        space station\n       </a>\n       , hence it being classified as\n       <i>\n        manned\n       </i>\n       by its manufacturer.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">\n        <a href="#cite_note-1">\n         [1]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">\n        <a href="#cite_note-2">\n         [2]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">\n        <a href="#cite_note-3">\n         [3]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       Progress is derived from the manned\n       <a href="/wiki/Soyuz_(spacecraft)" title="Soyuz (spacecraft)">\n        Soyuz spacecraft\n       </a>\n       and launches on the same\n       <a href="/wiki/Launch_vehicle" title="Launch vehicle">\n        vehicle\n       </a>\n       , a\n       <a href="/wiki/Soyuz_(rocket_family)" title="Soyuz (rocket family)">\n        Soyuz rocket\n       </a>\n       .\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       Progress has supported space stations as early as\n       <a href="/wiki/Salyut_6" title="Salyut 6">\n        Salyut 6\n       </a>\n       and as recently as the\n       <a href="/wiki/International_Space_Station" title="International Space Station">\n        International Space Station\n       </a>\n       . Each year there are between three and four Progress flights to the ISS. A Progress remains docked until shortly before being replaced with a new one or a Soyuz (which will use the same docking port). Then it is filled with waste, disconnected, and de-orbited, at which point it burns up in the atmosphere. Due to the variation in Progress vehicles flown to the ISS, NASA uses its own nomenclature where "\n       <b>\n        ISS 1P\n       </b>\n       " means the first Progress spacecraft to ISS.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       Progress was developed because of the need for a constant source of supplies to make long duration space missions possible. It was determined that cosmonauts needed an inflow of consumables (food, water, air, etc.), plus there was a need for maintenance items and scientific payloads that necessitated a dedicated cargo carrier. Such payloads were impractical to launch with passengers in the restricted space of a Soyuz. As of December 1, 2016 there have been 155\n       <a href="/wiki/List_of_Progress_missions" title="List of Progress missions">\n        Progress flights\n       </a>\n       with three failures. All three failures have occurred since 2011.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n      </p>\n      <div class="toc" id="toc">\n       <div class="toctitle">\n        <h2>\n         Contents\n        </h2>\n class="thumbcaption">\n         <div class="magnify">\n          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Progress-M_spacecraft_(with_captions).svg" title="Enlarge">\n          </a>\n         </div>\n         Diagram of the Progress-M spacecraft.\n        </div>\n       </div>\n      </div>\n      <p>\n       Progress is of much the same size and shape as\n       <a href="/wiki/Soyuz_(spacecraft)" title="Soyuz (spacecraft)">\n        Soyuz\n       </a>\n       . It consists of three modules:\n      </p>\n      <ul>\n       <li>\n        A pressurized forward module. This carries the supplies for the crew such as scientific equipment, clothes, prepackaged and fresh food, and letters from home. The docking drogue is similar to that of the Soyuz but features ducting for the\n        <a href="/wiki/Unsymmetrical_dimethylhydrazine" title="Unsymmetrical dimethylhydrazine">\n         UDMH\n        </a>\n        fuel and\n        <a href="/wiki/Dinitrogen_tetroxide" title="Dinitrogen tetroxide">\n         N\n         <sub>\n          2\n         </sub>\n         O\n         <sub>\n          4\n         </sub>\n        </a>\n        oxidiser.\n       </li>\n       <li>\n        A fuel compartment. The reentry module of the Soyuz was replaced with an unpressurized propellant and refueling compartment with ducting along the outside of the spacecraft. This meant that if a leak occurred, the poisonous gas would not enter the station\'s atmosphere. The fuel is carried in two tanks.\n       </li>\n       <li>\n        A propulsion module. The propulsion module, at the rear of the spacecraft, remained unchanged and contains the orientation engines used for the automatic docking. It may be used to boost the orbit of the station once docked.\n       </li>\n      </ul>\n      <p>\n       Reduction in\n       <a href="/wiki/Mass" title="Mass">\n        mass\n       </a>\n       was possible because the Progress was designed to be unmanned and disposable. This means that there is no need for bulky life support systems and\n       <a href="/wiki/Heat_shield" title="Heat shield">\n        heat shields\n       </a>\n       . A small amount of weight is saved due to the lack of automatic rescue crews system and lack of parachutes. The spacecraft also has no ability to split into separate modules. After undocking, the spacecraft performs a retrofire and burns up in the atmosphere.\n      </p>\n      <h2>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Versions">\n        Versions\n       </span>\n small variations between the different flights, but the major upgrades are reflected in the change of name.\n      </p>\n      <h3>\n       <span id="Progress_(1978\xe2\x80\x931990)">\n They began work on the design in mid-1973, assigning Progress the\n       <a href="/wiki/GRAU" title="GRAU">\n        GRAU\n       </a>\n       index 11F615A15. The design was complete by February 1974, and the first production model was ready for launch in November 1977.\n       <a href="/wiki/Progress_1" title="Progress 1">\n        Progress 1\n       </a>\n       launched on 20 January 1978 aboard the same rocket used to launch the Soyuz. It still featured the same launch shroud as the Soyuz, though this was purely for aerodynamic purposes as the\n       <a href="/wiki/Launch_escape_system" title="Launch escape system">\n        launch escape system\n       </a>\n       had been deactivated.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       This first version of Progress had a mass of 7,020\xc2\xa0kg and carried 2,300\xc2\xa0kg of cargo, or 30% of its\n       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Launch_mass&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Launch mass (page does not exist)">\n        launch mass\n       </a>\n       . It had the same diameter as the Soyuz at 2.2 metres, but was 8 metres in length\xe2\x80\x94slightly longer. The autonomous flight time was 3 days, the same time as that of the Soyuz ferry. It could spend one month docked. </div>\n       </div>\n      </div>\n      <p>\n       The upgraded\n       <b>\n        Progress M\n       </b>\n       (\n       <a href="/wiki/GRAU" title="GRAU">\n        GRAU\n       </a>\n The first 43 flights all went to\n       <a href="/wiki/Mir" title="Mir">\n        Mir\n       </a>\n       ; following Mir\'s re-entry, Progress was used as the resupply vehicle for the\n       <a href="/wiki/International_Space_Station" title="International Space Station">\n        International Space Station\n       </a>\n       . It can spend up to 30 days in autonomous flight and is able to carry 100\xc2\xa0kg more. Also, unlike the old Progress crafts, it can return items to Earth. This is accomplished by using the\n       <a href="/wiki/VBK-Raduga" title="VBK-Raduga">\n        Raduga\n       </a>\n       capsule, which can carry up to 150\xc2\xa0kg of cargo. It is 1.5 m long and 60\xc2\xa0cm in diameter and has a "\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dry_mass" title="Dry mass">\n        dry mass\n       </a>\n       " of 350\xc2\xa0kg. Progress M can also dock to the forward port of the station and still transfer fuel. It uses the same rendezvous system as the Soyuz, and it features solar panels for the first time.\n      </p>\n      <ul>\n       <li>\n        Launch mass 7,130\xc2\xa0kg\n       </li>\n       <li>\n        Cargo mass 2,600\xc2\xa0kg\n       </li>\n       <li>\n        Dry cargo mass 1,500\xc2\xa0kg\n       </li>\n       <li>\n        Liquid cargo mass 1,540\xc2\xa0kg\n       </li>\n       <li>\n        Length 7.23 m\n       </li>\n       <li>\n        Diameter of cargo modules 2.2 m\n       </li>\n       <li>\n        Maximum diameter 2.72 m\n       </li>\n       <li>\n        Dry cargo compartment volume 7.6 m\xc2\xb3\n       </li>\n       <li>\n        Solar array span 10.6 m\n       </li>\n      </ul>\n      <h3>\n       <span id="Progress-\xd0\x9c_11F615A60_(2008-2015)">\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Progress-.D0.9C_11F615A60_.282008-2015.29">\n        Progress-\xd0\x9c 11F615A60 (2008-2015)\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Progress_(spacecraft)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Progress-\xd0\x9c 11F615A60 (2008-2015)">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h3>\n      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">\n       Main article:\n       <a href="/wiki/Progress-M" title="Progress-M">\n        Progress-M\n       </a>\n      </div>\n      <p>\n       A new modification of the Progress spacecraft, with new TsVM-101 digital flight computer and MBITS digital telemetry system,\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">\n        <a href="#cite_note-5">\n         [5]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       was first launched on 26 November 2008, at 12:38 UTC from the Kazakhstan\'s Baikonur Cosmodrome spaceport aboard a Russian Soyuz rocket.
