The\n       <b>\n        tidal force\n       </b>\n       is an\n       <a href="/wiki/Fictitious_force" title="Fictitious force">\n        apparent force\n       </a>\n       that stretches a body towards the center of mass of another body due to a\n       <a href="/wiki/Gradient" title="Gradient">\n        gradient\n       </a>\n       (difference in strength) in\n       <a href="/wiki/Gravitational_field" title="Gravitational field">\n        gravitational field\n       </a>\n       from the other body; it is responsible for the diverse phenomena, including\n       <a href="/wiki/Tide" title="Tide">\n        tides\n       </a>\n       ,\n       <a href="/wiki/Tidal_locking" title="Tidal locking">\n        tidal locking\n       </a>\n       , breaking apart of celestial bodies and formation of\n       <a href="/wiki/Ring_system" title="Ring system">\n        ring systems\n       </a>\n       within\n       <a href="/wiki/Roche_limit" title="Roche limit">\n        Roche limit\n       </a>\n       , and in extreme cases,\n       <a href="/wiki/Spaghettification" title="Spaghettification">\n        spaghettification\n       </a>\n       of objects. It arises because the gravitational force exerted on one body by another is not constant across its parts: the nearest side is attracted more strongly than the farthest side. It is this difference that causes a body to get stretched. Thus, the tidal force is also known as the differential force, as well as a secondary effect of the gravitational force.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       In\n       <a href="/wiki/Celestial_mechanics" title="Celestial mechanics">\n        celestial mechanics\n       </a>\n       , the expression "tidal force" can refer to a situation in which a body or material (for example, tidal water) is mainly under the gravitational influence of a second body (for example, the Earth), but is also perturbed by the gravitational effects of a third body (for example, the Moon). The perturbing force is sometimes in such cases called a tidal force\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">\n        <a href="#cite_note-1">\n         [1]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       (for example, the\n       <a href="/wiki/Lunar_theory#Newton" title="Lunar theory">\n        perturbing force on the Moon\n       </a>\n       ): it is the difference between the force exerted by the third body on the second and the force exerted by the third body on the first.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">\n        <a href="#cite_note-2">\n         [2]\n        </a>\n <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Tidal_force&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit <div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;">\n        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Field_tidal.svg">\n         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="608" data-file-width="1123"     <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Field_tidal.svg" title="Enlarge">\n          </a>\n         </div>\n         Figure 2: The Moon\'s gravity\n         <i>\n          differential\n         </i>\n         field at the surface of the Earth is known (along with another and weaker differential effect due to the Sun) as the Tide Generating Force. This is the primary mechanism driving tidal action, explaining two tidal\n         <a href="/wiki/Equipotential" title="Equipotential">\n          equipotential\n         </a>\n         bulges, and accounting for two high tides per day. In this figure, the Earth is the central blue circle while the Moon is far off to the right. The\n         <b>\n          outward\n         </b>\n         direction of the arrows on the right and left indicates that where the Moon is overhead (or at the\n         <a href="/wiki/Nadir" title="Nadir">\n          nadir\n         </a>\n         ) its perturbing force opposes that between the earth and ocean.\n        </div>\n       </div>\n      </div>\n      <p>\n       When a body (body 1) is acted on by the gravity of another body (body 2), the field can vary significantly on body 1 between the side of the body facing body 2 and the side facing away from body 2. Figure 2 shows the differential force of gravity on a spherical body (body 1) exerted by another body (body 2). These so-called\n       <i>\n        tidal forces\n       </i>\n       cause strains on both bodies and may distort them or even, in extreme cases, break one or the other apart.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Penrose_3-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-Penrose-3">\n         [3]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       The\n       <a href="/wiki/Roche_limit" title="Roche limit">\n        Roche limit\n       </a>\n       is the distance from a planet at which tidal effects would cause an object to disintegrate because the differential force of gravity from the planet overcomes the attraction of the parts of the object for one another.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Blanc_4-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-Blanc-4">\n         [4]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       These strains would not occur if the gravitational field were uniform, because a uniform\n       <a href="/wiki/Field_(physics)" title="Field (physics)">\n        field\n       </a>\n       only causes the entire body to accelerate together in the same direction and at the same rate.\n      </p>\n      <h2>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Effects_of_tidal_forces">\n        Effects of tidal forces\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Tidal_force&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Effects of tidal forces">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h2>\n      <div class="thumb tleft">\n       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">\n        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Saturn-cassini-March-27-2004.jpg">\n         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="764" data-file-width="1024" height="164" //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/09/Saturn-cassini-March-27-2004.jpg/440px-Saturn-cassini-March-27-2004.jpg 2x" width="220"/>\n        </a>\n        <div class="thumbcaption">\n         <div class="magnify">\n          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Saturn-cassini-March-27-2004.jpg" title="Enlarge">\n          </a>\n         </div>\n         Figure 3:\n         <a href="/wiki/Saturn" title="Saturn">\n          Saturn\n         </a>\n         \'s rings are inside the orbits of its principal moons. Tidal forces oppose gravitational coalescence of the material in the rings to form moons.\n         <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MacKay_5-0">\n          <a href="#cite_note-MacKay-5">\n           [5]\n          </a>\n         </sup>\n        </div>\n       </div>\n      </div>\n      <p>\n       In the case of an infinitesimally small elastic sphere, the effect of a tidal force is to distort the shape of the body without any change in volume. The sphere becomes an\n       <a href="/wiki/Ellipsoid" title="Ellipsoid">\n        ellipsoid\n       </a>\n       with two bulges, pointing towards and away from the other body. Larger objects distort into an\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ovoid" title="Ovoid">\n        ovoid\n       </a>\n       , and are slightly compressed, which is what happens to the Earth\'s oceans under the action of the Moon. The Earth and Moon rotate about their common center of mass or\n       <a href="/wiki/Barycenter" title="Barycenter">\n        barycenter\n       </a>\n       , and their gravitational attraction provides the\n       <a href="/wiki/Centripetal_force" title="Centripetal force">\n        centripetal force\n       </a>\n       necessary to maintain this motion. To an observer on the Earth, very close to this barycenter, the situation is one of the Earth as body 1 acted upon by the gravity of the Moon as body 2. All parts of the Earth are subject to the Moon\'s gravitational forces, causing the water in the oceans to redistribute, forming bulges on the sides near the Moon and far from the Moon.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Americana_6-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-Americana-6">\n         [6]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       When a body rotates while subject to tidal forces, internal friction results in the gradual dissipation of its rotational kinetic energy as heat. In the case for the Earth, and Earth\'s Moon, the loss of rotational kinetic energy results in a gain of about 2 milliseconds per century. If the body is close enough to its primary, this can result in a rotation which is tidally locked to the orbital motion, as in the case of the Earth\'s moon.\n       <a href="/wiki/Tidal_heating" title="Tidal heating">\n        Tidal heating\n       </a>\n       produces dramatic volcanic effects on Jupiter\'s moon\n       <a href="/wiki/Io_(moon)" title="Io (moon)">\n        Io\n       </a>\n       .\n       <span id="Stress">\n       </span>\n       <a href="/wiki/Stress_(mechanics)" title="Stress (mechanics)">\n        Stresses\n       </a>\n       caused by tidal forces also cause a regular monthly pattern of\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Moonquake" title="Moonquake">\n        moonquakes\n       </a>\n       on Earth\'s Moon.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">\n        <a href="#cite_note-7">\n         [7]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       Tidal forces contribute to ocean currents, which moderate global temperatures by transporting heat energy toward the poles. It has been suggested that in addition to other factors,\n       <a href="/wiki/Beat_(acoustics)" title="Beat (acoustics)">\n        harmonic beat\n       </a>\n       variations in tidal forcing may contribute to climate changes. However, no strong link has been found to date.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">\n        <a href="#cite_note-8">\n         [8]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       Tidal effects become particularly pronounced near small bodies of high mass, such as\n       <a href="/wiki/Neutron_star" title="Neutron star">\n        neutron stars\n       </a>\n       or\n       <a href="/wiki/Black_hole" title="Black hole">\n        black holes\n       </a>\n       , where they are responsible for the "\n       <a href="/wiki/Spaghettification" title="Spaghettification">\n        spaghettification\n       </a>\n       " of infalling matter. Tidal forces create the oceanic\n       <a href="/wiki/Tide" title="Tide">\n        tide\n       </a>\n       of\n       <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">\n        Earth\n       </a>\n       \'s oceans, where the attracting bodies are the\n       <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">\n        Moon\n       </a>\n       and, to a lesser extent, the\n       <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">\n        Sun\n       </a>\n       . Tidal forces are also responsible for\n       <a href="/wiki/Tidal_locking" title="Tidal locking">\n        tidal locking\n       </a>\n       ,\n       <a href="/wiki/Tidal_acceleration" title="Tidal acceleration">\n        tidal acceleration\n       </a>\n       , and tidal heating.\n       <a href="/wiki/Tidal_triggering_of_earthquakes" title="Tidal triggering of earthquakes">\n        Tides may also induce seismicity\n       </a>\n       .\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       By generating conducting fluids within the interior of the Earth, tidal forces also affect the\n       <a
