id="bodyContent">\n    <div class="noprint" id="siteSub">\n     From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia\n    </div>\n    <div id="contentSub">\n    </div>\n    <div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">\n     Jump to:\n     <a href="#mw-head">\n      navigation\n     </a>\n     ,\n     <a href="#p-search">\n      search\n     </a>\n    </div>\n    <div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en">\n     <div class="mw-parser-output">\n      <p>\n       The\n       <b>\n        polar highs\n       </b>\n       are areas of high\n       <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_pressure" title="Atmospheric pressure">\n        atmospheric pressure\n       </a>\n       around the\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/North_pole" title="North pole">\n        north\n       </a>\n       and\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/South_pole" title="South pole">\n        south poles\n       </a>\n       ; the north polar high being the stronger one because land gains and loses heat more effectively than sea. The cold temperatures in the polar regions cause air to descend to create the high pressure (a process called\n       <a href="/wiki/Subsidence_(atmosphere)" title="Subsidence (atmosphere)">\n        subsidence\n       </a>\n       ), just as the warm temperatures around the\n       <a href="/wiki/Equator" title="Equator">\n        equator\n       </a>\n       cause air to rise to create the low pressure\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Intertropical_convergence_zone" title="Intertropical convergence zone">\n        intertropical convergence zone\n       </a>\n       . Rising air also occurs along bands of low pressure situated just below the polar highs around the 50th parallels of latitude. These extratropical convergence zones are occupied by the\n       <a href="/wiki/Polar_front" title="Polar front">\n        polar fronts\n       </a>\n       where\n       <a href="/wiki/Air_mass" title="Air mass">\n        air masses\n       </a>\n       of polar origin meet and clash with those of tropical or subtropical origin.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-The_Climate_System:_General_Circulation_and_Climate_Zones_1-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-The_Climate_System:_General_Circulation_and_Climate_Zones-1">\n         [1]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       This convergence of rising air completes the vertical cycle around the\n       <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_circulation#Polar_cell" title="Atmospheric circulation">\n        polar cell\n       </a>\n       in each latitudinal hemisphere. Closely related to this concept is the\n       <a href="/wiki/Polar_vortex" title="Polar vortex">\n        polar vortex\n       </a>\n       .\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       Surface\n       <a href="/wiki/Temperature" title="Temperature">\n        temperatures\n       </a>\n       under the polar highs are the coldest on Earth, with no month having an average temperature above freezing. Regions under the polar high also experience very low levels of\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Precipitation_(meteorology)" title="Precipitation (meteorology)">\n        precipitation\n       </a>\n       , which leads them to be known as "\n       <a href="/wiki/Polar_desert" title="Polar desert">\n        polar deserts\n       </a>\n       ".\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       Air flows outwards from the poles to create the\n       <a href="/wiki/Polar_easterlies" title="Polar easterlies">\n        polar easterlies\n       </a>\n       in the\n       <a href="/wiki/Arctic" title="Arctic">\n        arctic\n       </a>\n       and\n       <a href="/wiki/Antarctic" title="Antarctic">\n        antarctic\n       </a>\n       areas.\n      </p>\n      <h2>\n       <span
