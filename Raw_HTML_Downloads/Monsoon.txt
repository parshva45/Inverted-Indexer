 wind\n       </a>\n       accompanied by corresponding changes in\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Precipitation_(meteorology)" title="Precipitation (meteorology)">\n        precipitation\n       </a>\n       ,\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">\n        <a href="#cite_note-1">\n         [1]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       but is now used to describe seasonal changes in\n       <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth" title="Atmosphere of Earth">\n        atmospheric\n       </a>\n       circulation and precipitation associated with the asymmetric heating of land and sea.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">\n        <a href="#cite_note-2">\n         [2]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">\n        <a      </a>\n       of a seasonally changing pattern, although technically there is also a dry phase. The term is sometimes incorrectly used for locally heavy but short-term rains,\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">\n        <a href="#cite_note-4">\n         [4]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       although these rains meet the dictionary definition of monsoon.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">\n        <a href="#cite_note-5">\n         [5]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       The major monsoon systems of the world consist of the\n       <a href="/wiki/Monsoon#Africa" title="Monsoon">\n        West African\n       </a>\n       and\n       <a href="/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">\n        Asia\n       </a>\n       -\n       <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">\n        Australian\n       </a>\n       monsoons. <span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">\n        Etymology\n       </span>\n      </h2>\n      <div class="thumb tright">\n       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">\n        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Monsoon_clouds_Lucknow.JPG">\n         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1536" data-file-width="2048" height="165" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/91/Monsoon_clouds_Lucknow.JPG/330px-Monsoon_clouds_Lucknow.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/91/Monsoon_clouds_Lucknow.JPG/440px-Monsoon_clouds_Lucknow.JPG 2x" width="220"/>\n        </a>\n        <div class="thumbcaption">\n         <div class="magnify">\n          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Monsoon_clouds_Lucknow.JPG" title="Enlarge">\n          </a>\n         </div>\n         Monsoon clouds over\n         <a href="/wiki/Lucknow" title="Lucknow">\n          Lucknow\n         </a>\n         ,\n         <a href="/wiki/Uttar_Pradesh" title="Uttar Pradesh">\n          Uttar Pradesh\n         </a>\n        </div>\n       </div>\n      </div>\n      <p>\n       The English\n       <i>\n        monsoon\n       </i>\n       came from Portuguese\n       <i>\n        mon\xc3\xa7\xc3\xa3o\n       </i>\n       , ultimately from\n       <a href="/wiki/Arabic" title="Arabic">\n        Arabic\n       </a>\n       <i>\n        mawsim\n       </i>\n       (\xd9\x85\xd9\x88\xd8\xb3\xd9\x85 "season") and/or\n       <a href="/wiki/Hindi" title="Hindi">\n        Hindi\n       </a>\n       "mausam", "perhaps partly via early modern Dutch\n       <i>\n        monsun\n       </i>\n       ".\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">\n        <a href="#cite_note-9">\n         [9]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <h2>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="History">\n        History\n       </span>\n      </h2>\n      <p>\n       Strengthening of the Asian monsoon has been linked to the uplift of the\n       <a href="/wiki/Tibetan_Plateau" title="Tibetan Plateau">\n        Tibetan Plateau\n       </a>\n       after the collision of the\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Indian_sub-continent" title="Indian sub-continent">\n        Indian sub-continent\n       </a>\n       and Asia around 50 million years ago.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">\n        <a href="#cite_note-10">\n         [10]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       Because of studies of records from the\n       <a href="/wiki/Arabian_Sea" title="Arabian Sea">\n        Arabian Sea\n       </a>\n       and that of the wind-blown dust in the\n       <a href="/wiki/Loess_Plateau" title="Loess Plateau">\n        Loess Plateau\n       </a>\n       of\n       <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">\n        China\n       </a>\n       , many geologists believe the monsoon first became strong around 8 million years ago. More recently, studies of plant fossils in China and new long-duration\n       <a href="/wiki/Sediment" title="Sediment">\n        sediment\n       </a>\n       records from the\n       <a href="/wiki/South_China_Sea" title="South China Sea">\n        South China Sea\n       </a>\n       led to a timing of the monsoon beginning 15\xe2\x80\x9320 million years ago and linked to early Tibetan uplift.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">\n        <a href="#cite_note-11">\n         [11]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       Testing of this hypothesis awaits deep ocean sampling by the\n       <a href="/wiki/Integrated_Ocean_Drilling_Program" title="Integrated Ocean Drilling Program">\n        Integrated Ocean Drilling Program\n       </a>\n       .\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">\n        <a href="#cite_note-12">\n         [12]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       The monsoon has varied significantly in strength since this time, largely linked to global climate change, especially the cycle of the\n       <a href="/wiki/Pleistocene" title="Pleistocene">\n        Pleistocene\n       </a>\n       ice ages.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">\n        <a href="#cite_note-13">\n         [13]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       A study of marine plankton suggested that the Indian Monsoon strengthened around 5 million years ago. Then, during ice periods, the sea level fell and the\n       <a href="/wiki/Geology_of_Indonesia#Indonesian_Seaway" title="Geology of Indonesia">\n        Indonesian Seaway\n       </a>\n       closed. When this happened, cold waters in the Pacific were impeded from flowing into the Indian Ocean. It is believed that the resulting increase in sea surface temperatures in the Indian Ocean increased the intensity of monsoons.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">\n        <a href="#cite_note-14">\n         [14]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       Five episodes during the\n       <a href="/wiki/Quaternary" title="Quaternary">\n        Quaternary\n       </a>\n       at 2.22\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Megaannum" title="Megaannum">\n        Ma\n       </a>\n       (PL-1), 1.83 Ma (PL-2), 0.68 Ma (PL-3), 0.45 Ma (PL-4) and 0.04 Ma (PL-5) were identified which showed a weakening of\n       <a href="/wiki/Leeuwin_Current" title="Leeuwin Current">\n        Leeuwin Current (LC)\n       </a>\n       . The weakening of the LC would have an effect on the\n       <a href="/wiki/Sea_surface_temperature" title="Sea surface temperature">\n        sea surface temperature (SST) field\n       </a>\n       in the Indian Ocean, as the Indonesian through flow generally warms the Indian Ocean. Thus these five intervals could probably be those of considerable lowering of SST in the Indian Ocean and would have influenced Indian monsoon intensity. During the weak LC, there is the possibility of reduced intensity of the Indian winter monsoon and strong summer monsoon, because of change in the Indian Ocean dipole due to reduction in net heat input to the Indian Ocean through the Indonesian through flow. Thus a better understanding of the possible links between\n       <a href="/wiki/El_Ni%C3%B1o" title="El Ni\xc3\xb1o">\n        El Ni\xc3\xb1o\n       </a>\n       , Western Pacific Warm Pool,\n       <a href="/wiki/Indonesian_Throughflow" title="Indonesian Throughflow">\n        Indonesian Throughflow\n       </a>\n       , wind pattern off western Australia, and ice volume expansion and contraction can be obtained by studying the behaviour of the LC during Quaternary at close stratigraphic intervals.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">\n        <a href="#cite_note-15">\n         [15]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <h2>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Strength_of_impact">\n        Strength of impact\n       </span>\n      </h2>\n      <div In some places there is just a likelihood of having a little more or less rain. In other places, quasi semi-deserts are turned into vivid green grasslands where all sorts of plants and crops can flourish.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       The Indian Monsoon turns large parts of India from a kind of semi-desert into green lands. See photos only taken 3 months apart in the Western Ghats. In places like this it is crucial for farmers to have the right timing for putting the seeds on the fields, as it is essential to use all the rain that is available for growing crops.\n      </p>\n      <h2>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Process">\n        Process\n       </span>\n      </h2>\n      <p>\n       Monsoons are large-scale\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sea_breezes" title="Sea breezes">\n        sea breezes\n       </a>\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">\n        <a href="#cite_note-16">\n         [16]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       which occur when the temperature on land is significantly warmer or cooler than the temperature of the ocean. These temperature imbalances happen because oceans and land absorb heat in different ways. Over oceans, the air temperature remains relatively stable for two reasons: water has a relatively high\n       <a href="/wiki/Heat_capacity" title="Heat capacity">\n        heat capacity\n       </a>\n       (3.9 to 4.2 J g\n       <sup>\n        \xe2\x88\x921\n       </sup>\n       K\n       <sup>\n        \xe2\x88\x921\n       </sup>\n       ),\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17">\n        <a href="#cite_note-17">\n         [17]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       and because both\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Conduction_(heat)" title="Conduction (heat)">\n        conduction\n       </a>\n       and\n       <a href="/wiki/Convective_heat_transfer" title="Convective heat transfer">\n        convection\n       </a>\n       will equilibrate a hot or cold surface with deeper water (up to 50 metres). In contrast, dirt, sand, and rocks have lower heat capacities (0.19 to 0.35 J g\n       <sup>\n        \xe2\x88\x921\n       </sup>\n       K\n       <sup>\n        \xe2\x88\x921\n       </sup>\n       ),\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18">\n        <a href="#cite_note-18">\n         [18]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       and they can only transmit heat into the earth by conduction and not by convection. Therefore, bodies of water stay at a more even temperature, while land temperature are more variable.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       During warmer months sunlight heats the surfaces of both land and oceans, but land temperatures rise more quickly. As the land\'s surface becomes warmer, the air above it expands and an area of\n       <a href="/wiki/Thermal_low" title="Thermal low">\n        low pressure\n       </a>\n       develops. Meanwhile, the ocean remains at a lower temperature than the land, and the air above it retains a higher pressure. This difference in pressure causes\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sea_breezes" title="Sea breezes">\n        sea breezes\n       </a>\n       to blow from the ocean to the land, bringing moist air inland. This moist air rises to a higher altitude over land and then it flows back toward the ocean (thus completing the cycle). However, when the air rises, and while it is still over the land, the air\n       <a href="/wiki/Joule%E2%80%93Thomson_effect" title="Joule\xe2\x80\x93Thomson effect">\n        cools\n       </a>\n       . This decreases the air\'s\n       <a href="/wiki/Relative_humidity" title="Relative humidity">\n        ability to hold water\n       </a>\n       , and this causes\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Precipitation_(meteorology)" title="Precipitation (meteorology)">\n        precipitation\n       </a>\n       over the land. This is why summer monsoons cause so much rain over land.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       In the colder months, the cycle is reversed. Then the land cools faster than the oceans and the air over the land has higher pressure than air over the ocean. This causes the air over the land to flow to the ocean. When humid air rises over the ocean, it cools, and this causes precipitation over the oceans. (The cool air then flows towards the land to complete the cycle.)\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       Most summer monsoons have a dominant westerly component and a strong tendency to ascend and produce copious amounts of rain (because of the condensation of water vapor in the rising air). The intensity and duration, however, are not uniform from year to year. Winter monsoons, by contrast, have a dominant easterly component and a strong tendency to diverge, subside and cause drought.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19">\n        <a href="#cite_note-19">\n         [19]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       Similar\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Precipitation_(meteorology)" title="Precipitation (meteorology)">\n        rainfall\n       </a>\n       is caused when moist ocean air is lifted upwards by mountains,\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MT_20-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-MT-20">\n         [20]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       surface heating,\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21">\n        <a href="#cite_note-21">\n         [21]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       convergence at the surface,\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22">\n        <a href="#cite_note-22">\n         [22]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       divergence aloft, or from storm-produced outflows at the surface.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23">\n        <a href="#cite_note-23">\n         [23]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       However the lifting occurs, the air cools due to expansion in lower pressure, and this produces\n       <a href="/wiki/Condensation" title="Condensation">\n        condensation\n       </a>\n       .\n      </p>\n      <h2>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Global_monsoon">\n        Global monsoon\n       </span>\n      </h2>\n      <h3>\n       <span class="mw-headline" Intertropical Convergence Zone\n       </a>\n       and the great seasonal temperature and humidity differences between the\n       <a href="/wiki/Sahara" title="Sahara">\n        Sahara\n   </a>\n       , are interrupted by the northern shift in the\n       <a href="/wiki/Intertropical_Convergence_Zone" title="Intertropical Convergence Zone">\n        ITCZ\n       </a>\n       and resultant southerly, rain-bearing winds during the summer.  <b>\n        North American monsoon\n       </b>\n       (\n       <b>\n        NAM\n       </b>\n       ) occurs from late June or early July into September, originating over Mexico and spreading into the southwest United States by mid-July. It affects Mexico along the\n       <a href="/wiki/Sierra_Madre_Occidental" title="Sierra Madre Occidental">\n        Sierra Madre Occidental\n       </a>\n       as well as\n       <a href="/wiki/Arizona" title="Arizona">\n        Arizona\n       </a>\n       ,\n       <a href="/wiki/New_Mexico" title="New Mexico">\n        New Mexico\n       </a>\n       ,\n       <a href="/wiki/Nevada" title="Nevada">\n        Nevada\n       </a>\n       ,\n       <a href="/wiki/Utah" title="Utah">\n        Utah\n       </a>\n       ,\n       <a href="/wiki/Colorado" title="Colorado">\n        Colorado\n       </a>\n       ,\n       <a href="/wiki/West_Texas" title="West Texas">\n        West Texas\n       </a>\n       and\n       <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">\n        California\n       </a>\n       . It pushes as far west as the\n       <a href="/wiki/Peninsular_Ranges" title="Peninsular Ranges">\n        Peninsular Ranges\n       </a>\n       and\n       <a href="/wiki/Transverse_Ranges" title="Transverse Ranges">\n        Transverse Ranges\n       </a>\n       of\n       <a href="/wiki/Southern_California" title="Southern California">\n        Southern California\n       </a>\n       , but rarely reaches the coastal strip (a wall of desert thunderstorms only a half-hour\'s drive away is a common summer sight from the sunny skies along the coast during the monsoon). The North American monsoon is known to many as the\n       <i>\n        Summer\n       </i>\n       ,\n       <i>\n        Southwest\n       </i>\n       ,\n       <i>\n        Mexican\n       </i>\n       or\n       <i>\n        Arizona\n       </i>\n       monsoon.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32">\n        <a href="#cite_note-32">\n         [32]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33">\n        <a href="#cite_note-33">\n         [33]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       It is also sometimes called the\n       <i>\n        Desert monsoon\n       </i>\n       as a large part of the affected area are the\n       <a href="/wiki/Mojave_Desert" title="Mojave Desert">\n        Mojave\n       </a>\n       and\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sonoran_desert" title="Sonoran desert">\n        Sonoran deserts\n       </a>\n       . However, it is debatable whether the\n       <a href="/wiki/North_American_Monsoon" title="North American Monsoon">\n        North\n       </a>\n       and\n       <a href="/wiki/South_America" title="South America">\n        South American\n       </a>\n       weather patterns with incomplete wind reversal should be counted as true monsoons.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rohli_6-1">\n        <a href="#cite_note-Rohli-6">\n         [6]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <h3>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Asia">\n        Asia\n       </span>\n      </h3>\n      <p>\n       The Asian monsoons may be classified into a few sub-systems, such as the Indian Subcontinental Monsoon which affects the Indian subcontinent and surrounding regions including Nepal, and the East Asian Monsoon which affects\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/People%27s_Republic_of_China" title="People\'s Republic of China">\n        southern China\n       </a>\n       ,\n       <a href="/wiki/Taiwan" title="Taiwan">\n        Taiwan\n       </a>\n       ,\n       <a href="/wiki/Korea" title="Korea">\n        Korea\n       </a>\n       and parts of\n       <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">\n        Japan\n       </a>\n       .\n      </p>\n      <h4>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="South_Asian_monsoon">\n        South Asian monsoon\n       </span>\n      </h4>\n      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">\n       Main article:\n       <a href="/wiki/Monsoon_of_South_Asia" title="Monsoon of South Asia">\n        Monsoon of South Asia\n       </a>\n      </div>\n      <h5>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Southwest_monsoon">\n        Southwest monsoon\n       </span>\n      </h5>\n      <div class="thumb tright">\n       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">\n        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:India_southwest_summer_monsoon_onset_map_en.svg">\n         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1852" data-file-width="1639" height="249" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/59/India_southwest_summer_monsoon_onset_map_en.svg/220px-India_southwest_summer_monsoon_onset_map_en.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/59/India_southwest_summer_monsoon_onset_map_en.svg/330px-India_southwest_summer_monsoon_onset_map_en.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/59/India_southwest_summer_monsoon_onset_map_en.svg/440px-India_southwest_summer_monsoon_onset_map_en.svg.png 2x" width="220"/>\n        </a>\n        <div class="thumbcaption">\n         <div class="magnify">\n          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:India_southwest_summer_monsoon_onset_map_en.svg" title="Enlarge">\n          </a>\n         </div>\n         Onset dates and prevailing wind currents of the southwest summer monsoons in India\n        </div>\n       </div>\n      </div>\n      <p>\n       The southwestern summer monsoons occur from July through September. The\n       <a href="/wiki/Thar_Desert" title="Thar Desert">\n        Thar Desert\n       </a>\n       and adjoining areas of the northern and central Indian subcontinent heat up considerably during the hot summers. This causes a low pressure area over the northern and central Indian subcontinent. To fill this void, the moisture-laden winds from the\n       <a href="/wiki/Indian_Ocean" title="Indian Ocean">\n        Indian Ocean\n       </a>\n       rush into the subcontinent. These winds, rich in moisture, are drawn towards the\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Himalaya" title="Himalaya">\n        Himalayas\n       </a>\n       . The Himalayas act like a high wall, blocking the winds from passing into\n       <a href="/wiki/Central_Asia" title="Central Asia">\n        Central Asia\n       </a>\n       , and forcing them to rise. As the clouds rise their\n       <a href="/wiki/Temperature" title="Temperature">\n        temperature\n       </a>\n       drops and precipitation occurs. Some areas of the subcontinent receive up to 10,000\xc2\xa0mm (390\xc2\xa0in) of rain annually.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       The southwest monsoon is generally expected to begin around the beginning of June and fade away by the end of September. The moisture-laden winds on reaching the southernmost point of the\n       <a href="/wiki/Indian_subcontinent" title="Indian subcontinent">\n        Indian Peninsula\n       </a>\n       , due to its topography, become divided into two parts: the\n       <i>\n        Arabian Sea Branch\n       </i>\n       and the\n       <i>\n        Bay of Bengal Branch\n       </i>\n       .\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       The\n       <i>\n        Arabian Sea Branch\n       </i>\n       of the Southwest Monsoon first hits the\n       <a href="/wiki/Western_Ghats" title="Western Ghats">\n        Western Ghats\n       </a>\n       of the coastal state of\n       <a href="/wiki/Kerala" title="Kerala">\n        Kerala, India\n       </a>\n       , thus making this area the first state in India to receive rain from the Southwest Monsoon. This branch of the monsoon moves northwards along the\n       <a href="/wiki/Western_Ghats" title="Western Ghats">\n        Western Ghats\n       </a>\n       (\n       <a href="/wiki/Konkan" title="Konkan">\n        Konkan\n       </a>\n       and\n       <a href="/wiki/Goa" title="Goa">\n        Goa\n       </a>\n       ) with precipitation on coastal areas, west of the Western Ghats. The eastern areas of the Western Ghats do not receive much rain from this monsoon as the wind does not cross the Western Ghats.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       The\n       <i>\n        Bay of Bengal Branch\n       </i>\n       of Southwest Monsoon flows over the\n       <a href="/wiki/Bay_of_Bengal" title="Bay of Bengal">\n        Bay of Bengal\n       </a>\n       heading towards\n       <a href="/wiki/North-East_India" title="North-East India">\n        North-East India\n       </a>\n       and\n       <a href="/wiki/Bengal" title="Bengal">\n        Bengal\n       </a>\n       , picking up more moisture from the Bay of Bengal. The winds arrive at the\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Himalaya" title="Himalaya">\n        Eastern Himalayas\n       </a>\n       with large amounts of rain.\n       <a href="/wiki/Mawsynram" title="Mawsynram">\n        Mawsynram\n       </a>\n       , situated on the southern slopes of the Khasi Hills in\n       <a href="/wiki/Meghalaya" title="Meghalaya">\n        Meghalaya, India\n       </a>\n       , is one of the wettest places on Earth. After the arrival at the Eastern Himalayas, the winds turns towards the\n       <a href="/wiki/West" title="West">\n        west\n       </a>\n       , travelling over the\n       <a href="/wiki/Indo-Gangetic_Plain" title="Indo-Gangetic Plain">\n        Indo-Gangetic Plain\n       </a>\n       at a rate of roughly 1\xe2\x80\x932 weeks per state,\n       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">\n        [\n        <i>\n         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">\n          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2007)">\n           citation needed\n          </span>\n         </a>\n        </i>\n        ]\n       </sup>\n       pouring rain all along its way. June 1 is regarded as the date of onset of the monsoon in India, as indicated by the arrival of the monsoon in the southernmost state of Kerala.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       The monsoon accounts for 80% of the rainfall in India.\n       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">\n        [\n        <i>\n         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">\n          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2007)">\n           citation needed\n          </span>\n         </a>\n        </i>\n        ]\n       </sup>\n       Indian agriculture (which accounts for 25% of the GDP and employs 70% of the population) is heavily dependent on the rains, for growing crops especially like\n       <a href="/wiki/Cotton" title="Cotton">\n        cotton\n       </a>\n       ,\n       <a href="/wiki/Rice" title="Rice">\n        rice\n       </a>\n       ,\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oilseeds" title="Oilseeds">\n        oilseeds\n       </a>\n       and coarse grains. A delay of a few days in the arrival of the monsoon can badly affect the economy, as evidenced in the numerous droughts in India in the 1990s.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       The monsoon is widely welcomed and appreciated by city-dwellers as well, for it provides relief from the climax of summer heat in June.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34">\n        <a href="#cite_note-34">\n         [34]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       However, the roads take a battering every year. Often houses and streets are waterlogged and\n       <a href="/wiki/Slum" title="Slum">\n        slums\n       </a>\n       are flooded despite drainage systems. A lack of city infrastructure coupled with changing climate patterns causes severe economic loss including damage to property and loss of lives, as evidenced in the\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/2005_Maharashtra_floods" title="2005 Maharashtra floods">\n        2005 flooding in Mumbai\n       </a>\n       that brought the city to a standstill.\n       <a href="/wiki/Bangladesh" title="Bangladesh">\n        Bangladesh\n       </a>\n       and certain regions of India like\n       <a href="/wiki/Assam" title="Assam">\n        Assam\n       </a>\n       and\n       <a href="/wiki/West_Bengal" title="West Bengal">\n        West Bengal\n       </a>\n       , also frequently experience heavy\n       <a href="/wiki/Flood" title="Flood">\n        floods\n       </a>\n       during this season. Recently, areas in India that used to receive scanty rainfall throughout the year, like the\n       <a href="/wiki/Thar_Desert" title="Thar Desert">\n        Thar Desert\n       </a>\n       , have surprisingly ended up receiving floods due to the prolonged monsoon season.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       The influence of the Southwest Monsoon is felt as far north as in China\'s\n       <a href="/wiki/Xinjiang" title="Xinjiang">\n        Xinjiang\n       </a>\n       . It is estimated that about 70% of all precipitation in the central part of the\n       <a href="/wiki/Tian_Shan" title="Tian Shan">\n        Tian Shan Mountains\n       </a>\n       falls during the three summer months, when the region is under the monsoon influence; about 70% of that is directly of "cyclonic" (i.e., monsoon-driven) origin (as opposed to "\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Convection_rain" title="Convection rain">\n        local convection\n       </a>\n       ").\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35">\n        <a href="#cite_note-35">\n         [35]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <h5>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Northeast_monsoon">\n        Northeast monsoon\n       </span>\n      </h5>\n      <div class="thumb tright">\n       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">\n        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Heavy_clouds_over_Salt_Lake,_Calcutta.JPG">\n         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2848" data-file-width="4288" height="146" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7e/Heavy_clouds_over_Salt_Lake%2C_Calcutta.JPG/220px-Heavy_clouds_over_Salt_Lake%2C_Calcutta.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7e/Heavy_clouds_over_Salt_Lake%2C_Calcutta.JPG/330px-Heavy_clouds_over_Salt_Lake%2C_Calcutta.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7e/Heavy_clouds_over_Salt_Lake%2C_Calcutta.JPG/440px-Heavy_clouds_over_Salt_Lake%2C_Calcutta.JPG 2x" width="220"/>\n        </a>\n        <div class="thumbcaption">\n         <div class="magnify">\n          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Heavy_clouds_over_Salt_Lake,_Calcutta.JPG" title="Enlarge">\n          </a>\n         </div>\n         Monsoon clouds in\n         <a href="/wiki/Kolkata" title="Kolkata">\n          Kolkata\n         </a>\n        </div>\n       </div>\n      </div>\n      <p>\n       Around September, with the sun fast retreating south, the northern land mass of the Indian subcontinent begins to cool off rapidly. With this air pressure begins to build over northern India, the Indian Ocean and its surrounding atmosphere still holds its heat. This causes cold wind to sweep down from the\n       <a href="/wiki/Himalayas" title="Himalayas">\n        Himalayas\n       </a>\n       and\n       <a href="/wiki/Indo-Gangetic_Plain" title="Indo-Gangetic Plain">\n        Indo-Gangetic Plain\n       </a>\n       towards the vast spans of the Indian Ocean south of the\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Deccan" title="Deccan">\n        Deccan\n       </a>\n       peninsula. This is known as the Northeast Monsoon or Retreating Monsoon.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       While travelling towards the Indian Ocean, the dry cold wind picks up some moisture from the\n       <a href="/wiki/Bay_of_Bengal" title="Bay of Bengal">\n        Bay of Bengal\n       </a>\n       and pours it over peninsular India and parts of\n       <a href="/wiki/Sri_Lanka" title="Sri Lanka">\n        Sri Lanka\n       </a>\n       . Cities like\n       <a href="/wiki/Chennai" title="Chennai">\n        Chennai\n       </a>\n       , which get less rain from the Southwest Monsoon, receive rain from this Monsoon. About 50% to 60% of the rain received by the state of\n       <a href="/wiki/Tamil_Nadu" title="Tamil Nadu">\n        Tamil Nadu\n       </a>\n       is from the Northeast Monsoon.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36">\n        <a href="#cite_note-36">\n         [36]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       In Southern\n       <a href="/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">\n        Asia\n       </a>\n       , the northeastern monsoons take place from December to early March when the surface\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/High-pressure_system" title="High-pressure system">\n        high-pressure system\n       </a>\n       is strongest.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37">\n        <a href="#cite_note-37">\n         [37]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       The\n       <a href="/wiki/Jet_stream" title="Jet stream">\n        jet stream\n       </a>\n       in this region splits into the southern subtropical jet and the polar jet. The subtropical flow directs northeasterly winds to blow across southern Asia, creating dry\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Air_stream" title="Air stream">\n        air streams\n       </a>\n       which produce clear skies over India. Meanwhile, a low pressure system develops over\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/South-East_Asia" title="South-East Asia">\n        South-East Asia\n       </a>\n       and\n       <a href="/wiki/Australasia" title="Australasia">\n        Australasia\n       </a>\n       and winds are directed toward\n       <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">\n        Australia\n       </a>\n       known as a\n       <a href="/wiki/Monsoon_trough" title="Monsoon trough">\n        monsoon trough\n       </a>\n       .\n      </p>\n      <h4>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="East_Asian_Monsoon">\n        East Asian Monsoon\n       </span>\n      </h4>\n      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">\n       Main article:\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/East_Asian_monsoon" title="East Asian monsoon">\n        East Asian monsoon\n       </a>\n      </div>\n      <p>\n       The East Asian monsoon affects large parts of\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Indo-China" title="Indo-China">\n        Indo-China\n       </a>\n       ,\n       <a href="/wiki/Philippines" title="Philippines">\n        Philippines\n       </a>\n       ,\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/People%27s_Republic_of_China" title="People\'s Republic of China">\n        China\n       </a>\n       ,\n       <a href="/wiki/Taiwan" title="Taiwan">\n        Taiwan\n       </a>\n       ,\n       <a href="/wiki/Korea" title="Korea">\n        Korea\n       </a>\n       and\n       <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">\n        Japan\n       </a>\n       . It is characterised by a warm, rainy summer monsoon and a cold, dry winter monsoon. The rain occurs in a concentrated belt that stretches east-west except in East China where it is tilted east-northeast over Korea and Japan. The seasonal rain is known as\n       <i>\n        Meiyu\n       </i>\n       in China,\n       <i>\n        Jangma\n       </i>\n       in Korea, and\n       <i>\n        Bai-u\n       </i>\n       in Japan, with the latter two resembling frontal rain.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       The onset of the summer monsoon is marked by a period of premonsoonal rain over South China and Taiwan in early May. From May through August, the summer monsoon shifts through a series of dry and rainy phases as the rain belt moves northward, beginning over\n       <a href="/wiki/Indochina" title="Indochina">\n        Indochina\n       </a>\n       and the\n       <a href="/wiki/South_China_Sea" title="South China Sea">\n        South China Sea\n       </a>\n       (May), to the\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yangtze_River" title="Yangtze River">\n        Yangtze River Basin\n       </a>\n       and Japan (June) and finally to\n       <a href="/wiki/North_China" title="North China">\n        North China\n       </a>\n       and Korea (July). When the monsoon ends in August, the rain belt moves back to\n       <a href="/wiki/Northern_and_southern_China" title="Northern and southern China">\n        South China\n       </a>\n       .\n      </p>\n      <h3>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Australia">\n        Australia\n       </span>\n      </h3>\n      <div class="thumb tright">\n       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">\n        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Evening_monsoonal_squall.jpg">\n         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="997" data-file-width="1500" height="146" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/03/Evening_monsoonal_squall.jpg/220px-Evening_monsoonal_squall.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/03/Evening_monsoonal_squall.jpg/330px-Evening_monsoonal_squall.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/03/Evening_monsoonal_squall.jpg/440px-Evening_monsoonal_squall.jpg 2x" width="220"/>\n        </a>\n        <div class="thumbcaption">\n         <div class="magnify">\n          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Evening_monsoonal_squall.jpg" title="Enlarge">\n          </a>\n         </div>\n         Monsoonal squall nears\n         <a href="/wiki/Darwin,_Northern_Territory" title="Darwin, Northern Territory">\n          Darwin, Northern Territory\n         </a>\n         ,\n         <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">\n          Australia\n         </a>\n        </div>\n       </div>\n      </div>\n      <p>\n       Also known as the\n       <b>\n        Indo-Australian Monsoon\n       </b>\n       . The rainy season occurs from September to February and it is a major source of energy for the Hadley circulation during boreal winter. The\n       <i>\n        Maritime Continent Monsoon\n       </i>\n       and the\n       <i>\n        Australian Monsoon\n       </i>\n       may be considered to be the same system, the Indo-Australian Monsoon.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       It is associated with the development of the\n       <a href="/wiki/Siberian_High" title="Siberian High">\n        Siberian High\n       </a>\n       and the movement of the heating maxima from the\n       <a href="/wiki/Northern_Hemisphere" title="Northern Hemisphere">\n        Northern Hemisphere\n       </a>\n       to the\n       <a href="/wiki/Southern_Hemisphere" title="Southern Hemisphere">\n        Southern Hemisphere\n       </a>\n       . North-easterly winds flow down\n       <a href="/wiki/Southeast_Asia" title="Southeast Asia">\n        Southeast Asia\n       </a>\n       , are turned north-westerly/westerly by\n       <a href="/wiki/Borneo" title="Borneo">\n        Borneo\n       </a>\n       topography towards\n       <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">\n        Australia\n       </a>\n       . This forms a cyclonic circulation vortex over Borneo, which together with descending cold surges of winter air from higher latitudes, cause significant weather phenomena in the region. Examples are the formation of a rare low-latitude tropical storm in 2001,\n       <a href="/wiki/Tropical_Storm_Vamei" title="Tropical Storm Vamei">\n        Tropical Storm Vamei\n       </a>\n       , and the\n       <a href="/wiki/2007_Jakarta_flood" title="2007 Jakarta flood">\n        devastating flood of Jakarta\n       </a>\n       in 2007.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       The onset of the monsoon over the Maritime Continent tends to follow the heating maxima down\n       <a href="/wiki/Vietnam" title="Vietnam">\n        Vietnam\n       </a>\n       and the\n       <a href="/wiki/Malay_Peninsula" title="Malay Peninsula">\n        Malay Peninsula\n       </a>\n       (September), to\n       <a href="/wiki/Sumatra" title="Sumatra">\n        Sumatra\n       </a>\n       ,\n       <a href="/wiki/Borneo" title="Borneo">\n        Borneo\n       </a>\n       and the\n       <a href="/wiki/Philippines" title="Philippines">\n        Philippines\n       </a>\n       (October), to\n       <a href="/wiki/Java" title="Java">\n        Java\n       </a>\n       ,\n       <a href="/wiki/Sulawesi" title="Sulawesi">\n        Sulawesi\n       </a>\n       (November),\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Irian_Jaya" title="Irian Jaya">\n        Irian Jaya\n       </a>\n       and\n       <a href="/wiki/Northern_Australia" title="Northern Australia">\n        Northern Australia\n       </a>\n       (December, January). However, the monsoon is not a simple response to heating but a more complex interaction of topography, wind and sea, as demonstrated by its abrupt rather than gradual withdrawal from the region. The Australian monsoon (the "Wet") occurs in the southern summer when the monsoon trough develops over Northern Australia. Over three-quarters of annual rainfall in Northern Australia falls during this time.\n      </p>\n      <h3>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Europe">\n        Europe\n       </span>\n      </h3>\n      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">\n       See also:\n       <a href="/wiki/Climate_of_Europe" title="Climate of Europe">\n        Climate of Europe\n       </a>\n      </div>\n      <p>\n       The\n       <b>\n        European Monsoon\n       </b>\n       (more commonly known as the\n       <b>\n        return of the westerlies\n       </b>\n       ) is the result of a resurgence of westerly winds from the\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atlantic" title="Atlantic">\n        Atlantic\n       </a>\n       , where they become loaded with wind and rain.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38">\n        <a href="#cite_note-38">\n         [38]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       These westerly winds are a common phenomenon during the European winter, but they ease as spring approaches in late March and through April and May. The winds pick up again in June, which is why this phenomenon is also referred to as "the return of the westerlies".\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39">\n        <a href="#cite_note-39">\n         [39]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       The rain usually arrives in two waves, at the beginning of June and again in mid- to late June. The European monsoon is not a monsoon in the traditional sense in that it doesn\'t meet all the requirements to be classified as such. Instead the return of the westerlies is more regarded as a conveyor belt that delivers a series of low pressure centres to\n       <a href="/wiki/Western_Europe" title="Western Europe">\n        Western Europe\n       </a>\n       where they create unsettled weather. These storms generally feature significantly lower than average temperatures, fierce rain or hail, thunder and strong winds.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40">\n        <a href="#cite_note-40">\n         [40]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       The return of the westerlies affects Europe\'s\n       <a href="/wiki/Northern_Europe" title="Northern Europe">\n        Northern Atlantic\n       </a>\n       coastline, more precisely\n       <a href="/wiki/Ireland" title="Ireland">\n        Ireland\n       </a>\n       ,\n       <a href="/wiki/Great_Britain" title="Great Britain">\n        Great Britain\n       </a>\n       , the\n       <a href="/wiki/Benelux" title="Benelux">\n        Benelux countries\n       </a>\n       ,\n       <a href="/wiki/Western_Germany" title="Western Germany">\n        Western Germany\n       </a>\n       ,\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Northern_France" title="Northern France">\n        Northern France\n       </a>\n       and parts of\n       <a href="/wiki/Scandinavia" title="Scandinavia">\n        Scandinavia\n       </a>\n       .\n      </p>\n      <h2>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">\n        See also\n       </span>\n      </h2>\n      <ul>\n       <li>\n        <a href="/wiki/Tropical_monsoon_climate" title="Tropical monsoon climate">\n         Tropical monsoon climate\n        </a>\n       </li>\n      </ul>\n      <h2>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="References">\n        References\n       </span>\n      </h2>\n      <div class="reflist columns references-column-width" style="-moz-column-width: 30em;
