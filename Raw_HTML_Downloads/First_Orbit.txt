   </table>\n      <p>\n       <i>\n        <b>\n         First Orbit\n        </b>\n       </i>\n       is a feature-length, experimental documentary film about\n       <a href="/wiki/Vostok_1" title="Vostok 1">\n        Vostok 1\n       </a>\n       , the first manned space flight around the\n       <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">\n        Earth\n       </a>\n       . By matching the orbit of the\n       <a href="/wiki/International_Space_Station" title="International Space Station">\n        International Space Station\n       </a>\n       to that of Vostok 1 as closely as possible, in terms of\n       <a href="/wiki/Ground_track" title="Ground track">\n        ground track\n       </a>\n       and time of day, documentary filmmaker\n       <a href="/wiki/Christopher_Riley" title="Christopher Riley">\n        Christopher Riley\n       </a>\n       and\n       <a href="/wiki/European_Space_Agency" title="European Space Agency">\n        European Space Agency\n       </a>\n       astronaut\n       <a href="/wiki/Paolo_Nespoli" title="Paolo Nespoli">\n        Paolo Nespoli\n       </a>\n       were able to film the view that\n       <a href="/wiki/Yuri_Gagarin" title="Yuri Gagarin">\n        Yuri Gagarin\n       </a>\n       saw on his pioneering orbital space flight. This new footage was cut together with the original Vostok 1 mission audio recordings sourced from the Russian State Archive of Scientific and Technical Documentation. The film features the music of composer Philip Sheppard.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n      </p>\n      <div class="toc" id="toc">\n       <div class="toctitle">\n        <h2>\n         Contents\n   <span class="toctext">\n           External links\n          </span>\n         </a>\n        </li>\n       </ul>\n      </div>\n      <p>\n      </p>\n      <h2>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Production">\n        Production\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=First_Orbit&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Production">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h2>\n      <p>\n       Although the film archive of Gagarin\xe2\x80\x99s training, preparations, and subsequent world tour is extensive, footage of the actual Vostok 1 flight hardly exists. In 2010 Riley came up with the idea to film Yuri\xe2\x80\x99s original view of the Earth from space through the International Space Station\'s new\n       <a href="/wiki/Cupola_(ISS_module)" title="Cupola (ISS module)">\n        giant-windowed cupola\n       </a>\n       .\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       The International Space Station (ISS) orbits the Earth once every 90 minutes, but it does not always follow the same route that Gagarin took. To find out when filming opportunities might occur, the European Space Agency (ESA) teamed Riley up with German orbital mechanical engineer, Gerald Ziegler. Ziegler discovered that the ISS covered similar ground to Gagarin\'s Vostok 1 spaceship approximately every week. To complicate things further, the filmmakers needed to film at exactly the same time of day that Gagarin made his flight: passing over Gagarin\xe2\x80\x99s launch site, near the Aral Sea, at 06:07 UT and into the nightside of Earth over the Pacific Ocean at 06:37, before emerging into sunlight again over the Southern Atlantic at 07:10 UT and passing across the African continent and the Middle East, returning to the ground at 07:55 UT, just north of the Caspian Sea.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       Further calculations confirmed that opportunities to film this trajectory, with the correct sun angles, at this exact time of day, only came around once every six weeks. According to Riley, the second challenge was fitting these filming opportunities into crew time on board the space station. "The astronauts have a busy schedule; conducting a packed programme of experiments, Earth observations and activities like sleep, exercise and meal times. This meant that accommodating the extra filming request for\n       <i>\n        First Orbit\n       </i>\n       was yet another challenge for the ESA mission directors," he told BBC news in a March 2011 interview.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gagarin.27s_spaceflight_1_1-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-Gagarin.27s_spaceflight_1-1">\n         [1]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       On the final flight path back towards Gagarin\'s landing site, the scenes shot for\n       <i>\n        First Orbit\n       </i>\n       are slightly to the east of the original Vostok 1 trajectory. Because the vantage point is so high, the vista was similar to that of Gagarin\'s vantage point.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       Mission directors Roland Luettgens and Giovanni Gravili worked closely with the team to turn the filming opportunities into precise technical notes which translated Chris\'s camera directions into instructions for the crew. After a brief test shoot in November 2010, conducted by NASA\'s\n       <a href="/wiki/Expedition_25" title="Expedition 25">\n        Expedition 25\n       </a>\n       astronaut\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Doug_Wheelock" title="Doug Wheelock">\n        Doug Wheelock\n       </a>\n       , European Space Agency astronaut\n       <a href="/wiki/Paolo_Nespoli" title="Paolo Nespoli">\n        Paolo Nespoli\n       </a>\n       filmed most of the footage for the project in early January 2011. This new footage showed the Earth as Gagarin would have seen it almost exactly fifty years before. The film does include views of the moon, though. When Gagarin flew into the nightside of the Earth on 12 April 1961, there was a crescent moon and, according to his autobiography,\n       <i>\n        <a href="/wiki/Road_to_the_Stars" title="Road to the Stars">\n         Road to the Stars\n        </a>\n       </i>\n       , Gagarin tried looking for the moon out of curiosity, to see what it looked like from space. Unfortunately, it was not in his field of view. The filmmakers added the moon into the scene as a tribute.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Recreating_Yuri_Gagarin.27s_journey_into_orbit_2-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-Recreating_Yuri_Gagarin.27s_journey_into_orbit-2">\n         [2]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <h3>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Music">\n        Music\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=First_Orbit&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Music">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h3>\n      <p>\n       The music in\n       <i>\n        First Orbit\n       </i>\n       was composed by Philip Sheppard and was taken from his album\n       <i>\n        Cloud Songs\n       </i>\n       . Riley first worked with Sheppard in 2006 on the Sundance award-winning feature documentary film,\n       <i>\n        <a href="/wiki/In_the_Shadow_of_the_Moon_(film)" title="In the Shadow of the Moon (film)">\n         In the Shadow of the Moon\n        </a>\n       </i>\n       . Since that time, he had worked on a new suite of music inspired by space flight, which he donated to this new film project.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       Coincidentally, the music in the film was taken on board the ISS by NASA astronaut\n       <a href="/wiki/Catherine_Coleman" title="Catherine Coleman">\n        Catherine Coleman\n       </a>\n       before it was used in the film. Catherine Coleman flew to the ISS with astronaut and\n       <i>\n        First Orbit\n       </i>\n       cameraman\n       <a href="/wiki/Paolo_Nespoli" title="Paolo Nespoli">\n        Paolo Nespoli\n       </a>\n       without either of them knowing about the other\'s connection to the film project.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-The_Making_Of_.27First_Orbit.27_3-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-The_Making_Of_.27First_Orbit.27-3">\n         [3]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <h3>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Archive_audio">\n        Archive audio\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=First_Orbit&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Archive audio">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h3>\n      <p>\n       The producers sourced portions of Gagarin\'s original mission audio for\n       <i>\n        First Orbit\n       </i>\n       from the Russian State Archive. These historic recordings principally document the conversation between Yuri Gagarin on board Vostok 1 and\n       <a href="/wiki/Sergei_Korolev" title="Sergei Korolev">\n        Sergei Korolev\n       </a>\n       on the ground. According to Riley, it is the first time that such quantities of the flight have been heard outside Russia.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gagarin.27s_spaceflight_1_1-1">\n        <a href="#cite_note-Gagarin.27s_spaceflight_1-1">\n         [1]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       Additional archive audio in the film comes from a Radio Moscow report broadcast during the flight and news bulletins from the\n       <a href="/wiki/BBC" title="BBC">\n        BBC\n       </a>\n       and the\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Information_Telegraph_Agency_of_Russia" title="Information Telegraph Agency of Russia">\n        Information Telegraph Agency of Russia\n       </a>\n       TASS. The Russian recordings in the film are subtitled in English.\n      </p>\n      <h2>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Global_distribution">\n        Global distribution\n       </span>\n global\n       <a href="/wiki/YouTube" title="YouTube">\n        YouTube\n       </a>\n       premiere on 12 April 2011. On the day of release the film was viewed 1.3 million times within 24 hours and 2.3 million times within 48 hours.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">\n        <a href="#cite_note-4">\n         [4]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       In addition to this online premi\xc3\xa8re the film opened on over 1600 screens around the world in more than 130 countries,\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">\n        <a href="#cite_note-5">\n         [5]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       including UNESCO HQ in Paris, Boeing HQ in Seattle, and the European Space Agency\xe2\x80\x99s Columbus Control Centre near Munich. In October 2011 the producers announced a call to action to translate the film into as many languages as possible.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">\n        <a href="#cite_note-6">\n         [6]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       Their translation campaign resulted in 30 new languages which were published for free on the project\'s web site. To make these translations as accessible as possible the producers launched a crowd funding campaign on\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/IndieGoGo" title="IndieGoGo">\n        IndieGoGo\n       </a>\n       to manufacture multi-language DVD and Blu-ray discs of the film.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">\n        <a href="#cite_note-7">\n         [7]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">\n        <a href="#cite_note-8">\n         [8]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       They raised only 20% of their target amount, but went ahead with the production anyway, releasing a limited run of discs on 12 March 2012, through the project\'s web site, and Amazon, almost exactly a year after the film was originally launched.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">\n        <a href="#cite_note-9">\n         [9]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <h2>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="References">\n
