     </sup>\n        </td>\n       </tr>\n      </table>\n      <p>\n       The\n       <b>\n        radius of outermost closed isobar\n       </b>\n       (\n       <b>\n        ROCI\n       </b>\n       ) is one of the quantities used to determine the size of a\n       <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone" title="Tropical cyclone">\n        tropical cyclone\n       </a>\n       . It is determined by measuring the radii from the center of the storm to its outermost closed\n       <a href="/wiki/Contour_line#Barometric_pressure" title="Contour line">\n        isobar\n       </a>\n       in four quadrants, which is then averaged to come up with a\n       <a href="/wiki/Scalar_(physics)" title="Scalar (physics)">\n        scalar\n       </a>\n       value. It generally delimits the outermost extent of a tropical cyclone\'s wind circulation.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">\n        <a href="#cite_note-2">\n         [2]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       Use of this measure has objectively determined that tropical cyclones in the northwest Pacific Ocean are the largest on earth on average, with\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/North_Atlantic_hurricane" title="North Atlantic hurricane">\n        North Atlantic tropical cyclones\n       </a>\n       roughly half their size.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Merrill_3-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-Merrill-3">\n         [3]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       <a href="/wiki/Active_database" title="Active database">\n        Active databases\n       </a>\n       of ROCI are maintained by the\n       <a href="/wiki/National_Hurricane_Center" title="National Hurricane Center">\n        National Hurricane Center\n       </a>\n       for systems tracked in the eastern north Pacific and north Atlantic basins.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n      </p>\n      <div class="toc" id="toc">\n       <div class="toctitle">\n </div>\n       </div>\n      </div>\n      <p>\n       Active databases of ROCI are maintained by the\n       <a href="/wiki/National_Hurricane_Center" title="National Hurricane Center">\n        National Hurricane Center\n       </a>\n       for systems tracked in the eastern north Pacific and north Atlantic basins, within a database known as the Extended Best Track Database. The values are determined in real-time every six hours. The eastern north Pacific database runs from 2001 to present, while the north Atlantic database runs from 1988 to present.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">\n        <a href="#cite_note-4">\n         [4]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       Other than these official databases, a global once-daily dataset was compiled for a 1984 research paper, which covered global tropical cyclones between 1957 and 1977.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Merrill_3-1">\n        <a href="#cite_note-Merrill-3">\n         [3]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       Previously, a database was created to determine ROCI values for the western north Pacific Ocean in 1972, using data from 1945 to 1968.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">\n        <a href="#cite_note-5">\n         [5]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       Another database with additional ROCI information is currently being modified at the\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydrometeorological_Prediction_Center" title="Hydrometeorological Prediction Center">\n        Hydrometeorological Prediction Center\n       </a>\n       for use in matching ongoing tropical cyclones to past systems for the purposes of finding rainfall analogs to an ongoing event,\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">\n        <a href="#cite_note-6">\n         [6]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       which has fairly continuous data running back to 1959 for the north\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atlantic_ocean" title="Atlantic ocean">\n        Atlantic ocean\n       </a>\n       .\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CLIQR_7-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-CLIQR-7">\n         [7]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <h2>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Variation">\n        Variation\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Radius_of_outermost_closed_isobar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Variation">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h2>\n      <p>\n       Tropical cyclones tend to be smaller during the mid-summer, and largest in October in the\n       <a href="/wiki/Northern_Hemisphere" title="Northern Hemisphere">\n        Northern Hemisphere\n       </a>\n       . As tropical cyclones initially develop, the size of their ROCI initially contracts.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Brooks_8-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-Brooks-8">\n         [8]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       Once tropical cyclones reach maturity, their isobaric patterns increase in size. During the mature stage, the broadening pressure pattern leads to some reduction in their\n       <a href="/wiki/Maximum_sustained_wind" title="Maximum sustained wind">\n        maximum sustained wind\n       </a>\n       , but the extent of their tropical storm and hurricane-force winds is the most extensive within the storm\'s life cycle.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">\n        <a href="#cite_note-9">\n         [9]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       An increase in size is also noted as a tropical cyclone gains latitude.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Brooks_8-1">\n        <a href="#cite_note-Brooks-8">\n         [8]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       As tropical cyclones weaken, their ROCI values diminish. In general, the size of a tropical cyclone shows little relation to its intensity. Use of this measure has objectively determined that tropical cyclones in the northwest Pacific Ocean are the largest on earth on average, with\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/North_Atlantic_hurricane" title="North Atlantic hurricane">\n        North Atlantic tropical cyclones\n       </a>\n       roughly half their size.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Merrill_3-2">\n        <a href="#cite_note-Merrill-3">\n         [3]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <h2>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">\n        See also\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Radius_of_outermost_closed_isobar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: See also">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h2>\n      <ul>\n       <li>\n        <a href="/wiki/Radius_of_maximum_wind" title="Radius of maximum wind">\n         Radius of maximum wind\n        </a>\n       </li>\n      </ul>\n      <h2>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="References">\n        References\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Radius_of_outermost_closed_isobar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: References">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h2>\n      <div class="reflist columns references-column-width" style="-moz-column-width: 30em;
