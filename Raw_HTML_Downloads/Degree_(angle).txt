The use of a calendar with 360 days may be related to the use of\n       <a href="/wiki/Sexagesimal" title="Sexagesimal">\n        sexagesimal\n       </a>\n       numbers.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       Another theory is that the Babylonians subdivided the circle using the angle of an\n       <a href="/wiki/Equilateral_triangle" title="Equilateral triangle">\n        equilateral triangle\n       </a>\n       as the basic unit and further subdivided the latter into 60 parts following their\n       <a href="/wiki/Sexagesimal" title="Sexagesimal">\n        sexagesimal\n       </a>\n       numeric system.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">\n        <a href="#cite_note-6">\n         [6]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">\n        <a href="#cite_note-7">\n         [7]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       The\n       <a href="/wiki/History_of_trigonometry" title="History of trigonometry">\n        earliest trigonometry\n       </a>\n       , used by the\n       <a href="/wiki/Babylonian_astronomy" title="Babylonian astronomy">\n        Babylonian astronomers\n       </a>\n       and their\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Greek_astronomy" title="Greek astronomy">\n        Greek\n       </a>\n       successors, was based on\n       <a href="/wiki/Chord_(geometry)" title="Chord (geometry)">\n        chords\n       </a>\n       of a circle. A chord of length equal to the radius made a natural base quantity. One sixtieth of this, using their standard\n       <a href="/wiki/Sexagesimal" title="Sexagesimal">\n        sexagesimal\n       </a>\n       divisions, was a degree.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       <a  </a>\n       seem to have been among the first\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Greek_astronomy" title="Greek astronomy">\n        Greek scientists\n       </a>\n       to exploit <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">\n        <a href="#cite_note-9">\n         [9]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       <a href="/wiki/Timocharis" title="Timocharis">\n        Timocharis\n       </a>\n       , Aristarchus,\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aristillus" title="Aristillus">\n        Aristillus\n       </a>\n       ,\n       <a href="/wiki/Archimedes" </a>\n       </sup>\n       <a href="/wiki/Eratosthenes" title="Eratosthenes">\n        Eratosthenes\n       </a>\n       used a simpler\n       <a href="/wiki/Sexagesimal" title="Sexagesimal">\n        sexagesimal\n       </a>\n       system dividing a circle into 60 parts.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       The division of the circle into 360 parts also occurred in ancient\n       <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">\n        India\n       </a>\n       , as evidenced in the\n       <a href="/wiki/Rigveda" title="Rigveda">\n        Rigveda\n       </a>\n       :\n       <sup number 360 may have been that it is\n       <a href="/wiki/Highly_composite_number" title="Highly composite number">\n        readily divisible\n       </a>\n       : 360 has 24\n       <a href="/wiki/Divisor" title="Divisor">\n        divisors\n       </a>\n       ,\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">\n        <a href="#cite_note-12">\n         [note 1]\n        </a>\n href="//oeis.org/A072938" title="oeis:A072938">\n         A072938\n        </a>\n       </span>\n       in the\n       <a href="/wiki/On-Line_Encyclopedia_of_Integer_Sequences" title="On-Line </sup>\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">\n        <a href="#cite_note-14">\n         [13]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       Furthermore, it is divisible by every number from 1 to 10 except 7.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">\n        <a href="#cite_note-15">\n         [note 2]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       This property has many useful applications, such as dividing the world into 24\n       <a href="/wiki/Time_zone" title="Time zone">\n        time zones\n       </a>\n       , each of which is nominally 15\xc2\xb0 of\n       <a href="/wiki/Longitude" According to that theory, the number is approximately 365 because of the apparent movement of the sun against the celestial sphere and that it was rounded to 360 for some of the mathematical reasons cited above.\n      </p>\n      <h2>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Subdivisions">\n        Subdivisions\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Degree_(angle)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Subdivisions">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h2>\n      <p>\n       For many practical purposes, a degree is a small enough angle that whole degrees provide sufficient precision. When this is not the case, as in\n       <a href="/wiki/Astronomy" title="Astronomy">\n        astronomy\n       </a>\n       or for\n       <a href="/wiki/Geographic_coordinate_system" title="Geographic coordinate system">\n        geographic coordinates\n       </a>\n       (\n       <a href="/wiki/Latitude" title="Latitude">\n        latitude\n       </a>\n       and\n       <a href="/wiki/Longitude" title="Longitude">\n        longitude\n       </a>\n       ), degree measurements may be written using\n       <a href="/wiki/Decimal_degrees" title="Decimal degrees">\n        decimal degrees\n       </a>\n       , with the degree symbol behind the decimals; for example, 40.1875\xc2\xb0.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       Alternatively, the traditional\n       <a href="/wiki/Sexagesimal" title="Sexagesimal">\n        sexagesimal\n       </a>\n       <a href="/wiki/Units_of_measurement" title="Units of measurement">\n        unit\n       </a>\n       subdivisions can be used. One degree is divided into 60\n       <i>\n        minutes (of arc)\n       </i>\n       , and one minute into 60\n       <i>\n        seconds (of arc)\n       </i>\n       . Use of degrees-minutes-seconds is also called DMS notation. These subdivisions, also called the\n       <i>\n        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arcminute" title="Arcminute">\n         arcminute\n        </a>\n       </i>\n       and\n       <i>\n        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arcsecond" title="Arcsecond">\n         arcsecond\n        </a>\n       </i>\n       , are respectively represented by a single and double\n       <a href="/wiki/Prime_(symbol)" title="Prime (symbol)">\n        prime\n       </a>\n       . For example,\n       <span class="nowrap">\n        40.1875\xc2\xb0 = 40\xc2\xb0 11\xe2\x80\xb2 15\xe2\x80\xb3\n       </span>\n       , or, using\n       <a href="/wiki/Quotation_mark" title="Quotation mark">\n        quotation mark\n       </a>\n       characters,\n       <span class="nowrap">\n        40\xc2\xb0 11\' 15"\n       </span>\n       . Additional precision can be provided using decimals for the arcseconds component.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       The older system of\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Third_(angle)" title="Third (angle)">\n        thirds\n       </a>\n       ,\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fourth_(angle)" title="Fourth (angle)">\n        fourths\n       </a>\n       , etc., which continues the sexagesimal unit subdivision, was used by\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Al-Kashi" title="Al-Kashi">\n        al-Kashi\n       </a>\n       and other ancient astronomers, but is rarely used today. These subdivisions were denoted\n       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">\n        [\n        <i>\n         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">\n          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2008)">\n           citation needed\n          </span>\n         </a>\n        </i>\n        ]\n       </sup>\n       by writing the\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Roman_numeral" title="Roman numeral">\n        Roman numeral\n       </a>\n       for the number of sixtieths in superscript: 1\n       <sup>\n        I\n       </sup>\n       for a "\n       <a href="/wiki/Prime_(symbol)" title="Prime (symbol)">\n        prime\n       </a>\n       " (minute of arc), 1\n       <sup>\n        II\n       </sup>\n       for a\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Double_prime" title="Double prime">\n        second\n       </a>\n       , 1\n       <sup>\n        III\n       </sup>\n       for a\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Triple_prime" title="Triple prime">\n        third\n       </a>\n       , 1\n       <sup>\n        IV\n       </sup>\n       for a\n       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Quarter_prime&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Quarter prime (page does not exist)">\n        fourth\n       </a>\n       , etc. Hence the modern symbols for the minute and second of arc, and the word "second" also refer to this system.\n       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">\n        [\n        <i>\n         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">\n          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. These considerations outweigh the convenient divisibility of the number 360. One complete\n       <a href="/wiki/Turn_(geometry)" title="Turn (geometry)">\n        turn\n       </a>\n       (360\xc2\xb0) is equal to 2\n       <i>\n        <a href="/wiki/Pi" title="Pi">\n         <span class="texhtml">\n          \xcf\x80\n         </span>\n        </a>\n       </i>\n       radians, so 180\xc2\xb0 is equal to\n       <span class="texhtml">\n        \xcf\x80\n       </span>\n       radians, or equivalently, the degree is a\n       <a href="/wiki/Mathematical_constant" title="Mathematical constant">\n        mathematical constant\n       </a>\n       : 1\xc2\xb0 =\n       <span class="nowrap">\n       </span>\n       <span class="frac nowrap">\n        <sup>\n         <span class="texhtml">\n          \xcf\x80\n         </span>\n        </sup>\n        \xe2\x81\x84\n        <sub>\n         180\n        </sub>\n       </span>\n       .\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       The\n       <a href="/wiki/Turn_(geometry)" title="Turn (geometry)">\n        turn\n       </a>\n       (or revolution, full circle, full rotation, cycle) is used in technology and science. One turn is equal to 360\xc2\xb0.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       With the invention of the\n       <a href="/wiki/Metric_system" title="Metric system">\n        metric system\n       </a>\n       , based on powers of ten, there was an attempt to replace degrees by decimal "degrees"\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">\n        <a href="#cite_note-16">\n         [note 3]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       called\n       <i>\n        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Grad_(angle)" title="Grad (angle)">\n         grad\n        </a>\n       </i>\n       or\n       <i>\n        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gon_(angle)" title="Gon (angle)">\n         gon\n        </a>\n       </i>\n       , where the number in a right angle is equal to 100\xc2\xa0gon with 400\xc2\xa0gon in a full circle (1\xc2\xb0 =\n       <span class="nowrap">\n       </span>\n       <span class="frac nowrap">\n        <sup>\n         10\n        </sup>\n        \xe2\x81\x84\n        <sub>\n         9\n        </sub>\n       </span>\n       gon). Although that idea was abandoned by Napoleon, grades continued to be used in several fields and many\n       <a href="/wiki/Scientific_calculator" title="Scientific calculator">\n        scientific calculators\n       </a>\n       support them. Decigrades (\n       <span class="nowrap">\n       </span>\n       <span class="frac nowrap">\n        <sup>\n         1\n        </sup>\n        \xe2\x81\x84\n        <sub>\n         4,000\n        </sub>\n       </span>\n       ) were used with French artillery sights in World War I.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       An\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Angular_mil" title="Angular mil">\n        angular mil\n       </a>\n       , which is most used in military applications, has at least three specific variants, ranging from\n       <span class="nowrap">\n       </span>\n       <span class="frac nowrap">\n        <sup>\n         1\n        </sup>\n        \xe2\x81\x84\n        <sub>\n         6,400\n        </sub>\n       </span>\n       to\n       <span class="nowrap">\n       </span>\n       <span class="frac nowrap">\n        <sup>\n         1\n        </sup>\n        \xe2\x81\x84\n        <sub>\n         6,000\n        </sub>\n       </span>\n       . It is approximately equal to one milliradian (\n       <abbr title="circa">\n        c.\n       </abbr>\n       <span class="nowrap">\n       </span>\n       <span class="frac nowrap">\n        <sup>\n         1\n        </sup>\n        \xe2\x81\x84\n        <sub>\n         6,283\n        </sub>\n       </span>\n       ). A mil measuring\n       <span class="nowrap">\n       </span>\n       <span class="frac nowrap">\n        <sup>\n         1\n        </sup>\n        \xe2\x81\x84\n        <sub>\n         6,000\n        </sub>\n       </span>\n       of a revolution originated in the imperial Russian army, where an equilateral chord was divided into tenths to give a circle of 600 units.
