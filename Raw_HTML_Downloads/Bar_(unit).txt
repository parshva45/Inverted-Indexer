\xce\xb2\xce\xac\xcf\x81\xce\xbf\xcf\x82\n       </span>\n       (\n       <span lang="el-Latn" xml:lang="el-Latn">\n        <i>\n         baros\n        </i>\n       </span>\n       ), meaning\n       <a href="/wiki/Weight" title="Weight">\n        weight\n       </a>\n       . The unit\'s official symbol is\n       <b>\n        bar\n       </b>\n       ; the earlier symbol\n       <b>\n        b\n       </b>\n       is now deprecated and conflicts with the use of\n       <b>\n        b\n       </b>\n       denoting the unit\n       <a href="/wiki/Barn_(unit)" title="Barn (unit)">\n        barn\n       </a>\n       , but it is still encountered, especially as\n       <b>\n        mb\n       </b>\n       (rather than the proper\n       <b>\n        mbar\n       </b>\n       ) to denote the millibar. Between 1793 and 1795, the word bar was used for a unit of weight in an early version of the metric system.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">\n        <a href="#cite_note-6">\n         [6]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <h2>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Usage">\n        Usage\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Bar_(unit)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Usage">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h2>\n      <p>\n       <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_pressure" title="Atmospheric pressure">\n        Atmospheric air pressure\n       </a>\n       is often given in millibars, where\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Standard_atmospheric_pressure" title="Standard atmospheric pressure">\n        standard atmospheric pressure\n       </a>\n       at\n       <a href="/wiki/Sea_level" title="Sea level">\n        sea level\n       </a>\n       is defined as 1013.25 mbar, 101.325\n       <a href="/wiki/Pascal_(unit)" title="Pascal (unit)">\n        kPa\n       </a>\n       , 1.01325\xc2\xa0bar, which is about 14.7\n       <a href="/wiki/Pounds_per_square_inch" title="Pounds per square inch">\n        pounds per square inch\n       </a>\n       Despite the millibar not being an\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SI" title="SI">\n        SI\n       </a>\n       unit, meteorologists and weather reporters worldwide have long measured air pressure in millibars as the values are convenient. After the advent of SI units, some meteorologists began using\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hectopascals" title="Hectopascals">\n        hectopascals\n       </a>\n       (symbol hPa) which are numerically equivalent to millibars; for the same reason, the hectopascal is now the standard unit used to express barometric pressures in aviation in most countries. For example, the weather office of\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Environment_Canada" title="Environment Canada">\n        Environment Canada\n       </a>\n       uses kilopascals and hectopascals on their weather maps.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">\n        <a href="#cite_note-7">\n         [7]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">\n        <a href="#cite_note-8">\n         [8]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       In contrast, Americans are familiar with the use of the millibar in US reports of\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hurricane" title="Hurricane">\n        hurricanes\n       </a>\n       and other cyclonic storms.\n       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">\n        [\n        <i>\n         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">\n          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2012)">\n           citation needed\n          </span>\n         </a>\n        </i>\n        ]\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       In fresh water, there is an approximate numerical equivalence between the change in pressure in decibars and the change in depth from the water surface in\n       <a href="/wiki/Metre" title="Metre">\n        metres\n       </a>\n       . Specifically, an increase of 1 decibar occurs for every 1.019716\xc2\xa0m increase in depth. In sea water with respect to the gravity variation, the latitude and the geopotential anomaly the pressure can be converted into meters depth according to an empirical formula (UNESCO Tech. Paper 44, p.\xc2\xa025).\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">\n        <a href="#cite_note-9">\n         [9]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       As a result, decibars are commonly used in\n       <a href="/wiki/Oceanography" title="Oceanography">\n        oceanography\n       </a>\n       .\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       Many engineers worldwide use the bar as a unit of pressure because, in much of their work, using pascals would involve using very large numbers.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       Engineers that specialize in technical safety for offshore petrochemical facilities would be expected to exclusively refer to explosion loads in units of bar or bars. A bar is a convenient unit of measure for pressures generated by low frequency vapor cloud explosions that are commonly considered as part of accidental loading risk studies.\n      </p>\n      <div     <p>\n       In the automotive field,\n       <a href="/wiki/Turbocharger" title="Turbocharger">\n        turbocharger\n       </a>\n       boost is often described in bars outside the USA.\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tire_pressure" title="Tire pressure">\n        Tire pressure\n       </a>\n       is often specified in bar.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       <a href="/wiki/Unicode" title="Unicode">\n        Unicode\n       </a>\n       has characters for "mb" (\xe3\x8f\x94, U+33D4) and "bar" (\xe3\x8d\xb4, U+3374), but they exist only for compatibility with legacy Asian encodings and are not intended to be used in new documents.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       The kilobar, equivalent to 100\xc2\xa0MPa, is commonly used in geological systems, particularly in experimental\n       <a href="/wiki/Petrology" title="Petrology">\n        petrology\n       </a>\n       .\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       "Bar(a)" and "bara" are sometimes used to indicate\n       <a href="/wiki/Pressure_measurement#Absolute.2C_gauge_and_differential" title="Pressure measurement">\n        absolute pressures\n       </a>\n       and "bar(g)" and "barg" for\n       <a href="/wiki/Pressure_measurement#Absolute.2C_gauge_and_differential" title="Pressure measurement">\n        gauge pressures\n       </a>\n       . This usage is deprecated and fuller descriptions such as "gauge pressure of 2 bar" or "2 bar gauge" are recommended.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BSI_2-3">\n        <a
