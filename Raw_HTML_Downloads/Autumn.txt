fall\n       </b>\n       in\n       <a href="/wiki/American_English" title="American English">\n        American\n       </a>\n       and\n       <a href="/wiki/Canadian_English" title="Canadian English">\n        Canadian\n       </a>\n       English,\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">\n        <a href="#cite_note-1">\n         [1]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       is one of the four\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Temperate" title="Temperate">\n        temperate\n       </a>\n       <a href="/wiki/Season" title="Season">\n        seasons\n       </a>\n       . Autumn marks the transition from\n       <a href="/wiki/Summer" title="Summer">\n        summer\n       </a>\n       to\n       <a href="/wiki/Winter" title="Winter">\n        winter\n       </a>\n       , in September (\n       <a href="/wiki/Northern_Hemisphere" title="Northern Hemisphere">\n        Northern Hemisphere\n       </a>\n       ) or March (\n       <a href="/wiki/Southern_Hemisphere" title="Southern Hemisphere">\n        Southern Hemisphere\n       </a>\n       ), when the duration of\n       <a href="/wiki/Daylight" title="Daylight">\n        daylight\n       </a>\n       becomes noticeably shorter and the temperature cools down considerably. One of its main features is the\n       <a href="/wiki/Leaf#Seasonal_leaf_loss" title="Leaf">\n        shedding of leaves\n       </a>\n       from\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Deciduous_trees" title="Deciduous trees">\n        deciduous trees\n       </a>\n       .\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       Some cultures regard the autumnal\n       <a href="/wiki/Equinox" title="Equinox">\n        equinox\n       </a>\n       as "mid-autumn", while others with a longer\n       <a href="/wiki/Seasonal_lag" title="Seasonal lag">\n        temperature lag\n       </a>\n       treat it as the start of autumn.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">\n        <a href="#cite_note-2">\n         [2]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       Meteorologists (and most of the\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Temperate_countries" title="Temperate countries">\n        temperate countries\n       </a>\n       in the southern hemisphere)\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">\n        <a href="#cite_note-3">\n         [3]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       use a definition based on months, with autumn being September, October and November in the northern hemisphere,\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">\n        <a href="#cite_note-4">\n         [4]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       and March, April and May in the southern hemisphere.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       In\n       <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">\n        North America\n       </a>\n       , autumn is usually considered to start with the\n       <a href="/wiki/September_equinox" title="September equinox">\n        September equinox\n       </a>\n       (21 to 24 September)\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">\n        <a href="#cite_note-5">\n         [5]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       and end with the\n       <a href="/wiki/Winter_solstice" title="Winter solstice">\n        winter solstice\n       </a>\n       (21 or 22 December).\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">\n        <a href="#cite_note-6">\n         [6]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       Popular culture in North America associates\n       <a href="/wiki/Labor_Day" title="Labor Day">\n        Labor Day\n       </a>\n       , the first Monday in September, as the end of summer and the start of autumn; certain summer traditions, such as wearing white, are discouraged after that date.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">\n        <a href="#cite_note-7">\n         [7]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       As daytime and nighttime temperatures decrease, trees shed their leaves.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">\n        <a href="#cite_note-8">\n         [8]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       In traditional East Asian\n       <a href="/wiki/Solar_term" title="Solar term">\n        solar term\n       </a>\n       , autumn starts on or around 8 August and ends on or about 7 November. In\n       <a href="/wiki/Ireland" title="Ireland">\n        Ireland\n       </a>\n       , the autumn months according to the national meteorological service,\n       <a href="/wiki/Met_%C3%89ireann" title="Met \xc3\x89ireann">\n        Met \xc3\x89ireann\n       </a>\n       , are September, October and November.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-met.ie_9-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-met.ie-9">\n         [9]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       However, according to the\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Irish_Calendar" title="Irish Calendar">\n        Irish Calendar\n       </a>\n       , which is based on ancient\n       <a href="/wiki/Gaelic_Ireland" title="Gaelic Ireland">\n        Gaelic\n       </a>\n       traditions, autumn lasts throughout the months of August, September and October, or possibly a few days later, depending on tradition\n       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">\n        [\n        <i>\n         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">\n          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2017)">\n           citation needed\n          </span>\n         </a>\n        </i>\n        ]\n       </sup>\n       . In\n       <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">\n        Australia\n       </a>\n       and\n       <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand" title="New Zealand">\n        New Zealand\n       </a>\n       , autumn officially begins on 1 March and ends on 31 May.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">\n        <a href="#cite_note-10">\n         [10]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n Leaves of a\n         <a href="/wiki/Maple" title="Maple">\n          maple\n         </a>\n         tree in autumn\n        </div>\n       </div>\n      </div>\n      <p>\n       The word\n       <i>\n        autumn\n       </i>\n       comes from the ancient\n       <a href="/wiki/Etruscan_language" title="Etruscan language">\n        Etruscan\n       </a>\n       root\n       <i>\n        autu-\n       </i>\n       and has within it connotations of the passing of the year.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">\n        <a href="#cite_note-11">\n         [11]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       It was borrowed by the neighbouring\n       <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Rome" title="Ancient Rome">\n        Romans\n       </a>\n       , and became the\n       <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">\n        Latin\n       </a>\n       word\n       <i>\n        <span lang="la" xml:lang="la">\n         autumnus\n        </span>\n       </i>\n       .\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">\n        <a href="#cite_note-12">\n         [12]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       After the Roman era, the word continued to be used as the\n       <a href="/wiki/Old_French" title="Old French">\n        Old French\n       </a>\n       word\n       <i>\n        <span lang="fro" xml:lang="fro">\n         autompne\n        </span>\n       </i>\n       (\n       <i>\n        <span lang="fr" xml:lang="fr">\n         automne\n        </span>\n       </i>\n       in\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Modern_French" title="Modern French">\n        modern French\n       </a>\n       ) or\n       <i>\n        <span lang="enm" xml:lang="enm">\n         autumpne\n        </span>\n       </i>\n       in Middle English,\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">\n        <a href="#cite_note-13">\n         [13]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       and was later normalised to the original Latin. In the\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Medieval" title="Medieval">\n        Medieval\n       </a>\n       period, there are rare examples of its use as early as the 12th century, but by the 16th century, it was in common use.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       Before the 16th century,\n       <i>\n        <a href="/wiki/Harvest" title="Harvest">\n         harvest\n        </a>\n       </i>\n       was the term usually used to refer to the season, as it is common in other\n       <a href="/wiki/West_Germanic_languages" title="West Germanic languages">\n        West Germanic languages\n       </a>\n       to this day (\n       <a href="/wiki/Cf." title="Cf.">\n        cf.\n       </a>\n       Dutch\n       <i>\n        <span lang="nl" xml:lang="nl">\n         herfst\n        </span>\n       </i>\n       , German\n       <i>\n        <span lang="de" xml:lang="de">\n         Herbst\n        </span>\n       </i>\n       and Scots\n       <i>\n        <span lang="sco" xml:lang="sco">\n         hairst\n        </span>\n       </i>\n       ). However, as more people gradually moved from working the land to living in towns, the word\n       <i>\n        harvest\n       </i>\n       lost its reference to the time of year and came to refer only to the actual activity of reaping, and\n       <i>\n        autumn\n       </i>\n       , as well as\n       <i>\n        fall\n       </i>\n       , began to replace it as a reference to the season.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">\n        <a href="#cite_note-14">\n         [14]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">\n        <a href="#cite_note-15">\n         [15]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       The alternative word\n       <i>\n        fall\n       </i>\n       for the season traces its origins to old\n       <a href="/wiki/Germanic_languages" title="Germanic languages">\n        Germanic languages\n       </a>\n       . The exact derivation is unclear, with the\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Old_English_language" title="Old English language">\n        Old English\n       </a>\n       <i>\n        <span lang="ang" xml:lang="ang">\n         fi\xc3\xa6ll\n        </span>\n       </i>\n       or\n       <i>\n        <span lang="ang" xml:lang="ang">\n         feallan\n        </span>\n       </i>\n       and the\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Old_Norse_language" title="Old Norse language">\n        Old Norse\n       </a>\n       <i>\n        <span lang="non" xml:lang="non">\n         fall\n        </span>\n       </i>\n       all being possible candidates. However, these words all have the meaning "to fall from a height" and are clearly derived either from a common root or from each other. The term came to denote the season in\n       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=16th-century_England&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="16th-century England (page does not exist)">\n        16th-century England\n       </a>\n       , a contraction of\n       <a href="/wiki/Middle_English" title="Middle English">\n        Middle English\n       </a>\n       expressions like "\n       <a href="/wiki/Abscission" title="Abscission">\n        fall of the leaf\n       </a>\n       " and "fall of the year".\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">\n        <a href="#cite_note-16">\n         [16]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       During the 17th century,\n       <a href="/wiki/British_colonization_of_the_Americas" title="British colonization of the Americas">\n        English emigration to the British colonies\n       </a>\n       in North America was at its peak, and the new settlers took the English language with them. While the term\n       <i>\n        fall\n       </i>\n       gradually became obsolete in Britain, it became the more common term in North America.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       The name\n       <i>\n        backend\n       </i>\n       , a once common name for the season in\n       <a href="/wiki/Northern_England" title="Northern England">\n        Northern England\n       </a>\n       , has today been largely replaced by the name autumn.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17">\n        <a href="#cite_note-17">\n         [17]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <h2>\n Association with the transition from warm to cold weather, and its related status as the season of the primary\n       <a href="/wiki/Harvest" title="Harvest">\n        harvest\n       </a>\n       , has dominated its themes and popular images. In Western cultures, personifications of autumn are usually pretty, well-fed females adorned with fruits, vegetables and grains that ripen at this time. Many cultures feature autumnal\n       <a href="/wiki/Harvest_festival" title="Harvest festival">\n        harvest festivals\n       </a>\n       , often the most important on their calendars. Still extant echoes of these celebrations are found in the autumn\n       <a href="/wiki/Thanksgiving" title="Thanksgiving">\n        Thanksgiving\n       </a>\n       holiday of the United States and Canada, and the Jewish\n       <a href="/wiki/Sukkot" title="Sukkot">\n        Sukkot\n       </a>\n       holiday with its roots as a full-moon harvest festival of "tabernacles" (living in outdoor huts around the time of harvest).\n       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">\n        [\n        <i>\n         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">\n          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2012)">\n           citation needed\n          </span>\n         </a>\n        </i>\n        ]\n       </sup>\n       There are also the many North American Indian festivals tied to harvest of ripe foods gathered in the wild, the Chinese Mid-Autumn or\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Moon_festival" title="Moon festival">\n        Moon festival\n       </a>\n       , and many others. The predominant mood of these autumnal celebrations is a gladness for the fruits of the earth mixed with a certain melancholy linked to the imminent arrival of harsh weather.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       This view is presented in English poet\n       <a href="/wiki/John_Keats" title="John Keats">\n        John Keats\n       </a>\n       \' poem\n       <i>\n        <a href="/wiki/To_Autumn" title="To Autumn">\n         To Autumn\n        </a>\n       </i>\n       , where he describes the season as a time of bounteous fecundity, a time of \'mellow fruitfulness\'.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       While most foods are harvested during the autumn, foods particularly associated with the season include\n       <a href="/wiki/Pumpkin" title="Pumpkin">\n        pumpkins\n       </a>\n       (which are integral parts of both Thanksgiving and\n       <a href="/wiki/Halloween" title="Halloween">\n        Halloween\n       </a>\n       ) and\n       <a href="/wiki/Apple" title="Apple">\n        apples\n       </a>\n       , which are used to make the seasonal beverage\n       <a href="/wiki/Apple_cider" title="Apple cider">\n        apple cider\n       </a>\n       .\n      </p>\n Western\n         <a href="/wiki/Estonia" title="Estonia">\n          Estonia\n         </a>\n        </div>\n       </div>\n      </div>\n      <p>\n       Autumn, especially in poetry, has often been associated with\n       <a href="/wiki/Melancholia" title="Melancholia">\n        melancholia\n       </a>\n       . The possibilities of summer are gone, and the chill of winter is on the horizon. Skies turn grey, the amount of usable daylight drops rapidly, and many people turn inward, both physically and mentally.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18">\n        <a href="#cite_note-18">\n         [18]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       It has been referred to as an unhealthy season.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19">\n        <a href="#cite_note-19">\n         [19]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       Similar examples may be found in Irish poet\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/William_Butler_Yeats" title="William Butler Yeats">\n        William Butler Yeats\n       </a>\n       \' poem\n       <i>\n        <a href="/wiki/The_Wild_Swans_at_Coole" title="The Wild Swans at Coole">\n         The Wild Swans at Coole\n        </a>\n       </i>\n       where the maturing season that the poet observes symbolically represents his own ageing self. Like the natural world that he observes, he too has reached his prime and now must look forward to the inevitability of old age and death. French poet\n       <a href="/wiki/Paul_Verlaine" title="Paul Verlaine">\n        Paul Verlaine\n       </a>\n       \'s "\n       <i>\n        <a href="/wiki/Chanson_d%27automne" title="Chanson d\'automne">\n         Chanson d\'automne\n        </a>\n       </i>\n       " ("Autumn Song") is likewise characterised by strong, painful feelings of sorrow.\n       <a href="/wiki/John_Keats" title="John Keats">\n        Keats\n       </a>\n       \'\n       <i>\n        <a href="/wiki/To_Autumn" title="To Autumn">\n         To Autumn\n        </a>\n       </i>\n       , written in  <a href="/wiki/White" title="White">\n        white\n       </a>\n       , the\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bai_Hu" title="Bai Hu">\n        White Tiger of the West\n       </a>\n       , and death and mourning.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       In the United States,\n       <a href="/wiki/Labor_Day" title="Labor Day">\n        Labor Day\n       </a>\n       is a\n       <a
