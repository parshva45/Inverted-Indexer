id="contentSub">\n    </div>\n    <div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">\n     Jump to:\n     <a href="#mw-head">\n      navigation\n     </a>\n     ,\n     <a href="#p-search">\n      search\n     </a>\n    </div>\n    <div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en">\n     <div class="mw-parser-output">\n      <p>\n       In\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Astrodynamics" title="Astrodynamics">\n        astrodynamics\n       </a>\n       , the\n       <a href="/wiki/Orbital_maneuver" title="Orbital maneuver">\n        orbital maneuvers\n       </a>\n       made by\n       <a href="/wiki/Spacecraft_propulsion" title="Spacecraft propulsion">\n        thruster burns\n       </a>\n       that are needed to keep a spacecraft in a particular assigned\n       <a href="/wiki/Orbit" title="Orbit">\n        orbit\n       </a>\n       are called\n       <b>\n        orbital station-keeping\n       </b>\n       .\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       For many Earth satellites the effects of the\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Orbital_perturbation_analysis_(spacecraft)" title="Orbital perturbation analysis (spacecraft)">\n        non-Keplerian\n       </a>\n       forces, i.e. the deviations of the gravitational force of the Earth from that of a\n       <a href="/wiki/Shell_theorem" title="Shell theorem">\n        homogeneous sphere\n       </a>\n       , gravitational forces from Sun/Moon,\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solar_radiation_pressure" title="Solar radiation pressure">\n        solar radiation pressure\n       </a>\n       and\n       <a href="/wiki/Drag_(physics)" title="Drag (physics)">\n        air-drag\n       </a>\n       , must be counteracted.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       The deviation of Earth\'s gravity field from that of a\n       <a href="/wiki/Shell_theorem" title="Shell theorem">\n        homogeneous sphere\n       </a>\n       and gravitational forces from Sun/Moon will in general perturb the orbital plane. For a\n       <a href="/wiki/Sun-synchronous_orbit" title="Sun-synchronous orbit">\n        sun-synchronous orbit\n       </a>\n       the precession of the orbital plane caused by the oblateness of the Earth is a desirable feature that is part of the mission design but the inclination change caused by the gravitational forces of Sun/Moon is undesirable. For\n       <a href="/wiki/Geostationary_orbit" title="Geostationary orbit">\n        geostationary spacecraft\n       </a>\n       the inclination change caused by the gravitational forces of Sun/Moon must be counteracted by a rather large expense of fuel, as the inclination should be kept sufficiently small for the spacecraft to be tracked by a non-steerable antenna.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       For spacecraft in low orbits the effects of\n       <a href="/wiki/Drag_(physics)" title="Drag (physics)">\n        atmospheric drag\n       </a>\n       must often be compensated for. For some missions this is needed simply to avoid re-entry; for other missions, typically missions for which the orbit should be accurately synchronized with Earth rotation, this is necessary to avoid the orbital period shortening.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       Solar radiation pressure will in general perturb the eccentricity (i.e. the eccentricity vector), see\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Orbital_perturbation_analysis_(spacecraft)" title="Orbital perturbation analysis (spacecraft)">\n        Orbital perturbation analysis (spacecraft)\n       </a>\n       . For some missions this must be actively counter-acted with manoeuvres. For\n       <a href="/wiki/Geostationary_orbit" title="Geostationary orbit">\n        geostationary spacecraft\n       </a>\n       the eccentricity must be kept sufficiently small for a spacecraft to be tracked with a non-steerable antenna. Also for\n       <a href="/wiki/Earth_observation_satellite" title="Earth observation satellite">\n        Earth observation spacecraft\n       </a>\n       for which a very repetitive orbit with a fixed\n       <a href="/wiki/Ground_track" title="Ground track">\n        ground track\n       </a>\n       is desirable, the eccentricity vector should be kept as fixed as possible. A large part of this compensation can be done by using a\n       <a href="/wiki/Frozen_orbit" title="Frozen orbit">\n        frozen orbit\n       </a>\n       design, but for the fine control manoeuvres with thrusters are needed.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       For spacecraft in a\n       <a href="/wiki/Halo_orbit" title="Halo orbit">\n        halo orbit\n       </a>\n       around a\n       <a href="/wiki/Lagrangian_point" title="Lagrangian point">\n        Lagrangian point\n       </a>\n       stationkeeping is even more fundamental as such an orbit is unstable; without an active control with thruster burns the smallest deviation in position/velocity would result in the spacecraft leaving the orbit completely.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-esa20090614_1-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-esa20090614-1">\n         [1]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n      </p>\n      <div class="toc" id="toc">\n       <div class="toctitle">\n        <h2>\n <p>\n      </p>\n      <h2>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Station-keeping_in_low_Earth_orbit">\n        Station-keeping in low Earth orbit\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Orbital_station-keeping&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Station-keeping in low Earth orbit">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h2>\n      <p>\n       For a spacecraft in a very low orbit the\n       <a href="/wiki/Drag_(physics)" title="Drag (physics)">\n        atmospheric drag\n       </a>\n       is sufficiently strong to cause a re-entry before the intended end of mission if orbit raising manoeuvres are not executed from time to time.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       An example of this is the\n       <a href="/wiki/International_Space_Station" title="International Space Station">\n        International Space Station\n       </a>\n       (ISS), which has an operational altitude above Earth\'s surface of between 330 and 410\xc2\xa0km. Due to\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atmospheric_drag" title="Atmospheric drag">\n        atmospheric drag\n       </a>\n       the space station is constantly losing orbital energy. In order to compensate for this loss, which would eventually lead to a re-entry of the station, it has from time to time been re-boosted to a higher orbit. The chosen orbital altitude is a trade-off between the average thrust needed to counter-act the air drag and the\n       <a href="/wiki/Delta-v" title="Delta-v">\n        delta-v\n       </a>\n       needed to send payloads and people to the station. The upper limitation of orbit altitude is due to the constraints imposed by the\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Soyuz_spacecraft" title="Soyuz spacecraft">\n        Soyuz spacecraft\n       </a>\n       . On 25 April 2008, the\n       <a href="/wiki/Automated_Transfer_Vehicle" title="Automated Transfer Vehicle">\n        Automated Transfer Vehicle\n       </a>\n       "\n       <a href="/wiki/Jules_Verne_ATV" title="Jules Verne ATV">\n        Jules Verne\n       </a>\n       " raised the orbit of the ISS for the first time, thereby proving its ability to replace (and outperform) the Soyuz at this task.\n       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">\n        [\n        <i>\n         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">\n          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2009)">\n           citation needed\n          </span>\n         </a>\n        </i>\n        ]\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/GOCE" title="GOCE">\n        GOCE\n       </a>\n       which orbited at 255\xc2\xa0km (later reduced to 235\xc2\xa0km) used\n       <a href="/wiki/Ion_thruster" title="Ion thruster">\n        ion thrusters\n       </a>\n       to provide up to 20\xc2\xa0mN of thrust to compensate for the drag on its frontal area of about 1 m\n       <sup>\n        2\n       </sup>\n       .\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">\n        <a href="#cite_note-2">\n         [2]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <h2>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Station-keeping_for_Earth_observation_spacecraft">\n        Station-keeping for Earth observation spacecraft\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Orbital_station-keeping&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Station-keeping for Earth observation spacecraft">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h2>\n      <p>\n       For\n       <a href="/wiki/Earth_observation_satellite" title="Earth observation satellite">\n        Earth observation spacecraft\n       </a>\n       typically operated in an altitude above the Earth surface of about 700 \xe2\x80\x93 800\xc2\xa0km the air-drag is very faint and a re-entry due to air-drag is not a concern. But if the orbital period should be synchronous with the Earth\'s rotation to maintain a fixed\n       <a href="/wiki/Ground_track" title="Ground track">\n        ground track\n       </a>\n       , the faint air-drag at this high altitude must also be counter-acted by orbit raising manoeuvres in the form of thruster burns tangential to the orbit. These manoeuvres will be very small, typically in the order of a few mm/s of\n       <a href="/wiki/Delta-v" title="Delta-v">\n        delta-v\n       </a>\n       . If a\n       <a href="/wiki/Frozen_orbit" title="Frozen orbit">\n        frozen orbit\n       </a>\n       design is used these very small orbit raising manoeuvres are sufficient to also control the eccentricity vector.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       To maintain a fixed ground track it is also necessary to make out-of-plane manoeuvres to compensate for the inclination change caused by Sun/Moon gravitation. These are executed as thruster burns orthogonal to the orbital plane. For Sun-synchronous spacecraft having a constant geometry relative to the Sun, the inclination change due to the solar gravitation is particularly large; a delta-v in the order of 1\xe2\x80\x932\xc2\xa0m/s per year can be needed to keep the inclination constant.\n      </p>\n      <h2>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Station-keeping_in_geostationary_orbit">\n        Station-keeping in geostationary orbit\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Orbital_station-keeping&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Station-keeping in geostationary orbit">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h2>\n      <div class="thumb tright">\n       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:352px;">\n        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Orbital_Planes.svg">\n         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="700" data-file-width="1250" height="196" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/23/Orbital_Planes.svg/350px-Orbital_Planes.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/23/Orbital_Planes.svg/525px-Orbital_Planes.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/23/Orbital_Planes.svg/700px-Orbital_Planes.svg.png 2x" width="350"/>\n        </a>\n        <div class="thumbcaption">\n         <div class="magnify">\n          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Orbital_Planes.svg" title="Enlarge">\n          </a>\n         </div>\n         Inclined orbital planes\n        </div>\n       </div>\n      </div>\n      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">\n       For more details on this topic, see\n       <a href="/wiki/Geostationary_orbit#Orbital_stability" title="Geostationary orbit">\n        Geostationary_orbit \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa0Orbital_stability\n       </a>\n       .\n      </div>\n      <p>\n       For geostationary spacecraft, thruster burns orthogonal to the orbital plane must be executed to compensate for the effect of the lunar/solar gravitation that perturbs the orbit pole with typically 0.85 degrees per year.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Anderson2015_3-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-Anderson2015-3">\n         [3]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       The delta-v needed to compensate for this perturbation keeping the inclination to the equatorial plane amounts to in the order 45\xc2\xa0m/s per year. This part of the GEO station-keeping is called North-South control.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">\n        <a href="#cite_note-4">\n         [4]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       The East-West control is the control of the orbital period and the eccentricity vector performed by making thruster burns tangential to the orbit. These burns are then designed to keep the orbital period perfectly synchronous with the Earth rotation and to keep the eccentricity sufficiently small. Perturbation of the orbital period results from the imperfect rotational symmetry of the Earth relative the North/South axis, sometimes called the ellipticity of the Earth equator. The eccentricity (i.e. the eccentricity vector) is perturbed by the\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solar_radiation_pressure" title="Solar radiation pressure">\n        solar radiation pressure\n       </a>\n       . The fuel needed for this East-West control is much less than what is needed for the North-South control, (about 2\xc2\xa0m/s per year, depending on the desired longitude). (See\n       <a href="/wiki/Geostationary_orbit#Orbital_stability" title="Geostationary orbit">\n        Geostationary orbit#Orbital stability\n       </a>\n       )\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       To extend the life-time of ageing geostationary spacecraft with little fuel left one sometimes discontinues the North-South control only continuing with the East-West control. As seen from an observer on the rotating Earth the spacecraft will then move North-South with a period of 24 hours. When this North-South movement gets too large a steerable antenna is needed to track the spacecraft. An example of this\n       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">\n        [\n        <i>\n         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Dates_and_numbers#Chronological_items" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Dates and numbers">\n          <span title="The time period mentioned near this tag is ambiguous. (January 2016)">\n           when?\n          </span>\n         </a>\n        </i>\n        ]\n       </sup>\n       is\n       <a href="/wiki/Artemis_(satellite)" title="Artemis (satellite)">\n        Artemis\n       </a>\n       .\n       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">\n        [\n        <i>\n         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">\n          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2016)">\n           citation needed\n          </span>\n         </a>\n        </i>\n        ]\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       To save weight, it is crucial for GEO satellites to have the most fuel-efficient\n       <a href="/wiki/Spacecraft_propulsion" title="Spacecraft propulsion">\n        propulsion\n       </a>\n       system. Some modern satellites are therefore employing a high\n       <a href="/wiki/Specific_impulse" title="Specific impulse">\n        specific impulse\n       </a>\n       system like\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Plasma_thruster" title="Plasma thruster">\n        plasma\n       </a>\n       or\n       <a href="/wiki/Ion_thruster" title="Ion thruster">\n        ion thrusters\n       </a>\n       .\n      </p>\n      <h2>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Station-keeping_at_libration_points">\n        Station-keeping at libration points\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Orbital_station-keeping&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Station-keeping at libration points">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h2>\n      <p>\n       Orbits of spacecraft are also possible around\n       <a href="/wiki/Lagrangian_point" title="Lagrangian point">\n        Lagrangian points\n       </a>\n       \xe2\x80\x94also referred to as\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Libration_point" title="Libration point">\n        libration points\n       </a>\n       \xe2\x80\x94\n       <a href="/wiki/Gravity_well" title="Gravity well">\n        gravity wells\n       </a>\n       that exist at five points in relation to two larger solar system bodies. For example, there are five of these points in the Sun-Earth system, five in the Earth-Moon system, and so on. Small spacecraft may orbit around these gravity wells with a minimum of propellant required for station-keeping purposes. Two orbits that have been used for such purposes include\n       <a href="/wiki/Halo_orbit" title="Halo orbit">\n        halo\n       </a>\n       and\n       <a href="/wiki/Lissajous_orbit" title="Lissajous orbit">\n        Lissajous\n       </a>\n       orbits.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nasa20110101_5-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-nasa20110101-5">\n         [5]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       Orbits around libration points are dynamically unstable, meaning small departures from equilibrium grow exponentially over time.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-esa20090614_1-1">\n        <a href="#cite_note-esa20090614-1">\n         [1]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       As a result, spacecraft in libration point orbits must use\n       <a href="/wiki/Reaction_control_system" title="Reaction control system">\n        propulsion systems\n       </a>\n       to perform orbital station-keeping.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       One important libration point is Earth-Sun\n       <a href="/wiki/Lagrangian_point#L1" title="Lagrangian point">\n        L\n        <sub>\n         1\n        </sub>\n       </a>\n       , and three\n       <a href="/wiki/Heliophysics" title="Heliophysics">\n        heliophysics\n       </a>\n       missions have been orbiting L1 since approximately 2000. Station-keeping propellant use can be quite low, facilitating missions that can potentially last decades should other spacecraft systems remain operational. The three spacecraft\xe2\x80\x94\n       <a href="/wiki/Advanced_Composition_Explorer" title="Advanced Composition Explorer">\n        Advanced Composition Explorer\n       </a>\n       (ACE),\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solar_Heliospheric_Observatory" title="Solar Heliospheric Observatory">\n        Solar Heliospheric Observatory\n       </a>\n       (SOHO), and the\n       <a href="/wiki/WIND_(spacecraft)" title="WIND (spacecraft)">\n        Global Geoscience WIND\n       </a>\n       satellite\xe2\x80\x94each have annual\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Delta_V" title="Delta V">\n        station-keeping propellant requirements\n       </a>\n       of approximately 1\xc2\xa0m/s or less.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nasa20110101_5-1">\n        <a href="#cite_note-nasa20110101-5">\n         [5]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       Earth-Sun\n       <a href="/wiki/Lagrangian_point#L2" title="Lagrangian point">\n        L\n        <sub>\n         2\n        </sub>\n       </a>\n       \xe2\x80\x94approximately 1.5 million kilometers from Earth in the anti-sun direction\xe2\x80\x94is another important Lagrangian point, and the\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ESA" title="ESA">\n        ESA\n       </a>\n       <a href="/wiki/Herschel_Space_Observatory" title="Herschel Space Observatory">\n        Herschel\n       </a>\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Space_observatory" title="Space observatory">\n        space observatory\n       </a>\n       operated there in a Lissajous orbit during 2009\xe2\x80\x932013, at which time it ran out of coolant for the\n       <a href="/wiki/Space_telescope" title="Space telescope">\n        space telescope\n       </a>\n       . Small station-keeping orbital maneuvers were executed approximately monthly to maintain the spacecraft in the station-keeping orbit.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-esa20090614_1-2">\n        <a href="#cite_note-esa20090614-1">\n         [1]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       The\n       <a href="/wiki/James_Webb_Space_Telescope" title="James Webb Space Telescope">\n        James Webb Space Telescope\n       </a>\n       needs to use propellant to maintain its halo orbit around the Earth-Sun L2, which provides an upper limit to its designed lifetime: it is being designed to carry enough for ten years.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">\n        <a href="#cite_note-6">\n         [6]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n
