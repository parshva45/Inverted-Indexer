      An\n       <b>\n        aerosol\n       </b>\n       is a\n       <a href="/wiki/Colloid" title="Colloid">\n        colloid\n       </a>\n       of fine\n       <a href="/wiki/Solid" href="/wiki/Gas" title="Gas">\n        gas\n       </a>\n       .\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hinds.2C_1999.2C_p._3_1-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-Hinds.2C_1999.2C_p._3-1">\n         [1]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       Aerosols can be natural or anthropogenic. Examples of natural aerosols are\n       <a href="/wiki/Fog" title="Fog">\n        fog\n       </a>\n       , dust, forest\n       <a href="/wiki/Exudate" title="Exudate">\n        exudates\n       </a>\n       and\n       <a href="/wiki/Geyser" title="Geyser">\n        geyser\n       </a>\n       <a href="/wiki/Steam" title="Steam">\n        steam\n       </a>\n       . Examples of anthropogenic aerosols are\n       <a href="/wiki/Haze" title="Haze">\n        haze\n       </a>\n       , particulate\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Air_pollutants" title="Air pollutants">\n        air pollutants\n       </a>\n       and\n       <a href="/wiki/Smoke" title="Smoke">\n        smoke\n       </a>\n       .\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hinds.2C_1999.2C_p._3_1-1">\n        <a href="#cite_note-Hinds.2C_1999.2C_p._3-1">\n         [1]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       The liquid or solid particles have diameter mostly smaller than 1 \xce\xbcm or so; larger particles with a significant settling speed make the mixture a\n       <a href="/wiki/Suspension_(chemistry)" title="Suspension (chemistry)">\n        suspension\n       </a>\n       , but the distinction is not clear-cut. In general conversation,\n       <i>\n        aerosol\n       </i>\n       usually refers to an\n       <a href="/wiki/Aerosol_spray" title="Aerosol spray">\n        aerosol spray\n       </a>\n       that delivers a consumer product from a can or similar container. Other technological applications of aerosols include dispersal of pesticides, medical treatment of respiratory illnesses, and combustion technology.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hidy.2C_1984.2C_p._254_2-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-Hidy.2C_1984.2C_p._254-2">\n         [2]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       <a href="/wiki/Airborne_disease" title="Airborne disease">\n        Diseases can also spread\n       </a>\n       by means of small droplets in the\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Breath" title="Breath">\n        breath\n       </a>\n       , also called aerosols.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">\n        <a href="#cite_note-3">\n         [3]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       Aerosol science class="mw-headline" id="Definitions">\n        Definitions\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a </span>\n       </span>\n      </h2>\n      <div class="thumb tright">\n       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">\n        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Fly_Ash_FHWA_dot_gov.jpg">\n         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="274" data-file-width="347" height="174" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6f/Fly_Ash_FHWA_dot_gov.jpg/220px-Fly_Ash_FHWA_dot_gov.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6f/Fly_Ash_FHWA_dot_gov.jpg/330px-Fly_Ash_FHWA_dot_gov.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/6/6f/Fly_Ash_FHWA_dot_gov.jpg 2x" width="220"/>\n        </a>\n        <div class="thumbcaption">\n         <div class="magnify">\n          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Fly_Ash_FHWA_dot_gov.jpg" title="Enlarge">\n          </a>\n         </div>\n         Photomicrograph made with a Scanning Electron Microscope (SEM):\n         <a href="/wiki/Fly_ash" title="Fly ash">\n          Fly ash\n         </a>\n         particles at 2,000\xc3\x97 magnification. Most of the particles in this aerosol are nearly spherical.\n        </div>\n       </div>\n      </div>\n      <p>\n       An aerosol is defined as a colloidal system of solid or liquid particles in a gas. An aerosol includes both the particles and the suspending gas, which is usually air.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hinds.2C_1999.2C_p._3_1-3">\n        <a href="#cite_note-Hinds.2C_1999.2C_p._3-1">\n         [1]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       <a href="/wiki/Frederick_G._Donnan" title="Frederick G. Donnan">\n        Frederick G. Donnan\n       </a>\n       presumably first used the term\n       <i>\n        aerosol\n       </i>\n       during\n       <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">\n        World War I\n       </a>\n       to describe an aero-\n       <a href="/wiki/Solution" title="Solution">\n        solution\n       </a>\n       , clouds of microscopic particles in air. This term developed analogously to the term\n       <a href="/wiki/Sol_(colloid)" title="Sol (colloid)">\n        hydrosol\n       </a>\n       , a colloid system with water as the dispersing medium.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">\n        <a href="#cite_note-4">\n         [4]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       <i>\n        Primary aerosols\n       </i>\n       contain particles introduced directly into the gas;\n       <i>\n        secondary aerosols\n       </i>\n       form through gas-to-particle conversion.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">\n        <a href="#cite_note-5">\n         [5]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       Various types of aerosol, classified according to physical form and how they were generated, include dust, fume, mist, smoke and fog.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">\n        <a href="#cite_note-6">\n         [6]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       There are several measures of aerosol concentration. Environmental science and health often uses the\n       <i>\n        <a href="/wiki/Mass_concentration_(chemistry)" title="Mass concentration (chemistry)">\n         mass concentration\n        </a>\n       </i>\n       (\n       <i>\n        M\n       </i>\n       ), defined as the mass of particulate matter per unit volume with units such as \xce\xbcg/m\n       <sup>\n        3\n       </sup>\n       . Also commonly used is the\n       <i>\n        <a href="/wiki/Number_density" title="Number density">\n         number concentration\n        </a>\n       </i>\n       (\n       <i>\n        N\n       </i>\n       ), the number of particles per unit volume with units such as number/m\n       <sup>\n        3\n       </sup>\n       or number/cm\n       <sup>\n        3\n       </sup>\n       .\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">\n        <a href="#cite_note-7">\n         [7]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       The size of particles has a major influence on their properties, and the aerosol particle radius or diameter (\n       <i>\n        d\n        <sub>\n         p\n        </sub>\n       </i>\n       ) is a key property used to characterise aerosols.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       Aerosols vary in their\n       <a href="/wiki/Dispersity" title="Dispersity">\n        dispersity\n       </a>\n       . A\n       <i>\n        monodisperse\n       </i>\n       aerosol, producible in the laboratory, contains particles of uniform size. Most aerosols, however, as\n       <i>\n        polydisperse\n       </i>\n       colloidal systems, exhibit a range of particle sizes.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">\n        <a href="#cite_note-8">\n         [8]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       Liquid droplets are almost always nearly spherical, but scientists use an\n       <i>\n        equivalent diameter\n       </i>\n       to characterize the properities of various shapes of solid particles, some very irregular. The equivalent diameter is the diameter of a spherical particle with the same value of some physical property as the irregular particle.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">\n        <a href="#cite_note-9">\n         [9]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       The\n       <i>\n        equivalent volume diameter\n       </i>\n       (\n       <i>\n        d\n        <sub>\n         e\n At nanotechnology workplaces, workers can be exposed via inhalation to potentially toxic substances during handling and processing of nanomaterials. Nanoparticles in the air often form agglomerates due to attractive inter-particle forces, such as van der Waals force or electrostatic force if the particles are charged. As a result, aerosol particles are usually observed as agglomerates rather than individual particles. For exposure and risk assessments of airborne nanoparticles, it is important to know about the size distribution of aerosols. When inhaled by humans, particles with different diameters are deposited in varied locations of the central and periphery respiratory system. Particles in nanoscale have been shown to penetrate the air-blood barrier in lungs and be translocated into secondary organs in the human body, such as the brain, heart and liver. Therefore, the knowledge on stability of nanoparticle agglomerates is important for predicting the size of aerosol particles, which helps assess the potential risk of them to human bodies.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       Different experimental systems have been established to test the stability of airborne particles and their potentials to deagglomerate under various conditions. A comprehensive system recently reported by Ding &amp; Riediker (2015)\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45">\n        <a href="#cite_note-45">\n         [45]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       is able to maintain robust aerosolization process and generate aerosols with stable number concentration and mean size from nano-powders. The deagglomeration potential of various airborne nanomaterials can be also studied using critical orifices. This process was also investigated by Stahlmecke et al. (2009).\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46">\n        <a href="#cite_note-46">\n         [46]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       In addition, an impact fragmentation device was developed to investigate bonding energies between particles.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47">\n        <a href="#cite_note-47">\n         [47]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       A standard deagglomeration testing procedure could be foreseen with the developments of the different types of existing systems. The likeliness of deagglomeration of aerosol particles in occupational settings can be possibly ranked for different nanomaterials if a reference method is available. For this purpose, inter-laboratory comparison of testing results from different setups could be launched in order to explore the influences of system characteristics on properties of generated nanomaterials aerosols.\n      </p>\n      <h2>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Detection">\n        Detection\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Aerosol&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Detection">\n         edit\n sensing\n       </a>\n       techniques.\n      </p>\n      <h3>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="In_situ_observations">\n        In situ observations\n       </span>\n       <span     </a>\n        (WPS)\n       </li>\n       <li>\n        <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Micro-Orifice_Uniform_Deposit_Impactor&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Micro-Orifice Uniform      <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Aerosol&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Remote sensing approach">\n  <ul>\n       <li>\n        <a href="/wiki/Sun_photometer" title="Sun photometer">\n         Sun photometer\n        </a>\n       </li>\n       <li>\n        <a href="/wiki/Lidar" title="Lidar">\n      <h3>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Size_selective_sampling">\n        Size selective sampling\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Aerosol&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Size selective sampling">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h3>\n      <p>\n       Particles can deposit in the\n       <a href="/wiki/Human_nose" title="Human nose">\n        nose\n       </a>\n       ,\n       <a href="/wiki/Human_mouth" title="Human mouth">\n        mouth\n       </a>\n       ,\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Human_pharynx" title="Human pharynx">\n        pharynx\n       </a>\n       and\n       <a href="/wiki/Larynx" title="Larynx">\n        larynx\n       </a>\n       (the head airways region), deeper within the respiratory tract (from the\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vertebrate_trachea" title="Vertebrate trachea">\n        trachea\n       </a>\n       to the\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Terminal_bronchioles" title="Terminal bronchioles">\n        terminal bronchioles\n       </a>\n       ), or in the\n       <a href="/wiki/Pulmonary_alveolus" title="Pulmonary alveolus">\n        alveolar region\n       </a>\n       .\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48">\n        <a href="#cite_note-48">\n         [48]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       The location of deposition of aerosol particles within the respiratory system strongly determines the health effects of exposure to such aerosols.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49">\n        <a href="#cite_note-49">\n         [49]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       This phenomenon led people to invent aerosol samplers that select a subset of the aerosol particles that reach certain parts of the respiratory system.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50">\n        <a href="#cite_note-50">\n         [50]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       Examples of these subsets of the particle-size distribution of an aerosol, important in occupational health, include the inhalable, thoracic, and respirable fractions. The fraction that can enter each part of the respiratory system depends on the deposition of particles in the upper parts of the airway.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51">\n        <a href="#cite_note-51">\n         [51]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       The inhalable fraction of particles, defined as the proportion of particles originally in the air that can enter the nose or mouth, depends on external wind speed and direction and on the particle-size distribution by aerodynamic diameter.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52">\n        <a href="#cite_note-52">\n         [52]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       The thoracic fraction is the proportion of the particles in ambient aerosol that can reach the thorax or chest region.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53">\n        <a href="#cite_note-53">\n         [53]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       The respirable fraction is the proportion of particles in the air that can reach the alveolar region.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54">\n        <a href="#cite_note-54">\n         [54]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       To measure the respirable fraction of particles in air, a pre-collector is used with a sampling filter. The pre-collector excludes particles as the airways remove particles from inhaled air. The sampling filter collects the particles for measurement. It is common to use\n       <a href="/wiki/Cyclonic_separation" title="Cyclonic separation">\n        cyclonic separation\n       </a>\n       for the pre-collector, but other techniques include impactors, horizontal\n       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Elutriator&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Elutriator (page does not exist)">\n        elutriators\n       </a>\n       , and large pore\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Membrane_filter" title="Membrane filter">\n        membrane filters\n       </a>\n       .\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55">\n        <a href="#cite_note-55">\n         [55]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       Two alternative size-selective criteria, often used in atmospheric monitoring, are PM\n       <sub>\n        10\n       </sub>\n       and PM\n       <sub>\n        2.5\n       </sub>\n       . PM\n       <sub>\n        10\n       </sub>\n       is defined by\n       <a href="/wiki/International_Organization_for_Standardization" title="International Organization for Standardization">\n        ISO\n       </a>\n       as\n       <i>\n        particles which pass through a size-selective inlet with a 50% efficiency cut-off at 10 \xce\xbcm aerodynamic diameter\n       </i>\n       and PM\n       <sub>\n        2.5\n       </sub>\n       as\n       <i>\n        particles which pass through a size-selective inlet with a 50% efficiency cut-off at 2.5 \xce\xbcm aerodynamic diameter\n       </i>\n       . PM\n       <sub>\n        10\n       </sub>\n       corresponds to the \xe2\x80\x9cthoracic convention\xe2\x80\x9d as defined in ISO 7708:1995, Clause 6; PM\n       <sub>\n        2.5\n       </sub>\n       corresponds to the \xe2\x80\x9chigh-risk respirable convention\xe2\x80\x9d as defined in ISO 7708:1995, 7.1.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56">\n        <a href="#cite_note-56">\n         [56]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       The\n       <a href="/wiki/United_States_Environmental_Protection_Agency" title="United States Environmental Protection Agency">\n        United States Environmental Protection Agency\n       </a>\n       replaced the older standards for particulate matter based on Total Suspended Particulate with another standard based on PM\n       <sub>\n        10\n       </sub>\n       in 1987\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57">\n        <a href="#cite_note-57">\n         [57]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       and then introduced standards for PM\n       <sub>\n        2.5\n       </sub>\n       (also known as fine particulate matter) in 1997.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58">\n        <a href="#cite_note-58">\n         [58]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <h2>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Atmospheric">\n        Atmospheric\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Aerosol&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Atmospheric">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h2>\n      <div class="thumb tright">\n       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">\n          India\n         </a>\n         and\n         <a href="/wiki/Bangladesh" title="Bangladesh">\n          Bangladesh\n         </a>\n        </div>\n       </div>\n      </div>\n      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">\n       Main article:\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atmospheric_particulate_matter" title="Atmospheric particulate matter">\n        Atmospheric particulate matter\n       </a>\n      </div>\n      <p>\n       Several types of atmospheric aerosol have a significant effect on Earth\'s climate: volcanic, desert dust, sea-salt, that originating from biogenic sources and human-made. Volcanic aerosol forms in the stratosphere after an eruption as droplets of\n       <a href="/wiki/Sulfuric_acid" title="Sulfuric acid">\n        sulfuric acid\n       </a>\n       that can prevail for up to two years, and reflect sunlight, lowering temperature. Desert dust, mineral particles blown to high altitudes, absorb heat and may be responsible for inhibiting storm cloud formation. Human-made sulfate aerosols, primarily from burning oil and coal, affect the behavior of clouds.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59">\n        <a href="#cite_note-59">\n         [59]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       Although all hydrometeors, solid and liquid, can be described as aerosols, a distinction is commonly made between such dispersions (i.e. clouds) containing activated drops and crystals, and aerosol particles. The\n       <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth" title="Atmosphere of Earth">\n        atmosphere of Earth\n       </a>\n       contains aerosols of various types and concentrations, including quantities of:\n      </p>\n      <ul>\n       <li>\n        natural\n        <a href="/wiki/Inorganic_chemistry" title="Inorganic chemistry">\n         inorganic\n        </a>\n        materials: fine dust, sea salt, or water droplets\n       </li>\n       <li>\n        natural\n        <a href="/wiki/Organic_chemistry" title="Organic chemistry">\n         organic\n        </a>\n        materials: smoke,\n        <a href="/wiki/Pollen" title="Pollen">\n         pollen\n        </a>\n        ,\n        <a href="/wiki/Spore" title="Spore">\n         spores\n        </a>\n        , or\n        <a href="/wiki/Bacteria" title="Bacteria">\n         bacteria\n        </a>\n       </li>\n       <li>\n        <a href="/wiki/Human_impact_on_the_environment" title="Human impact on the environment">\n         anthropogenic\n        </a>\n        products of combustion such as: smoke,\n        <a href="/wiki/Fly_ash" title="Fly ash">\n         ashes\n        </a>\n        or dusts\n       </li>\n      </ul>\n      <p>\n       Aerosols can be found in urban\n       <a href="/wiki/Ecosystem" title="Ecosystem">\n        ecosystems\n       </a>\n       in various forms, for example:\n      </p>\n      <ul>\n       <li>\n        Dust\n       </li>\n       <li>\n        Cigarette smoke\n       </li>\n       <li>\n        Mist from\n        <a href="/wiki/Aerosol_spray" title="Aerosol spray">\n         aerosol spray\n        </a>\n        cans\n       </li>\n       <li>\n        <a href="/wiki/Soot" title="Soot">\n         Soot\n        </a>\n        or fumes in car exhaust\n       </li>\n      </ul>\n      <p>\n       The presence of aerosols in the earth\'s atmosphere can influence its climate, as well as human health.\n      </p>\n      <h3>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Effects">\n        Effects\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Aerosol&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Effects">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h3>\n      <ul>\n       <li>\n        Volcanic eruptions release large amounts of\n        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sulphuric_acid" title="Sulphuric acid">\n         sulphuric acid\n        </a>\n        ,\n        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydrogen_sulphide" title="Hydrogen sulphide">\n         hydrogen sulphide\n        </a>\n        and\n        <a href="/wiki/Hydrochloric_acid" title="Hydrochloric acid">\n         hydrochloric acid\n        </a>\n        into the atmosphere. These gases represent aerosols and eventually return to earth as\n        <a href="/wiki/Acid_rain" title="Acid rain">\n         acid rain\n        </a>\n        , having a number of\n        <a href="/wiki/Acid_rain#Adverse_effects" title="Acid rain">\n         adverse effects\n        </a>\n        on the environment and human life.\n        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nasa_60-0">\n         <a href="#cite_note-nasa-60">\n          [60]\n         </a>\n        </sup>\n       </li>\n      </ul>\n      <ul>\n       <li>\n        Aerosols interact with the Earth\'s energy budget in two ways, directly and indirectly.\n       </li>\n      </ul>\n      <dl>\n       <dd>\n        <dl>\n         <dd>\n          E.g., a\n          <i>\n           direct\n          </i>\n          effect is that aerosols scatter sunlight directly back into space. This can lead to a significant decrease in the temperature, being an additional element to the greenhouse effect and therefore contributing to the global climate change.\n          <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kommalapati_61-0">\n           <a href="#cite_note-kommalapati-61">\n            [61]\n           </a>\n          </sup>\n         </dd>\n        </dl>\n       </dd>\n      </dl>\n      <dl>\n       <dd>\n        <dl>\n         <dd>\n          The\n          <i>\n           indirect\n          </i>\n          effects refer to the aerosols interfering with formations that interact directly with radiation. For example, they are able to modify the size of the cloud particles in the lower atmosphere, thereby changing the way clouds reflect and absorb light and therefore modifying the Earth\'s energy budget.\n          <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nasa_60-1">\n           <a href="#cite_note-nasa-60">\n            [60]\n           </a>\n          </sup>\n         </dd>\n        </dl>\n       </dd>\n      </dl>\n      <ul>\n       <li>\n        When aerosols absorb pollutants, it facilitates the deposition of pollutants to the surface of the earth as well as to bodies of water.\n        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kommalapati_61-1">\n         <a href="#cite_note-kommalapati-61">\n          [61]\n         </a>\n        </sup>\n        This has the potential to be damaging to both the environment and human health.\n       </li>\n       <li>\n        Aerosol particles with an effective diameter smaller than 10 \xce\xbcm can enter the bronchi, while the ones with an effective diameter smaller than 2.5 \xce\xbcm can enter as far as the gas exchange region in the lungs,\n        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-volcanic_emissions_62-0">\n         <a href="#cite_note-volcanic_emissions-62">\n          [62]\n         </a>\n        </sup>\n        which can be hazardous to human health.\n       </li>\n      </ul>\n      <h2>\n       <span
