    </b>\n       (\n       <span class="mwe-math-element">\n        <span class="mwe-math-mathml-inline mwe-math-mathml-a11y" style="display: none;">\n         <math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">\n          <semantics>\n           <mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">\n            <mstyle displaystyle="true" scriptlevel="0">\n             <mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">\n              <mi mathvariant="bold-italic">\n               \xce\xb2\n               <!-- \xce\xb2 -->\n              </mi>\n             </mrow>\n            </mstyle>\n           </mrow>\n           <annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\n            {\\displaystyle {\\boldsymbol {\\beta }}}\n           </annotation>\n          </semantics>\n         </math>\n        </span>\n        <img alt="{\\boldsymbol {\\beta }}" aria-hidden="true" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline" src="https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/702cafc420cc00c54896f6d125112820956aaf6b" style="vertical-align: -0.671ex; width:1.545ex; height:2.509ex;"/>\n       </span>\n       ) is a measurement that is used most notably in\n       <a href="/wiki/Spaceflight" title="Spaceflight">\n        spaceflight\n       </a>\n       . The beta angle determines the percentage of time an object such as a spacecraft in\n       <a href="/wiki/Low_Earth_orbit" title="Low Earth orbit">\n        low Earth orbit\n       </a>\n       (LEO) spends in direct sunlight, absorbing solar energy.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mcc_1-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-mcc-1">\n         [1]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       Beta angle is defined as the angle between the\n       <a href="/wiki/Orbit" title="Orbit">\n        orbital\n       </a>\n       plane of the spacecraft and the\n       <a href="/wiki/Euclidean_vector" title="Euclidean vector">\n        vector\n       </a>\n       to the\n       <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">\n        sun\n       </a>\n       (i.e., which direction the Sun is shining from).\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-knk_2-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-knk-2">\n         [2]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       The beta angle is the smaller angle (there are two angles) between the Sun vector (where the Sun is shining from in the sky) and the\n       <a href="/wiki/Plane_(geometry)" title="Plane (geometry)">\n        plane\n       </a>\n       of the object\'s orbit. Note that the beta angle does not define a unique orbit plane; all satellites in orbit with a given beta angle at a given altitude have the same exposure to the Sun, even though they may be orbiting in completely different planes around the Earth.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-orbit_3-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-orbit-3">\n         [3]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       The beta angle varies between +90\xc2\xb0 and \xe2\x88\x9290\xc2\xb0, and the direction the satellite revolves around the body it orbits determines whether the beta angle sign is positive or negative. An imaginary observer standing on the Sun defines a beta angle as positive if the satellite in question orbits in a counter clockwise direction and negative if it revolves clockwise.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-orbit_3-1">\n        <a href="#cite_note-orbit-3">\n         [3]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       The maximum amount of time that a satellite in a normal low Earth orbit mission can spend in the Earth\'s shadow occurs at a beta angle of zero. In such an orbit, the satellite is in sunlight no more than 59% of the time.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mcc_1-1">\n        <a href="#cite_note-mcc-1">\n         [1]\n        </a>\n </span>\n         </a>\n        </li>\n       </ul>\n      </div>\n      <p>\n      </p>\n      <h2>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Light_and_shadow">\n        Light and shadow\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Beta_angle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Light and shadow">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h2>\n      <p>\n       The degree of orbital shadowing an object in LEO experiences is determined by that object\'s beta angle. An object launched into an initial orbit with an inclination equal to the complement of the Earth\'s inclination to the ecliptic results in an initial beta angle of 0 degrees (\n       <span class="mwe-math-element">\n        <span class="mwe-math-mathml-inline mwe-math-mathml-a11y" style="display: none;">\n         <math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">\n          <semantics>\n           <mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">\n            <mstyle displaystyle="true" scriptlevel="0">\n             <mi>\n              \xce\xb2\n              <!-- \xce\xb2 -->\n             </mi>\n            </mstyle>\n           </mrow>\n           <annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\n            {\\displaystyle \\beta }\n           </annotation>\n          </semantics>\n         </math>\n        </span>\n        <img alt="\\beta " aria-hidden="true" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline" src="https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7ed48a5e36207156fb792fa79d29925d2f7901e8" style="vertical-align: -0.671ex; width:1.342ex; height:2.509ex;"/>\n       </span>\n       = 0\xc2\xb0) for the orbiting object. This allows the object to spend the maximum possible amount of its\n       <a href="/wiki/Orbital_period" title="Orbital period">\n        orbital period\n       </a>\n       in the Earth\'s shadow, and results in extremely reduced absorption of solar energy. At a LEO of 280 kilometers, the object is in sunlight through 59% of its orbit (approximately 53 minutes in Sunlight, and 37 minutes in shadow.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-knk_2-2">\n        <a href="#cite_note-knk-2">\n         [2]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       ) On the other extreme, an object launched into an orbit parallel to the\n       <a href="/wiki/Terminator_(solar)" title="Terminator (solar)">\n        terminator\n       </a>\n       results in a beta angle of 90 degrees (\n       <span class="mwe-math-element">\n        <span class="mwe-math-mathml-inline mwe-math-mathml-a11y" style="display: none;">\n         <math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">\n          <semantics>\n           <mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">\n            <mstyle displaystyle="true" scriptlevel="0">\n             <mi>\n              \xce\xb2\n              <!-- \xce\xb2 -->\n             </mi>\n            </mstyle>\n           </mrow>\n           <annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\n            {\\displaystyle \\beta }\n           </annotation>\n          </semantics>\n         </math>\n        </span>\n        <img alt="\\beta " aria-hidden="true" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline" src="https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7ed48a5e36207156fb792fa79d29925d2f7901e8" style="vertical-align: -0.671ex; width:1.342ex; height:2.509ex;"/>\n       </span>\n       = 90\xc2\xb0), and the object is in sunlight 100% of the time.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-knk_2-3">\n        <a href="#cite_note-knk-2">\n         [2]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       An example would be a polar orbit initiated at local dawn or dusk on an\n       <a href="/wiki/Equinox" title="Equinox">\n        equinox\n       </a>\n       . Beta angle can be controlled to keep a satellite as cool as possible (for instruments that require low temperatures, such as infrared cameras) by keeping the beta angle as close to zero as possible, or, conversely, to keep a satellite in sunlight as much as possible (for conversion of sunlight by its solar panels, for solar stability of sensors, or to study the Sun) by maintaining a beta angle as close to +90 or -90 as possible.\n      </p>\n      <h2>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Determination_and_application_of_beta_angles">\n        Determination and application of beta angles\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Beta_angle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Determination and application of beta angles">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h2>\n      <p>\n       The above discussion defines the beta angle of satellites orbiting the Earth, but a beta angle can be calculated for any orbiting three body system: the same definition can be applied to give the beta angle of other objects. For example, the beta angle of a satellite in orbit around Mars, with respect to the Earth, defines how much of the time the satellite has a line of sight to the Earth - that is, it determines how long the Earth is shining on the satellite and how long the Earth is blocked from view. That same satellite also will have a beta angle with respect to the Sun, and in fact it has a beta angle for any celestial object one might wish to calculate one for: any satellite orbiting a body (i.e. the Earth) will be in that body\'s shadow with respect to a given celestial object (like a star) some of the time, and in its line-of-sight the rest of the time. Beta angles describing non-\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Geocentric" title="Geocentric">\n        geocentric\n       </a>\n       orbits are important when space agencies launch satellites into orbits around other bodies in the Solar System.\n      </p>\n      <h2>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Importance_in_spaceflight">\n        Importance in spaceflight\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Beta_angle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Importance in spaceflight">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h2>\n      <p>\n       When the\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Space_shuttle" title="Space shuttle">\n        space shuttle\n       </a>\n       was in service on missions to the\n       <a href="/wiki/International_Space_Station" title="International Space Station">\n        International Space Station\n       </a>\n       , the beta angle of the space station\'s orbit was a crucial consideration; periods referred to as "beta cutout",\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mcc_1-2">\n        <a href="#cite_note-mcc-1">\n         [1]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       during which the shuttle could not safely be launched to the ISS, were a direct result of the beta angle of the space station at those times. When the orbiter was in-flight (not docked to ISS) and it flew to a beta angle greater than 60 degrees, the orbiter went into "rotisserie" mode, and slowly rotated around its X-axis (nose to tail axis). For flights to ISS, the shuttle could launch during an ISS beta cutout if the ISS would be at a beta less than 60 degrees at dock, and throughout the docked phase.\n       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">\n        [\n        <i>\n         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">\n          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2009)">\n           citation needed\n          </span>\n         </a>\n        </i>\n        ]\n       </sup>\n       Therefore, the mission duration affected launch timing when the beta cutout dates were approaching.\n      </p>\n      <h2>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">\n        See also\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span
