      was the most intense hurricane to make landfall in the United States on record\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">\n        <a href="#cite_note-1">\n         [1]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       and the most intense Atlantic hurricane until\n       <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Gilbert" title="Hurricane Gilbert">\n        Hurricane Gilbert\n       </a>\n       . The second\n       <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone" title="Tropical cyclone">\n        tropical cyclone\n       </a>\n       , second hurricane, and second\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saffir-Simpson_Hurricane_Scale" title="Saffir-Simpson Hurricane Scale">\n        major hurricane\n       </a>\n       of the\n       <a href="/wiki/1935_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="1935 Atlantic hurricane season">\n        1935 Atlantic hurricane season\n       </a>\n       , the Labor Day Hurricane was the first of three\n       <a href="/wiki/List_of_Category_5_Atlantic_hurricanes" title="List of Category 5 Atlantic hurricanes">\n        Category\xc2\xa05 hurricanes\n       </a>\n       to strike the United States at that intensity during the 20th century (the other two being\n       <a href="/wiki/1969_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="1969 Atlantic hurricane season">\n        1969\n       </a>\n       \'s\n       <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Camille" title="Hurricane Camille">\n        Hurricane Camille\n       </a>\n       and\n       <a href="/wiki/1992_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="1992 Atlantic hurricane season">\n        1992\n       </a>\n       \'s\n       <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Andrew" title="Hurricane Andrew">\n        Hurricane Andrew\n       </a>\n       ). After forming as a weak tropical storm east of\n       <a href="/wiki/The_Bahamas" title="The Bahamas">\n        the Bahamas\n       </a>\n       on August 29, it slowly proceeded westward and became a hurricane on September 1.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       On\n       <a href="/wiki/Long_Key" title="Long Key">\n        Long Key\n       </a>\n       , it struck about midway through the calm. The waters quickly receded after carving new channels connecting the bay with the ocean; however, gale force winds and high seas persisted into Tuesday, preventing rescue efforts. The storm continued northwest along the Florida west coast, weakening before its second landfall near\n       <a href="/wiki/Cedar_Key,_Florida" title="Cedar Key, Florida">\n        Cedar Key, Florida\n       </a>\n       , on September 4.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       The compact and intense hurricane caused extreme damage in the upper Florida Keys, as a\n       <a href="/wiki/Storm_surge" title="Storm surge">\n        storm surge\n       </a>\n       of approximately 18 to 20\xc2\xa0feet (5.5\xe2\x80\x936 meters) swept over the low-lying islands. The hurricane\'s strong winds and the surge destroyed nearly all the structures between\n       <a href="/wiki/Tavernier,_Florida" title="Tavernier, Florida">\n        Tavernier\n       </a>\n       and\n       <a href="/wiki/Marathon,_Florida" title="Marathon, Florida">\n        Marathon\n       </a>\n       . The town of\n       <a href="/wiki/Islamorada,_Florida" title="Islamorada, Florida">\n        Islamorada\n       </a>\n       was obliterated. Portions of the\n       <a href="/wiki/Overseas_Railroad" title="Overseas Railroad">\n        Key West Extension\n       </a>\n       of the\n       <a href="/wiki/Florida_East_Coast_Railway" title="Florida East Coast Railway">\n        Florida East Coast Railway\n       </a>\n       were severely damaged or destroyed. The hurricane also caused additional damage in northwest Florida,\n       <a href="/wiki/Georgia_(U.S._state)" title="Georgia (U.S. state)">\n        Georgia\n       </a>\n       , and the\n       <a href="/wiki/The_Carolinas" title="The Carolinas">\n        Carolinas\n       </a>\n       .\n      </p>\n      <p>\n      </p>\n      <div class="toc" id="toc">\n       <div class="toctitle">\n    </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h2>\n      <div class="thumb tleft">\n       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">\n        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:1935_Labor_Day_hurricane_track.png">\n         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1897" data-file-width="2700" height="155" //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d9/1935_Labor_Day_hurricane_track.png/440px-1935_Labor_Day_hurricane_track.png 2x" width="220"/>\n        </a>\n        <div class="thumbcaption">\n         <div class="magnify">\n          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:1935_Labor_Day_hurricane_track.png" title="Enlarge">\n          </a>\n         </div>\n         Map plotting the track and intensity of the storm, according to the\n         <a href="/wiki/Saffir%E2%80%93Simpson_scale" title="Saffir\xe2\x80\x93Simpson scale">\n          Saffir\xe2\x80\x93Simpson scale\n         </a>\n        </div>\n       </div>\n      </div>\n      <p>\n       \xe2\x80\x9cThe first indications of conditions favorable to the origin of this disturbance were noted during the last 2 or 3 days of August, to the eastward and northward of Turks Islands; but it was not until August 31 that a definite depression appeared near Long Island in the southeastern Bahamas, and deepened rapidly as it moved westward. \xe2\x80\xa6 Hurricane intensity was doubtless reached by the developing disturbance near the south end of Andros Island on September 1.\xe2\x80\x9d\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-American_Meteorological_Society_2-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-American_Meteorological_Society-2">\n         [2]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       \xe2\x80\x9cA broad recurve brought it to the Florida Keys late on September 2. By that time it was of small diameter but of tremendous force. \xe2\x80\xa6 There was a calm of forty minutes on Lower Matecumbe Key and about fifty-five minutes at the Long Key fishing camp, 9:20 to 10:15 p.m. The rate of progress of the hurricane was about ten miles an hour and the calm center must have been nine to ten miles in diameter. After leaving the Keys, the storm skirted the Florida Gulf coast on a broad recurve, passed inland at Cedar Keys and finally left the continent near Cape Henry.\xe2\x80\x9d\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">\n        <a href="#cite_note-3">\n         [3]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       Heavy rainfall spread in advance and magnified to the left of the track across the Mid-Atlantic states. Rainfall totals of 16.7 inches (420\xc2\xa0mm) in\n       <a href="/wiki/Easton,_Maryland" title="Easton, Maryland">\n        Easton, Maryland\n       </a>\n       , and 13.4 inches (340\xc2\xa0mm) at\n       <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_City,_New_Jersey" title="Atlantic City, New Jersey">\n        Atlantic City, New Jersey\n       </a>\n       , were the highest seen with the storm.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">\n        <a href="#cite_note-4">\n         [4]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       The storm continued into \xe2\x80\x9cthe North Atlantic Ocean, where, off southern Greenland, it was lost on September 10 by merging with a cyclone of extratropical origin.\xe2\x80\x9d\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-American_Meteorological_Society_2-1">\n        <a href="#cite_note-American_Meteorological_Society-2">\n         [2]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       The first recorded instance of an aircraft flown for the specific purpose of locating a hurricane occurred on the afternoon of September 2. The Weather Bureau\xe2\x80\x99s 1:30 PM advisory\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">\n        <a href="#cite_note-5">\n         [5]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       placed the center of the hurricane at north latitude 23\xc2\xb0 20\xe2\x80\x99, west longitude 80\xc2\xb0 15\xe2\x80\x99, moving slowly westward. This was about 27 miles north of\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Isabela_de_Sagua" title="Isabela de Sagua">\n        Isabela de Sagua\n       </a>\n       , Villa Clara, Cuba, and 145 miles east of Havana. Captain Leonard Povey of the Cuban Army Air Corps volunteered to investigate the threat to the capital. Flying a\n       <a href="/wiki/Curtiss_F11C_Goshawk" title="Curtiss F11C Goshawk">\n        Curtis Hawk II\n       </a>\n       , Captain Povey, an American expatriate who was the CEAC\'s chief training officer, observed the storm north of its reported position but, flying an open-cockpit biplane, opted not to fly into it.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">\n        <a href="#cite_note-6">\n         [6]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Povey_7-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-Povey-7">\n         [7]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">\n        <a href="#cite_note-8">\n         [8]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       He later proposed an aerial hurricane patrol.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">\n        <a href="#cite_note-9">\n         [9]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       Nothing further came of this idea until June 1943 when Colonel Joe Duckworth and Lieutenant Ralph O\'Hair flew into\n       <a href="/wiki/1943_Surprise_Hurricane" title="1943 Surprise Hurricane">\n        a hurricane\n       </a>\n       near\n       <a href="/wiki/Galveston,_Texas" title="Galveston, Texas">\n        Galveston, Texas\n       </a>\n       .\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">\n        <a href="#cite_note-10">\n         [10]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <h3>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Records">\n        Records\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=1935_Labor_Day_hurricane&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Records">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h3>\n      <p>\n       The Labor Day Hurricane was the most intense storm ever known to have struck the United States (and any land areas in the Western Hemisphere), having the lowest sea level pressure ever recorded in the United States\xe2\x80\x94a central pressure of 892 mbar (26.35 inHg)\xe2\x80\x94suggesting an intensity of between 162 kn and 164 kn (186.4\xc2\xa0mph \xe2\x80\x93 188.7\xc2\xa0mph). The somewhat compensating effects of a slow (7 kn, 8.1\xc2\xa0mph) translational velocity along with an extremely tiny radius of maximum wind (5 nmi, 9.3 miles) led to an analyzed intensity at landfall of 160 kt (184.1\xc2\xa0mph, Category 5). This is the highest intensity for a U.S. landfalling hurricane in\n       <a class="external text" href="http://www.nhc.noaa.gov/data/#hurdat" rel="nofollow">\n        HURDAT2\n       </a>\n       , as 1969\xe2\x80\x99s\n       <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Camille" title="Hurricane Camille">\n        Hurricane Camille\n       </a>\n       has been recently reanalyzed to have the second highest landfalling intensity with 150 kn (172.6\xc2\xa0mph).\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">\n        <a href="#cite_note-11">\n         [11]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <blockquote>\n       <p>\n        <i>\n         Records of the lowest pressure were secured from three\n         <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Manometer#aneroid" title="Manometer">\n          aneroid\n         </a>\n         <a href="/wiki/Barometer" title="Barometer">\n          barometers\n         </a>\n         , the values ranging from 26.75 to 26.98 inches. However, none of these barometers had previously been compared to standard. One of the barometers, owned by Ivar Olson, was shipped to the Weather Bureau in Washington where it was tested in the Instrument Division. Careful laboratory tests showed it to be an exceptionally responsive and reliable instrument and that the correct reading at the position of the needle indicated by Mr. Olson at the center of the storm was 26.35 inches. This is the world\'s lowest record of pressure at a land station.\n         <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">\n          <a href="#cite_note-12">\n           [12]\n          </a>\n </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=1935_Labor_Day_hurricane&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Preparations">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h2>\n      <p>\n       Northeast storm warnings\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Flickr_15-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-Flickr-15">\n         [15]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       were ordered displayed from\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fort_Pierce" title="Fort Pierce">\n        Fort Pierce\n       </a>\n       to\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fort_Myers" title="Fort Myers">\n        Fort Myers\n       </a>\n       in the September 1, 9:30 AM Weather Bureau advisory.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HathiTrust_Digital_Library_16-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-HathiTrust_Digital_Library-16">\n         [16]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       Upon receipt of this advisory the U.S. Coast Guard Station, Miami, FL, sent a plane along the coast to advise boaters and campers of the impending danger by dropping message blocks. A second flight was made Sunday afternoon. All planes were placed in the hangar and its door closed at 10:00 AM Monday morning.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17">\n        <a href="#cite_note-17">\n         [17]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18">\n        <a href="#cite_note-18">\n         [18]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       The 3:30 AM advisory, September 2 (\n       <a href="/wiki/Labor_Day" title="Labor Day">\n        Labor Day\n       </a>\n       ), predicted the disturbance "will probably pass through the\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Florida_Straits" title="Florida Straits">\n        Florida Straits\n       </a>\n       Monday" and cautioned "against high tides and gales Florida Keys and ships in path."\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19">\n        <a href="#cite_note-19">\n         [19]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       The 1:30 PM advisory ordered hurricane warnings\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Flickr_15-1">\n        <a href="#cite_note-Flickr-15">\n         [15]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       for the\n       <a href="/wiki/Key_West" title="Key West">\n        Key West\n       </a>\n       district\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HathiTrust_Digital_Library_16-1">\n        <a href="#cite_note-HathiTrust_Digital_Library-16">\n         [16]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       which extended north to\n       <a href="/wiki/Key_Largo" title="Key Largo">\n        Key Largo\n       </a>\n       .\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20">\n        <a href="#cite_note-20">\n         [20]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       At around 2:00 PM, Fred Ghent, Assistant Administrator, Florida Emergency Relief Administration, requested a special train to evacuate the veterans work camps located in the upper keys.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21">\n        <a href="#cite_note-21">\n         [21]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       It departed Miami at 4:25 PM; delayed by a draw bridge opening, obstructions across the track, poor visibility and the necessity to back the locomotive below Homestead (so it could head out on the return trip\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22">\n        <a href="#cite_note-22">\n         [22]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       ) the train finally arrived at the Islamorada station on Upper Matecumbe Key at about 8:20 PM. This coincided with an abrupt wind shift from northeast (Florida Bay) to southeast (Atlantic Ocean) and the arrival on the coast of the\n       <a href="/wiki/Storm_surge" title="Storm surge">\n        storm surge\n       </a>\n       .\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-American_Meteorological_Society_2-2">\n        <a href="#cite_note-American_Meteorological_Society-2">\n         [2]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <h2>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Impact">\n        Impact\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=1935_Labor_Day_hurricane&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Impact">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h2>\n      <p>\n       Three ships ran afoul of the storm. "The Danish motorship\n       <i>\n        Leise Maersk\n       </i>\n       was carried over\n       <a href="/wiki/Alligator_Reef" title="Alligator Reef">\n        Alligator Reef\n       </a>\n       and grounded nearly 4 miles beyond, after being totally disabled by the wind and sea, with engine room flooded."\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Monthly_Weather_Review.2C_September_1935_23-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-Monthly_Weather_Review.2C_September_1935-23">\n         [23]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       This was just offshore of Upper Matecumbe Key. No one died and the ship was salvaged on Sept. 20. "The American tanker\n       <i>\n        Pueblo\n       </i>\n       drifted helplessly in the storm from 2 to 10 pm of September 2; she went out of control near\n       <span class="plainlinks nourlexpansion">\n        <a class="external text" href="//tools.wmflabs.org/geohack/geohack.php?pagename=1935_Labor_Day_hurricane&amp;params=24_40_N_80_25_W_">\n         <span class="geo-default">\n          <span class="geo-dms" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location">\n           <span class="latitude">\n            24\xc2\xb040\xe2\x80\xb2N\n           </span>\n           <span class="longitude">\n            80\xc2\xb025\xe2\x80\xb2W\n           </span>\n          </span>\n         </span>\n         <span class="geo-multi-punct">\n          \xef\xbb\xbf / \xef\xbb\xbf\n         </span>\n         <span class="geo-nondefault">\n          <span class="geo-dec" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location">\n           24.667\xc2\xb0N 80.417\xc2\xb0W\n          </span>\n          <span style="display:none">\n           \xef\xbb\xbf /\n           <span class="geo">\n            24.667; -80.417\n           </span>\n          </span>\n         </span>\n        </a>\n       </span>\n       , and was carried completely around the storm center, finding herself in 8 hours about 25 miles northeastward of her original position, and just barely able to claw off\n       <a href="/wiki/Molasses_Reef" title="Molasses Reef">\n        Molasses Reef\n       </a>\n       ."\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Monthly_Weather_Review.2C_September_1935_23-1">\n        <a href="#cite_note-Monthly_Weather_Review.2C_September_1935-23">\n         [23]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       However, the ship that made the headlines was the American steamship\n       <i>\n        Dixie\n       </i>\n       out of New Orleans, with a crew of 121 and 229 passengers.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24">\n        <a href="#cite_note-24">\n         [24]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       It ran aground on\n       <a href="/wiki/French_Reef" title="French Reef">\n        French Reef\n       </a>\n       , near Key Largo, without loss of life. It was re-floated on September 19 and towed to New York.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-American_Meteorological_Society_2-3">\n        <a href="#cite_note-American_Meteorological_Society-2">\n         [2]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <div class="thumb tright">\n       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;">\n        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Train_derailed_by_the_1935_hurricane.jpg">\n         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="753" data-file-width="600" height="314" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6c/Train_derailed_by_the_1935_hurricane.jpg/250px-Train_derailed_by_the_1935_hurricane.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6c/Train_derailed_by_the_1935_hurricane.jpg/375px-Train_derailed_by_the_1935_hurricane.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6c/Train_derailed_by_the_1935_hurricane.jpg/500px-Train_derailed_by_the_1935_hurricane.jpg 2x" width="250"/>\n        </a>\n        <div class="thumbcaption">\n         <div class="magnify">\n          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Train_derailed_by_the_1935_hurricane.jpg" title="Enlarge">\n          </a>\n         </div>\n         Florida East Coast Railway Overseas Railroad relief train derailed near Islamorada, Florida, during the 1935 Labor Day hurricane.\n        </div>\n       </div>\n      </div>\n      <p>\n       On Upper Matecumbe Key, near Islamorada, an eleven-car evacuation train encountered a powerful storm surge topped by cresting waves. Eleven cars\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25">\n        <a href="#cite_note-25">\n         [25]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       were swept from the tracks, leaving only the\n       <a href="/wiki/Locomotive" title="Locomotive">\n        locomotive\n       </a>\n       and\n       <a href="/wiki/Tender_(rail)" title="Tender (rail)">\n        tender\n       </a>\n       upright and still on the rails. Remarkably, everyone on the train survived.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26">\n        <a href="#cite_note-26">\n         [26]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       The locomotive and tender were both\n       <a href="/wiki/Barge" title="Barge">\n        barged\n       </a>\n       back to\n       <a href="/wiki/Miami" title="Miami">\n        Miami\n       </a>\n       several months later.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       The hurricane left a path of near destruction in the Upper Keys, centered on what is today the village of\n       <a href="/wiki/Islamorada,_Florida" title="Islamorada, Florida">\n        Islamorada\n       </a>\n       . The eye of the storm passed a few miles to the southwest creating a calm of about 40 minutes duration over Lower Matecumbe and 55 minutes (9:20\xe2\x80\x9310:15 PM) over Long Key. At Camp #3 on Lower Matecumbe the surge arrived near the end of the calm with the wind close behind.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27">\n        <a href="#cite_note-27">\n         [27]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       Nearly every structure was demolished, and some bridges and railway embankments were washed away. The links\xe2\x80\x94rail, road, and\n       <a href="/wiki/Ferry" title="Ferry">\n        ferry\n       </a>\n       boats\xe2\x80\x94that chained the islands together were broken. The main transportation route linking the Keys to mainland\n       <a href="/wiki/Florida" title="Florida">\n        Florida\n       </a>\n       had been a single railroad line, the\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Florida_Overseas_Railroad" title="Florida Overseas Railroad">\n        Florida Overseas Railroad\n       </a>\n       portion of the\n       <a href="/wiki/Florida_East_Coast_Railway" title="Florida East Coast Railway">\n        Florida East Coast Railway\n       </a>\n       . The Islamorada area was devastated, although the hurricane\'s destructive path was narrower than most tropical cyclones. Its eye was eight miles (13\xc2\xa0km.) across and the fiercest winds extended 15\xc2\xa0miles (24\xc2\xa0km.) off the center, less than 1992\'s\n       <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Andrew" title="Hurricane Andrew">\n        Hurricane Andrew\n       </a>\n       , which was also a relatively small and catastrophic Category\xc2\xa05 hurricane.\n       <a href="/wiki/Craig_Key" title="Craig Key">\n        Craig Key\n       </a>\n       ,\n       <a href="/wiki/Long_Key" title="Long Key">\n        Long Key\n       </a>\n       , and\n       <a href="/wiki/Upper_Matecumbe_Key" title="Upper Matecumbe Key">\n        Upper Matecumbe\n       </a>\n       and\n       <a href="/wiki/Lower_Matecumbe_Key" title="Lower Matecumbe Key">\n        Lower Matecumbe\n       </a>\n       Keys suffered the worst. The storm caused wind and flood damage along the\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Florida_panhandle" title="Florida panhandle">\n        Florida panhandle\n       </a>\n       and into\n       <a href="/wiki/Georgia_(U.S._state)" title="Georgia (U.S. state)">\n        Georgia\n       </a>\n       , and significant damage to the\n       <a href="/wiki/Tampa_Bay_Area" title="Tampa Bay Area">\n        Tampa Bay Area\n       </a>\n       .\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28">\n        <a href="#cite_note-28">\n         [28]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       After the third day of the storm corpses swelled and split open in the subtropical heat, according to rescue workers. Public health officials ordered plain wood coffins holding the dead to be stacked and burned in several locations. The\n       <a href="/wiki/National_Weather_Service" title="National Weather Service">\n        National Weather Service\n       </a>\n       estimated 408 deaths from the hurricane. Bodies were recovered as far away as\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Flamingo,_Florida" title="Flamingo, Florida">\n        Flamingo\n       </a>\n       and Cape Sable on the southwest tip of the Florida mainland.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       The\n       <a href="/wiki/United_States_Coast_Guard" title="United States Coast Guard">\n        United States Coast Guard\n       </a>\n       and other federal and state agencies organized evacuation and relief efforts. Boats and airplanes carried injured survivors to Miami. The railroad was never rebuilt, but temporary bridges and ferry landings were under construction as soon as materials arrived. On March 29, 1938, the last gap in the\n       <a href="/wiki/Overseas_Highway" title="Overseas Highway">\n        Overseas Highway\n       </a>\n       linking Key West to the mainland was completed. The new highway incorporated the roadbed and surviving bridges of the railway.\n      </p>\n      <h2>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Aftermath">\n        Aftermath\n       </span>\n on\n       <a href="/wiki/Windley_Key" title="Windley Key">\n        Windley Key\n       </a>\n       , #3 and #5 on\n       <a href="/wiki/Lower_Matecumbe_Key" title="Lower Matecumbe Key">\n        Lower Matecumbe Key\n       </a>\n       .\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29">\n        <a href="#cite_note-29">\n         [29]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       The camp payrolls for complete the\n       <a href="/wiki/Overseas_Highway" title="Overseas Highway">\n        Overseas Highway\n       </a>\n       connecting the mainland with Key West. The camps, including 7 in Florida and 4 in South Carolina, were established by\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Harry_L._Hopkins" title="Harry L. Hopkins">\n        Harry L. Hopkins\n       </a>\n       , director of the\n       <a href="/wiki/Federal_Emergency_Relief_Administration" title="Federal Emergency Relief Administration">\n        Federal Emergency Relief Administration\n       </a>\n       (FERA). In the autumn of 1934 the problem of transient veterans in Washington DC \xe2\x80\x9cthreatened \xe2\x80\xa6 to become acute and did become acute in January.\xe2\x80\x9d\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31">\n        <a href="#cite_note-31">\n         [31]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       Facilities in the capital were inadequate to handle the large numbers of veterans seeking jobs.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32">\n        <a href="#cite_note-32">\n         [32]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33">\n        <a href="#cite_note-33">\n         [33]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       President\n       <a href="/wiki/Franklin_D._Roosevelt" title="Franklin D. Roosevelt">\n        Franklin D. Roosevelt\n       </a>\n       met with Mr. Hopkins and\n       <a href="/wiki/Robert_Fechner" title="Robert Fechner">\n        Robert Fechner\n       </a>\n       , director of the\n       <a href="/wiki/Civilian_Conservation_Corps" title="Civilian Conservation Corps">\n        Civilian Conservation Corps\n       </a>\n       (CCC) to discuss solutions. He \xe2\x80\x9csuggested the Southern camp plan and approved the program worked out by Mr. Hopkins for their establishment and maintenance.\xe2\x80\x9d\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34">\n        <a href="#cite_note-34">\n         [34]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       The VA identified eligible veterans.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35">\n        <a href="#cite_note-35">\n         [35]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       FERA offered grants to the states for their construction projects if they would accept the veterans as laborers. The state Emergency Relief Administrations were responsible for the daily management of the camps.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36">\n        <a href="#cite_note-36">\n         [36]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       In practice the state ERAs were very much the creatures of FERA, to the extent of handpicking the administrators.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37">\n        <a href="#cite_note-37">\n         [37]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38">\n        <a href="#cite_note-38">\n         [38]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       That only two states participated was perhaps attributable to the then popular impression that the transient veterans were \xe2\x80\x9cdiseased\xe2\x80\x9d bums and hoboes.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39">\n        <a href="#cite_note-39">\n         [39]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       It was a characterization enthusiastically fed by the media. In August 1935 both Time Magazine\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40">\n        <a href="#cite_note-40">\n         [40]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       and the New York Times published sensational articles.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41">\n        <a href="#cite_note-41">\n         [41]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42">\n        <a href="#cite_note-42">\n         [42]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       On Aug. 15, 1935, Hopkins announced the termination of the veterans work program and closure of all the camps.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43">\n        <a href="#cite_note-43">\n         [43]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       On August 26 and 27, 1935, one of the veterans, Albert C. Keith, wrote letters to both the President and\n       <a href="/wiki/Eleanor_Roosevelt" title="Eleanor Roosevelt">\n        Eleanor Roosevelt\n       </a>\n       urging that the camps not be closed. Keith was editor of the weekly camp paper, the Key Veteran News. He was emphatic that the veterans were being defamed and that their work program was actually a success story, rehabilitating many veterans for return to civilian life. The News published occasional reports from Camp #2, Mullet Key,\n       <a href="/wiki/St._Petersburg,_Florida" title="St. Petersburg, Florida">\n        St. Petersburg, Florida\n       </a>\n       , at the entrance to Tampa Bay. This was the "colored" veterans camp; the Keys camps were white only. In early August the colored veterans were transferred to the new Camp #8 in\n       <a href="/wiki/Gainesville,_Florida" title="Gainesville, Florida">\n        Gainesville, Florida\n       </a>\n       .\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44">\n        <a href="#cite_note-44">\n         [44]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <h4>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Rescue">\n        Rescue\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=1935_Labor_Day_hurricane&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Rescue">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h4>\n      <p>\n       Improved weather conditions on Wednesday, September 4, permitted the evacuation of survivors to begin. Participating in the rescue were\n      </p>\n      <ul>\n       <li>\n        <a href="/wiki/American_Red_Cross" title="American Red Cross">\n         American Red Cross\n        </a>\n        ,\n       </li>\n       <li>\n        <a href="/wiki/Florida_National_Guard" title="Florida National Guard">\n         Florida National Guard\n        </a>\n        ,\n       </li>\n       <li>\n        <a href="/wiki/Federal_Emergency_Relief_Administration" title="Federal Emergency Relief Administration">\n         Federal Emergency Relief Administration\n        </a>\n        (FERA),\n       </li>\n       <li>\n        <a href="/wiki/Works_Progress_Administration" title="Works Progress Administration">\n         Works Progress Administration\n        </a>\n        (WPA),\n       </li>\n       <li>\n        <a href="/wiki/Civilian_Conservation_Corps" title="Civilian Conservation Corps">\n         Civilian Conservation Corps\n        </a>\n        (CCC),\n       </li>\n       <li>\n        <a href="/wiki/United_States_Coast_Guard" title="United States Coast Guard">\n         United States Coast Guard\n        </a>\n        ,\n       </li>\n       <li>\n        <a href="/wiki/American_Legion" title="American Legion">\n         American Legion\n        </a>\n        ,\n        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45">\n         <a href="#cite_note-45">\n          [45]\n         </a>\n        </sup>\n       </li>\n       <li>\n        <a href="/wiki/Veterans_of_Foreign_Wars" title="Veterans of Foreign Wars">\n         Veterans of Foreign Wars\n        </a>\n        ,\n       </li>\n       <li>\n        Dade County Undertakers Association,\n       </li>\n       <li>\n        Dade County Medical Society,\n       </li>\n       <li>\n        city and county officials,\n       </li>\n       <li>\n        and numerous individuals (including\n        <a href="/wiki/Ernest_Hemingway" title="Ernest Hemingway">\n         Ernest Hemingway\n        </a>\n        ). Headquarters of the operation was the near shore of Snake Creek on Plantation Key. With the bridge over the creek washed out this was the farthest point south on the highway. On September 5 at a meeting of all public and private agencies involved Governor\n        <a href="/wiki/David_Sholtz" title="David Sholtz">\n         David Sholtz\n        </a>\n        placed the sheriffs of Monroe and Dade Counties in overall control.\n        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46">\n         <a href="#cite_note-46">\n          [46]\n         </a>\n        </sup>\n       </li>\n      </ul>\n      <p>\n       On the evening of September 4, 1935, Brigadier General\n       <a href="/wiki/Frank_T._Hines" title="Frank T. Hines">\n        Frank T. Hines\n       </a>\n       , VA Administrator, received a phone call from\n       <a href="/wiki/Hyde_Park,_New_York" title="Hyde Park, New York">\n        Hyde Park, New York\n       </a>\n       . It was\n       <a href="/wiki/Stephen_Early" title="Stephen Early">\n        Stephen Early\n       </a>\n       , the President\xe2\x80\x99s press secretary. He had orders from the President who was very distressed by the news from Florida. The VA was to: 1. Cooperate with FERA in seeing that everything possible be done for those injured in the hurricane; 2. See that the bodies were properly cared for shipment home to relatives, and that those bodies for which shipment home was not requested be sent to\n       <a href="/wiki/Arlington_National_Cemetery" title="Arlington National Cemetery">\n        Arlington National Cemetery\n       </a>\n       ; and, 3. Conduct a very careful joint investigation with Mr. Hopkins\xe2\x80\x99 organization, to determine whether there was any fault that would lie against anyone in the Administration. Hines\xe2\x80\x99 representative in Florida was to take full charge of the situation and all other organizations and agencies were ordered to cooperate.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47">\n        <a href="#cite_note-47">\n         [47]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       The President\xe2\x80\x99s first order was straight forward and promptly executed. 124 injured veterans were treated in Miami area hospitals; 9 of these died. 56 were later transferred to VA medical facilities.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48">\n        <a href="#cite_note-48">\n         [48]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       Uninjured veterans were removed to Camp Foster in Jacksonville and evaluated for transfer to the CCC; those declining transfer or deemed unemployable were paid off and given tickets home.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49">\n        <a href="#cite_note-49">\n         [49]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       All of the FERA transient camps were closed in November 1935.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50">\n        <a href="#cite_note-50">\n         [50]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       In December 1935 FERA itself was absorbed within the new WPA, also directed by Hopkins.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51">\n        <a href="#cite_note-51">\n         [51]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <h4>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Recovery">\n        Recovery\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=1935_Labor_Day_hurricane&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Recovery">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h4>\n      <p>\n       The second and third orders, however, were almost immediately compromised. At a news conference on September 5, Hopkins asserted that there was no negligence traceable to FERA in the failed evacuation of the camps as the Weather Bureau advisories had given insufficient warning. He also dispatched his assistant,\n       <a href="/wiki/Aubrey_Willis_Williams" title="Aubrey Willis Williams">\n        Aubrey Willis Williams\n       </a>\n       , to Florida to coordinate FERA efforts and to investigate the deaths.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52">\n        <a href="#cite_note-52">\n         [52]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       Williams and Hines\xe2\x80\x99 assistant, Colonel George E. Ijams, both arrived in Miami on September 6. Ijams concentrated on the dead, their collection, identification and proper disposition.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53">\n        <a href="#cite_note-53">\n         [53]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       This was to prove exceptionally difficult. Bodies were scattered throughout the Keys and their rapid decomposition created ghastly conditions. State and local health officials demanded a ban on all movement of bodies and their immediate burial or cremation in place; the next day Governor Sholtz so ordered.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54">\n        <a href="#cite_note-54">\n         [54]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       This was reluctantly agreed to by Hines with the understanding that those buried would be later disinterred and shipped home or to Arlington when permitted by the State health authorities.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55">\n        <a href="#cite_note-55">\n         [55]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       The cremations began on Saturday, September 7; 38 bodies were burned together at the collection point beside Snake Creek. Over the next week 136 bodies were cremated on Upper Matecumbe Key at 12 different locations. On Lower Matecumbe Key 82 were burned at 20 sites. On numerous small keys in Florida Bay bodies were either burned or buried where found. This effort continued into November. 123 bodies had been transported to Miami before the embargo. These were processed at a temporary morgue staffed by fingerprint experts and 8 volunteer undertakers under tents at Woodlawn Park Cemetery (3260 SW 8th St, Miami). The intention was to identify the remains and prepare them for burial or further shipment. With the embargo in force immediate burial of all the bodies at Woodlawn was mandatory. FERA purchased a plot in Section 2A. The VA coordinated the ceremony with full military honors on September 8.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56">\n        <a href="#cite_note-56">\n         [56]\n        </a>\n        </div>\n       </div>\n      </div>\n      <p>\n       The Florida Emergency Relief Administration reported that as of November 19, 1935, the total of dead stood at 423: 259 veterans and 164 civilians. These numbers are reflected on the Veterans Storm Relief Map (which see). By March 1, 1936, 62 additional bodies had been recovered bringing the total to 485: 257 veterans and 228 civilians.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62">\n        <a href="#cite_note-62">\n         [62]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       The discrepancy in veterans\xe2\x80\x99 deaths resulted from the difficulty in identifying bodies, particularly those found months after the hurricane, and a question of definition; whether to count just those on the camp payrolls or to include others, not enrolled, who happened to be veterans.\n       <br/>\n       The\n       <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_Veterans_Affairs" title="United States Department of Veterans Affairs">\n        Veterans\xe2\x80\x99 Affairs Administration\n       </a>\n       (VA) compiled its own list of veterans\xe2\x80\x99 deaths:\n       <br/>\n       121 Dead-positive identification,\n       <br/>\n       90 Missing, and 45 Dead-identification tentative, totaling 256.\n       <br/>\n       Five others are named in a footnote. One proved to be a misidentification of a previously listed veteran; two were state employees working at the camps; and two were unaffiliated veterans caught in the storm. This gives a total for all veterans of 260.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63">\n        <a href="#cite_note-63">\n         [63]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       Adding this to the Florida Emergency Relief Administration number for civilians gives a total of 488 for all deaths. 12 of the dead were listed as "colored".\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64">\n        <a href="#cite_note-64">\n         [64]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       <a href="/wiki/Ernest_Hemingway" title="Ernest Hemingway">\n        Ernest Hemingway\n       </a>\n       visited the veteran\'s camp by boat after weathering the hurricane at his home in Key West; he wrote about the devastation in a critical article titled "Who Killed the Vets?" for\n       <i>\n        <a href="/wiki/The_New_Masses" title="The New Masses">\n         The New Masses\n        </a>\n       </i>\n       magazine.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-65">\n        <a href="#cite_note-65">\n         [65]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       Hemingway implied that the\n       <a href="/wiki/Federal_Emergency_Relief_Administration" title="Federal Emergency Relief Administration">\n        FERA\n       </a>\n       workers and families, who were unfamiliar with the risks of Florida hurricane season, were unwitting victims of a system that appeared to lack concern for their welfare. From\n       <a href="/wiki/Ernest_Hemingway" title="Ernest Hemingway">\n        Ernest Hemingway\'s\n       </a>\n       statement on the tragedy:\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-66">\n        <a href="#cite_note-66">\n         [66]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <blockquote>\n       <p>\n        <i>\n         ...wealthy people, yachtsmen, fishermen such as President Hoover and President Roosevelt, do not come to the Florida Keys in hurricane months.... There is a known danger to property. But veterans, especially the bonus-marching variety of veterans, are not property. They are only human beings; unsuccessful human beings, and all they have to lose is their lives. They are doing coolie labor for a top wage of $45 a month and they have been put down on the Florida Keys where they can\'t make trouble. It is hurricane months, sure, but if anything comes up, you can always evacuate them, can\'t you?...It is not necessary to go into the deaths of the civilians and their families since they were on the Keys of their own free will; they made their living there, had property and knew the hazards involved. But the veterans had been sent there; they had no opportunity to leave, nor any protection against hurricanes; and they never had a chance for their lives. Who sent nearly a thousand war veterans, many of them husky, hard-working and simply out of luck, but many of them close to the border of pathological cases, to live in frame shacks on the Florida Keys in hurricane months?\n        </i>\n       </p>\n      </blockquote>\n      <p>\n       In the same issue of\n       <i>\n        The New Masses\n       </i>\n       appeared an editorial charging criminal negligence and a cartoon by Russell T. Limbach, captioned,\n       <i>\n        An Act of God\n       </i>\n       , depicting burning corpses.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-67">\n        <a href="#cite_note-67">\n         [67]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       A\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Washington_Post" title="Washington Post">\n        Washington Post\n       </a>\n       editorial on Sept. 5, titled\n       <i>\n        Ruin in the Veterans\' Camps\n       </i>\n       , stated the widely held opinion that the \xe2\x80\xb3camps were havens of rest designed to keep Bonus Marchers away from Washington... Most of these veterans are drifters, psychopathic cases or habitual troublemakers... Those who are nor physically or mentally handicapped have no claim whatsoever to special rewards in return for bonus agitation."\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-68">\n        <a href="#cite_note-68">\n         [68]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <h4>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Investigation">\n        Investigation\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=1935_Labor_Day_hurricane&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Investigation">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h4>\n      <p>\n       Williams meanwhile rushed to complete the investigation. He finished on Sunday, September 8, the day an elaborate memorial service and mass burial of hurricane victims (both coordinated by Ijams) were held in Miami.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-69">\n        <a href="#cite_note-69">\n         [69]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       Ijams who had been too busy to participate in the investigation and had not questioned any of the 12 witnesses interrogated by Williams, nonetheless signed the 15 page report to the President.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-70">\n        <a href="#cite_note-70">\n         [70]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       That night Williams released it to the Miami press in a radio broadcast immediately following the memorial ceremony. Ijams considered the timing unfortunate after receiving several critical telephone messages.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-71">\n        <a href="#cite_note-71">\n         [71]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       The report exonerated everyone involved and concluded: \xe2\x80\x9cTo our mind the catastrophe must be characterized as an\n       <a href="/wiki/Act_of_God" title="Act of God">\n        act of God\n       </a>\n       and was by its very nature beyond the power of man or instruments at his disposal to foresee sufficiently far enough in advance to permit the taking of adequate precautions capable of preventing the death and desolation which occurred.\xe2\x80\x9d\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-72">\n        <a href="#cite_note-72">\n         [72]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       Early also found the publicity around the report "unfortunate". In a telegram to his colleague, assistant Presidential secretary\n       <a href="/wiki/Marvin_H._McIntyre" title="Marvin H. McIntyre">\n        Marvin H. McIntyre\n       </a>\n       , Early wrote that he had authorized Hines to proceed with a "complete and exhaustive" joint investigation with Hopkins. Significantly Hines was to "instruct his investigator that under no circumstances will any statement be made to the Press until final report has been submitted to the President."\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-73">\n        <a href="#cite_note-73">\n         [73]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       Hopkins gave similar instructions to his investigator. McIntyre also was involved in damage control. On Sept. 10, 1935, the Greater Miami Ministerial Association wrote the President an angry letter labeling the report a "\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Whitewash_(censorship)" title="Whitewash (censorship)">\n        whitewash\n       </a>\n       ". McIntyre forwarded it to FERA for a response. Williams returned a draft for the President\'s signature on Sept. 25th insisting the report was only preliminary and that the "final and detailed report ... will be both thorough and searching".\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-74">\n        <a href="#cite_note-74">\n         [74]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       Williams assigned\n       <a href="/wiki/John_Abt" title="John Abt">\n        John Abt\n       </a>\n       , assistant general counsel for FERA, to complete the investigation. On Sept. 11, 1935, Hines directed the skeptical and meticulous David W. Kennamer to investigate the disaster. There was immediate friction between them; Kennamer believed Abt did not have an open mind and Abt felt further investigation was unnecessary.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-75">\n        <a href="#cite_note-75">\n         [75]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       Working with Harry W. Farmer, another VA investigator, Kennamer completed his 2 volume report on October 30, 1935. Farmer added a third volume concerning the identification of the veterans. Kennamer\'s report included 2,168 pages of exhibits,\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-76">\n        <a href="#cite_note-76">\n         [76]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       118 pages of findings,\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-77">\n        <a href="#cite_note-77">\n         [77]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       and a 19 page general comment.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-78">\n        <a href="#cite_note-78">\n         [78]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       His findings differed substantially from those of Williams, citing three officials of the Florida Emergency Relief Administration as negligent (Administrator Conrad Van Hyning, Asst. Administrator Fred Ghent and Camp Superintendent Ray Sheldon). In a response to Abt\'s draft report to the President,\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-79">\n        <a href="#cite_note-79">\n         [79]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       Ijams sided with Kennamer.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-80">\n        <a href="#cite_note-80">\n         [80]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       Hines and Hopkins never agreed on a final report, and Kennamer\xe2\x80\x99s findings were suppressed. They remained so for decades.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-81">\n        <a href="#cite_note-81">\n         [81]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       One might speculate that Hines wished to avoid a public quarrel with Hopkins, who had enjoyed Roosevelt\xe2\x80\x99s patronage since his term as New York Governor. Hines was a holdover from the Hoover administration. Such an internal dispute would embarrass the Roosevelt administration at the time a vote on the\n       <a href="/wiki/Adjusted_Compensation_Payment_Act" title="Adjusted Compensation Payment Act">\n        Adjusted Compensation Payment Act\n       </a>\n       (\xe2\x80\x9cBonus Bill\xe2\x80\x9d) was upcoming (it passed on Jan. 27, 1936, over the President\xe2\x80\x99s veto).\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-82">\n        <a href="#cite_note-82">\n         [82]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       Also, 1936 was a presidential election year. Kennamer did appear at the House hearings in April 1936, along with Hines, Ijams, Williams and the 3 officials he pilloried in his report. He was not questioned about his controversial findings nor did he volunteer his opinions.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-83">\n        <a href="#cite_note-83">\n         [83]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       On November 1, 1935, the American Legion completed its own report on the hurricane. The Legion\'s National Commander, Ray Murphy, mailed a copy to President Roosevelt. It concluded that:\n      </p>\n      <blockquote>\n       <p>\n        ...the blame for the loss of life can be placed on "Inefficiency, Indifference, and Ignorance." Inefficiency in the setup of the camps. Indifference of someone in charge as to the safety of the men. Ignorance of the real danger from a tropical hurricane. And these "I\'s" can be added together and they spell "Murder at Matacombe" [sic].\n       </p>\n       <p>\n        [The] committee early in its investigation noticed a tendency on the part of some to reflect on the character of the men who were veterans in the camps. Several parties referred to them as "bums," "drunkards," "crazy men," "riff-raff" and the like. They seem to think that "they got what was coming to them."\n       </p>\n       <p>\n        How anyone could arrive at such a conclusion is impossible for us to determine.\n       </p>\n       <p>\n        If these men were "bums," "drunkards," "crazy men" etc. then it was all the more necessary that every precaution be taken to protect them. If they fell into this category they were subnormal men and should have been treated as such. If they were incapable of caring for themselves then the government should have placed them in hospitals and not have sent them to a wilderness in the high-seas on a so called "rehabilitation program."\n       </p>\n       Others testified that the men were well-behaved and that a great majority of them would have preferred to have been placed under military discipline in the camps. But these observations are of no real value except to show that some people are trying to "cover up" the real guilt of responsible parties.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-84">\n        <a href="#cite_note-84">\n         [84]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </blockquote>\n      <p>\n       Williams prepared a response for the President stating: "A final report, based upon the facts obtained in this investigation [by the VA and FERA], will be submitted to me shortly. At that time I shall transmit a copy of the report to you for your information and consideration."\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-85">\n        <a href="#cite_note-85">\n         [85]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <h3>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Memorials">\n        Memorials\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=1935_Labor_Day_hurricane&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Memorials">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h3>\n      <h4>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Islamorada">\n        Islamorada\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=1935_Labor_Day_hurricane&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Islamorada">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h4>\n      <p>\n       Standing just east of\n       <a href="/wiki/U.S._Route_1_in_Florida" title="U.S. Route 1 in Florida">\n        U.S. 1\n       </a>\n       at mile marker 82 in\n       <a href="/wiki/Islamorada,_Florida" title="Islamorada, Florida">\n        Islamorada\n       </a>\n       , near where Islamorada\'s post office stood, is a simple monument\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-86">\n        <a href="#cite_note-86">\n         [86]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       designed by the Florida Division of the\n       <a href="/wiki/Federal_Art_Project" title="Federal Art Project">\n        Federal Art Project\n       </a>\n       and constructed using Keys\n       <a href="/wiki/Limestone" title="Limestone">\n        limestone\n       </a>\n       ("\n       <a href="/wiki/Keystone_(limestone)" title="Keystone (limestone)">\n        keystone\n       </a>\n       ") by the\n       <a href="/wiki/Works_Progress_Administration" title="Works Progress Administration">\n        Works Progress Administration\n       </a>\n       . It was unveiled on November 14, 1937, with several hundred people attending.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-flickr.com_87-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-flickr.com-87">\n         [87]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       Hines had been invited to speak but he declined.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-88">\n        <a href="#cite_note-88">\n         [88]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       His attitude to the project was unenthusiastic. In a letter to Williams on June 24, 1937, regarding what to do with the many skeletons of veterans recently discovered in the Keys, he wrote: \xe2\x80\xb3It occurs to me that if a large memorial is erected adjacent to this highway at the place of the disaster it will be observed by all persons using the highway and will serve as a constant reminder of the unfortunate catastrophe which occurred.\xe2\x80\xb3\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-89">\n        <a href="#cite_note-89">\n         [89]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       Hines recommended the remains be buried at Woodlawn. A\n       <a href="/wiki/Frieze" title="Frieze">\n        frieze\n       </a>\n       depicts\n       <a href="/wiki/Arecaceae" title="Arecaceae">\n        palm\n       </a>\n       trees amid curling waves, fronds bent in the wind. In front of the sculpture a\n       <a href="/wiki/Ceramic" title="Ceramic">\n        ceramic\n       </a>\n       -\n       <a href="/wiki/Tile" title="Tile">\n        tile\n       </a>\n       <a href="/wiki/Mural" title="Mural">\n        mural\n       </a>\n       of the Keys covers a stone\n       <a href="/wiki/Crypt" title="Crypt">\n        crypt\n       </a>\n       , which holds victims\' ashes from the makeshift funeral pyres, commingled with the skeletons.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-flickr.com_87-1">\n        <a href="#cite_note-flickr.com-87">\n         [87]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       Although this is a grave site not a single name appears anywhere on the monument. This is not a requirement for the estimated 228 civilian dead, 55 of whom were buried where found or in various cemeteries.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-90">\n        <a href="#cite_note-90">\n         [90]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       A memorial with identifying information is a statutory entitlement for the veterans.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-91">\n        <a href="#cite_note-91">\n         [91]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       170 were cremated or never identified. The VA has chosen not to memorialize them, despite current Federal law and President Roosevelt\'s order that Hines provide a burial with full military honors for every veteran not claimed by his family.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       On February 18, 1939, President Roosevelt had the opportunity to see the memorial for himself. His motorcade passed through Islamorada on the way to Key West where he was to begin a visit with the battle fleet on maneuvers. But there is no record that he made a stop; no mention in newspaper reports,\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-92">\n        <a href="#cite_note-92">\n         [92]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       the President\xe2\x80\x99s daily calendar,\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-93">\n        <a href="#cite_note-93">\n         [93]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       or in a press conference\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-94">\n        <a href="#cite_note-94">\n         [94]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       held during a lunch stop at the CCC camp on West Summerland Key (renamed\n       <a href="/wiki/Scout_Key" title="Scout Key">\n        Scout Key\n       </a>\n       in 2010). This omission is puzzling in that he had sent a telegram to the dedication in which he expressed \xe2\x80\x9cheartfelt sympathy\xe2\x80\x9d and said \xe2\x80\x9cthe disaster which made desolate the hearts of so many of our people brought a personal sorrow to me because some years ago I knew many residents of the keys.\xe2\x80\x9d\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-flickr.com_87-2">\n        <a href="#cite_note-flickr.com-87">\n         [87]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       The welcoming committee included Key West Mayor Willard M. Albury, and other local officials.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-95">\n        <a href="#cite_note-95">\n         [95]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <div class="thumb FL, on site of mass grave\n        </div>\n       </div>\n      </div>\n      <p>\n       The memorial was added to the U.S.\n       <a href="/wiki/National_Register_of_Historic_Places" title="National Register of Historic Places">\n        National Register of Historic Places\n       </a>\n       on March 16, 1995.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-96">\n        <a href="#cite_note-96">\n         [96]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       A Heritage Monument Trail plaque mounted on a coral boulder before the memorial reads:\n      </p>\n      <blockquote>\n       <p>\n        The Florida Keys Memorial, known locally as the "Hurricane Monument," was built to honor hundreds of American veterans and local citizens who perished in the "Great Hurricane" on Labor Day, September 2, 1935. Islamorada sustained winds of 200 miles per hour and a barometer reading of 26.35 inches for many hours on that fateful holiday; most local buildings and the Florida East Coast Railway were destroyed by what remains the most savage hurricane on record. Hundreds of World War I veterans who had been camped in the Matecumbe area while working on the construction of U.S. Highway One for the Works Progress Administration (WPA) were killed. In 1937 the cremated remains of approximately 300 people were placed within the tiled crypt in front of the monument. The monument is composed of native keystone, and its striking frieze depicts coconut palm trees bending before the force of hurricane winds while the waters from an angry sea lap at the bottom of their trunks. Monument construction was funded by the WPA and regional veterans\' associations. Over the years the Hurricane Monument has been cared for by local veterans, hurricane survivors, and descendants of the victims.\n        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-97">\n         <a href="#cite_note-97">\n          [97]\n         </a>\n        </sup>\n       </p>\n      </blockquote>\n      <p>\n       Local residents hold ceremonies at the monument every year on Labor Day (on the Monday holiday) and on Memorial Day to honor the veterans and the civilians who died in the hurricane.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-98">\n        <a href="#cite_note-98">\n         [98]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <h4>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Woodlawn_Park_Cemetery">\n        Woodlawn Park Cemetery\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=1935_Labor_Day_hurricane&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Woodlawn Park Cemetery">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h4>\n      <p>\n       On January 31, 1936, Harvey W. Seeds Post No. 29, American Legion, Miami, Florida, petitioned FERA for the deed to the Woodlawn plot.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-99">\n        <a href="#cite_note-99">\n         [99]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       The Legion would use the empty grave sites for the burial of indigent veterans and accept responsibility for care of the plot. After some initial confusion as to the actual owner,\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-100">\n        <a href="#cite_note-100">\n         [100]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       the State of Florida approved the title transfer. A monument was placed on the plot, inscribed:\n       <i>\n        Erected by Harvey W. Seeds Post No. 29, The American Legion, in Memory of Our Comrades Who Lost Their Lives on the Florida Keys during the 1935 Hurricane, Lest We Forget\n       </i>\n       .\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-101">\n        <a href="#cite_note-101">\n         [101]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       As with the Islamorada memorial no names appear. Nor were the individual grave sites marked. Again the VA chose not to obey the President\xe2\x80\x99s order, this time to rebury the unclaimed bodies at Arlington. Two bodies were, however, exhumed by the families: Brady C. Lewis on Nov. 12, 1936;\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-102">\n        <a href="#cite_note-102">\n         [102]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       and, Thomas K. Moore\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-103">\n        <a href="#cite_note-103">\n         [103]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       on Jan. 20, 1937 (reburied at Arlington). Five more received grave markers at Woodlawn, leaving 74 unmarked graves of identified veterans. Efforts are ongoing to mark all these graves.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-104">\n        <a href="#cite_note-104">\n         [104]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       One other veteran killed in the storm rests at Arlington, Daniel C. Main.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-105">\n        <a href="#cite_note-105">\n         [105]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       His was a special case, the only veteran who died in the camps who was neither cremated in the Keys nor buried at Woodlawn. Main was the camp medical director and was killed in the collapse of the small hospital at Camp #1. His body was quickly recovered by survivors and shipped to his family before the embargo.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-106">\n        <a href="#cite_note-106">\n         [106]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-107">\n        <a href="#cite_note-107">\n         [107]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <h4>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Veterans_Key">\n        Veterans Key\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=1935_Labor_Day_hurricane&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Veterans Key">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h4>\n      <p>\n       On February 27, 2006, the\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._Board_on_Geographic_Names" title="U.S. Board on Geographic Names">\n        U.S. Board on Geographic Names\n       </a>\n       approved a proposal by\n       <a class="external text" href="http://www.keyshistory.org/index.html" rel="nofollow">\n        Jerry Wilkinson\n       </a>\n       , President, Historical Preservation Society of the Upper Keys, to name a small island off the southern tip of Lower Matecumbe Key for the veterans who died in the hurricane. It is near where Camp #3 was located.\n       <i>\n        Veterans Key\n       </i>\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-108">\n        <a href="#cite_note-108">\n         [108]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       and several concrete pilings are all that remain of the 1935 bridge construction project.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-109">\n        <a href="#cite_note-109">\n         [109]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <h4>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Department_of_Veterans_Affairs_Actions">\n        Department of Veterans Affairs Actions\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=1935_Labor_Day_hurricane&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Department of Veterans Affairs Actions">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h4>\n      <p>\n       Government furnished grave markers are provided for eligible veterans buried in National Cemeteries, State veterans cemeteries and private cemeteries. Under VA regulations the applicant for a marker may only be the veteran\xe2\x80\x99s\n       <a href="/wiki/Next_of_kin" title="Next of kin">\n        next of kin\n       </a>\n       ; so, too, for memorials when the body is not available for burial.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-110">\n        <a href="#cite_note-110">\n         [110]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       Prior to a 2009 revision, not enforced until 2012, any person with knowledge of the veteran could apply. The revision prompted objections from groups and volunteers working to mark the many unmarked veterans\xe2\x80\x99 graves, mostly from the Civil War era. They argued that the next-of-kin (if any) was often impossible to locate and that the very existence of an unmarked grave was evidence of the family\xe2\x80\x99s indifference.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-111">\n        <a href="#cite_note-111">\n         [111]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       Two bills were introduced in Congress, H. R. 2018 and S. 2700 which would have again allowed unrelated applicants. Both bills died in committee. On October 1, 2014, the VA proposed a rule change which would include in the categories of applicants unrelated individuals.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-112">\n        <a href="#cite_note-112">\n         [112]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <h2>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">\n        See also\n       </span>\n       <span In routine operations locomotives were reversed using the "\n          <a href="/wiki/Wye_(rail)" title="Wye (rail)">\n           wye (rail)\n          </a>\n          " junctions at Homestead, Marathon or Key West. In this case it was decided to use the Homestead way and run the locomotive backward to Camp #3 on Lower Matecumbe, and then, using a siding, move it to the other end of the train facing forward for the return trip. Using the Marathon wye would have allowed running the engine forward on both legs, but would have added 45.6 miles to the route, much of which was over open water. As it happened the hurricane\'s eye passed directly over the Long Key crossings.
