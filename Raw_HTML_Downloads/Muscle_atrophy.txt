title="d:Q1557686">\n           edit on Wikidata\n          </a>\n          ]\n         </div>\n        </td>\n       </tr>\n      </table>\n      <p>\n       <b>\n        Muscle atrophy\n       </b>\n       is defined as a decrease in the mass of the\n       <a href="/wiki/Muscle" title="Muscle">\n        muscle\n       </a>\n       ; it can be a partial or complete wasting away of muscle, and is most commonly experienced when persons suffer temporary disabling circumstances such as being restricted in movement and/or confined to bed as when hospitalized. When a muscle atrophies, this leads to\n       <a href="/wiki/Muscle_weakness" title="Muscle weakness">\n        muscle weakness\n       </a>\n       , since the ability to exert force is related to mass. Modern medicine\'s understanding of the quick onset of muscle atrophy is a major factor behind the practice of getting hospitalized patients out of bed and moving about as active as possible as soon as is feasible, despite sutures, wounds, broken bones and pain.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       Muscle atrophy results from a co-morbidity of several common diseases, including\n       <a href="/wiki/Cancer" title="Cancer">\n        cancer\n       </a>\n       ,\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/AIDS" title="AIDS">\n        AIDS\n       </a>\n       ,\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Congestive_heart_failure" title="Congestive heart failure">\n        congestive heart failure\n       </a>\n       ,\n       <a href="/wiki/Chronic_obstructive_pulmonary_disease" title="Chronic obstructive pulmonary disease">\n        COPD\n       </a>\n       (chronic obstructive pulmonary disease),\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Renal_failure" title="Renal failure">\n        renal failure\n       </a>\n       , and severe\n       <a href="/wiki/Burn" title="Burn">\n        burns\n       </a>\n       ; patients who have "\n       <a href="/wiki/Cachexia" title="Cachexia">\n        cachexia\n       </a>\n       " in these disease settings have a poor\n       <a href="/wiki/Prognosis" title="Prognosis">\n        prognosis\n       </a>\n       . Moreover, starvation eventually leads to muscle atrophy.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       Disuse of the muscles, such as when muscle tissue is immobilized for even a few days of unuse \xe2\x80\x93 when the patient has a primary injury such as an immobilized broken\n       <a href="/wiki/Bone" title="Bone">\n        bone\n       </a>\n       (set in a cast or immobilized in traction), for example \xe2\x80\x93 will also lead rapidly to disuse atrophy. Minimizing such occurrences as soon as possible is a primary mission of\n       <a href="/wiki/Occupational_therapist" title="Occupational therapist">\n        occupational\n       </a>\n       and\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Physical_therapist" title="Physical therapist">\n        physical therapists\n       </a>\n       employed within hospitals working in co-ordination with orthopedic surgeons.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       Neurogenic atrophy, which has a similar effect, is muscle atrophy resulting from damage to the nerve which stimulates the muscle, causing a shriveling around otherwise healthy limbs. Also, time in a circa\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Zero_g" title="Zero g">\n        zero g\n       </a>\n       environment without exercise\n       <a href="/wiki/Effect_of_spaceflight_on_the_human_body#Loss_of_bone_and_muscle_mass" title="Effect of spaceflight on the human body">\n        will lead to atrophy\n       </a>\n       . This is partially due to the smaller amount of exertion needed to move about, and the fact that muscles are not used to maintain posture. In a similar effect, patients with a broken leg joint undergoing as little as three weeks of traction can lose enough back and buttocks muscle mass and strength as to have difficulty sitting without assistance, and experience pain, stress and burning even after a very short ten-minute exposure, when such positioning is contrived during recovery.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n      </p>\n      <div class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Muscle_atrophy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Signs and symptoms">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h2>\n      <p>\n       Muscular atrophy decreases qualities of life as the sufferer becomes unable to perform certain tasks or worsen the risks of accidents while performing those (like walking). Muscular atrophy increases the risks of falling in conditions such as\n       <a href="/wiki/Inclusion_body_myositis" title="Inclusion body myositis">\n        inclusion body myositis\n       </a>\n       (IBM) . Muscular atrophy affects a high number of the elderly.\n      </p>\n      <h2>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Causes">\n        Causes\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Muscle_atrophy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Causes">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h2>\n      <p>\n       There are many diseases and conditions which cause a decrease in\n       <a href="/wiki/Muscle" title="Muscle">\n        muscle\n       </a>\n       mass, known as\n       <a href="/wiki/Atrophy" title="Atrophy">\n        atrophy\n       </a>\n       , including activity, as seen when a cast is put on a limb, or upon extended\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bedrest" title="Bedrest">\n        bedrest\n       </a>\n       (which can occur during a prolonged illness);\n       <a href="/wiki/Cachexia" title="Cachexia">\n        cachexia\n       </a>\n       - which is a syndrome that is a co-morbidity of\n       <a href="/wiki/Cancer" title="Cancer">\n        cancer\n       </a>\n       and\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Congestive_heart_failure" title="Congestive heart failure">\n        congestive heart failure\n       </a>\n       ;\n       <a href="/wiki/Chronic_obstructive_pulmonary_disease" title="Chronic obstructive pulmonary disease">\n        chronic obstructive pulmonary disease\n       </a>\n       ; burns, liver failure, etc., and the wasting\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dejerine-Sottas_syndrome" title="Dejerine-Sottas syndrome">\n        Dejerine-Sottas syndrome\n       </a>\n       (\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hereditary_motor_and_sensory_neuropathies" title="Hereditary motor and sensory neuropathies">\n        HMSN Type III\n       </a>\n       ).\n       <a href="/wiki/Glucocorticoid" title="Glucocorticoid">\n        Glucocorticoids\n       </a>\n       , a class of medications used to treat allergic and other inflammatory conditions can induce muscle atrophy by increasing break-down of muscle proteins.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid8049126_1-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-pmid8049126-1">\n         [1]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       Other syndromes or conditions which can induce skeletal muscle atrophy are liver disease, and starvation.\n      </p>\n      <h2>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Pathophysiology">\n        Pathophysiology\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a       </span>\n       </span>\n      </h2>\n      <div class="thumb tright">\n       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">\n        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:1025_Atrophy.png">\n         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="672" data-file-width="1135" height="130" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/61/1025_Atrophy.png/220px-1025_Atrophy.png"         </div>\n         Muscle mass is reduced as muscles atrophy with disuse.\n        </div>\n       </div>\n      </div>\n      <p>\n       Muscle atrophy occurs by a change in the normal balance between protein synthesis and protein degradation. During atrophy, there is a down-regulation of protein synthesis pathways, and an activation of protein degradation.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">\n        <a href="#cite_note-2">\n         [2]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       The particular protein degradation pathway which seems to be responsible for much of the muscle loss seen in a muscle undergoing atrophy is the\n       <a href="/wiki/Adenosine_triphosphate" title="Adenosine triphosphate">\n        ATP\n       </a>\n       -dependent\n       <a href="/wiki/Ubiquitin" title="Ubiquitin">\n        ubiquitin\n       </a>\n       /\n       <a href="/wiki/Proteasome" title="Proteasome">\n        proteasome\n       </a>\n       pathway. In this system, particular proteins are targeted for destruction by the ligation of at least four copies of a small peptide called\n       <a href="/wiki/Ubiquitin" title="Ubiquitin">\n        ubiquitin\n       </a>\n       onto a substrate protein. When a substrate is thus "poly-ubiquitinated", it is targeted for destruction by the\n       <a href="/wiki/Proteasome" title="Proteasome">\n        proteasome\n       </a>\n       . Particular enzymes in the ubiquitin/proteasome pathway allow ubiquitination to be directed to some proteins but not others - specificity is gained by coupling targeted proteins to an "E3\n       <a href="/wiki/Ubiquitin_ligase" title="Ubiquitin ligase">\n        ubiquitin ligase\n       </a>\n       ". Each E3\n       <a href="/wiki/Ubiquitin_ligase" title="Ubiquitin ligase">\n        ubiquitin ligase\n       </a>\n       binds to a particular set of substrates, causing their ubiquitination.\n      </p>\n      <h2>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Diagnosis">\n        Diagnosis\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Muscle_atrophy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Diagnosis">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h2>\n      <h3>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Quantification">\n        Quantification\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Muscle_atrophy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Quantification">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h3>\n      <p>\n       A\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/X-ray_computed_tomography" title="X-ray computed tomography">\n        CT scan\n       </a>\n       can distinguish muscle tissue from other tissues and thereby estimate the amount of muscle tissue in the body.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       Fast loss of muscle tissue (relative to normal turnover), can be approximated by the amount of\n       <a href="/wiki/Urea" title="Urea">\n        urea\n       </a>\n       in the urine. The equivalent nitrogen content (in\n       <a href="/wiki/Gram" title="Gram">\n        gram\n       </a>\n       ) of urea (in\n       <a href="/wiki/Mole_(unit)" title="Mole (unit)">\n        mmol\n       </a>\n       ) can be estimated by the conversion factor 0.028 g/mmol.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">\n        <a href="#cite_note-3">\n         [3]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       Furthermore, 1 gram of nitrogen is roughly equivalent to 6 gram of protein, and 1 gram of protein is roughly equivalent to 4 gram of muscle tissue. Subsequently, in situations such as muscle wasting, 1\xc2\xa0mmol of excessive urea in the urine (as measured by urine volume in litres multiplied by urea concentration in mmol/l) roughly corresponds to a muscle loss of 0.67 gram.\n      </p>\n      <h3>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Differential_diagnosis">\n        Differential diagnosis\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Muscle_atrophy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Differential diagnosis">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h3>\n      <p>\n       During aging, there is a gradual decrease in the ability to maintain skeletal muscle function and mass. This condition is called "\n       <a href="/wiki/Sarcopenia" title="Sarcopenia">\n        sarcopenia\n       </a>\n       ". The exact cause of sarcopenia is unknown, but it may be due to a combination of the gradual failure in the "\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Satellite_cells" title="Satellite cells">\n        satellite cells\n       </a>\n       " which help to regenerate skeletal muscle fibers, and a decrease in sensitivity to or the availability of critical secreted\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Growth_factors" title="Growth factors">\n        growth factors\n       </a>\n       which are necessary to maintain muscle mass and satellite cell survival.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       In addition to the simple loss of muscle mass (\n       <a href="/wiki/Atrophy" title="Atrophy">\n        atrophy\n       </a>\n       ), or the age-related decrease in muscle function (sarcopenia), there are other diseases which may be caused by structural defects in the muscle (\n       <a href="/wiki/Muscular_dystrophy" title="Muscular dystrophy">\n        muscular dystrophy\n       </a>\n       ), or by inflammatory reactions in the body directed against muscle (the myopathies).\n      </p>\n      <h2>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Treatment">\n        Treatment\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Muscle_atrophy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Treatment">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h2>\n      <p>\n       Muscle atrophy can be opposed by the signaling pathways which induce muscle\n       <a href="/wiki/Hypertrophy" title="Hypertrophy">\n        hypertrophy\n       </a>\n       , or an increase in muscle size. Therefore, one way in which not exercise induces an increase in muscle mass is to down regulate the pathways which have the opposite effect.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/%CE%92-hydroxy_%CE%B2-methylbutyrate" title="\xce\x92-hydroxy \xce\xb2-methylbutyrate">\n        \xce\xb2-hydroxy \xce\xb2-methylbutyrate\n       </a>\n       (HMB), a metabolite of\n       <a href="/wiki/Leucine" title="Leucine">\n        leucine\n       </a>\n       which is sold as a\n       <a href="/wiki/Dietary_supplement" title="Dietary supplement">\n        dietary supplement\n       </a>\n       , has demonstrated efficacy in preventing the loss of muscle mass in several muscle wasting conditions in humans, particularly\n       <a href="/wiki/Sarcopenia" title="Sarcopenia">\n        sarcopenia\n       </a>\n       .\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sarcopenia_July_2015_review_4-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-Sarcopenia_July_2015_review-4">\n         [4]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Review_April_2016_5-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-Review_April_2016-5">\n         [5]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Meta-analytic_systematic_review_September_2015_6-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-Meta-analytic_systematic_review_September_2015-6">\n         [6]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       A growing body of evidence supports the efficacy of HMB as a treatment for reducing, or even reversing, the loss of muscle mass,\n       <a href="/wiki/Muscle#Physiology" title="Muscle">\n        muscle function\n       </a>\n       , and\n       <a href="/wiki/Muscle#Strength" title="Muscle">\n        muscle strength\n       </a>\n       in hypercatabolic disease states such as cancer cachexia;\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Molecular_Aspects_of_Medicine_2016_review_7-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-Molecular_Aspects_of_Medicine_2016_review-7">\n         [7]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Skeletal_muscle_crosstalk_2016_review_8-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-Skeletal_muscle_crosstalk_2016_review-8">\n         [8]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nutrition_supplements_for_athletes_2014_review_9-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-Nutrition_supplements_for_athletes_2014_review-9">\n         [9]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       consequently, as of June 2016\n       <sup class="plainlinks noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;">\n        <a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Muscle_atrophy&amp;action=edit">\n         [update]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       it is recommended that both the prevention and treatment of sarcopenia and muscle wasting in general include supplementation with HMB, regular\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Resistance_exercise" title="Resistance exercise">\n        resistance exercise\n       </a>\n       , and consumption of a\n       <a href="/wiki/High-protein_diet" title="High-protein diet">\n        high-protein diet\n       </a>\n       .\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Molecular_Aspects_of_Medicine_2016_review_7-1">\n        <a href="#cite_note-Molecular_Aspects_of_Medicine_2016_review-7">\n         [7]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Skeletal_muscle_crosstalk_2016_review_8-1">\n        <a href="#cite_note-Skeletal_muscle_crosstalk_2016_review-8">\n         [8]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       Based upon a\n       <a href="/wiki/Meta-analysis" title="Meta-analysis">\n        meta-analysis\n       </a>\n       of seven\n       <a href="/wiki/Randomized_controlled_trial" title="Randomized controlled trial">\n        randomized controlled trials\n       </a>\n       that was published in 2015, HMB supplementation has efficacy as a treatment for preserving lean muscle mass in older adults.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">\n        <a href="#cite_note-10">\n         [note 1]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Meta-analytic_systematic_review_September_2015_6-3">\n        <a href="#cite_note-Meta-analytic_systematic_review_September_2015-6">\n         [6]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       More research is needed to determine the precise effects of HMB on muscle strength and function in this age group.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Meta-analytic_systematic_review_September_2015_6-4">\n        <a href="#cite_note-Meta-analytic_systematic_review_September_2015-6">\n         [6]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       Since the absence of muscle-building\n       <a href="/wiki/Amino_acid" title="Amino acid">\n        amino acids\n       </a>\n       can contribute to muscle wasting (that which is torn down must be rebuilt with like material), amino acid therapy may be helpful for regenerating damaged or atrophied muscle tissue. The branched-chain amino acids or\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Branched-chain_amino_acids" title="Branched-chain amino acids">\n        BCAAs\n       </a>\n       (\n       <a href="/wiki/Leucine" title="Leucine">\n        leucine\n       </a>\n       ,\n       <a href="/wiki/Isoleucine" title="Isoleucine">\n        isoleucine\n       </a>\n       , and\n       <a href="/wiki/Valine" title="Valine">\n        valine\n       </a>\n       ) are critical to this process, in addition to\n       <a href="/wiki/Lysine" title="Lysine">\n        lysine\n       </a>\n       and other amino acids.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       In severe cases of muscular atrophy, the use of an anabolic steroid such as methandrostenolone may be administered to patients as a potential treatment.\n       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">\n        [\n        <i>\n         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Identifying_reliable_sources_(medicine)" title="Wikipedia:Identifying reliable sources (medicine)">\n          <span title="Material near this tag needs references to reliable medical sources. (May 2016)">\n           medical citation needed\n          </span>\n         </a>\n        </i>\n        ]\n       </sup>\n       A novel class of drugs, called SARM (\n       <a href="/wiki/Selective_androgen_receptor_modulator" title="Selective androgen receptor modulator">\n        selective androgen receptor modulators\n       </a>\n       ) are being investigated with promising results. They would have fewer\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Side-effects" title="Side-effects">\n        side-effects\n       </a>\n       , while still promoting muscle and bone tissue growth and regeneration. These claims are, however, yet to be confirmed in larger clinical trials.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       One important rehabilitation tool for muscle atrophy includes the use of\n       <a href="/wiki/Functional_electrical_stimulation" title="Functional electrical stimulation">\n        functional electrical stimulation\n       </a>\n       to stimulate the muscles. This has seen a large amount of success in the rehabilitation of paraplegic patients.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">\n        <a href="#cite_note-11">\n         [10]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <h2>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Hibernation">\n        Hibernation\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Muscle_atrophy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Hibernation">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h2>\n      <p>\n       Inactivity and starvation in mammals lead to atrophy of skeletal muscle, accompanied by a smaller number and size of the muscle cells as well as lower protein content.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid17904252_12-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-pmid17904252-12">\n         [11]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       In humans, prolonged periods of immobilization, as in the cases of bed rest or astronauts flying in space, are known to result in muscle weakening and atrophy. Such consequences are also noted in small hibernating mammals like the golden-mantled ground squirrels and brown bats.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid17307375_13-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-pmid17307375-13">\n         [12]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       <a href="/wiki/Bear" title="Bear">\n        Bears\n       </a>\n       are an exception to this rule; species in the family Ursidae are famous for their ability to survive unfavorable environmental conditions of low temperatures and limited nutrition availability during winter by means of\n       <a href="/wiki/Hibernation" title="Hibernation">\n        hibernation\n       </a>\n       . During that time, bears go through a series of physiological, morphological and behavioral changes.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid14506303_14-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-pmid14506303-14">\n         [13]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       Their ability to maintain skeletal muscle number and size at time of disuse is of significant importance.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       During hibernation, bears spend four to seven months of inactivity and anorexia without undergoing muscle atrophy and protein loss.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid17307375_13-1">\n        <a href="#cite_note-pmid17307375-13">\n         [12]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       There are a few known factors that contribute to the sustaining of muscle tissue. During the summer period, bears take advantage of the nutrition availability and accumulate muscle protein. The protein balance at time of dormancy is also maintained by lower levels of protein breakdown during the winter time.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid17307375_13-2">\n        <a href="#cite_note-pmid17307375-13">\n         [12]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       At times of immobility, muscle wasting in bears is also suppressed by a proteolytic inhibitor that is released in circulation.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid17904252_12-1">\n        <a href="#cite_note-pmid17904252-12">\n         [11]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       Another factor that contributes to the sustaining of muscle strength in hibernating bears is the occurrence of periodic voluntary contractions and involuntary contractions from shivering during\n       <a href="/wiki/Torpor" title="Torpor">\n        torpor\n       </a>\n       .\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Harlow_15-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-Harlow-15">\n         [14]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       The three to four daily episodes of muscle activity are responsible for the maintenance of muscle strength and responsiveness in bears during hibernation.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Harlow_15-1">\n        <a href="#cite_note-Harlow-15">\n         [14]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <h2>\n       <span class="mw-headline"
