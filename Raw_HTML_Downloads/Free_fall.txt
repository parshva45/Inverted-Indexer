In the context of\n       <a href="/wiki/General_relativity" title="General relativity">\n        general relativity\n       </a>\n       , where gravitation is reduced to a space-time curvature, a body in free fall has no force acting on it.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       An object in the technical sense of the term "free fall" may not necessarily be falling down in the usual sense of the term. An object moving upwards would not normally be considered to be falling, but if it is subject to the force of gravity only, it is said to be in free fall. The moon is thus in free fall.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       In a uniform gravitational field, in the absence of any other forces, gravitation acts on each part of the body equally and this is\n       <a href="/wiki/Weightlessness" title="Weightlessness">\n        weightlessness\n       </a>\n       , a condition that also occurs when the gravitational field is zero (such as when far away from any gravitating body).\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       The term "free fall" is often used more loosely than in the strict sense defined above. Thus, falling through an\n       <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere" title="Atmosphere">\n        atmosphere\n       </a>\n       without a deployed\n       <a href="/wiki/Parachute" title="Parachute">\n        parachute\n       </a>\n       , or lifting device, is also often referred to as\n       <i>\n        free fall\n       </i>\n       . The\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aerodynamic" title="Aerodynamic">\n        aerodynamic\n       </a>\n       drag forces in such situations prevent them from producing full weightlessness, and thus a skydiver\'s "free fall" after reaching\n       <a href="/wiki/Terminal_velocity" title="Terminal velocity">\n        terminal velocity\n       </a>\n       produces the sensation of the body\'s weight being supported on a cushion of air.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n      </p>\n      <div class="toc" id="toc">\n       <div class="toctitle">\n        <h2>\n </span>\n         </a>\n        </li>\n       </ul>\n      </div>\n      <p>\n      </p>\n      <h2>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="History">\n        History\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Free_fall&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h2>\n      <p>\n       In the Western world prior to the 16th century, it was generally assumed that the speed of a falling body would be proportional to its weight\xe2\x80\x94that is, a 10\xc2\xa0kg object was expected to fall ten times faster than an otherwise identical 1\xc2\xa0kg object through the same medium. The ancient Greek philosopher\n       <a href="/wiki/Aristotle" title="Aristotle">\n        Aristotle\n       </a>\n       (384\xe2\x80\x93322 BC) discussed falling objects in\n       <i>\n        <a href="/wiki/Physics_(Aristotle)" title="Physics (Aristotle)">\n         Physics\n        </a>\n       </i>\n       (Book VII) which was perhaps the first book on mechanics (see\n       <a href="/wiki/Aristotelian_physics" title="Aristotelian physics">\n        Aristotelian physics\n       </a>\n       ).\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       The Italian scientist\n       <a href="/wiki/Galileo_Galilei" title="Galileo Galilei">\n        Galileo Galilei\n       </a>\n       (1564\xe2\x80\x931642) subjected the Aristotelian theories to experimentation and careful observation. He then combined the results of these experiments with mathematical analysis in an unprecedented way.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       According to a tale that may be apocryphal, in 1589\xe2\x80\x9392 Galileo\n       <a href="/wiki/Galileo%27s_Leaning_Tower_of_Pisa_experiment" title="Galileo\'s Leaning Tower of Pisa experiment">\n        dropped two objects of unequal mass from the Leaning Tower of Pisa\n       </a>\n       . Given the speed at which such a fall would occur, it is doubtful that Galileo could have extracted much information from this experiment. Most of his observations of falling bodies were really of bodies rolling down ramps. This slowed things down enough to the point where he was able to measure the time intervals with water clocks and his own pulse (stopwatches having not yet been invented). This he repeated "a full hundred times" until he had achieved "an accuracy such that the deviation between two observations never exceeded one-tenth of a pulse beat." In 1589\xe2\x80\x9392, Galileo wrote\n       <i>\n        <a href="/wiki/De_Motu_Antiquiora" title="De Motu Antiquiora">\n         De Motu Antiquiora\n        </a>\n       </i>\n       , an unpublished manuscript on the motion of falling bodies.\n      </p>\n      <h2>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Examples">\n        Examples\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Free_fall&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Examples">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h2>\n      <p>\n       Examples of objects in free fall include:\n      </p>\n      <ul>\n       <li>\n        A\n        <a href="/wiki/Spacecraft" title="Spacecraft">\n         spacecraft\n        </a>\n        (in space) with propulsion off (e.g. in a continuous orbit, or on a suborbital trajectory (\n        <a href="/wiki/Ballistics" title="Ballistics">\n         ballistics\n        </a>\n        ) going up for some minutes, and then down).\n       </li>\n       <li>\n        An object dropped at the top of a\n        <a href="/wiki/Drop_tube" title="Drop tube">\n         drop tube\n        </a>\n        .\n       </li>\n       <li>\n        An object thrown upward or a person jumping off the ground at low speed (i.e. as long as air resistance is negligible in comparison to weight).\n       </li>\n      </ul>\n      <p>\n       Technically, an object is in free fall even when moving upwards or instantaneously at rest at the top of its motion. If gravity is the only influence acting, then the acceleration is always downward and has the same magnitude for all bodies, commonly denoted\n       <span class="mwe-math-element">\n        <span class="mwe-math-mathml-inline mwe-math-mathml-a11y" style="display: none;">\n         <math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">\n          <semantics>\n           <mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">\n            <mstyle displaystyle="true" scriptlevel="0">\n             <mi>\n              g\n             </mi>\n            </mstyle>\n           </mrow>\n           <annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\n            {\\displaystyle g}\n           </annotation>\n          </semantics>\n         </math>\n        </span>\n        <img alt="g" aria-hidden="true" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline" src="https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d3556280e66fe2c0d0140df20935a6f057381d77" style="vertical-align: -0.671ex; width:1.126ex; height:2.009ex;"/>\n       </span>\n       .\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       Since all objects fall at the same rate in the absence of other forces, objects and people will experience\n       <a href="/wiki/Weightlessness" title="Weightlessness">\n        weightlessness\n       </a>\n       in these situations.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       Examples of objects\n       <b>\n        not\n       </b>\n       in free fall:\n      </p>\n      <ul>\n       <li>\n        Flying in an aircraft: there is also an additional force of lift.\n       </li>\n       <li>\n        Standing on the ground: the gravitational force is counteracted by the\n        <a href="/wiki/Normal_force" title="Normal force">\n         normal force\n        </a>\n        from the ground.\n       </li>\n       <li>\n        Descending to the Earth using a parachute, which balances the force of gravity with an aerodynamic drag force (and with some parachutes, an additional lift force).\n       </li>\n      </ul>\n      <p>\n       The example of a falling skydiver who has not yet deployed a parachute is not considered free fall from a physics perspective, since he experiences a\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Drag_force" title="Drag force">\n        drag force\n       </a>\n       that equals his weight once he has achieved terminal velocity (see below). However, the term "free fall skydiving" is commonly used to describe this case in everyday speech, and in the skydiving community. It is not clear, though, whether the more recent sport of\n       <a href="/wiki/Wingsuit_flying" title="Wingsuit flying">\n        wingsuit flying\n       </a>\n       fits under the definition of free fall skydiving.\n      </p>\n      <div class="thumb tright">\n       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;">\n        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Drop_time.jpg">\n         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="600" data-file-width="800" height="225" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7d/Drop_time.jpg/300px-Drop_time.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7d/Drop_time.jpg/450px-Drop_time.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7d/Drop_time.jpg/600px-Drop_time.jpg 2x" width="300"/>\n        </a>\n        <div class="thumbcaption">\n         <div class="magnify">\n          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Drop_time.jpg" title="Enlarge">\n          </a>\n         </div>\n         Measured fall time of a small steel sphere falling from various heights. This demonstrated Galileo\'s discovery that, in the absence of air resistance, all objects experience the same acceleration due to gravity.      <h2>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Free_fall_in_general_relativity">\n        Free fall in general relativity\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Free_fall&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Free fall in general relativity">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h2>\n      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">\n       Main article:\n       <a href="/wiki/General_relativity" title="General relativity">\n        General relativity\n       </a>\n      </div>\n      <p>\n       In general relativity, an object in free fall is subject to no force and is an inertial body moving along a geodesic. Far away from any sources of space time curvature, where spacetime is flat, the Newtonian theory of free fall agrees with general relativity but otherwise the two disagree. The experimental observation that all objects in free fall accelerate at the same rate, as noted by Galileo and then embodied in Newton\'s theory as the equality of gravitational and inertial masses, and later confirmed to high accuracy by modern forms of the\n       <a href="/wiki/E%C3%B6tv%C3%B6s_experiment" title="E\xc3\xb6tv\xc3\xb6s experiment">\n        E\xc3\xb6tv\xc3\xb6s experiment\n       </a>\n       , is the basis of the\n       <a href="/wiki/Equivalence_principle" title="Equivalence principle">\n        equivalence principle\n       </a>\n       , from which basis Einstein\'s theory of general relativity initially took off.\n      </p>\n      <h2>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Record_free_fall_parachute_jumps">\n        Record free fall parachute jumps\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Free_fall&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Record free fall parachute jumps">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h2>\n      <div class="thumb tright">\n       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:182px;">\n        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Kittinger-jump.jpg">\n         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1800" data-file-width="1417" height="229" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/81/Kittinger-jump.jpg/180px-Kittinger-jump.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/81/Kittinger-jump.jpg/270px-Kittinger-jump.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/81/Kittinger-jump.jpg/360px-Kittinger-jump.jpg 2x" width="180"/>\n        </a>\n        <div class="thumbcaption">\n         <div class="magnify">\n          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Kittinger-jump.jpg" title="Enlarge">\n          </a>\n         </div>\n         <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Kittinger" title="Joseph Kittinger">\n          Joseph Kittinger\n         </a>\n         starting his record-breaking skydive in 1960. His record was broken only in 2012.\n        </div>\n       </div>\n      </div>\n      <p>\n       In 1914, while doing demonstrations for the\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._Army" title="U.S. Army">\n        U.S. Army\n       </a>\n       , a parachute pioneer named\n       <a href="/wiki/Tiny_Broadwick" title="Tiny Broadwick">\n        Tiny Broadwick\n       </a>\n       deployed her chute manually, thus becoming the first person to jump free-fall.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       According to the\n       <i>\n        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Guinness_Book_of_Records" title="Guinness Book of Records">\n         Guinness Book of Records\n        </a>\n       </i>\n       ,\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eugene_Andreev" title="Eugene Andreev">\n        Eugene Andreev\n       </a>\n       (\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/USSR" title="USSR">\n        USSR\n       </a>\n       ) holds the official FAI record for the longest free-fall parachute jump after falling for 24,500 metres (80,400\xc2\xa0ft) from an altitude of 25,458 metres (83,524\xc2\xa0ft) near the city of\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saratov,_Russia" title="Saratov, Russia">\n        Saratov, Russia\n       </a>\n       on November 1, 1962. Although later on jumpers would ascend higher altitudes, Andreev\'s record was set without the use of a\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Drogue_chute" title="Drogue chute">\n        drogue chute\n       </a>\n       during the jump and therefore remains the longest genuine\n       <i>\n        free fall\n       </i>\n       record.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">\n        <a href="#cite_note-5">\n         [5]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       During the late 1950s, Captain\n       <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Kittinger" title="Joseph Kittinger">\n        Joseph Kittinger\n       </a>\n       of the United States was assigned to the Aerospace Medical Research Laboratories at\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wright-Patterson_AFB" title="Wright-Patterson AFB">\n        Wright-Patterson AFB\n       </a>\n       in\n       <a href="/wiki/Dayton,_Ohio" title="Dayton, Ohio">\n        Dayton, Ohio\n       </a>\n       . For\n       <a href="/wiki/Project_Excelsior" title="Project Excelsior">\n        Project Excelsior\n       </a>\n       (meaning "ever upward", a name given to the project by Colonel\n       <a href="/wiki/John_Stapp" title="John Stapp">\n        John Stapp\n       </a>\n       ), as part of research into high altitude bailout, he made a series of three parachute jumps wearing a pressurized suit, from a helium balloon with an open gondola.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       The first, from 76,400 feet (23,290\xc2\xa0m) in November 1959 was a near tragedy when an equipment malfunction caused him to lose consciousness, but the automatic parachute saved him (he went into a flat spin at a rotational velocity of 120 rpm; the g-force at his extremities was calculated to be over 22 times that of gravity, setting another record). Three weeks later he jumped again from 74,700 feet (22,770\xc2\xa0m). For that return jump Kittinger was awarded the\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/A._Leo_Stevens_parachute_medal" title="A. Leo Stevens parachute medal">\n        A. Leo Stevens parachute medal\n       </a>\n       .\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       On August 16, 1960 he made the final jump from the Excelsior III at 102,800 feet (31,330\xc2\xa0m). Towing a small drogue chute for stabilization, he fell for 4 minutes and 36 seconds reaching a maximum speed of 614\xc2\xa0mph (988\xc2\xa0km/h)\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">\n        <a href="#cite_note-6">\n         [6]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       before opening his parachute at 14,000 feet (4,270\xc2\xa0m). Pressurization for his right glove malfunctioned during the ascent, and his right hand swelled to twice its normal size.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">\n        <a href="#cite_note-7">\n         [7]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       He set records for highest balloon ascent, highest parachute jump, longest drogue-fall (4 min), and fastest speed by a human through the atmosphere.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">\n        <a href="#cite_note-8">\n         [8]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       The jumps were made in a "rocking-chair" position, descending on his back, rather than the usual arch familiar to skydivers, because he was wearing a 60-pound (27\xc2\xa0kg) "kit" on his behind and his pressure suit naturally formed that shape when inflated, a shape appropriate for sitting in an airplane cockpit.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       For the series of jumps, Kittinger was decorated with an\n       <a href="/wiki/Oak_leaf_cluster" title="Oak leaf cluster">\n        oak leaf cluster\n       </a>\n       to his\n       <a href="/wiki/Distinguished_Flying_Cross_(United_States)" title="Distinguished Flying Cross (United States)">\n        Distinguished Flying Cross\n       </a>\n       and awarded the\n       <a href="/wiki/Harmon_Trophy" title="Harmon Trophy">\n        Harmon Trophy\n       </a>\n       by President\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dwight_Eisenhower" title="Dwight Eisenhower">\n        Dwight Eisenhower\n       </a>\n       .\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       In 2012, the\n       <a href="/wiki/Red_Bull_Stratos" title="Red Bull Stratos">\n        Red Bull Stratos\n       </a>\n       mission took place. On October 14, 2012,\n       <a href="/wiki/Felix_Baumgartner" title="Felix Baumgartner">\n        Felix Baumgartner\n       </a>\n       broke the records previously set by Kittinger for the highest free fall, the highest manned helium balloon flight, and the fastest free fall; he jumped from 128,100 feet (39,045\xc2\xa0m), reaching\n       <span class="nowrap">\n        833.9 mph\n       </span>\n       <span class="nowrap">\n        (1342 km/h) -\n       </span>\n       <span class="nowrap">\n        <a href="/wiki/Mach_number" title="Mach number">\n         Mach 1.24\n        </a>\n       </span>\n       . Kittinger was a member of the mission control and helped design the capsule and suit that Baumgartner ascended and jumped in.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       On October 24, 2014,\n       <a href="/wiki/Alan_Eustace" title="Alan Eustace">\n        Alan Eustace\n       </a>\n       broke the record previously set by Baumgartner for the highest free fall. He jumped from a height of 135,908 feet (41,425\xc2\xa0m).\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nyt2014_9-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-nyt2014-9">\n         [9]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <h2>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Surviving_falls">\n        Surviving falls\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Free_fall&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Surviving falls">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h2>\n      <p>\n       A falling person at low altitude will reach\n       <a href="/wiki/Terminal_velocity" title="Terminal velocity">\n        terminal velocity\n       </a>\n       after about 12 seconds, falling some 450\xc2\xa0m (1,500\xc2\xa0ft) in that time. He or she will then maintain this speed without falling any faster.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">\n        <a href="#cite_note-10">\n         [10]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       Terminal velocity at higher altitudes is greater due to the thinner atmosphere and consequent lower air resistance; free-fallers from high altitudes, including Kittinger, Baumgartner and Eustace discussed in this article, fell faster at higher altitudes.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       The severity of injury increases with the height of a free fall, but also depends on body and surface features and the manner that the body impacts on to the surface.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Atanasijevic2004_11-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-Atanasijevic2004-11">\n         [11]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       The chance of surviving increases if landing on a surface of high deformity, such as snow.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Atanasijevic2004_11-1">\n        <a href="#cite_note-Atanasijevic2004-11">\n         [11]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       Overall, the height at which 50% of children die from a fall is between four and five\n       <a href="/wiki/Storey" title="Storey">\n        storey\n       </a>\n       heights above the ground.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Barlow1983_12-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-Barlow1983-12">\n         [12]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       <a href="/wiki/Jat_Airways" title="Jat Airways">\n        JAT\n       </a>\n       stewardess\n       <a href="/wiki/Vesna_Vulovi%C4%87" title="Vesna Vulovi\xc4\x87">\n        Vesna Vulovi\xc4\x87\n       </a>\n       survived a fall of 10,000 metres (33,000\xc2\xa0ft)\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">\n        <a href="#cite_note-13">\n         [13]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       on January 26, 1972 when she was aboard\n       <a href="/wiki/JAT_Flight_367" title="JAT Flight 367">\n        JAT Flight 367\n       </a>\n       . The plane was brought down by explosives over\n       <a href="/wiki/Srbsk%C3%A1_Kamenice" title="Srbsk\xc3\xa1 Kamenice">\n        Srbsk\xc3\xa1 Kamenice\n       </a>\n       in the former\n       <a href="/wiki/Czechoslovakia" title="Czechoslovakia">\n        Czechoslovakia\n       </a>\n       (now\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Czech_Republic" title="The Czech Republic">\n        the Czech Republic\n       </a>\n       ). The\n       <a href="/wiki/Serbia" title="Serbia">\n        Serbian\n       </a>\n       stewardess suffered a broken skull, three broken vertebrae (one crushed completely), and was in a coma for 27 days. In an interview, she commented that, according to the man who found her, "\xe2\x80\xa6I was in the middle part of the plane. I was found with my head down and my colleague on top of me. One part of my body with my leg was in the plane and my head was out of the plane. A catering trolley was pinned against my spine and kept me in the plane. The man who found me, says I was very lucky. He was in the German Army as a medic during World War Two. He knew how to treat me at the site of the accident."\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">\n        <a href="#cite_note-14">\n         [14]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       In World War II there were several reports of\n       <a href="/wiki/Military" title="Military">\n        military\n       </a>\n       <a href="/wiki/Aircrew" title="Aircrew">\n        aircrew\n       </a>\n       surviving long falls from severely damaged aircraft: Flight Sergeant\n       <a href="/wiki/Nicholas_Alkemade" title="Nicholas Alkemade">\n        Nicholas Alkemade\n       </a>\n       jumped at 18,000 feet (5,500\xc2\xa0m) without a parachute and survived as he hit pine trees and soft snow. He suffered a sprained leg. Staff Sergeant\n       <a href="/wiki/Alan_Magee" title="Alan Magee">\n        Alan Magee\n       </a>\n       exited his aircraft at 22,000 feet (6,700\xc2\xa0m) without a parachute and survived as he landed on the glass roof of a train station. Lieutenant\n       <a href="/wiki/Ivan_Chisov" title="Ivan Chisov">\n        Ivan Chisov\n       </a>\n       bailed out at 23,000 feet (7,000\xc2\xa0m). While he had a parachute his plan was to delay opening it as he had been in the midst of an air-battle and was concerned about getting shot while hanging below the parachute. He lost consciousness due to lack of oxygen and hit a snow-covered slope while still unconscious. While he suffered severe injuries he was able to fly again in three months.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       It was reported that two of the victims of the\n       <a href="/wiki/Pan_Am_Flight_103" title="Pan Am Flight 103">\n        Lockerbie bombing\n       </a>\n       survived for a brief period after hitting the ground (with the forward nose section\n       <a href="/wiki/Fuselage" title="Fuselage">\n        fuselage\n       </a>\n       in freefall mode), but died from their injuries before help arrived.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">\n        <a href="#cite_note-15">\n         [15]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       <a href="/wiki/Juliane_Koepcke" title="Juliane Koepcke">\n        Juliane Koepcke\n       </a>\n       survived a long free fall resulting from the December 24, 1971, crash of\n       <a href="/wiki/LANSA_Flight_508" title="LANSA Flight 508">\n        LANSA Flight 508\n       </a>\n       (a\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/L%C3%ADneas_A%C3%A9reas_Nacionales_S._A._(Peru)" title="L\xc3\xadneas A\xc3\xa9reas Nacionales S. A. (Peru)">\n        LANSA\n       </a>\n       <a href="/wiki/Lockheed_L-188_Electra" title="Lockheed L-188 Electra">\n        Lockheed Electra\n       </a>\n       OB-R-941 commercial airliner) in the\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Peruvian_rainforest" title="Peruvian rainforest">\n        Peruvian rainforest\n       </a>\n       . The airplane was struck by\n       <a href="/wiki/Lightning" title="Lightning">\n        lightning\n       </a>\n       during a severe\n       <a href="/wiki/Thunderstorm" title="Thunderstorm">\n        thunderstorm\n       </a>\n       and exploded in mid air, disintegrating two miles (3.2\xc2\xa0km) up. K\xc3\xb6pcke, who was 17 years old at the time, fell to earth still strapped into her seat. The\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/German_Peruvian" title="German Peruvian">\n        German Peruvian\n       </a>\n       teenager survived the fall with only a broken\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Collarbone" title="Collarbone">\n        collarbone\n       </a>\n       , a gash to her right arm, and her right eye swollen shut.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CNN_16-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-CNN-16">\n         [16]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       As an example of "freefall survival" that was not as extreme as sometimes reported in the press, a skydiver from Staffordshire was said to have plunged 6,000\xc2\xa0metres without a parachute in Russia and survived. James Boole said that he was supposed to have been given a signal by another skydiver to open his parachute, but it came two seconds too late. Boole, who was filming the other skydiver for a television documentary, landed on snow-covered rocks and suffered a broken back and rib.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17">\n        <a href="#cite_note-17">\n         [17]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       While he was lucky to survive, this was not a case of true freefall survival, because he was flying a wingsuit, greatly decreasing his vertical speed. This was over descending terrain with deep snow cover, and he impacted while his parachute was beginning to deploy. Over the years, other skydivers have survived accidents where the press has reported that no parachute was open, yet they were actually being slowed by a small area of tangled parachute. They might still be very lucky to survive, but an impact at 80\xc2\xa0mph (129\xc2\xa0km/h) is much less severe than the 120\xc2\xa0mph (193\xc2\xa0km/h) that might occur in normal freefall.\n       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">\n        [\n        <i>\n         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:No_original_research" title="Wikipedia:No original research">\n          <span title="The material near this tag possibly contains original research. (December 2016)">\n           original research?\n          </span>\n         </a>\n        </i>\n        ]\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       Parachute jumper and stuntman\n       <a href="/wiki/Luke_Aikins" title="Luke Aikins">\n        Luke Aikins\n       </a>\n       successfully jumped without a parachute from about 25,000 feet (7,600\xc2\xa0m) into a 10,000-square-foot (930\xc2\xa0m\n       <sup>\n        2\n       </sup>\n       ) net in\n       <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">\n        California\n       </a>\n       , US, on 30 July 2016.\n
