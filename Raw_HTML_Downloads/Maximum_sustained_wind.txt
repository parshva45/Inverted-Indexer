    <span class="nowrap">\n          \xe2\x89\xa433 knots,\n         </span>\n         <span class="nowrap">\n          \xe2\x89\xa438 mph,\n         </span>\n         <span class="nowrap">\n          \xe2\x89\xa462 km/h\n         </span>\n        </td>\n       </tr>\n      </table>\n      <p>\n       The\n       <b>\n        maximum sustained wind\n       </b>\n       associated with a\n       <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone" title="Tropical cyclone">\n        tropical cyclone\n       </a>\n       is a common indicator of the intensity of the storm. Within a mature tropical cyclone, it is found within the\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eyewall" title="Eyewall">\n        eyewall\n       </a>\n       at a distance defined as the\n       <a href="/wiki/Radius_of_maximum_wind" title="Radius of maximum wind">\n        radius of maximum wind\n       </a>\n       , or RMW. Unlike\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gust_(wind)" title="Gust (wind)">\n        gusts\n       </a>\n       , the value of these winds are determined via their sampling and averaging the sampled results over a period of time. Wind measuring has been standardized globally to reflect the winds at 10 metres (33\xc2\xa0ft) above the Earth\'s surface, and the maximum sustained wind represents the highest average wind over either a one-minute (US) or ten-minute time span (see\n       <a href="/wiki/Maximum_sustained_wind#Definition" title="Maximum sustained wind">\n        the definition, below\n       </a>\n       ), anywhere within the tropical cyclone. Surface winds are highly variable due to friction between the atmosphere and the Earth\'s surface, as well as near hills and mountains over land.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       Over the ocean,\n       <a href="/wiki/Weather_satellite" title="Weather satellite">\n        satellite\n       </a>\n       <a href="/wiki/Satellite_imagery" title="Satellite imagery">\n        imagery\n       </a>\n       determines the value of the maximum sustained winds within a tropical cyclone. Land, ship,\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hurricane_Hunters" title="Hurricane Hunters">\n        aircraft reconnaissance\n       </a>\n       observations, and\n       <a href="/wiki/Weather_radar" title="Weather radar">\n        radar\n       </a>\n       imagery can also estimate this quantity, when available. This value helps determine damage expected from a tropical cyclone, through use of such scales as the\n       <a href="/wiki/Saffir%E2%80%93Simpson_scale" title="Saffir\xe2\x80\x93Simpson scale">\n        Saffir\xe2\x80\x93Simpson scale\n       </a>\n       .\n      </p>\n      <p>\n      </p>\n      <div class="toc" id="toc">\n       <div class="toctitle">\n        <h2>\n         Contents\n  </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a maximum wind">\n        Radius of maximum wind\n       </a>\n      </div>\n      <p>\n       The maximum sustained wind normally occurs at a distance from the center known as the radius of maximum wind, within a mature tropical cyclone\'s eyewall, before winds decrease at farther distances away from a tropical cyclone\'s center.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">\n        <a href="#cite_note-1">\n         [1]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       Most weather agencies use the definition for sustained winds recommended by the\n       <a href="/wiki/World_Meteorological_Organization" title="World Meteorological Organization">\n        World Meteorological Organization\n       </a>\n       (WMO), which specifies measuring winds at a height of 10 metres (33\xc2\xa0ft) for 10\xc2\xa0minutes, and then taking the average. However, the United States\n       <a href="/wiki/National_Weather_Service" title="National Weather Service">\n        National Weather Service\n       </a>\n       defines sustained winds within tropical cyclones by averaging winds over a period of one minute, measured at the same 10 metres (33\xc2\xa0ft) height.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NWSM_Defs_2-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-NWSM_Defs-2">\n         [2]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       This is an important distinction, as the value of the highest one-minute sustained wind is about 14% greater than a ten-minute sustained wind over the same period.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">\n        <a href="#cite_note-3">\n         [3]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <h2>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Determination_of_value">\n        Determination of value\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Maximum_sustained_wind&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Determination of value">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h2>\n      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">\n       Main article:\n       <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone_observation" title="Tropical cyclone observation">\n        Tropical cyclone observation\n       </a>\n      </div>\n      <p>\n       In most tropical cyclone basins, use of the satellite-based\n       <a href="/wiki/Dvorak_technique" title="Dvorak technique">\n        Dvorak technique\n       </a>\n       is the primary method used to determine a tropical cyclone\'s maximum sustained winds.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ODT_4-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-ODT-4">\n         [4]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       The extent of spiral banding and difference in temperature between the\n       <a href="/wiki/Eye_(cyclone)" title="Eye (cyclone)">\n        eye\n       </a>\n       and eyewall is used within the technique to assign a maximum sustained wind and pressure.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">\n        <a href="#cite_note-5">\n         [5]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_pressure" title="Atmospheric pressure">\n        Central pressure\n       </a>\n       values for their centers of\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Low_pressure" title="Low pressure">\n        low pressure\n       </a>\n       are approximate. The intensity of example hurricanes is derived from both the time of landfall and the maximum intensity.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NHC_SSHS_6-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-NHC_SSHS-6">\n         [6]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       The tracking of individual clouds on minutely satellite imagery could be used in the future in estimating surface winds speeds for tropical cyclones.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">\n        <a href="#cite_note-7">\n         [7]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       Ship and land observations are also used, when available. In the Atlantic as well as the Central and Eastern Pacific basins, reconnaissance aircraft are still utilized to fly through tropical cyclones to determine flight level winds, which can then be adjusted to provide a fairly reliable estimate of maximum sustained winds. A reduction of 10 percent of the winds sampled at flight level is used to estimate the maximum sustained winds near the surface, which has been determined during the past decade through the use of\n class="reference" id="cite_ref-Franklin_8-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-Franklin-8">\n         [8]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Doppler_weather_radar" title="Doppler weather radar">\n        Doppler weather radar\n       </a>\n       can be used in the same manner to determine surface winds with tropical cyclones near land.\n       <sup class="mw-headline" id="Variation">\n        Variation\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Maximum_sustained_wind&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Variation">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h2>\n      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">\n       See also:\n       <a href="/wiki/Planetary_boundary_layer" title="Planetary boundary layer">\n        Planetary boundary layer\n       </a>\n      </div>\n      <p>\n       Friction between the atmosphere and the Earth\'s surface causes a 20% reduction in the wind at the surface of the Earth.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">\n        <a href="#cite_note-10">\n         [10]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       Surface roughness also leads to significant variation of wind speeds. Over land, winds maximize at hill or mountain crests, while sheltering leads to lower wind speeds in valleys and lee slopes.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">\n        <a href="#cite_note-11">\n         [11]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       Compared to over water, maximum sustained winds over land average 8% lower.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">\n        <a href="#cite_note-12">\n         [12]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       More specifically, over a city or rough terrain, the wind gradient effect could cause a reduction of 40% to 50% of the\n       <a href="/wiki/Geostrophic_wind" title="Geostrophic wind">\n        geostrophic wind\n       </a>\n       speed aloft; while over open water or ice, the reduction is between 10% and 30%.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Franklin_8-1">\n        <a href="#cite_note-Franklin-8">\n         [8]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">\n        <a href="#cite_note-13">\n         [13]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Russell_14-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-Russell-14">\n         [14]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <h2>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Relationship_to_tropical_cyclone_strength_scales">\n        Relationship to tropical cyclone strength scales\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Maximum_sustained_wind&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Relationship to tropical cyclone strength scales">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h2>\n      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">\n       Main article:\n       <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone_scales" title="Tropical cyclone scales">\n        Tropical cyclone scales\n       </a>\n      </div>\n      <p>\n       In most basins, maximum sustained winds are used to define their category. In the Atlantic and northeast Pacific oceans, the\n       <a href="/wiki/Saffir%E2%80%93Simpson_scale" title="Saffir\xe2\x80\x93Simpson scale">\n        Saffir\xe2\x80\x93Simpson scale\n       </a>\n       is used. This scale can be used to determine possible storm surge and damage impact on land.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USA_Today_15-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-USA_Today-15">\n         [15]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       In most basins, the category of the tropical cyclone (for example, tropical depression, tropical storm, hurricane/typhoon, super typhoon, depression, deep depression, intense tropical cyclone) is determined from the\n       <a href="/wiki/Cyclone" title="Cyclone">\n        cyclone\n       </a>\n       \'s maximum sustained wind. Only in Australia is this quantity not used to define the tropical cyclone\'s category; in their basin, wind gusts are used.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">\n        <a href="#cite_note-16">\n         [16]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <h2>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">\n        See also\n       </span>\n       <span
