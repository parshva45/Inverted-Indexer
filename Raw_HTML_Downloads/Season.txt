The same is true of the\n       <a href="/wiki/Southern_Hemisphere" title="Southern Hemisphere">\n        Southern Hemisphere\n       </a>\n       in November, December, and January. angle on climate\n       </a>\n      </div>\n      <h3>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Axis_tilt">\n        Axis tilt\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Season&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Axis tilt">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h3>\n      <p>\n       The seasons result from the Earth\'s\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Axis_of_rotation" title="Axis of rotation">\n        axis of rotation\n       </a>\n       being\n       <a href="/wiki/Axial_tilt#Earth" title="Axial tilt">\n        tilted\n       </a>\n       with respect to its\n       <a href="/wiki/Orbital_plane_(astronomy)" title="Orbital plane (astronomy)">\n        orbital plane\n       </a>\n       by an angle of approximately 23.5\n       <a href="/wiki/Degree_(angle)" title="Degree (angle)">\n        degrees\n       </a>\n       .\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">\n        <a href="#cite_note-4">\n         [4]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       (This tilt is also known as "obliquity of the\n       <a href="/wiki/Ecliptic" title="Ecliptic">\n        ecliptic\n       </a>\n       ".)\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       Regardless of the time of year, the\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Northern_hemisphere" title="Northern hemisphere">\n        northern\n       </a>\n       and southern hemispheres always experience opposite seasons. This is because during summer or winter, one part of the planet is more directly exposed to the rays of the Sun (see\n       <i>\n        Fig. 1\n       </i>\n       ) than the other, and this exposure alternates as the Earth revolves in its orbit. For approximately half of the year (from around March 20 to around September 22), the northern hemisphere tips toward the Sun, with the maximum amount occurring on about June 21. For the other half of the year, the same happens, but in the southern hemisphere instead of the northern, with the maximum around December 21. The two instants when the Sun is directly overhead at the Equator are the equinoxes. Also at that moment, both the\n       <a href="/wiki/North_Pole" title="North Pole">\n        North Pole\n       </a>\n       and the\n       <a href="/wiki/South_Pole" title="South Pole">\n        South Pole\n       </a>\n       of the Earth are just on the\n       <a href="/wiki/Terminator_(solar)" title="Terminator (solar)">\n        terminator\n       </a>\n       , and hence day and night are equally divided between the northern and southern hemispheres. Around the March equinox, the northern hemisphere will be experiencing spring as the hours of daylight increase, and the southern hemisphere is experiencing autumn as daylight hours shorten.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       The effect of axial tilt is observable as the change in\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Day_length" title="Day length">\n        day length\n       </a>\n       and\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Altitude_(astronomy)" title="Altitude (astronomy)">\n        altitude\n       </a>\n       of the Sun at\n       <a href="/wiki/Noon" title="Noon">\n        noon\n       </a>\n       (the\n       <a href="/wiki/Culmination" title="Culmination">\n        culmination\n       </a>\n       of the Sun) during a\n       <a href="/wiki/Year" title="Year">\n        year\n       </a>\n       . The low angle of Sun during the winter months means that incoming rays of solar radiation are\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Effect_of_sun_angle_on_climate" title="Effect of sun angle on climate">\n        spread over a larger area of the Earth\'s surface\n       </a>\n       , so the light received is more indirect and of lower intensity. Between this effect and the shorter daylight hours, the axial tilt of the Earth accounts for most of the seasonal variation in climate in both hemispheres.\n      </p>\n      <div class="thumb         </p>\n         </div>\n        </div>\n       </li>\n      </ul>\n      <h3>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Elliptical_Earth_orbit">\n        Elliptical Earth orbit\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Season&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Elliptical Earth orbit">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h3>\n      <p>\n       Compared to axial tilt, other factors contribute little to seasonal temperature changes. The seasons are not the result of the variation in Earth\'s distance to the sun because of its elliptical orbit.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">\n        <a href="#cite_note-5">\n         [5]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       In fact, Earth reaches\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Perihelion" title="Perihelion">\n        perihelion\n       </a>\n       (the point in its orbit closest to the Sun) in January, and it reaches\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aphelion" title="Aphelion">\n        aphelion\n       </a>\n       (farthest point from the Sun) in July, so the slight contribution of orbital eccentricity opposes the temperature trends of the seasons in the northern hemisphere.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Science.40NASA_6-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-Science.40NASA-6">\n         [6]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       In general, the effect of orbital eccentricity on Earth\'s seasons is a 7% variation in sunlight received.\n      </p>\n      <div class="thumb tright">\n       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">\n        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:This_is_a_summer_tree.JPG">\n         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3456" data-file-width="4608" height="165" class="magnify">\n          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:This_is_a_summer_tree.JPG" title="Enlarge">\n          </a>\n         </div>\n         A tree in summer\n        </div>\n       </div>\n      </div>\n      <p>\n       <a href="/wiki/Orbital_eccentricity" title="Orbital eccentricity">\n        Orbital eccentricity\n       </a>\n       can influence temperatures, but on Earth, this effect is small and is more than counteracted by other factors; research shows that the Earth as a whole is actually slightly warmer when\n       <i>\n        farther\n       </i>\n       from the sun. This is because the northern hemisphere has more land than the southern, and land warms more readily than sea.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Science.40NASA_6-1">\n        <a href="#cite_note-Science.40NASA-6">\n         [6]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       Any noticeable intensification of the southern hemisphere\'s winters and summers due to Earth\'s elliptical orbit is mitigated by the abundance of water in the southern hemisphere.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">\n        <a href="#cite_note-7">\n         [7]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <h3>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Maritime_and_hemispheric">\n        Maritime and hemispheric\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Season&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Maritime and hemispheric">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h3>\n      <p>\n       Seasonal weather fluctuations (changes) also depend on factors such as proximity to\n       <a href="/wiki/Ocean" title="Ocean">\n        oceans\n       </a>\n       or other large bodies of water,\n       <a href="/wiki/Ocean_current" title="Ocean current">\n        currents\n       </a>\n       in those oceans,\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/El_Ni%C3%B1o-Southern_Oscillation" title="El Ni\xc3\xb1o-Southern Oscillation">\n        El Ni\xc3\xb1o\n       </a>\n       /ENSO and other oceanic cycles, and prevailing\n       <a href="/wiki/Wind" title="Wind">\n        winds\n       </a>\n       .\n      </p>\n      <div class="thumb tright">\n       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">\n        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:This_is_a_fall_tree.JPG">\n         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3456" class="magnify">\n          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:This_is_a_fall_tree.JPG" title="Enlarge">\n          </a>\n         </div>\n         A deciduous tree in autumn (fall)\n        </div>\n       </div>\n      </div>\n      <p>\n       In the temperate and polar regions, seasons are marked by changes in the amount of\n       <a href="/wiki/Sunlight" title="Sunlight">\n        sunlight\n       </a>\n       , which in turn often causes\n       <a href="/wiki/Biological_life_cycle" title="Biological life cycle">\n        cycles\n       </a>\n       of dormancy in plants and\n       <a href="/wiki/Hibernation" title="Hibernation">\n        hibernation\n       </a>\n       in animals. These effects vary with latitude and with proximity to bodies of water. For example, the South Pole is in the middle of the continent of\n       <a href="/wiki/Antarctica" title="Antarctica">\n        Antarctica\n       </a>\n       and therefore a considerable distance from the moderating influence of the southern oceans. The North Pole is in the\n       <a href="/wiki/Arctic_Ocean" title="Arctic Ocean">\n        Arctic Ocean\n       </a>\n       , and thus its temperature extremes are buffered by the water. The result is that the South Pole is consistently colder during the southern winter than the North Pole during the northern winter.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       The cycle of seasons in the polar and temperate zones of one hemisphere is opposite to that in the other. When it is summer in the northern hemisphere, it is winter in the southern hemisphere, and vice versa.\n      </p>\n      <h3>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Tropics">\n        Tropics\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Season&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Tropics">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h3>\n      <p>\n       In\n       <a href="/wiki/Tropics" title="Tropics">\n        tropical\n       </a>\n       and\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Subtropical" title="Subtropical">\n        subtropical\n       </a>\n       regions there is little annual fluctuation of sunlight. However, there are seasonal shifts of a rainy global-scale low pressure belt called the\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Intertropical_convergence_zone" title="Intertropical convergence zone">\n        Intertropical convergence zone\n       </a>\n       . As a result, the amount of\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Precipitation_(meteorology)" title="Precipitation (meteorology)">\n        precipitation\n       </a>\n       tends to vary more dramatically than the average temperature. When the convergence zone is north of the equator, the tropical areas of the northern hemisphere experience their wet season while the tropics south of the equator have their dry season. This pattern reverses when the convergence zone migrates to a position south of the equator.\n      </p>\n      <h3>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Mid-latitude_thermal_lag">\n        Mid-latitude thermal lag\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Season&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Mid-latitude thermal lag">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h3>\n      <p>\n       In meteorological terms, the summer\n       <a href="/wiki/Solstice" title="Solstice">\n        solstice\n       </a>\n       and winter solstice (or the maximum and minimum\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Insolation" title="Insolation">\n        insolation\n       </a>\n       , respectively) do not fall in the middles of summer and winter. The heights of these seasons occur up to seven weeks later because of seasonal lag. Seasons, though, are not always defined in meteorological terms.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       In\n       <a href="/wiki/Astronomy" title="Astronomy">\n        astronomical\n       </a>\n       reckoning by hours of daylight alone, the solstices and equinoxes are in the\n       <i>\n        middle\n       </i>\n       of the respective seasons. Because of seasonal lag due to thermal absorption and release by the oceans, regions with a continental climate which predominate in the northern hemisphere often consider these four dates to be the\n       <i>\n        start\n       </i>\n       of the seasons as in the diagram, with the\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cross-quarter_day" title="Cross-quarter day">\n        cross-quarter days\n       </a>\n       considered seasonal midpoints. The length of these seasons is not uniform because of the elliptical orbit of the earth and its\n       <a href="/wiki/Kepler%27s_laws_of_planetary_motion" title="Kepler\'s laws of planetary motion">\n        different speeds along that orbit\n       </a>\n       .\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">\n        <a href="#cite_note-8">\n         [8]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <h2>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Four-season_calendar_reckoning">\n        <span id="Reckoning">\n        </span>\n        Four-season calendar reckoning\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Season&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Four-season calendar reckoning">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h2>\n      <p>\n       Calendar-based reckoning defines the seasons in absolute rather than relative terms. Accordingly, if floral activity is regularly observed during the coolest quarter of the year in a particular area, it is still considered winter despite the traditional association of flowers with spring and summer. Additionally, the seasons are considered to change on the same dates everywhere that uses a particular calendar method regardless of variations in climate from one area to another. Most calendar-based methods use a four-season model to identify the warmest and coldest seasons, which are separated by two intermediate seasons.\n      </p>\n      <div class="thumb tright">\n      <h3>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Meteorological">\n        Meteorological\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n differences especially\n         <a href="/wiki/Snow" title="Snow">\n          snow\n         </a>\n         cover through the year\n        </div>\n       </div>\n      </div>\n      <p>\n       Meteorological seasons are reckoned by temperature, with summer being the hottest quarter of the year and winter the coldest quarter of the year. In 1780 the Societas Meteorologica Palatina (which became defunct in 1795), an early international organization for meteorology, defined seasons as groupings of three whole months as identified by the Gregorian calendar. Ever since, professional meteorologists all over the world have used this definition.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">\n        <a href="#cite_note-9">\n         [9]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       Therefore, for temperate areas in the northern hemisphere, spring begins on 1 March, summer on 1 June, autumn on 1 September, and winter on 1 December. For the southern hemisphere temperate zone, spring begins on 1 September, summer on 1 December, autumn on 1 March, and winter on 1 June.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Australian_weather_and_the_seasons_10-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-Australian_weather_and_the_seasons-10">\n         [10]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">\n        <a href="#cite_note-11">\n         [11]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       In Australasia the meteorological terms for seasons apply to the temperate zone that occupies Tasmania\n       </a>\n       , the south-eastern corner of\n       <a href="/wiki/South_Australia" title="South Australia">\n        South Australia\n       </a>\n       and the south-west of Western Australia, and the south east\n       <a href="/wiki/Queensland" title="Queensland">\n        Queensland\n       </a>\n       areas south of\n       <a href="/wiki/Brisbane" title="Brisbane">\n        Brisbane\n       </a>\n       .\n      </p>\n      <table class="wikitable">\n       <tr>\n        <th colspan="4">\n         Australasian seasons\n        </th>\n       </tr>\n       <tr>\n In Sweden and Finland, meteorologists use a non-calendar based\n       <a href="/wiki/Definition" title="Definition">\n        definition\n       </a>\n       for the seasons based on the temperature. Spring begins when the daily averaged temperature permanently rises above 0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xb0C, summer begins when the temperature permanently rises above +10\xc2\xa0\xc2\xb0C, summer ends when the temperature permanently falls below +10\xc2\xa0\xc2\xb0C and winter begins when the temperature permanently falls below 0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xb0C. "Permanently" here means that the daily averaged temperature has remained above or below the limit for seven consecutive days. This implies two things: first, the seasons do not begin at fixed dates but must be determined by observation and are known only after the fact; and second, a new season begins at different dates in different parts of the country. In Great Britain, the onset of spring used to be defined as when the maximum daily temperature reached 50\xc2\xa0\xc2\xb0F (10\xc2\xa0\xc2\xb0C) in a defined sequence of days. This almost always occurred in March. However, with global warming this temperature is now not uncommon in the winter.\n      </p>\n      <table align="center" class="wikitable" style="width: 700px;">\n       <tr>\n        <th designating the temperate seasons dates back at least to the Julian calendar used by the ancient Romans. It continues to be used on many modern Gregorian calendars worldwide, although some countries like Australia, New Zealand, and Russia prefer to use meteorological reckoning. The precise timing of the seasons is determined by the exact times of transit of the sun over the tropics of Cancer and Capricorn for the solstices and the times of the sun\'s transit over the equator for the\n       <a href="/wiki/Equinox" title="Equinox">\n        equinoxes\n       </a>\n       , or a traditional date close to these times.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">\n        <a href="#cite_note-15">\n         [15]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       The following diagram shows the relation between the line of solstice and the line of\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Apsides" title="Apsides">\n        apsides\n       </a>\n       of Earth\'s elliptical orbit. The orbital ellipse (with eccentricity exaggerated for effect) goes through each of the six Earth images, which are sequentially the\n       <a href="/wiki/Apsis#Earth.27s_perihelion_and_aphelion" title="Apsis">\n        perihelion\n       </a>\n       (periapsis\xe2\x80\x94nearest point to the sun) on anywhere from 2 January to 5 January, the point of March equinox on 19, 20 or 21 March, the point of June solstice on 20 or 21 June, the\n       <a href="/wiki/Apsis#Earth.27s_perihelion_and_aphelion" title="Apsis">\n        aphelion\n       </a>\n       (apoapsis\xe2\x80\x94farthest point from the sun) on anywhere from 4 July to 7 July, the September equinox on 22 or 23 September, and the December solstice on 21 or 22 December.\n      </p>\n      <div class="center">\n       <div class="thumb tnone">\n        <div class="thumbinner" style="width:702px;">\n         <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Seasons1.svg">\n          <img alt="Illustration of seasonal distances from Earth to the Sun" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1070" data-file-width="1826" height="410" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f0/Seasons1.svg/700px-Seasons1.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f0/Seasons1.svg/1050px-Seasons1.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f0/Seasons1.svg/1400px-Seasons1.svg.png 2x" width="700"/>\n         </a>\n         <div class="thumbcaption">\n          <div class="magnify">\n           <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Seasons1.svg" title="Enlarge">\n           </a>\n          </div>\n          Note: Distances are exaggerated and not to scale\n         </div>\n        </div>\n       </div>\n      </div>\n      <p>\n       These "astronomical" seasons are not of equal length, because of the\n       <a href="/wiki/Ellipse" title="Ellipse">\n        elliptical nature\n       </a>\n       of the orbit of the Earth, as discovered by\n       <a href="/wiki/Johannes_Kepler" title="Johannes Kepler">\n        Johannes Kepler\n       </a>\n       . From the March equinox it currently takes 92.75 days until the June solstice, then 93.65 days until the September equinox, 89.85 days until the December solstice and finally 88.99 days until the March equinox.\n      </p>\n      <h4>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Variation_due_to_calendar_misalignment">\n        Variation due to calendar misalignment\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Season&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Variation due to calendar misalignment">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h4>\n      <p>\n       The times of the equinoxes and solstices are not fixed with respect to the modern Gregorian calendar, but fall about six hours later every year, amounting to one full day in four years. They are reset by the occurrence of a leap year. The Gregorian calendar is designed to keep the March equinox no later than 21 March as accurately as is practical.\n       <i>\n        Also see:\n        <a href="/wiki/Gregorian_calendar#Calendar_seasonal_error" title="Gregorian calendar">\n         Gregorian calendar seasonal error\n        </a>\n        .\n       </i>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       The calendar equinox (used in the calculation of Easter) is 21 March, the same date as in the Easter tables current at the time of the Council of Nicaea in AD 325. The calendar is therefore framed to prevent the astronomical equinox wandering onto 22 March. From Nicaea to the date of the reform, the years 500, 600, 700, 900, 1000, 1100, 1300, 1400 and 1500, which would not have been leap years in the Gregorian calendar, amount to nine days, but astronomers directed that ten days be removed.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       Currently, the most common equinox and solstice dates are March 20, June 21, September 22 or 23 and December 21; the four-year average slowly shifts to earlier times as the century progresses. This shift is a full day in about 128 years (compensated mainly by the century "leap year" rules of the Gregorian calendar) and as 2000 was a leap year the current shift has been progressing since the beginning of the last century, when equinoxes and solstices were relatively late. This also means that in many years of the twentieth century, the dates of March 21, June 22, September 23 and December 22 were much more common, so older books teach (and older people may still remember) these dates.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       Note that all the times are given in\n       <a href="/wiki/Coordinated_Universal_Time" title="Coordinated Universal Time">\n        UTC\n       </a>\n       (roughly speaking, the time at\n       <a href="/wiki/Greenwich" title="Greenwich">\n        Greenwich\n       </a>\n       , ignoring British Summer Time). People living farther to the east (Asia and Australia), whose local times are in advance, will see the astronomical seasons apparently start later; for example, in\n       <a href="/wiki/Tonga" title="Tonga">\n        Tonga\n       </a>\n       (UTC+13), an equinox occurred on September 24, 1999, a date which will not crop up again until 2103. On the other hand, people living far to the west (America) whose clocks run behind UTC may experience an equinox as early as March 19.\n      </p>\n      <h4>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Change_over_time">\n        Change over time\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Season&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Change over time">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h4>\n      <p>\n       Over thousands of years, the Earth\'s\n       <a href="/wiki/Axial_tilt" title="Axial tilt">\n        axial tilt\n       </a>\n       and orbital eccentricity vary (see\n       <a href="/wiki/Milankovitch_cycles" title="Milankovitch cycles">\n        Milankovitch cycles\n       </a>\n       ). The equinoxes and solstices move westward relative to the stars while the perihelion and aphelion move eastward. Thus, ten thousand years from now Earth\'s northern winter will occur at aphelion and northern summer at perihelion. The severity of seasonal change \xe2\x80\x94 the average temperature difference between summer and winter in location \xe2\x80\x94 will also change over time because the Earth\'s axial tilt fluctuates between 22.1 and 24.5 degrees.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       Smaller irregularities in the times are caused by perturbations of the Moon and the other planets.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       <span id="China">\n       </span>\n       <span id="Japan">\n       </span>\n       <span id="East_Asia">\n       </span>\n      </p>\n      <h3>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Solar">\n        Solar\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Season&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Solar">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h3>\n      <div class="thumb tright">\n       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">\n        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Phases_of_the_Sun_(NHemi).png">\n         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="777" data-file-width="777" height="220" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d8/Phases_of_the_Sun_%28NHemi%29.png/220px-Phases_of_the_Sun_%28NHemi%29.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d8/Phases_of_the_Sun_%28NHemi%29.png/330px-Phases_of_the_Sun_%28NHemi%29.png 1.5x,    <div class="magnify">\n          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Phases_of_the_Sun_(NHemi).png" title="Enlarge">\n          </a>\n         </div>\n         The annual cycle of insolation (Sun energy, shown in blue) with key points for seasons (middle),\n         <a href="/wiki/Quarter_days" title="Quarter days">\n          quarter days\n         </a>\n         (top) and\n         <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cross-quarter_days" title="Cross-quarter days">\n          cross-quarter days\n         </a>\n         (bottom) along with months (lower) and Zodiac houses (upper). The cycle of temperature (shown in pink) is delayed by\n         <a href="/wiki/Seasonal_lag" title="Seasonal lag">\n          seasonal lag\n         </a>\n         .\n        </div>\n       </div>\n      </div>\n      <p>\n       Solar timing is based on insolation in which the solstices and equinoxes are seen as the midpoints of the seasons. It was the method for reckoning seasons in medieval Europe, especially by the\n       <a href="/wiki/Celts" title="Celts">\n        Celts\n       </a>\n       , and is still ceremonially observed in some east Asian countries. Summer is defined as the quarter of the year with the greatest insolation and winter as the quarter with the least.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       The solar seasons change at the cross-quarter days, which are about 3\xe2\x80\x934 weeks earlier than the meteorological seasons and 6\xe2\x80\x937 weeks earlier than seasons starting at equinoxes and solstices. Thus, the day of greatest insolation is designated "midsummer" as noted in\n       <a href="/wiki/William_Shakespeare" title="William Shakespeare">\n        William Shakespeare\n       </a>\n       \'s play\n       <i>\n        <a href="/wiki/A_Midsummer_Night%27s_Dream" title="A Midsummer Night\'s Dream">\n         A Midsummer Night\'s Dream\n        </a>\n       </i>\n       , which is set on the summer solstice. On the Celtic calendar, the traditional first day of winter is 1 November (\n       <a href="/wiki/Samhain" title="Samhain">\n        Samhain\n       </a>\n       , the Celtic origin of\n       <a href="/wiki/Halloween" title="Halloween">\n        Halloween\n       </a>\n       ); spring starts 1 February (\n       <a href="/wiki/Imbolc" title="Imbolc">\n        Imbolc\n       </a>\n       , the Celtic origin of\n       <a href="/wiki/Groundhog_Day" title="Groundhog Day">\n        Groundhog Day\n       </a>\n       ); summer begins 1 May (\n       <a href="/wiki/Beltane" title="Beltane">\n        Beltane\n       </a>\n       , the Celtic origin of\n       <a href="/wiki/May_Day" title="May Day">\n        May Day\n       </a>\n       ); the first day of autumn is 1 August (Celtic\n       <a href="/wiki/Lughnasadh" title="Lughnasadh">\n        Lughnasadh\n       </a>\n       ). The Celtic dates corresponded to four\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pagan" title="Pagan">\n        Pagan\n       </a>\n       agricultural festivals.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       The\n       <a href="/wiki/Chinese_calendar" title="Chinese calendar">\n        traditional calendar in China\n       </a>\n       forms the basis of other such systems in\n       <a href="/wiki/East_Asia" title="East Asia">\n        East Asia\n       </a>\n       . Its seasons are traditionally based on 24 periods known as\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solar_terms" title="Solar terms">\n        solar terms\n       </a>\n       .\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">\n        <a href="#cite_note-16">\n         [16]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       The four seasons\n       <i>\n        ch\xc5\xabn\n       </i>\n       (\n       <span lang="zh">\n        <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E6%98%A5" title="wikt:\xe6\x98\xa5">\n         \xe6\x98\xa5\n        </a>\n       </span>\n       ),\n       <i>\n        xi\xc3\xa0\n       </i>\n       (\n       <span lang="zh">\n        <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E5%A4%8F" title="wikt:\xe5\xa4\x8f">\n         \xe5\xa4\x8f\n        </a>\n       </span>\n       ),\n       <i>\n        qi\xc5\xab\n       </i>\n       (\n       <span lang="zh">\n        <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E7%A7%8B" title="wikt:\xe7\xa7\x8b">\n         \xe7\xa7\x8b\n        </a>\n       </span>\n       ), and\n       <i>\n        d\xc5\x8dng\n       </i>\n       (\n       <span lang="zh">\n        <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E5%86%AC" title="wikt:\xe5\x86\xac">\n         \xe5\x86\xac\n        </a>\n       </span>\n       ) are universally translated as "spring", "summer", "autumn", and "winter" but actually begin much earlier, with the solstices and equinoxes forming the\n       <i>\n        midpoint\n       </i>\n       of each season rather than their\n       <i>\n        start\n       </i>\n       . class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Season&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Polar day and night">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h2>\n      <p>\n       Any point north of the\n       <a href="/wiki/Arctic_Circle" title="Arctic Circle">\n        Arctic Circle\n       </a>\n       or south of the\n       <a href="/wiki/Antarctic_Circle" title="Antarctic Circle">\n        Antarctic Circle\n       </a>\n       will have one period in the summer when the sun does not set, and one period in the winter when the sun does not rise. At progressively higher latitudes, the maximum periods of "\n       <a href="/wiki/Midnight_sun" title="Midnight sun">\n        midnight sun\n       </a>\n       " and "\n       <a href="/wiki/Polar_night" title="Polar night">\n        polar night\n       </a>\n       " are progressively longer.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       For example, at the military and weather station\n       <a href="/wiki/Alert,_Nunavut" title="Alert, Nunavut">\n        Alert\n       </a>\n       located at 82\xc2\xb030\xe2\x80\xb205\xe2\x80\xb3N and 62\xc2\xb020\xe2\x80\xb220\xe2\x80\xb3W, on the northern tip of\n       <a href="/wiki/Ellesmere_Island" title="Ellesmere Island">\n        Ellesmere Island\n       </a>\n       ,\n       <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">\n        Canada\n       </a>\n       (about 450\n       <a href="/wiki/Nautical_mile" title="Nautical mile">\n        nautical miles\n       </a>\n       or 830\xc2\xa0km from the North Pole), the sun begins to peek above the horizon for minutes per day at the end of February and each day it climbs higher and stays up longer; by 21 March, the sun is up for over 12 hours. On 6 April the sun rises at 0522\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/UTC" title="UTC">\n        UTC\n       </a>\n       and remains above the horizon until it sets below the horizon again on 6 September at 0335 UTC. By October 13 the sun is above the horizon for only 1 hour 30 minutes and on October 14 it does not rise above the horizon at all and remains below the horizon until it rises again on 27 February.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17">\n        <a href="#cite_note-17">\n         [17]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       First light comes in late January because the sky has\n       <a href="/wiki/Twilight" title="Twilight">\n        twilight\n       </a>\n       , being a glow on the horizon, for increasing hours each day, for more than a month before the sun first appears with its disc above the horizon. From mid-November to mid-January, there is no twilight.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       In the weeks surrounding 21 June, in the northern polar region, the sun is at its highest elevation, appearing to circle the sky there without going below the horizon. Eventually, it does go below the horizon, for progressively longer periods each day until around the middle of October, when it disappears for the last time until the following February. For a few more weeks, "day" is marked by decreasing periods of twilight. Eventually, from mid-November to mid-January, there is no twilight and it is continuously dark. So, if we can observe a change in daily floral/animal events, the season is changing. In this sense, ecological seasons are defined in absolute terms, unlike calendar-based methods in which the seasons are relative. If specific conditions associated with a particular ecological season don\'t normally occur in a particular region, then that area cannot be said to experience that season on a regular basis.\n      </p>\n      <h3>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Modern_mid-latitude_ecological">\n        Modern mid-latitude ecological\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Season&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Modern mid-latitude ecological">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h3>\n      <p>\n       Six seasons can be distinguished which do not have fixed calendar-based dates like the meteorological and astronomical seasons.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18">\n        <a href="#cite_note-18">\n         [18]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       Mild\n       <a href="/wiki/Oceanic_climate" title="Oceanic climate">\n        oceanic\n       </a>\n       regions tend to experience the beginning of the hibernal season up to a month later than\n       <a href="/wiki/K%C3%B6ppen_climate_classification#Group_D:_Continental.2Fmicrothermal_climates" title="K\xc3\xb6ppen climate classification">\n        continental climates\n       </a>\n       , while the prevernal and vernal seasons begin up to a month earlier near to the sea. For example, prevernal\n       <a href="/wiki/Crocus" title="Crocus">\n        crocus\n       </a>\n       blooms typically appear as early as February in mild coastal areas of\n       <a href="/wiki/British_Columbia" title="British Columbia">\n        British Columbia\n       </a>\n       , the\n       <a href="/wiki/British_Isles" title="British Isles">\n        British Isles\n       </a>\n       , and western and southern\n       <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">\n        Europe\n       </a>\n       . The actual dates for each season vary by climate region and can shift from one year to the next. Average dates listed here are for mild and cool temperate climate zones in the Northern Hemisphere:\n      </p>\n      <ul>\n       <li>\n        <b>\n         Prevernal\n        </b>\n        (early or pre-spring): Begins February or late January (mild temperate), March (cool temperate). Deciduous tree buds begin to swell. Migrating birds fly from winter to summer habitats.\n       </li>\n       <li>\n        <b>\n         Vernal\n        </b>\n        (spring): Begins March (mild temperate), April (cool temperate). Tree buds burst into leaves. Birds establish territories and begin mating and nesting.\n       </li>\n       <li>\n        <b>\n         Estival\n        </b>\n        (high summer): Begins June in most temperate climates. Trees in full leaf. Birds hatch and raise offspring.\n       </li>\n       <li>\n        <b>\n         Serotinal\n        </b>\n        (late summer): Generally begins mid to late August. Deciduous leaves begin to change color. Young birds reach maturity and join other adult birds preparing for autumn migration. The traditional "harvest season" begins.\n       </li>\n       <li>\n        <b>\n         Autumnal\n        </b>\n        (autumn): Generally begins mid to late September. Tree leaves in full color then turn brown and fall to the ground. Birds migrate back to wintering areas.\n       </li>\n       <li>\n        <b>\n         Hibernal\n        </b>\n        (winter): Begins December (mild temperate), November (cool temperate). Deciduous trees are bare and fallen leaves begin to decay. Migrating birds settled in winter habitats.\n       </li>\n      </ul>\n      <h3>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Tropical_ecological">\n        Tropical ecological\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Season&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Tropical ecological">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h3>\n      <p>\n       In the tropics, where seasonal dates also vary, it is more common to speak of the\n       <a href="/wiki/Wet_season" title="Wet season">\n        rainy\n       </a>\n       (or wet, or\n       <a href="/wiki/Monsoon" title="Monsoon">\n        monsoon\n       </a>\n       ) season versus the\n       <a href="/wiki/Dry_season" title="Dry season">\n        dry season\n       </a>\n       . For example, in\n       <a href="/wiki/Nicaragua" title="Nicaragua">\n        Nicaragua\n       </a>\n       the dry season (November to April) is called \'summer\' and the rainy season (May to October) is called \'winter\', even though it is located in the northern hemisphere. In some tropical areas a three-way division into hot, rainy, and cool season is used. There is no noticeable change in the amount of sunlight at different times of the year. However, many regions (such as the northern\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Indian_ocean" title="Indian ocean">\n        Indian ocean\n       </a>\n       ) are subject to\n       <a href="/wiki/Monsoon" title="Monsoon">\n        monsoon\n       </a>\n       <a href="/wiki/Rain" title="Rain">\n        rain\n       </a>\n       and wind cycles.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       Floral and animal activity variation near the equator depends more on wet/dry cycles than seasonal temperature variations, with different species flowering (or emerging from cocoons) at specific times before, during, or after the monsoon season. Thus, the tropics are characterized by numerous "mini-seasons" within the larger seasonal blocks of time.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       In the tropical parts of Australia in the northern parts of\n       <a href="/wiki/Queensland" title="Queensland">\n        Queensland\n       </a>\n       ,\n       <a href="/wiki/Western_Australia" title="Western Australia">\n        Western Australia\n       </a>\n       and the\n       <a href="/wiki/Northern_Territory" title="Northern Territory">\n        Northern Territory\n       </a>\n       , wet and dry seasons are observed in addition to or in place of temperate season names.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19">\n        <a href="#cite_note-19">\n         [19]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <h3>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Indigenous_ecological">\n        Indigenous ecological\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Season&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Indigenous ecological">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h3>\n      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">\n       See also:\n       <a href="/wiki/Indigenous_Australian_seasons" title="Indigenous Australian seasons">\n        Indigenous Australian seasons\n       </a>\n      </div>\n      <p>\n       Indigenous people in polar, temperate and tropical climates of northern Eurasia, the Americas, Africa, Oceania, and Australia have traditionally defined the seasons ecologically by observing the activity of the plants, animals and weather around them. Each separate tribal group traditionally observes different seasons determined according to local criteria that can vary from the hibernation of polar bears on the arctic tundras to the growing seasons of plants in the tropical rainforests. In Australia, some tribes have up to eight seasons in a year,\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Australian_weather_and_the_seasons_10-1">\n        <a href="#cite_note-Australian_weather_and_the_seasons-10">\n         [10]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       as do the Sami people in Scandinavia. Many indigenous people who no longer live directly off the land in traditional often nomadic styles, now observe modern methods of seasonal reckoning according to what is customary in their particular country or region.\n      </p>\n      <h2>\n       <span id=\'"Official"_designations\'>\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id=".22Official.22_designations">\n        "Official" designations\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Season&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title=\'Edit section: "Official" designations\'>\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h2>\n      <p>\n       As noted, a variety of dates are used in different countries to mark the changes of seasons, especially those that are calendar based. These observances are often declared "official" within their respective jurisdictions by the local or national media, even when the weather or climate is contradictory.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-official_spring_20-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-official_spring-20">\n         [20]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       However they are mainly a matter of custom only, and have not generally been proclaimed by governments north or south of the equator for civil purposes.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-official_seasons_21-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-official_seasons-21">\n         [21]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22">\n        <a href="#cite_note-22">\n         [22]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       The\n       <a href="/wiki/Met_Office" title="Met Office">\n        Met Office\n       </a>\n       in the UK uses a three-month definition, e.g. declaring "spring" to be March, April, and May.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23">\n        <a href="#cite_note-23">\n         [23]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       At least one researcher writing in the\n       <i>\n        Bulletin of the American Meteorological Society\n       </i>\n       compared various definitions to actual temperature changes, and recommended this three-month definition for general use in the United States.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24">\n        <a href="#cite_note-24">\n         [24]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <h2>\n       <span
