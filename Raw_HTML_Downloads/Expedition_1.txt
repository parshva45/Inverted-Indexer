The three-person crew stayed aboard the station for 136 days, from November 2000 to March 2001. It was the beginning of an uninterrupted human presence on the station which continues as of November 2017.\n       <a href="/wiki/Expedition_2" title="Expedition 2">\n        Expedition 2\n       </a>\n       , which also had three crew members, immediately followed Expedition 1.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       The official start of the expedition occurred when the crew docked to the station on 2 November 2000, aboard the Russian spacecraft\n       <a href="/wiki/Soyuz_TM-31" title="Soyuz TM-31">\n        Soyuz TM-31\n       </a>\n       , which had launched two days earlier.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SPACE-Nov2_3-1">\n        <a href="#cite_note-SPACE-Nov2-3">\n         [2]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       During their mission, the Expedition 1 crew activated various systems on board the station, unpacked equipment that had been delivered, and hosted three visiting Space Shuttle crews and two unmanned Russian\n       <a href="/wiki/Progress_(spacecraft)" title="Progress (spacecraft)">\n        Progress resupply vehicles\n       </a>\n       . The crew was very busy throughout the mission,\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SPACE-Jan24_7-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-SPACE-Jan24-7">\n         [4]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       which was declared a success.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       The three visiting Space Shuttles brought equipment, supplies, and key components of the space station. The first of these,\n       <a href="/wiki/STS-97" title="STS-97">\n        STS-97\n       </a>\n       , docked in early December 2000, and brought the first pair of large\n       <a href="/wiki/Integrated_Truss_Structure#Solar_arrays" title="Integrated Truss Structure">\n        U.S. photovoltaic arrays\n       </a>\n       , which increased the station\'s power capabilities fivefold.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HSF-STS97_8-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-HSF-STS97-8">\n         [NASA 4]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       The second visiting shuttle mission was\n       <a href="/wiki/STS-98" title="STS-98">\n        STS-98\n       </a>\n       , which was docked in mid-February 2001, delivered the US$1.4\xc2\xa0billion research module\n       <a href="/wiki/Destiny_(ISS_module)" title="Destiny (ISS module)">\n        <i>\n         Destiny\n        </i>\n       </a>\n       , which increased the mass of the station beyond that of\n       <i>\n        <a href="/wiki/Mir" title="Mir">\n         Mir\n        </a>\n       </i>\n       for the first time.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HSF-STS98_9-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-HSF-STS98-9">\n         [NASA 5]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       Mid-March 2001 saw the final shuttle visit of the expedition,\n       <a href="/wiki/STS-102" title="STS-102">\n        STS-102\n       </a>\n       , whose main purpose was to exchange the Expedition 1 crew with the next three-person long-duration crew,\n       <a href="/wiki/Expedition_2" title="Expedition 2">\n        Expedition 2\n       </a>\n       .\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HSF-STS102_10-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-HSF-STS102-10">\n         [NASA 6]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       The expedition ended when\n       <a href="/wiki/Space_Shuttle_Discovery" title="Space Shuttle Discovery">\n        <i>\n         Discovery\n        </i>\n       </a>\n       undocked from the station on 18 March 2001.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       The Expedition 1 crew consisted of an American commander and two Russians. The commander,\n       <a href="/wiki/William_Shepherd" title="William Shepherd">\n        Bill Shepherd\n       </a>\n       , had been in space three times before, all on shuttle missions which lasted at most a week. The Russians,\n       <a href="/wiki/Yuri_Gidzenko" title="Yuri Gidzenko">\n        Yuri Gidzenko\n       </a>\n       and\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sergei_K._Krikalev" title="Sergei K. Krikalev">\n        Sergei K. Krikalev\n       </a>\n       , both had previous long-duration spaceflights on\n       <i>\n        Mir\n       </i>\n       , with Krikalev having spent over a full year in space.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NASAyuri_11-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-NASAyuri-11">\n         [NASA 7]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NASAsergei_12-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-NASAsergei-12">\n         [NASA 8]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TIME-Nov2_13-0">\n        <a agency about the choice of Shepherd as mission commander due to his lack of experience.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TIME-Nov2_13-1">\n        <a href="#cite_note-TIME-Nov2-13">\n         [5]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       Flight engineer\n       <a href="/wiki/Sergei_Krikalev" title="Sergei Krikalev">\n        Sergei Krikalev\n       </a>\n       had spent over a year in orbit, mostly on\n       <i>\n        Mir\n       </i>\n       , and would become the first person to visit the ISS twice.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ISS-49_15-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-ISS-49-15">\n         [NASA 10]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       He had felt excitement to have been one of the first people to enter the\n       <a href="/wiki/Zarya" title="Zarya">\n        Zarya\n       </a>\n       module (the first component of the space station) in 1998, during\n       <a href="/wiki/STS-88" title="STS-88">\n        STS-88\n       </a>\n       , and was looking forward to returning.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sergei-Int_16-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-Sergei-Int-16">\n         [NASA 11]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       <a href="/wiki/Yuri_Gidzenko" title="Yuri Gidzenko">\n        Yuri Gidzenko\n       </a>\n       was designated commander and pilot of the two-day Soyuz mission to the station, had one previous spaceflight, which was a 180-day stay aboard\n       <i>\n        <a href="/wiki/Mir" title="Mir">\n         Mir\n        </a>\n       </i>\n       .\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NASAyuri_11-1">\n        <a href="#cite_note-NASAyuri-11">\n         [NASA 7]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       Shepherd was only the second U.S. astronaut to be launched in a Russian spacecraft, the first being\n       <a href="/wiki/Norman_Thagard" title="Norman Thagard">\n        Norman Thagard\n       </a>\n       , who launched on\n       <a href="/wiki/Soyuz_TM-21" title="Soyuz TM-21">\n        Soyuz TM-21\n       </a>\n       to visit\n       <i>\n        Mir\n       </i>\n       in 1995.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NASApresskit_17-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-NASApresskit-17">\n         [NASA 12]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       Shepherd expected one of the biggest challenges for the ISS would be the compatibility of technologies, such as that between Russian and U.S. technologies.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Shep-Inv_18-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-Shep-Inv-18">\n         [NASA 13]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <h2>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Backup_Crew">\n        Backup Crew\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Expedition_1&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Backup Crew">\n </tr>\n      </table>\n      <h2>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Background">\n        Background\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Expedition_1&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Background">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h2>\n      <p>\n       The first component of the space station was the\n       <a href="/wiki/Zarya" title="Zarya">\n        Zarya\n       </a>\n       module, which was launched unmanned in November 1998.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NASAzarya_19-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-NASAzarya-19">\n         [NASA 14]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       Following this launch, and prior to Expedition 1, there were\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_human_spaceflights_to_the_ISS" title="List of human spaceflights to the ISS">\n        five manned Space Shuttle flights\n       </a>\n       and\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_unmanned_spaceflights_to_the_ISS" title="List of unmanned spaceflights to the ISS">\n        two unmanned Russian flights\n       </a>\n       to the ISS.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NASA-Objectives_20-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-NASA-Objectives-20">\n         [NASA 15]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       Some of these flights delivered\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ISS_assembly_sequence" title="ISS assembly sequence">\n        large modules\n       </a>\n       , such as the pressurized\n       <a href="/wiki/Unity_(ISS_module)" title="Unity (ISS module)">\n        <i>\n         Unity\n        </i>\n       </a>\n       and\n       <a href="/wiki/Zvezda_(ISS_module)" title="Zvezda (ISS module)">\n        <i>\n         Zvezda\n        </i>\n       </a>\n       modules, and the first piece of the\n       <a href="/wiki/Integrated_Truss_Structure" title="Integrated Truss Structure">\n        Integrated Truss Structure\n       </a>\n       . The manned flights were used for partial assembly of the ISS, as well as to start unpacking the supplies and equipment that were being delivered.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TIME-Nov2_13-2">\n        <a href="#cite_note-TIME-Nov2-13">\n         [5]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       Prior to Expedition 1, Krikalev expected the ISS to be very similar to his experience on\n       <i>\n        Mir\n       </i>\n       ten years previous, due to the physical similarities of the stations\' components.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sergei-Int_16-1">\n        <a href="#cite_note-Sergei-Int-16">\n         [NASA 11]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       The launch of the Expedition 1 crew occurred a week before the\n       <a href="/wiki/United_States_presidential_election,_2000" title="United States presidential election, 2000">\n        United States presidential election\n       </a>\n       , so it got little attention in the United States.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYT-Nov1_21-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-NYT-Nov1-21">\n         [6]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       At the time of the mission, the station was expected to be completed in 2006, and be continuously inhabited until at least 2015.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYT-STS97_22-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-NYT-STS97-22">\n         [7]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       Due to several delays, including the fallout from the\n       <a href="/wiki/Space_Shuttle_Columbia_disaster" title="Space Shuttle Columbia disaster">\n        Space Shuttle\n        <i>\n         Columbia\n        </i>\n        disaster\n       </a>\n       , the station was completed in late 2011, thanks to\n       <a href="/wiki/STS-134" title="STS-134">\n        STS-134\n       </a>\n       .\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LaunchSched_23-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-LaunchSched-23">\n         [NASA 16]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <h2>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Mission_highlights">\n        Mission highlights\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Expedition_1&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Mission highlights">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h2>\n      <p>\n       The crew of three were on board the International Space Station for four and a half months, from early November 2000 to mid-March 2001. Major events during this time include the three-week-long\n       <a href="/wiki/Space_Shuttle_program" title="Space Shuttle program">\n        Space Shuttle\n       </a>\n       visits, which occurred in early December, mid-February, and at the end of the expedition in March.\n      </p>\n      <h3>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Launch_and_docking">\n        Launch and docking\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a a\n       <a href="/wiki/Soyuz-U" title="Soyuz-U">\n        Soyuz-U\n       </a>\n       rocket on\n       <a href="/wiki/Soyuz_TM-31" title="Soyuz TM-31">\n        Soyuz TM-31\n       </a>\n       from the\n       <a href="/wiki/Baikonur_Cosmodrome" title="Baikonur Cosmodrome">\n        Baikonur Cosmodrome\n       </a>\n       in\n       <a href="/wiki/Kazakhstan" title="Kazakhstan">\n        Kazakhstan\n       </a>\n       ; they used launch pad\n       <a href="/wiki/Gagarin%27s_Start" title="Gagarin\'s Start">\n        Gagarin\'s Start\n       </a>\n       , from which the first human crew members entered the complex.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       <b>\n        Alpha\n       </b>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       At the end of the first day on the station, Shepherd requested the use      ".\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TIME-Nov2_13-4">\n        <a href="#cite_note-TIME-Nov2-13">\n         [5]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       The name "\n       <i>\n        Alpha\n       </i>\n       " had previously been used for the station in the early 90\'s,\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GAO_26-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-GAO-26">\n         [10]\n      Mir 2\n       </i>\n       " for the ISS.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SPACE-Nov3_27-1">\n        <a href="#cite_note-SPACE-Nov3-27">\n         [11]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n <div class="magnify">\n          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Unity-Zarya-Zvezda_STS-106.jpg" title="Enlarge">\n          </a>\n         </div>\n         The configuration of the ISS at the start of Expedition 1. At this time the station did not have enough electricity to heat all three pressurized modules, so\n       <a href="/wiki/Unity_(ISS_module)" title="Unity (ISS module)">\n        <i>\n         Unity\n        </i>\n       </a>\n       was left unused and unheated.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SPACE-Jan7_31-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-SPACE-Jan7-31">\n         [15]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       <i>\n        Unity\n       </i>\n       had been used for the past two years to allow U.S. flight controllers to command ISS systems and read station system data.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       The Russian unmanned resupply spacecraft\n       <a href="/wiki/Progress_M1-4" title="Progress M1-4">\n        Progress M1-4\n       </a>\n       docked to the station on 18 November. The\n       <a href="/wiki/Progress_(spacecraft)" title="Progress (spacecraft)">\n        Progress\n       </a>\n       spacecraft\'s automatic docking system failed, necessitating a manual docking controlled by Gidzenko using the\n       <a href="/wiki/TORU" title="TORU">\n        TORU\n       </a>\n       docking system.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SPACE-Nov18_32-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-SPACE-Nov18-32">\n         [16]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       Although manual dockings are routine, they have caused some concern among flight controllers since an attempt in 1997 which resulted in the spacecraft colliding with\n       <i>\n        Mir\n       </i>\n       , causing significant damage.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Spaceflightnow-Dec21_33-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-Spaceflightnow-Dec21-33">\n         [17]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       The astronauts had a heavy workload in the first month, as Shepherd told reporters in a space-to-ground interview: "To me, the biggest challenge is trying to pack 30 hours into an 18-hour work day."\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SPACE-Nov29_34-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-SPACE-Nov29-34">\n         [18]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       Some of the early tasks took longer than scheduled. The shuttle also brought the first pair of U.S. provided\n       <a href="/wiki/Integrated_Truss_Structure#Solar_arrays" title="Integrated Truss Structure">\n        photovoltaic arrays\n       </a>\n       , which would provide\n       <a href="/wiki/Electrical_system_of_the_International_Space_Station" title="Electrical system of the International Space Station">\n        crucial electricity\n       </a>\n       for further development of the station. In total, STS-97 brought 17\xc2\xa0tons of equipment to the ISS, which also included expandable metal girders, batteries, electronics and cooling equipment.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYT-STS97_22-1">\n        <a href="#cite_note-NYT-STS97-22">\n         [7]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       Three\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Extra-vehicular_activity" title="Extra-vehicular activity">\n        spacewalks\n       </a>\n       were conducted by the crew of STS-97, all of which were completed prior to opening the hatch between shuttle and station. On 8 December, the hatch between the two was opened and the two crews greeted each other for the first time. It had remained closed to maintain their respective atmospheric pressures.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NASA-STS97_36-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-NASA-STS97-36">\n         [NASA 17]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       The Expedition 1 crew took this opportunity to leave the station and tour the inside of the space shuttle, which was thought to be good for their psychological well-being.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SPACE-Jan7_31-1">\n        <a href="#cite_note-SPACE-Jan7-31">\n         [15]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       <b>\n        Progress M1-4\n       </b>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       Prior to\n       <i>\n        Endeavour\n       </i>\n       docking, the Russian resupply spacecraft\n       <a href="/wiki/Progress_M1-4" title="Progress M1-4">\n        Progress M1-4\n       </a>\n       , which came to the station in mid-November, was undocked to make room for the space shuttle.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYT-Dec2_37-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-NYT-Dec2-37">\n         [20]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       This Progress spacecraft remained undocked for the duration of STS-97, parked in orbit about a mile away from the station. It docked manually again with the station on 26 December by Gidzenko, after\n       <i>\n        Endeavour\n       </i>\n       left. The automatic docking system for this Progress spacecraft had failed on the first docking in November.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DecLog_38-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-DecLog-38">\n         [NASA 18]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       The crew spent much of the following week unloading the Progress spacecraft.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ISS-63_39-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-ISS-63-39">\n         [NASA 19]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       <b>\n        Christmas and New Year\n       </b>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       On Christmas Day, the Expedition 1 crew were given the day off work. They opened presents delivered by\n       <i>\n        Endeavour\n       </i>\n       and the Progress supply ship.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BBC-Dec25_40-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-BBC-Dec25-40">\n         [21]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       They also each took turns speaking to their families. In the following days they did several video\n       <a href="/wiki/Telecommunications_link" title="Telecommunications link">\n        downlinks\n       </a>\n       , some with Russian TV stations.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DecLog_38-1">\n        <a href="#cite_note-DecLog-38">\n         [NASA 18]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       The crew had a quiet New Year. Citing a Naval tradition, for the New Year\'s entry of the station\'s log, Shepherd provided a poem on behalf of the crew.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ISS-64_41-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-ISS-64-41">\n         [NASA 20]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <h3>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="STS-98">\n        STS-98\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Expedition_1&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: STS-98">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h3>\n      <div class="thumb tleft">\n       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;">\n        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Zvezda_port_hole.jpg">\n         <img alt="photograph" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2336" data-file-width="2689" height="174" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bc/Zvezda_port_hole.jpg/200px-Zvezda_port_hole.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bc/Zvezda_port_hole.jpg/300px-Zvezda_port_hole.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bc/Zvezda_port_hole.jpg/400px-Zvezda_port_hole.jpg 2x" width="200"/>\n        </a>\n        <div class="thumbcaption">\n         <div class="magnify">\n          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Zvezda_port_hole.jpg" title="Enlarge">\n          </a>\n         </div>\n         <a href="/wiki/Sergei_Krikalev" title="Sergei Krikalev">\n          S. (NASA)\n        </div>\n       </div>\n      </div>\n      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">\n       Main article:\n       <a href="/wiki/STS-98" title="STS-98">\n        STS-98\n       </a>\n      </div>\n      <p>\n       On 9 February 2001, Space Shuttle\n       <a href="/wiki/Space_Shuttle_Atlantis" title="Space Shuttle Atlantis">\n        <i>\n         Atlantis\n        </i>\n       </a>\n       docked to the ISS, bringing the five American crew members of\n       <a href="/wiki/STS-98" title="STS-98">\n        STS-98\n       </a>\n       temporarily to the station. The mission was originally planned for mid-January, but was delayed due to NASA\'s concerns about some cables on the shuttles.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BBC-Feb7_42-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-BBC-Feb7-42">\n         [22]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       This mission brought the U.S. built\n       <a href="/wiki/Destiny_(ISS_module)" title="Destiny (ISS module)">\n        Destiny laboratory\n       </a>\n       , which has a mass of 16\n       <a href="/wiki/Short_ton" title="Short ton">\n        short tons\n       </a>\n       . It was installed with the use of the shuttle\'s robotic\n       <a href="/wiki/Canadarm" title="Canadarm">\n        Canadarm\n       </a>\n       , controlled by\n       <a href="/wiki/Marsha_Ivins" title="Marsha Ivins">\n        Marsha Ivins\n       </a>\n       . Astronauts\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thomas_D._Jones" title="Thomas D. Jones">\n        Thomas D. Jones\n       </a>\n       and\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Robert_L._Curbeam" title="Robert L. Curbeam">\n        Robert L. Curbeam\n       </a>\n       helped with the installation during a spacewalk. The\n       <i>\n        Destiny\n       </i>\n       module had a cost of US$1.4\xc2\xa0billion, and would be used primarily for scientific research.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NASA-STS98_43-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-NASA-STS98-43">\n         [NASA 21]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BBC-STS98_44-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-BBC-STS98-44">\n         [23]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       During the spacewalk an\n       <a href="/wiki/Ammonia" title="Ammonia">\n        ammonia\n       </a>\n       coolant leak created a contamination scare, which happened when Curbeam was hooking up coolant lines to\n       <i>\n        Destiny\n       </i>\n       .\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SPACE-Feb10_45-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-SPACE-Feb10-45">\n         [24]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       The other two spacewalks went ahead without any problems. While the Shuttle was docked, the control of the station\'s orientation was switched from propellants to electrically powered gyroscopes, which had been installed in September 2000. The gyroscopes had not been used earlier due to the lack of key navigational electronics.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NASA-STS97-7_46-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-NASA-STS97-7-46">\n         [NASA 22]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       By the end of STS-98, the crew of Expedition 1 had been on the station for over three months, and Shepherd stated that he was "ready to come home".\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SPACE-Feb20_47-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-SPACE-Feb20-47">\n         [25]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       NASA used several techniques to prevent the three crew members from suffering the effects of the "three-month wall" psychological barrier, which had caused depression in previous astronauts. For example, they allowed more time for the crew to speak to their families via videophone, and they also encouraged them to watch movies and listen to music they like.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SPACE-Jan7_31-2">\n        <a href="#cite_note-SPACE-Jan7-31">\n         [15]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       <b>\n        Progress M-44\n       </b>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       On 28 February the third\n       <a href="/wiki/Progress_(spacecraft)" title="Progress (spacecraft)">\n        Progress spacecraft\n       </a>\n       to visit the ISS,\n       <a href="/wiki/Progress_M-44" title="Progress M-44">\n        Progress M-44\n       </a>\n       , docked to the Zvezda module. It brought air, food, rocket fuel and other equipment.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BBC-Feb26_48-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-BBC-Feb26-48">\n         [26]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       It remained docked until\n       <a href="/wiki/Expedition_2" title="Expedition 2">\n        Expedition 2\n       </a>\n       , when it was intentionally burnt up during\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atmospheric_reentry" title="Atmospheric reentry">\n        atmospheric reentry\n       </a>\n       , like all\n       <a href="/wiki/Progress_(spacecraft)" title="Progress (spacecraft)">\n        Progress spacecraft\n       </a>\n       .\n      </p>\n      <h3>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="STS-102">\n        STS-102\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Expedition_1&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: STS-102">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h3>\n      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">\n       Main article:\n       <a href="/wiki/STS-102" title="STS-102">\n        STS-102\n       </a>\n      </div>\n      <p>\n       Space Shuttle\n       <a href="/wiki/Space_Shuttle_Discovery" title="Space Shuttle Discovery">\n        <i>\n         Discovery\n        </i>\n       </a>\n       docked on 10 March 2001, bringing to the ISS the new long-duration three-person crew of\n       <a href="/wiki/Expedition_2" title="Expedition 2">\n        Expedition 2\n       </a>\n       , as well as four short-term crew members of\n       <a href="/wiki/STS-102" title="STS-102">\n        STS-102\n       </a>\n       . A few hours after docking, the hatch opened, and all ten astronauts greeted each other, setting a new record for the number people simultaneously in the ISS.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NASA-STS102_49-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-NASA-STS102-49">\n         [NASA 23]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       The day after docking, American astronauts\n       <a href="/wiki/James_S._Voss" title="James S. Voss">\n        Jim Voss\n       </a>\n       and\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Susan_J._Helms" title="Susan J. Helms">\n        Susan Helms\n       </a>\n       began a spacewalk which ended up being nearly nine hours long, and still holds the record for the longest spacewalk ever performed, as of August 2010. The length of the spacewalk was partially due to some mistakes, including Voss accidentally releasing a small tool. Unable to retrieve it, NASA engineers tracked the tool, and decided to use\n       <i>\n        Discovery\'\n       </i>\n       s thrusters on 14 March to boost the station four kilometers higher, to ensure the ISS would not collide with the piece of\n       <a href="/wiki/Space_debris" title="Space debris">\n        space debris\n       </a>\n       .\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BBC-Mar15_50-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-BBC-Mar15-50">\n         [27]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       <b>\n        Transferring expedition crews\n       </b>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       By 14 March, the expedition crews had completed the change over, but until the shuttle undocked, Shepherd officially remained commander of the station.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BBC-Mar14_51-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-BBC-Mar14-51">\n         [28]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       The morning of the 14th the astronauts\' wake-up call was the song "\n       <a href="/wiki/Should_I_Stay_or_Should_I_Go" title="Should I Stay or Should I Go">\n        Should I Stay or Should I Go\n       </a>\n       " by\n       <a href="/wiki/The_Clash" title="The Clash">\n        The Clash\n       </a>\n       , at the request of Shepherd\'s wife.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BBC-Mar15_50-1">\n        <a href="#cite_note-BBC-Mar15-50">\n         [27]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       Shepherd, a former\n       <a href="/wiki/United_States_Navy_SEALs" title="United States Navy SEALs">\n        Navy SEAL\n       </a>\n       , said during the change over ceremony: "May the good will, spirit and sense of mission we had enjoyed on board endure. Sail her well."\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SPACE-Mar18_52-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-SPACE-Mar18-52">\n         [29]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       The commander of\n       <i>\n        Discovery\n       </i>\n       ,\n       <a href="/wiki/Jim_Wetherbee" title="Jim Wetherbee">\n        Jim Wetherbee\n       </a>\n       , said ""For Captain Shepherd and his crew, we hold you in admiration as we prepare to bring you home. This has been an arduous duty for you. This ship was not built in a safe harbor. It was built on the high seas."\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SPACE-Mar18_52-1">\n        <a href="#cite_note-SPACE-Mar18-52">\n         [29]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <h3>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Undocking_and_landing">\n        Undocking and landing\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Expedition_1&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Undocking and landing">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h3>\n      <p>\n       The crew\'s four and a half-month tour aboard the ISS officially ended on 18 March 2001, when\n       <i>\n        Discovery\n       </i>\n       undocked. The Expedition 1 crew returned home to Earth on\n       <a href="/wiki/STS-102" title="STS-102">\n        STS-102\n       </a>\n       , landing on 21 March 2001, on a rare night landing at 2:30\xc2\xa0am local time.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BBC-Mar21_53-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-BBC-Mar21-53">\n         [30]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       Two days after the landing, coincidentally,\n       <i>\n        <a href="/wiki/Mir" title="Mir">\n         Mir\n        </a>\n       </i>\n       was intentionally burned up during\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atmospheric_reentry" title="Atmospheric reentry">\n        atmospheric reentry\n       </a>\n       , ending its 15 years in orbit.\n      </p>\n      <h2>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Daily_activities">\n        Daily activities\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Expedition_1&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Daily activities">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h2>\n      <p>\n       In a typical day, each crew member divided his time between physical exercise, station assembly and maintenance, experiments, communications with ground personnel, personal time, and bio-needs activities (such as rest and eating).\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ISSpaper_54-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-ISSpaper-54">\n         [31]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       The crew\'s daily schedule usually operated on UTC; for example, a typical morning had been scheduled to begin with an electronic wake-up tone at about 05:00 UTC.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NASApresskit_17-2">\n        <a href="#cite_note-NASApresskit-17">\n         [NASA 12]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       But during the expedition, a more typical wake-up time was actually between 06:00 and 07:00 UTC.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DecLog_38-2">\n        <a href="#cite_note-DecLog-38">\n         [NASA 18]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JanLog_55-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-JanLog-55">\n         [NASA 24]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       The crew\'s sleep habits were sometimes shifted to accommodate the schedules of visiting shuttles or resupply vehicles.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ISSreportNov9_56-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-ISSreportNov9-56">\n         [NASA 25]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       Following the wake-up call, the crew was given some time to clean up, have breakfast, and read e-mail which had been\n       <a href="/wiki/Telecommunications_link" title="Telecommunications link">\n        uplinked\n       </a>\n       to them from flight controllers.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NASApresskit_17-3">\n        <a href="#cite_note-NASApresskit-17">\n         [NASA 12]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       Their work day included a lunch break at midday (UTC), and ended with a mid-afternoon planning session with flight controllers, regarding the next day\'s activities.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NASApresskit_17-4">\n        <a href="#cite_note-NASApresskit-17">\n         [NASA 12]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SPACE-NOV3_35-1">\n        <a href="#cite_note-SPACE-NOV3-35">\n         [19]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       Most days ended with some entertainment, with the crew watching all or part of a movie; this was thought to be good for crew bonding as well as their psychological well-being.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SPACE-Jan7_31-3">\n        <a href="#cite_note-SPACE-Jan7-31">\n         [15]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       After watching\n       <a href="/wiki/2010_(film)" title="2010 (film)">\n        <i>\n         2010\n        </i>\n       </a>\n       , the sequel to\n       <i>\n        <a href="/wiki/2001:_A_Space_Odyssey_(film)" title="2001: A Space Odyssey (film)">\n         2001: A Space Odyssey\n        </a>\n       </i>\n       , Shepherd commented, "[There is] something strange about watching a movie about a space expedition when you\'re actually on a space expedition".\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JanLog_55-1">\n        <a href="#cite_note-JanLog-55">\n         [NASA 24]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       An important part of the crew\'s schedule was regular exercise. They had three pieces of equipment for this: a\n       <a href="/wiki/Stationary_bicycle" title="Stationary bicycle">\n        stationary bicycle\n       </a>\n       , a treadmill (\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Treadmill_with_Vibration_Isolation_System" title="Treadmill with Vibration Isolation System">\n        TVIS\n       </a>\n       ), and a resistance device (IRED) for weight-lifting. The bicycle malfunctioned in mid-December 2000, and wasn\'t fixed until March.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DecLog_38-3">\n        <a href="#cite_note-DecLog-38">\n         [NASA 18]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FebMarLog_57-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-FebMarLog-57">\n         [NASA 26]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       The treadmill, which used\n       <a href="/wiki/Bungee_cord" title="Bungee cord">\n        bungee cords\n       </a>\n       to keep the crew member in place, was designed to reduce the vibrations caused by running. A normal treadmill would have produced enough vibrations to shake the station, and potentially affect the sensitive science experiments on board. The treadmill malfunctioned near the end of February, but some in-flight maintenance fixed the problem within a week.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FebMarLog_57-1">\n        <a href="#cite_note-FebMarLog-57">\n         [NASA 26]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <h3>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Ground_communications">\n        Ground communications\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Expedition_1&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Ground communications">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h3>\n      <div class="thumb tright">\n       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">\n        <a class="image" Gidzenko">\n          Yuri Gidzenko\n         </a>\n         , in the\n         <i>\n          Zvezda\n         </i>\n         Service Module, communicates with ground controllers\n        </div>\n       </div>\n      </div>\n      <p>\n       Until the\n       <i>\n        Unity\n       </i>\n       module was available for use a month into the mission, the astronauts used the Russian\n       <a href="/wiki/Very_high_frequency" title="Very high frequency">\n        VHF\n       </a>\n       communications gear (also called the "Regul radio link") in\n       <i>\n        Zvezda\n       </i>\n       and the\n       <i>\n        Zarya\n       </i>\n       module to communicate with the Russian Mission Control Center (known as "TsUP") in Korolev, outside Moscow.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TIME-Nov2_13-5">\n        <a href="#cite_note-TIME-Nov2-13">\n         [5]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RuSpaceComm_58-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-RuSpaceComm-58">\n         [32]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FloridaToday_59-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-FloridaToday-59">\n         [33]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       The Russian technology didn\'t have the use of satellites, so they were restricted to ground passes (called a "comm pass") which lasted for only 10\xe2\x80\x9320 minutes. With the arrival of the solar arrays on\n       <a href="/wiki/STS-97" title="STS-97">\n        STS-97\n       </a>\n       , they activated the S-band Early Communication gear in the\n       <a href="/wiki/Unity_(ISS_module)" title="Unity (ISS module)">\n        Unity Module\n       </a>\n       , allowing for more continuous communication with Mission Control in Houston via NASA\'s network of\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tracking_and_Data_Relay_Satellite" title="Tracking and Data Relay Satellite">\n        Tracking and Data Relay Satellites\n       </a>\n       .\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       During\n       <a href="/wiki/STS-106" title="STS-106">\n        STS-106\n       </a>\n       in September 2000, the equipment for a\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ham_radio" title="Ham radio">\n        ham radio\n       </a>\n       was delivered to the station. The first \'ham\' contact with the ground by the Expedition 1 crew was on 13 November 2000 on a pass over Moscow, shortly followed by contact with\n       <a href="/wiki/Goddard_Space_Flight_Center" title="Goddard Space Flight Center">\n        Goddard Space Flight Center\n       </a>\n       in\n       <a href="/wiki/Greenbelt,_Maryland" title="Greenbelt, Maryland">\n        Greenbelt, Maryland\n       </a>\n       .\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NovLog_60-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-NovLog-60">\n         [NASA 27]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HFRadio_61-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-HFRadio-61">\n         [34]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       The crew reported that "Voice quality of ham radio continues to be well above any of our other links."\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NovLog_60-1">\n        <a href="#cite_note-NovLog-60">\n         [NASA 27]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       The\n       <i>\n        <a href="/wiki/Amateur_Radio_on_the_International_Space_Station" title="Amateur Radio on the International Space Station">\n         Amateur Radio on the International Space Station\n        </a>\n       </i>\n       project had the crew of the station to make brief windows to radio contact with schools and clubs on the ground. The first school to be contacted by the ISS was\n       <a href="/wiki/Luther_Burbank" title="Luther Burbank">\n        Luther Burbank\n       </a>\n       School in southwest\n       <a href="/wiki/Chicago" title="Chicago">\n        Chicago\n       </a>\n       . The contact had been planned for 19 December 2000, but due to technical problems, it was delayed to 21 December 2000.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jan30-school_62-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-Jan30-school-62">\n         [NASA 28]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       Due to the speed of the space station, the window of radio contact only lasted for 5\xe2\x80\x9310 minutes, which was usually enough for 10 to 20 questions.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ARISS_63-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-ARISS-63">\n         [NASA 29]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <h3>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Science_activities">\n        Science activities\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Expedition_1&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Science activities">\n         edit\n        </a>\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h3>\n      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">\n       Main article:\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Scientific_research_on_the_ISS" title="Scientific research on the ISS">\n        Scientific research on the ISS\n       </a>\n      </div>\n      <p>\n       Unlike subsequent expeditions, the crew of Expedition 1 had a somewhat modest amount of science experiments to conduct, due to the priority placed on station construction.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NASApresskit_17-5">\n        <a href="#cite_note-NASApresskit-17">\n         [NASA 12]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       The plasma crystal experiment, known as\n       <i>\n        PKE-Nefedov\n       </i>\n       , was one of the first\n       <a href="/wiki/Natural_science" title="Natural science">\n        natural science\n       </a>\n       experiments conducted on the space station. It was a collaboration between the\n       <a href="/wiki/Max_Planck_Institute_for_Extraterrestrial_Physics" title="Max Planck Institute for Extraterrestrial Physics">\n        Max Planck Institute for Extraterrestrial Physics\n       </a>\n       in\n       <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">\n        Germany\n       </a>\n       , and the\n       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Institute_for_High_Energy_Densities&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Institute for High Energy Densities (page does not exist)">\n        Institute for High Energy Densities\n       </a>\n       (part of the\n       <a href="/wiki/Russian_Academy_of_Sciences" title="Russian Academy of Sciences">\n        Russian Academy of Sciences\n       </a>\n       ).\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MPG_64-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-MPG-64">\n         [35]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MPG2_65-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-MPG2-65">\n         [36]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <div class="thumb tleft">\n       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:232px;">\n        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Popocatepetl-nasa.jpg">\n         <img alt="photograph" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1018" data-file-width="1530" height="153" volcanic ash from\n         <a href="/wiki/Popocat%C3%A9petl" title="Popocat\xc3\xa9petl">\n          Popocat\xc3\xa9petl\n         </a>\n         <a href="/wiki/Volcano" title="Volcano">\n          volcano\n         </a>\n         ,\n         <a href="/wiki/Mexico" title="Mexico">\n          Mexico\n         </a>\n         .\n        </div>\n       </div>\n      </div>\n      <p>\n       Like previous missions, the astronauts took many photos of Earth from the station, over 700 in total, which have been made freely available.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Photos_66-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-Photos-66">\n         [NASA 30]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       These\n       <i>\n        Crew Earth Observations\n       </i>\n       , are intended to record dynamic events on the Earth\'s surface such as storms, fires, or\n       <a href="/wiki/Volcano" title="Volcano">\n        volcanoes\n       </a>\n       .\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NASAceo_67-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-NASAceo-67">\n         [NASA 31]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       For example, a photo from 1 January 2001 shows\n       <a href="/wiki/Mount_Cleveland_(Alaska)" title="Mount Cleveland (Alaska)">\n        Mount Cleveland\n       </a>\n       ,\n       <a href="/wiki/Alaska" title="Alaska">\n        Alaska\n       </a>\n       , with a plume of smoke, prior to its eruption the following month.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-68">\n        <a href="#cite_note-68">\n         [NASA 32]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       On 23 January 2001, the crew observed a unique perspective of a plume of volcanic ash coming from\n       <a href="/wiki/Popocat%C3%A9petl" title="Popocat\xc3\xa9petl">\n        Popocat\xc3\xa9petl\n       </a>\n       , an active volcano 70 kilometres southeast of\n       <a href="/wiki/Mexico_City" title="Mexico City">\n        Mexico City\n       </a>\n       .\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MexicoVolcano_69-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-MexicoVolcano-69">\n         [NASA 33]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       An example of a low-maintenance experiment was the\n       <a href="/wiki/Protein_crystallization" title="Protein crystallization">\n        protein crystal growth\n       </a>\n       experiment, which had also been flown on previous shuttle missions.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NASApcg_70-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-NASApcg-70">\n         [NASA 34]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       The goal was to produce better protein crystallizations than those produced on the Earth, and hence allowing for a more accurate model of\n       <a href="/wiki/Protein_structure" title="Protein structure">\n        protein structures\n       </a>\n       . Of the 23 proteins and\n       <a href="/wiki/Virus" title="Virus">\n        viruses\n       </a>\n       attempted during Expedition 1, only four resulted in successful crystallizations, which was a lower success rate than predicted.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NASApcg_70-1">\n        <a href="#cite_note-NASApcg-70">\n         [NASA 34]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       Of those successful was the low-calorie sweetener\n       <a href="/wiki/Thaumatin" title="Thaumatin">\n        Thaumatin\n       </a>\n       , whose crystals diffracted at a higher resolution than Earth-grown crystal, which resulted in a more accurate protein structure model.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NASApcg_70-2">\n        <a href="#cite_note-NASApcg-70">\n         [NASA 34]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       Another research activity was measuring the crew\'s heart rates and the station\'s carbon dioxide levels to determine the effect of exercise on the station.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NASApresskit_17-6">\n        <a href="#cite_note-NASApresskit-17">\n         [NASA 12]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n
