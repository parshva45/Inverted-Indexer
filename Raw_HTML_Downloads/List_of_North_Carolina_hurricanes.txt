class="mw-headline" id="Climatology">\n        Climatology\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         [\n        </span>\n        <a class="mw-editsection-bracket">\n         ]\n        </span>\n       </span>\n      </h2>\n      <p>\n       As to statistical hurricane research between 1883 and 1996 by the North Carolina State Climatology Office, a tropical cyclone makes\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Landfall_(meteorology)" title="Landfall (meteorology)">\n        landfall\n       </a>\n       along the coastline about once every four years. An estimated 17.5\xc2\xa0percent of all\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/North_Atlantic_tropical_cyclones" title="North Atlantic tropical cyclones">\n        North Atlantic tropical cyclones\n       </a>\n       have affected the state.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ncclimate_4-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-ncclimate-4">\n         [4]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       Additionally, the remnants of a few\n       <a href="/wiki/Pacific_hurricane" title="Pacific hurricane">\n        Pacific\n       </a>\n       tropical cyclones struck the state.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WPCSE_5-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-WPCSE-5">\n         [5]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       <a href="/wiki/Cape_Hatteras" title="Cape Hatteras">\n        Cape Hatteras\n       </a>\n       is most affected by storms within the state, though\n       <a href="/wiki/Cape_Lookout_(North_Carolina)" title="Cape Lookout (North Carolina)">\n        Cape Lookout\n       </a>\n       and\n       <a href="/wiki/Cape_Fear_(headland)" title="Cape Fear (headland)">\n        Cape Fear\n       </a>\n       are also regularly affected; the increased activity in three areas is because it protrudes from elsewhere along the Atlantic coastline.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ncclimate_4-1">\n        <a href="#cite_note-ncclimate-4">\n         [4]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       After Southern Florida, Cape Hatteras has the lowest return period, or the frequency at which a certain intensity or category of hurricane can be expected within 86\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Statute_mile" title="Statute mile">\n        mi\n       </a>\n       (139\xc2\xa0km) of a given location, in the country.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">\n        <a href="#cite_note-6">\n         [6]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       As the\n       <a href="/wiki/Outer_Banks" title="Outer Banks">\n        Outer Banks\n       </a>\n       are a narrow strip of low-lying land, hurricanes occasionally leave portions of the land partially or fully submerged.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">\n        <a href="#cite_note-7">\n         [7]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       Additionally, the remnants of inland tropical cyclones have produced flooding and landslides in the state\'s western region.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-0069rain_8-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-0069rain-8">\n         [8]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       Tropical cyclones have affected North Carolina in every month between May and December; about 35\xc2\xa0percent of the storms struck the state in September, and 80\xc2\xa0percent affected the state between August and October, which coincides with the peak of the hurricane season. The earliest storm to affect the state was\n       <a href="/wiki/Subtropical_Storm_Andrea_(2007)" title="Subtropical Storm Andrea (2007)">\n        Subtropical Storm Andrea\n       </a>\n       on May 7, 2007, and the latest was a tropical storm that moved across the Outer Banks on December 2, 1925. The strongest storm to strike the state was\n       <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Hazel" title="Hurricane Hazel">\n        Hurricane Hazel\n       </a>\n       on October 15, 1954, which made landfall as a\n       <a href="/wiki/List_of_Category_4_Atlantic_hurricanes" title="List of Category 4 Atlantic hurricanes">\n        Category 4\n       </a>\n       hurricane on the\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saffir%E2%80%93Simpson_Hurricane_Scale" title="Saffir\xe2\x80\x93Simpson Hurricane Scale">\n        Saffir\xe2\x80\x93Simpson Hurricane Scale\n       </a>\n       .\n title="North Carolina">\n        North Carolina\n       </a>\n       . Collectively, cyclones in North Carolina during the time period resulted in over 775\xc2\xa0direct fatalities during the period. Seven cyclones affected the state in the\n       <a href="/wiki/1893_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="1893 Atlantic hurricane season">\n        1893 season\n       </a>\n       , which was the year with the most tropical cyclones devastating the state during the time period. From the beginning of the official Atlantic hurricane record in 1851 to 1899, there were 12\xc2\xa0years without a known tropical cyclone affecting the state.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       Historical data prior to 1700 is sparse due to lack of significant European settlements along the coastline; the few storms listed are largely records from\n       <a href="/wiki/Roanoke_Colony" title="Roanoke Colony">\n        Roanoke Colony\n       </a>\n       and later the\n       <a href="/wiki/Province_of_Carolina" title="Province of Carolina">\n        Province of Carolina\n       </a>\n       . Collectively, cyclones during the time period resulted in 37\xc2\xa0total fatalities during the period, as well as about $3\xc2\xa0billion in damage (2008\xc2\xa0USD). A cyclone affected the state in every year during the time period, and in three seasons a total of five cyclones assailed the state. The strongest hurricane to hit the state during the time period was\n       <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Hazel" title="Hurricane Hazel">\n        Hurricane Hazel\n       </a>\n       , which struck the state as a Category\xc2\xa04 hurricane on the\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saffir%E2%80%93Simpson_Hurricane_Scale" title="Saffir\xe2\x80\x93Simpson Hurricane Scale">\n        Saffir\xe2\x80\x93Simpson Hurricane Scale\n       </a>\n       .
