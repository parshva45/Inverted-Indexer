      The process of assembling the\n       <a href="/wiki/International_Space_Station" title="International Space Station">\n        International Space Station\n       </a>\n       (ISS) has been under way since the 1990s.\n       <i>\n        <a href="/wiki/Zarya" title="Zarya">\n         Zarya\n        </a>\n       </i>\n       , the first ISS module, was launched by a\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Proton_(rocket)" title="Proton (rocket)">\n        Proton rocket\n       </a>\n       on 20 November 1998. The\n       <a href="/wiki/STS-88" title="STS-88">\n        STS-88\n       </a>\n       shuttle mission followed two weeks after\n       <i>\n        Zarya\n       </i>\n       was launched, bringing\n       <i>\n        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Unity_Module" title="Unity Module">\n         Unity\n        </a>\n       </i>\n       , the first of three node modules, and connecting it to\n       <i>\n        Zarya\n       </i>\n       . This bare 2-module core of the ISS remained unmanned for the next one and a half years, until in July 2000 the Russian module\n       <i>\n        <a href="/wiki/Zvezda_(ISS_module)" title="Zvezda (ISS module)">\n         Zvezda\n        </a>\n       </i>\n       was added, allowing a maximum crew of two astronauts or cosmonauts to be on the ISS permanently.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       The ISS has a planned pressurized volume of approximately 1,000 cubic meters, a mass of approximately 400,000 kilograms, approximately 100 kilowatts of power output, a truss 108.4 meters long, modules 74 meters long, and a crew of six. Building the complete station will require more than 40 assembly flights. As of March 2011,\n       <a href="/wiki/Assembly_of_the_International_Space_Station#Assembly_sequence" title="Assembly of the International Space Station">\n        26 Space Shuttle flights\n       </a>\n       have docked with ISS to add elements, and\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_human_spaceflights_to_the_ISS" title="List of human spaceflights to the ISS">\n        9 other\n       </a>\n       Shuttle flights have flown logistics-servicing missions to ISS without adding major external elements. These 35 Shuttle missions include 9\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SpaceHab" title="SpaceHab">\n        SpaceHab\n       </a>\n       and\n       <a href="/wiki/Multi-Purpose_Logistics_Module#Completed_missions" title="Multi-Purpose Logistics Module">\n        10 MPLM\n       </a>\n       logistics-servicing missions in various combinations. The last two planned Shuttle flights are due to add one of the two final elements of ISS, followed by one last Proton launch with the planned delivery of the\n       <a href="/wiki/European_Robotic_Arm" title="European Robotic Arm">\n        ERA\n       </a>\n       . Other assembly flights have consisted of modules lifted by the Russian Proton rocket or in the case of\n       <a href="/wiki/Pirs_(ISS_module)" title="Pirs (ISS module)">\n        Pirs\n       </a>\n       and\n       <a href="/wiki/Poisk_(ISS_module)" title="Poisk (ISS module)">\n        Poisk\n       </a>\n       by a\n       <a href="/wiki/Soyuz-U" title="Soyuz-U">\n        Soyuz-U\n       </a>\n       rocket.\n       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">\n        [\n        <i>\n         <a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atmospheric_drag" title="Atmospheric drag">\n        atmospheric drag\n       </a>\n       and\n       <a href="/wiki/Reboost" title="Reboost">\n        reboosts\n       </a>\n       ). It orbits Earth in a\n       <a href="/wiki/Orbital_period" title="Orbital period">\n        period\n       </a>\n       of about 90 minutes; by August 2007 it had completed more than 50,000 orbits since launch of\n       <i>\n        Zarya\n       </i>\n       on 20 November 1998.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       A total of 14 main pressurized modules are scheduled to be part of the ISS by its completion date in 2010.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-launch_manifest_1-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-launch_manifest-1">\n         [1]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       A number of smaller pressurized sections will be adjunct to them (\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Soyuz_spacecraft" title="Soyuz spacecraft">\n        Soyuz spacecraft\n       </a>\n       (permanently 2 as lifeboats - 6 months rotations),\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Progress_spacecraft" title="Progress spacecraft">\n        Progress\n       </a>\n       transporters (2 or more), the\n       <a href="/wiki/Quest_Joint_Airlock" title="Quest Joint Airlock">\n        Quest\n       </a>\n       and Pirs airlocks, as well as periodically the\n       <a href="/wiki/Multi-Purpose_Logistics_Module" title="Multi-Purpose Logistics Module">\n        Multi-Purpose Logistics Module\n       </a>\n       , the\n       <a href="/wiki/Automated_Transfer_Vehicle" title="Automated Transfer Vehicle">\n        Automated Transfer Vehicle\n       </a>\n       and the\n       <a href="/wiki/H-II_Transfer_Vehicle" title="H-II Transfer Vehicle">\n        H-II Transfer Vehicle\n       </a>\n       ).\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       The ISS, when completed, will consist of a set of communicating pressurized modules connected to a\n       <a href="/wiki/Integrated_Truss_Structure" title="Integrated Truss Structure">\n        truss\n       </a>\n       , on which four large pairs of\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Photovoltaic" title="Photovoltaic">\n        photovoltaic\n       </a>\n       modules (solar panels) are attached. The pressurized modules and the truss will be perpendicular: the truss spanning from\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Starboard" title="Starboard">\n        starboard\n       </a>\n       to\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Port_(nautical)" title="Port (nautical)">\n        port\n       </a>\n       and the habitable zone extending on the\n       <a href="/wiki/Aft" title="Aft">\n        aft\n       </a>\n       -forward axis. Although during the construction the station\n       <a href="/wiki/Attitude_control" title="Attitude control">\n        attitude\n       </a>\n       may vary, when all four photovoltaic modules are in their definitive position the aft-forward axis will be parallel to the velocity vector.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">\n        <a href="#cite_note-2">\n         [2]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       In addition to the assembly and utilization flights, approximately 30 Progress spacecraft flights are required to provide logistics until 2010. Experimental equipment, fuel and consumables are and will be delivered by all vehicles visiting the ISS: the Shuttle, the\n       <a href="/wiki/SpaceX_Dragon" title="SpaceX Dragon">\n        SpaceX Dragon\n       </a>\n       , the Russian Progress, the European\n       <a href="/wiki/Automated_Transfer_Vehicle" title="Automated Transfer Vehicle">\n        ATV\n       </a>\n       and the Japanese\n       <a href="/wiki/H-II_Transfer_Vehicle" title="H-II Transfer Vehicle">\n        HTV\n       </a>\n       , and space station\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Downmass" title="Downmass">\n        downmass\n       </a>\n       will be carried back to Earth facilities on both the Shuttle and the Dragon.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sen20121224_3-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-sen20121224-3">\n         [3]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <h2>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Columbia_disaster_and_changes_in_construction_plans">\n </div>\n      <p>\n       At one point, there was some uncertainty over the future of the ISS. The\n       <a href="/wiki/Space_Shuttle_Columbia_disaster" title="Space Shuttle Columbia disaster">\n        Space Shuttle\n        <i>\n         Columbia\n        </i>\n        disaster\n       </a>\n       on 1 February 2003, the subsequent two and a half-year suspension of the U.S.\n       <a href="/wiki/Space_Shuttle_program" title="Space Shuttle program">\n        Space Shuttle program\n       </a>\n       , followed by problems with resuming flight operations in 2005, were major obstacles.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       The Space Shuttle program resumed flight on 26 July 2005, with the\n       <a href="/wiki/STS-114" title="STS-114">\n        STS-114\n       </a>\n       mission of\n       <a href="/wiki/Space_Shuttle_Discovery" title="Space Shuttle Discovery">\n        <i>\n         Discovery\n        </i>\n       </a>\n       . This mission to the ISS was intended both to test new safety measures implemented since the\n       <i>\n        Columbia\n       </i>\n       disaster, and to deliver supplies to the station. Although the mission succeeded safely, it was not without risk; foam was shed by the\n       <a href="/wiki/Space_Shuttle_external_tank" title="Space Shuttle external tank">\n        external tank\n       </a>\n       , leading NASA to announce future missions would be grounded until this issue was resolved.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       Between the\n       <i>\n        Columbia\n       </i>\n       disaster and the resumption of Shuttle launches, crew exchanges were carried out solely using the Russian\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Soyuz_spacecraft" title="Soyuz spacecraft">\n        Soyuz spacecraft\n       </a>\n       . Starting with\n       <a href="/wiki/Expedition_7" title="Expedition 7">\n        Expedition 7\n       </a>\n       , two-astronaut caretaker crews were launched in contrast to the previously launched crews of three. Because the ISS had not been visited by a shuttle for an extended period, a larger than planned amount of waste accumulated, temporarily hindering station operations in 2004. However Progress transports and the\n       <a href="/wiki/STS-114" title="STS-114">\n        STS-114\n       </a>\n       shuttle flight took care of this problem.\n      </p>\n      <h3>\n over New Zealand.\n        </div>\n       </div>\n      </div>\n      <p>\n       ISS construction experienced delays due to the halting of all NASA Shuttle flights following the\n       <a href="/wiki/Space_Shuttle_Columbia_disaster" title="Space Shuttle Columbia disaster">\n        <i>\n         Columbia\n        </i>\n        disaster\n       </a>\n       in early 2003, although there had been prior delays due to Shuttle problems, and the Russian space agency\'s budget constraints. During the shuttle stand-down, construction of the ISS was halted and the science conducted aboard was limited due to the crew size of two. Many changes were made to the originally planned ISS, even before the\n       <i>\n        Columbia\n       </i>\n       disaster. Modules and other structures were cancelled or replaced and the number of Shuttle flights to the ISS was reduced from previously planned numbers. However more than 80% of the hardware intended to be part of the ISS in the late 1990s was orbited and is now part of the ISS\'s configuration.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       In March 2006, a meeting of the heads of the five participating space agencies accepted the new ISS construction schedule that planned to complete the ISS by 2010.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">\n        <a href="#cite_note-4">\n         [4]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       A crew of six has been established as of May 2009, following 12 Shuttle construction flights after the second "Return to Flight" mission\n       <a href="/wiki/STS-121" title="STS-121">\n        STS-121\n       </a>\n       . Requirements for stepping up the crew size included enhanced environmental support on the ISS, a second Soyuz permanently docked on the station to function as a second \'lifeboat\', more frequent Progress flights to provide double the amount of consumables, more fuel for orbit raising maneuvers, and a sufficient supply line of experimental equipment.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       Later additions included the\n       <a href="/wiki/Bigelow_Expandable_Activity_Module" title="Bigelow Expandable Activity Module">\n        Bigelow Expandable Activity Module\n       </a>\n       (BEAM) in 2016, and numerous Russian components are planned as part of the in-orbit construction of\n       <a href="/wiki/Orbital_Piloted_Assembly_and_Experiment_Complex" title="Orbital Piloted Assembly and and one European module (\n       <a href="/wiki/Columbus_(ISS_module)" title="Columbus (ISS module)">\n        <i>\n         Columbus\n        </i>\n       </a>\n       ). One more Russian pressurized module (\n       <a href="/wiki/Nauka_(ISS_module)" title="Nauka (ISS module)">\n        <i>\n         Nauka\n        </i>\n       </a>\n       ) is scheduled to be added to the station.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       Although not permanently docked with the ISS,\n       <a href="/wiki/Multi-Purpose_Logistics_Module" title="Multi-Purpose Logistics Module">\n        Multi-Purpose Logistics Modules\n       </a>\n       (MPLMs) form part of the ISS during some Shuttle missions.
