       Tracks of all known Category 5 Atlantic hurricanes between 1851 and 2014\n        </div>\n       </div>\n      </div>\n      <p>\n       A Category\xc2\xa05 Atlantic hurricane is one that is considered by the United States\n       <a href="/wiki/National_Hurricane_Center" title="National Hurricane Center">\n        National Hurricane Center\n       </a>\n       (NHC), to have had sustained wind speeds greater than 136 knots (157\xc2\xa0mph; 252\xc2\xa0km/h; 70\xc2\xa0m/s) on the\n       <a href="/wiki/Saffir%E2%80%93Simpson_scale" title="Saffir\xe2\x80\x93Simpson scale">\n        Saffir\xe2\x80\x93Simpson scale\n       </a>\n       . The NHC considers sustained wind speeds to be those that occur over a one-minute period at 10 metres (32.8\xc2\xa0ft) above ground. These wind speeds are estimated by using a blend of data from a variety of sources, which include observations from nearby ships, reconnaissance aircraft, or automatic weather stations and pictures from various satellites.\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       Between\n       <a href="/wiki/1924_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="1924 Atlantic hurricane season">\n        1924\n       </a>\n       and\n       <a href="/wiki/2017_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="2017 Atlantic hurricane season">\n        2017\n       </a>\n       , 33 hurricanes were recorded at Category\xc2\xa05 strength. No Category\xc2\xa05 hurricanes were observed officially before 1924. It can be presumed that earlier storms reached Category\xc2\xa05 strength over open waters, but the strongest winds were not measured. The\n       <a href="/wiki/Anemometer" title="Anemometer">\n        anemometer\n       </a>\n       , a device used for measuring wind speed, was invented in 1846. However, during major hurricane strikes the instruments as a whole were often blown away, leaving the hurricane\'s peak intensity unrecorded. For example, as the\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Great_Beaufort_Hurricane" title="Great Beaufort Hurricane">\n        Great Beaufort Hurricane\n       </a>\n       of 1879 struck North Carolina, the anemometer cups were blown away when indicating 138\xc2\xa0mph (222\xc2\xa0km/h).\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hudgins_2-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-Hudgins-2">\n         [2]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       A\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atlantic_hurricane_reanalysis" title="Atlantic hurricane reanalysis">\n        reanalysis\n       </a>\n       of weather data is ongoing by researchers who may upgrade or downgrade other Atlantic hurricanes currently listed at Categories 4 and 5.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-reanalysis_3-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-reanalysis-3">\n         [3]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       For example, the\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/1825_Santa_Ana_hurricane" title="1825 Santa Ana hurricane">\n        1825 Santa Ana hurricane\n       </a>\n       is suspected to have reached Category 5 strength.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">\n        <a href="#cite_note-4">\n         [4]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       Furthermore,\n       <a href="/wiki/Paleotempestology" title="Paleotempestology">\n        paleotempestological\n       </a>\n       research aims to identify past major hurricanes by comparing sedimentary evidence of recent and past hurricane strikes. For example, a "giant hurricane" significantly more powerful than\n       <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Hattie" title="Hurricane Hattie">\n        Hurricane Hattie\n       </a>\n       (Category 5) has been identified in\n       <a href="/wiki/Belize" title="Belize">\n        Belizean\n       </a>\n       sediment, having struck the region sometime\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pre-1600_Atlantic_hurricane_seasons" title="Pre-1600 Atlantic hurricane seasons">\n        before 1500\n       </a>\n       .\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mccloskey2009_5-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-Mccloskey2009-5">\n         [5]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <div class="thumb tright">\n       <div class="thumbinner" decade with the most Category\xc2\xa05 hurricanes is 2000\xe2\x80\x932009, with eight Category\xc2\xa05 hurricanes having occurred: Isabel (2003), Ivan (2004), Emily (2005), Katrina (2005), Rita (2005), Wilma (2005), Dean (2007), and Felix (2007). The previous decades with the most Category\xc2\xa05 hurricanes were the 1930s and 1960s, with six occurring between 1930 and 1939 (before naming began).\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT_1-1">\n        <a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-1">\n         [1]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       Nine Atlantic hurricanes\xe2\x80\x94\n       <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Camille" title="Hurricane Camille">\n        Camille\n       </a>\n       ,\n       <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Allen" title="Hurricane Allen">\n        Allen\n       </a>\n       ,\n       <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Andrew" title="Hurricane Andrew">\n        Andrew\n       </a>\n       ,\n       <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Isabel" title="Hurricane Isabel">\n        Isabel\n       </a>\n       ,\n       <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Ivan" title="Hurricane Ivan">\n        Ivan\n       </a>\n       ,\n       <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Dean" title="Hurricane Dean">\n        Dean\n       </a>\n       ,\n       <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Felix" title="Hurricane Felix">\n        Felix\n       </a>\n       ,\n       <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Irma" title="Hurricane Irma">\n        Irma\n       </a>\n       and\n       <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Maria" title="Hurricane Maria">\n        Maria\n       </a>\n       \xe2\x80\x94reached Category\xc2\xa05 intensity on more than one occasion; that is, by reaching Category\xc2\xa05 intensity, weakening to a Category\xc2\xa04 or lower, and then becoming a Category\xc2\xa05 again. Such hurricanes have their dates shown together. Camille, Andrew, Dean, Felix, Irma, and Maria each attained Category 5 status twice during their lifespans. Allen, Isabel, and Ivan reached Category 5 intensity on three separate occasions. However, no Atlantic hurricane has reached Category\xc2\xa05 intensity more than three times during its lifespan. The November 1932 Cuba hurricane holds the record for most time spent as a Category\xc2\xa05 (although it took place before satellite or aircraft reconnaissance, so this record may be somewhat suspect). Irma holds the record for longest continuous span as a Category 5 storm in the satellite era.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT_1-2">\n        <a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-1">\n         [1]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Andrew_reanalysis_6-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-Andrew_reanalysis-6">\n         [6]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       Thirty-three Category\xc2\xa05s have been recorded in the\n       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atlantic_basin" title="Atlantic basin">\n        Atlantic basin\n       </a>\n       since 1851, when records began. Only one Category 5 has been recorded in July, eight in August, nineteen in September, five in October, and one in November. There have been no officially recorded June or off-season Category\xc2\xa05 hurricanes.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT_1-3">\n        <a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-1">\n         [1]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       The July and August Category\xc2\xa05s reached their high intensities in both the\n       <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_Mexico" title="Gulf of Mexico">\n        Gulf of Mexico\n       </a>\n       and the\n       <a href="/wiki/Caribbean_Sea" title="Caribbean Sea">\n        Caribbean Sea\n       </a>\n       . These are the areas most favorable for tropical cyclone development in those months.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT_1-4">\n        <a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-1">\n         [1]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-climatology_7-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-climatology-7">\n         [7]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       September sees the most Category\xc2\xa05 hurricanes. This coincides with the climatological peak of the Atlantic hurricane season, which occurs in early September.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-September_8-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-September-8">\n         [8]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       September Category\xc2\xa05s reached their strengths in any of the\n       <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_Mexico" title="Gulf of Mexico">\n        Gulf of Mexico\n       </a>\n       ,\n       <a href="/wiki/Caribbean_Sea" title="Caribbean Sea">\n        Caribbean Sea\n       </a>\n       , and open Atlantic. These places are where September tropical cyclones are likely to form.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-climatology_7-1">\n        <a href="#cite_note-climatology-7">\n         [7]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       Many of these hurricanes are either\n       <a href="/wiki/Cape_Verde_hurricane" title="Cape Verde hurricane">\n        Cape Verde-type storms\n       </a>\n       , which develop their strength by having a great deal of open water; or so-called Bahama busters, which intensify over the warm\n       <a href="/wiki/Loop_Current" title="Loop Current">\n        Loop Current\n       </a>\n       in the Gulf of Mexico.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CapeVerde_9-0">\n        <a href="#cite_note-CapeVerde-9">\n         [9]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n      </p>\n      <p>\n       All six Category\xc2\xa05s in October and November reached their intensities in the western Caribbean, a region that Atlantic hurricanes strongly gravitate toward late in the season.\n       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-climatology_7-2">\n        <a href="#cite_note-climatology-7">\n         [7]\n        </a>\n       </sup>\n       This is due to the climatology of the area, which sometimes has a high-altitude\n       <a href="/wiki/Anticyclone" title="Anticyclone">\n        anticyclone\n       </a>\n       that promotes rapid intensification late in the season, as well as warm waters.\n      </p>\n      <h2>\n       <span class="mw-headline" id="Systems">\n        Systems\n       </span>\n       <span class="mw-editsection">\n        <span
